Section 1 · Drug Discovery, Development & Clinical Evaluation

Covers drug discovery, from medicinal plants to computer-aided drug design.

▸★★★Discuss in detail the complete process of new drug discovery and development (methods, preclinical, clinical phases, regulatory, recent advances). Add a note on microdosing & ethics.

Definition: Drug development is the systematic, regulated, multi-stage conversion of a validated biological target into a safe and effective marketed medicine. G&G frames it as moving "from medicinal plants to computer-aided drug design." It takes ~10–15 years and >US$1–2.5 billion, with attrition of ~1 in 5000–10000 screened molecules.

I. Target identification & validation

  • Target identification: a protein/gene/pathway whose modulation alters disease — from genomics, proteomics, GWAS, disease biology.
  • Target validation: proving modulation produces benefit — knockout/knock-in animals, siRNA/CRISPR, human genetic evidence.
  • Druggability: does the target possess a binding pocket amenable to a small molecule or biologic?

II. Methods / approaches to drug discovery

ApproachPrinciple / Example
High-Throughput Screening (HTS)Robotic assay of large compound libraries (10⁵–10⁶) against a target; gives "hits."
Rational / structure-based designDesigning ligand to fit a known 3-D target structure (X-ray, cryo-EM). e.g. HIV protease inhibitors, captopril.
SAR (structure–activity relationship)Systematic chemical modification of a lead to optimise potency/selectivity/PK.
CADD (computer-aided drug design)In-silico docking, molecular dynamics, pharmacophore & QSAR modelling, virtual screening.
Fragment-based designSmall low-affinity fragments grown/linked into a high-affinity lead (e.g. vemurafenib).

III. Preclinical evaluation

  • Pharmacodynamics/PK/ADME in cell lines and ≥2 animal species (one rodent + one non-rodent).
  • Toxicity battery: acute (single dose, LD₅₀ concept), sub-acute & chronic, reproductive/teratogenicity, mutagenicity (Ames), carcinogenicity, safety pharmacology.
  • Alternatives & 3R/4R: Replacement, Reduction, Refinement (+ Rehabilitation as 4th R) — in-vitro, organ-on-chip, in-silico, organoids.
  • Indian oversight: CPCSEA / CCSEA guidelines and the IAEC (Institutional Animal Ethics Committee) approve animal protocols.

IV. Investigational New Drug (IND) → clinical trials

After successful preclinical data, an IND (US-FDA) / application under New Drugs & Clinical Trials Rules 2019 + erstwhile Schedule Y (India, CDSCO/DCGI) is filed before human dosing.

PhaseSubjectsAim
Phase 0 (microdosing)6–15Sub-therapeutic dose; early human PK/PD; "exploratory IND."
Phase I20–80 healthy volunteersSafety, tolerability, PK, MTD, dose-escalation.
Phase II100–300 patientsEfficacy proof-of-concept & dose-finding (IIa/IIb).
Phase III1000–3000 patientsConfirmatory efficacy & safety vs standard; basis for approval (NDA).
Phase IVPost-marketingPharmacovigilance, rare ADRs, new indications.

V. Trial design concepts

  • Adaptive design: pre-planned modification of design (dose, sample size, arms) using accumulating data — efficient, ethical (e.g. RECOVERY, SOLIDARITY for COVID).
  • Sequential design: data analysed as they accrue; trial stopped when significance/futility reached.
  • Crossover design: each subject receives both treatments in sequence (own control), needs washout; good for chronic stable conditions.
  • Protocol deviation = minor unplanned departure not affecting subject rights/safety/data integrity; Protocol violation = serious departure that does (e.g. enrolling ineligible subject, dosing error).

VI. Regulatory & GCP

IND/NDA, ICH-GCP, ethics-committee (IEC) approval, informed consent, audit-trail, ICMR ethical guidelines (2017), and post-approval surveillance.

Recent advances: AI-discovered candidates entering trials (Insilico's INS018_055 for IPF), AlphaFold-driven structure-based design, master/platform & basket-umbrella trials, decentralised & real-world-evidence trials, and India's CDSCO online SUGAM portal.

Note — Microdosing (Phase 0)

Administration of ≤1/100th of the pharmacologically active dose (≤100 µg) to obtain early human PK using ultrasensitive assays (AMS, PET). Advantages: human data earlier, reduced cost, less animal toxicity testing, early de-selection of poor candidates. Ethics: minimal risk yet still requires consent & IEC approval; cannot give efficacy data and is not predictive at therapeutic doses (non-linear PK).

▸★Principles, applications, advantages & limitations of microdosing (Phase 0) studies.

Definition/principle: Phase 0 "exploratory IND" giving a microdose — ≤100 µg or ≤1/100th of the dose predicted to give a pharmacological effect — to a few humans (6–15) to obtain early PK/PD before full Phase I.

Detection methods

  • Accelerator Mass Spectrometry (AMS) with ¹⁴C-label.
  • PET for receptor occupancy/tissue distribution.
  • Ultra-sensitive LC-MS/MS.

Applications

  • Early human PK & bioavailability; candidate ranking/selection.
  • Confirm target engagement/receptor occupancy (PET).
  • Reduce late-stage attrition due to poor PK.

Advantages

  • Human data earlier; lower cost and animal use; faster go/no-go decisions; minimal subject risk.

Limitations

  • No efficacy or toxicity information.
  • Non-linear PK → microdose may not predict therapeutic-dose kinetics.
  • Requires costly specialised assays (AMS/PET); not suitable for drugs with active metabolites or saturable processes.

AMS vs PET: AMS uses ¹⁴C-label (t½≈5740 yr) — chiefly PK, samples assayable up to ~100 days post-dose. PET uses ¹¹C-label (t½≈20 min) — chiefly PD/real-time imaging, useful PK window only ~2 h. Neither distinguishes parent drug from metabolite alone — LC-MS chromatographic separation is added.

Recent advances: Intra-Target Microdosing (ITM) delivers drug to a small (~1% body) compartment at locally therapeutic concentration, yielding PD/target-engagement data — not PK alone — from a Phase 0 study [PMID 39210357]. A ¹⁴C-microtracer (10 mg, 14.8 kBq) of AZD4831 read by AMS recovered ~84% of dose and mapped UGT1A1-derived metabolism at a therapeutically-relevant dose with very low radiolabel activity [PMID 36639243]; co-administered IV ¹⁴C-microtracer (30 µg) with inhaled velsecorat (720 µg) resolved clearance/Vd/metabolite pattern in one ADME study [PMID 34853068]. FDA's Exploratory IND (eIND) framework remains the regulatory basis: microdose ≤1/100th predicted active dose and ≤100 µg, read by AMS/PET/LC-MS/MS [FDA eIND].

Phase I designs

Adaptive design

Pre-specified modification of trial features using interim data — dose-dropping, sample-size re-estimation, response-adaptive randomisation, seamless Phase II/III.

  • Advantages: efficient, ethical (more patients to better arm), faster, fewer subjects, cost-saving.
  • Challenges: risk of bias/type-I error inflation, statistical & logistic complexity, need for fast data capture and regulatory acceptance, operational unblinding risk.
  • Examples: I-SPY 2 (breast cancer), RECOVERY & SOLIDARITY (COVID-19).
▸★Constitution, functions & role of IAEC and CPCSEA guidelines. Compare CCSEA and ARRIVE guidelines.

CPCSEA / CCSEA

CPCSEA (Committee for the Purpose of Control & Supervision of Experiments on Animals), now renamed CCSEA (Committee for Control & Supervision of Experiments on Animals), is a statutory body under the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Act 1960 (MoEFCC). It registers establishments, frames guidelines, and oversees ethical animal use.

IAEC — constitution

Each institution forms an Institutional Animal Ethics Committee with (typically):

  • Chairperson; Member-Secretary; Biological/Veterinary scientist(s).
  • Scientist from another discipline; a scientist from outside the institute.
  • CCSEA-nominated main & link nominee (mandatory) — a non-affiliated member / socially aware person.

Functions

  • Review & approve animal-use protocols (justification, numbers, species, endpoints).
  • Ensure 3R compliance, humane care, anaesthesia/analgesia, euthanasia standards.
  • Inspect facilities; maintain records; can suspend non-compliant projects.

▸★Contribution of animal experimentation in biomedical research — limitations & alternatives (3R/4R).

Contributions

  • Understanding physiology/pathology; disease models (diabetes, hypertension, cancer xenografts).
  • Drug efficacy (PD), PK/ADME, and the toxicity battery before human exposure.
  • Vaccine & biologic development; surgical technique training; insulin, antibiotics, polio vaccine historically.

Limitations

  • Species differences in PK/PD → poor human predictivity (e.g. TGN1412 disaster despite animal safety).
  • Ethical concerns & cost; stress/handling artefacts; small numbers; reproducibility issues.

Alternatives & the 3R/4R principle (Russell & Burch, 1959)

PrincipleMeaning / example
ReplacementUse non-animal methods: in-vitro cell lines, organoids, organ-on-chip, in-silico/QSAR, microdosing in humans.
ReductionFewer animals via better statistical design & shared data.
RefinementMinimise pain/distress — analgesia, anaesthesia, humane endpoints, enrichment.
Rehabilitation (4th R)Rehoming/rehabilitating animals after experiments where possible.
Recent: US FDA Modernization Act 2.0 (2022) permits non-animal alternatives (organ-chips, AI models) for IND data — a major shift away from mandatory animal testing.

Section 2 · Pharmacokinetics (ADME, TDM & Individualisation)

Covers pharmacokinetics, membrane transporters, drug metabolism, and pharmacogenetics.

▸★★★Pharmacokinetics in detail — ADME, membrane transport, bioavailability, Vd, clearance, kinetics (1st/0-order), metabolism (Phase I/II, inducers/inhibitors), excretion, chrono- & toxicokinetics, genetic polymorphism.

Definition: Pharmacokinetics = "what the body does to the drug" — the quantitative study of Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism and Excretion (ADME). G&G: PK determines the concentration of drug at its site of action over time.

Membrane transport mechanisms

  • Passive diffusion (lipid-soluble, unionised drugs; governed by Fick's law (rate ∝ concentration gradient × area / thickness) and the pH-partition hypothesis — Henderson-Hasselbalch predicts that the unionised fraction of a weak acid/base diffuses across membranes; weak acids absorbed better in acidic stomach, weak bases in alkaline intestine).
  • Filtration through aqueous channels/paracellular pores (small molecules, e.g. glomerular filtration) & facilitated diffusion (carrier-assisted, no ATP, e.g. GLUT transporters).
  • Active transport — carrier-mediated, ATP-dependent, against a gradient; saturable & competitively inhibitable. Two transporter superfamilies: ABC (ATP-Binding Cassette) efflux pumps (P-glycoprotein/MDR1, BCRP, MRP2) and SLC (Solute Carrier) influx/efflux carriers (OATP, OCT, OAT, PEPT1) (G&G Ch.4). Secondary active transport (symport/antiport) uses an ion gradient (e.g. Na⁺) rather than direct ATP hydrolysis.
  • Vesicular transport — endocytosis/pinocytosis for large molecules (peptides, nanoparticles).
  • P-glycoprotein is the prototype ABC efflux pump — limits oral bioavailability & CNS penetration, mediates multidrug resistance, and a major site of drug–drug interactions (verapamil, ketoconazole inhibit it).

A — Absorption & Bioavailability (F)

Bioavailability = fraction of administered dose reaching systemic circulation unchanged. IV = 100%. Reduced by first-pass metabolism (gut/liver), poor solubility, efflux. F = AUC(oral)/AUC(IV). Factors: formulation, GI pH/motility, food, surface area, blood flow.

D — Distribution & Volume of distribution (Vd)

Vd = Dose / plasma concentration (C₀) — apparent volume relating amount of drug in body to plasma concentration. High Vd = extensive tissue binding (e.g. digoxin ~500 L, chloroquine huge); low Vd = vascular/protein-bound (warfarin). Affected by plasma-protein binding (albumin, α1-acid glycoprotein), tissue binding, lipid solubility, BBB/placenta.

M — Metabolism (biotransformation)

Clinical importance: inducers ↓ effect (e.g. OC pill failure with rifampicin); inhibitors ↑ toxicity (e.g. ↑warfarin INR). Basis of many drug–drug interactions.

E — Excretion

  • Renal: glomerular filtration, active tubular secretion, passive reabsorption (pH-dependent — alkalinise urine to excrete acidic aspirin/phenobarbitone).
  • Biliary & enterohepatic recirculation; lungs (volatiles); milk, sweat, saliva.

Kinetics & clearance

  • First-order: constant fraction eliminated/unit time; rate ∝ concentration; constant t½ (most drugs).
  • Zero-order: constant amount eliminated; saturable; e.g. phenytoin, ethanol, aspirin (high dose), warfarin, theophylline — small dose change → large concentration rise.
  • Clearance (CL) = volume of plasma cleared/unit time; CL = rate of elimination/C = Dose/AUC; determines maintenance dose (dosing rate = CL × Css). Clearances are additive (CLtotal = CLrenal + CLhepatic + CLother).
  • CL and Vd are the two truly independent ("primary") PK parameters; t½ and the elimination rate constant (k) are derived/secondary — t½ = 0.693 × Vd / CL and k = CL/Vd = 0.693/t½, so t½ rises if CL falls or Vd rises (either can change independently in disease). Steady state — and ~94% drug elimination — is reached in ~4–5 half-lives, independent of dose.
  • Extraction ratio (E) = (Carterial−Cvenous)/Carterial. High-extraction drugs (E→1: lidocaine, propranolol, morphine, diltiazem, imipramine) have flow-limited hepatic clearance (sensitive to hepatic blood flow); low-extraction drugs have capacity-limited clearance (sensitive to enzyme activity/protein binding).

Special kinetics

  • Chronokinetics: circadian variation in ADME (e.g. evening statin/antihypertensive dosing).
  • Toxicokinetics: PK at toxic doses — saturation, altered protein binding, prolonged t½ (overdose management).
  • Genetic polymorphism: CYP2D6 (poor/ultra-rapid metabolisers — codeine), CYP2C19 (clopidogrel/PPIs), NAT2 (isoniazid fast/slow acetylators), TPMT (azathioprine), DPYD (5-FU) — basis of pharmacogenomics.
Recent: Physiologically-Based PK (PBPK) modelling & model-informed drug development now guide dosing in special populations and DDI prediction for regulatory submissions. Model-informed precision dosing (MIPD) using population-PK models (e.g. the Eleveld model) individualises propofol TCI dosing in obese patients; AUC-guided (clearance-based) vancomycin dosing significantly lowers nephrotoxicity versus old trough-only targeting (now the 2020 consensus standard); 2024–25 guidance favours race-free eGFR over Cockcroft-Gault creatinine clearance for renal dose adjustment.
▸★★Define TDM. Indications, sampling strategy, interpretation, dose-regimen calculation, applications for specific drugs.

Definition: Therapeutic Drug Monitoring is the measurement of drug concentration in body fluids (usually plasma) at designated intervals to individualise dosage and keep levels within the therapeutic range.

Indications (when to do TDM)

  • Narrow therapeutic index; poor concentration–dose correlation; zero-order kinetics (phenytoin).
  • No clear clinical endpoint; suspected toxicity vs under-dosing; non-adherence.
  • Altered PK — renal/hepatic disease, paediatric/geriatric/pregnancy, drug interactions.

Sampling strategy

  • Trough just before next dose (most useful for efficacy/toxicity); peak for aminoglycoside efficacy.
  • Sample at steady state (after 4–5 t½); for digoxin sample ≥6–8 h post-dose (distribution complete).

Interpretation & dose calculation

Adjust using linear (first-order) proportionality: New dose = current dose × (target conc / measured conc). Maintenance dose = Css × CL; Loading dose = Css × Vd. Caution with phenytoin (non-linear — use Michaelis-Menten, small increments).

▸★Drug-dosage individualisation in special populations (paediatric, geriatric, renal & hepatic impairment) & role of pharmacogenomics.

Paediatric

  • Immature CYP/UGT (neonatal "gray baby" with chloramphenicol), higher body water, ↓protein binding, immature renal function.
  • Dose by body weight (mg/kg) or BSA; formulae — Clark's, Young's, BSA-based.

Geriatric

  • ↓ renal clearance & hepatic mass/flow, ↑ fat → ↑ Vd of lipophilic drugs & t½, ↑ sensitivity (CNS, anticoagulants), polypharmacy.
  • "Start low, go slow"; use Beers criteria; estimate renal function (Cockcroft-Gault).

Renal impairment

  • Reduce dose / lengthen interval per eGFR/CrCl for renally-cleared drugs (aminoglycosides, digoxin, metformin, vancomycin); avoid nephrotoxins.

Hepatic impairment

  • ↓ metabolism & first-pass, hypoalbuminaemia, altered protein binding; use Child-Pugh to guide; avoid hepatotoxins; caution with high-extraction drugs.

Section 3 · Pharmacodynamics — Receptors, Signaling & Dose-Response

Covers pharmacodynamics and the molecular mechanisms of drug action.

▸★★★Explain the receptor concept of drug action. Classify receptor types (location, signal transduction, an agonist & antagonist each, clinical importance). Discuss GPCRs in detail (G-protein types & second messengers).

Receptor concept: A receptor is a macromolecule (usually protein) that recognises and binds a drug/ligand to initiate a response. "Receptors determine quantitative relations between dose and pharmacological effect; they are responsible for selectivity; and they mediate the actions of agonists and antagonists" (Ehrlich's "corpora non agunt nisi fixata"; occupancy theory of Clark). Key terms: affinity, intrinsic activity/efficacy, spare receptors, agonist, antagonist.

DRC vs log-DRC: plotting response against log dose converts the hyperbola into a symmetrical sigmoid, expands the low-dose region, and gives a straight middle segment — easier to compare EC₅₀ and parallelism (agonist/antagonist analysis).

Key definitions

  • Potency: dose required for a given effect (position on x-axis; lower EC₅₀ = more potent). e.g. bumetanide more potent than furosemide; morphine 10 mg ≈ pethidine 100 mg (morphine 10× more potent).
  • Efficacy (intrinsic activity): maximal effect achievable (Emax; height of curve). e.g. morphine > codeine; loop diuretics > thiazides. Efficacy > potency clinically.
  • Potency & efficacy are independent: aspirin is less potent and less efficacious than morphine; pethidine is less potent but equally efficacious as morphine; furosemide is less potent but more efficacious than metolazone (high ceiling — useful in renal failure); diazepam is more potent but less efficacious a CNS depressant than pentobarbitone (lower efficacy → relative overdose safety).
  • Spare receptors (receptor reserve, Stephenson 1956): a full agonist can produce the maximal tissue response while occupying <1% of receptors — the surplus shifts the log-DRC leftward (↑ apparent potency), so occupancy and response are not linearly related and a concentration-effect curve cannot directly measure agonist affinity.
  • Therapeutic Index (TI) = TD₅₀ / ED₅₀ (or LD₅₀/ED₅₀). Higher = safer. Low-TI drugs: digoxin, warfarin, lithium, phenytoin, aminoglycosides. Worked example: LD₅₀ 400, ED₅₀ 100 → TI = 4.
  • Certain safety factor = LD₁/ED₉₉ — uses curve extremes (not medians) to account for individual variability that the median-ratio TI ignores; more conservative than TI.
  • Therapeutic window: concentration range between minimum effective and minimum toxic concentration — a more clinically useful safety measure than TI; acceptable width depends on disease severity (wide for headache, narrow-but-tolerated for life-threatening disease).
Diagram: Log-dose (x) vs % response (y): two sigmoid curves — ED₅₀ (efficacy) and TD₅₀/LD₅₀; gap between them = therapeutic window/safety margin.
Recent: FDA's Project Optimus initiative shifts oncology dose-selection away from the classical maximum-tolerated-dose (MTD) approach toward an "optimal dose" balancing efficacy and long-term tolerability; reviews find approved oncology doses are often well above this optimum, supporting model-informed dose individualisation. ▸★★★SERMs and SPRMs — classification, molecular basis of tissue selectivity, individual-agent pharmacokinetics, clinical applications & recent advances.

SERMs — definition & molecular basis of tissue selectivity

SERMs are non-steroidal compounds that bind the estrogen receptor (ER) and act as estrogen agonists in some tissues (bone, liver, CVS) and antagonists in others (breast, and in some agents the endometrium) — contrasted with pure agonists (estradiol), pure antiestrogens (clomiphene) and SERDs/selective ER down-regulators (fulvestrant), which act uniformly across tissues.

  • All SERMs competitively occupy the ER ligand-binding pocket (ERα/ERβ), but the ligand–receptor conformation differs for each drug (estradiol vs tamoxifen vs raloxifene), especially in the AF-2 (activation function-2) domain — this dictates coactivator vs corepressor recruitment at the ERE on a given gene promoter.
  • Tamoxifen's residual endometrial agonist activity is mediated by the ligand-independent AF-1 domain of ERα (ERβ lacks functional AF-1, so tamoxifen cannot activate it); raloxifene induces a distinct ERα conformation acting via a separate "raloxifene response element" (RRE).
  • Because coactivator/corepressor levels differ by cell type, the same drug switches between agonist and antagonist across tissues.

Section 4 · Novel & Targeted Drug Delivery Systems (NDDS)

Supplemented from standard pharmaceutics/pharmacology references.

▸★★★What are NDDS? Objectives, properties of an ideal targeted delivery system, strategies of drug targeting (with examples), particulate carriers/nanoparticles/nanomedicine, recent advances.

Definition: Novel Drug Delivery Systems are advanced formulations designed to deliver a drug at a controlled rate, for a sustained duration, and/or to a specific target site, improving efficacy and safety over conventional dosage forms.

Objectives

  • Maintain plasma concentration within the therapeutic window (controlled/sustained release).
  • Target the site of action → ↑ efficacy, ↓ dose & systemic toxicity.
  • Improve bioavailability of poorly soluble/unstable drugs; better patient compliance.
  • Protect drug from degradation; cross biological barriers (BBB).

Properties of an ideal targeted delivery system

  • Biocompatible, biodegradable, non-immunogenic, non-toxic carrier.
  • Site-specific, with controlled & predictable release at the target only.
  • Physically/chemically stable; easy to manufacture & sterilise; cost-effective.
  • Adequate drug-loading; protects drug; eliminated safely after delivery.

Modified-release (rate-controlled) systems — the "controlled-time" arm of NDDS

Alongside targeted/carrier systems, NDDS includes modified-release oral & transdermal products that alter the temporal profile: Extended-release (ER) gives ≥2-fold ↓ in dosing frequency vs IR (matrix — Higuchi √t kinetics; reservoir/membrane-controlled — near zero-order; osmotic pump/OROS-GITS — semipermeable membrane + laser-drilled orifice, zero-order & pH-independent, e.g. push-pull Procardia-XL, osmotic-trilayer Concerta; ion-exchange resin); Delayed-release (DR) = enteric coating (EC aspirin, mesalamine); Targeted-release to a physiologic site (mesalamine to colon); ODT/oral film (aripiprazole ODT). Transdermal (TTS) patches (backing, drug reservoir/matrix, rate-controlling membrane, adhesive, release liner) avoid first-pass over 1–7 days (GTN, fentanyl, nicotine, estradiol patches in India; clonidine Catapres-TTS elsewhere). Depot/implant parenterals give weeks–months release (leuprolide depot, levonorgestrel rods, carmustine Gliadel wafer).

Key hazard — dose dumping: rapid release of the entire MR dose → toxic levels (alcohol can dump some opioid ER formulations); MR/EC units must never be crushed or chewed. ER is best suited to drugs of intermediate t½ (impractically large tablets needed for short-t½ drugs) and is not rational for drugs with inherently long t½.

Section 5 · Rational Use of Drugs & Clinical Pharmacology Concepts

Covers public policy and biosimilars; supplemented with WHO/Indian guidance.

▸★★★Rational Use of Drugs & the 'P-drug' concept — importance, methods to improve RUD using technology; note on rational/irrational antibiotic use & antibiotic policy.

WHO definition of RUD: patients receive medications appropriate to their clinical needs, in doses that meet their own individual requirements, for an adequate period, at the lowest cost to them and their community.

Criteria of a rational prescription

  • Right drug, right patient, right dose, right route, right time, right duration, right cost, and right information.

P-drug ("Personal drug") concept

A WHO "Guide to Good Prescribing" tool: each prescriber pre-selects a set of preferred drugs for common conditions based on Efficacy, Safety, Suitability, Cost. Steps:

  1. Define the diagnosis/therapeutic objective.
  2. Make an inventory of effective drug groups.
  3. Choose a group by Efficacy/Safety/Suitability/Cost.
  4. Choose a P-drug (active substance, form, dose, duration).
  5. Verify suitability for the individual patient; write prescription; inform/monitor.

Importance: reduces polypharmacy, ADRs, resistance, and cost; standardises prescribing; improves outcomes.

Methods to improve RUD using technology

  • e-Prescribing & CDSS (clinical decision support, drug-interaction alerts).
  • Computerised Physician Order Entry (CPOE); EHR-integrated formularies; STG & Essential Medicines List (NLEM).
  • Audit & feedback, drug-utilisation reviews, m-health/apps, AI prescription screening.

Antibiotic use & policy

  • Irrational use: for viral illness, wrong drug/dose/duration, inadequate course, OTC misuse → AMR.
  • Antibiotic policy / stewardship (AMSP): hospital antibiogram, restricted/reserve lists (WHO AWaRe: Access, Watch, Reserve), de-escalation, culture-directed therapy.
  • Indian context: Schedule H1, ICMR treatment guidelines, National Action Plan on AMR (NAP-AMR), Red Line campaign.

▸★★Influence of disease states on drug action.

Concept: drug action depends not just on the drug/dose/route but on the patient's physiological/pathological status. Disease alters drug response in two ways — pharmacokinetically (PK: absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion) and pharmacodynamically (PD: target-organ/receptor sensitivity). Effects are most marked for narrow-therapeutic-index drugs and form the rational basis for dose individualisation; different drugs are affected to different extents.

1. GI disease

Malabsorption ↓ amoxicillin but paradoxically ↑ cephalexin/co-trimoxazole absorption; gastric stasis (e.g. migraine) retards absorption; achlorhydria ↓ aspirin absorption (favours ionisation); NSAIDs aggravate peptic ulcer disease.

2. Hepatic disease (cirrhosis)

  • ↑ bioavailability of high first-pass drugs (propranolol, morphine, lidocaine) from porto-caval shunting.
  • ↓ albumin → ↑ free fraction of acidic drugs (diclofenac, warfarin).
  • ↓ metabolism of morphine, lidocaine, propranolol — reduce dose; prefer oxazepam/lorazepam (glucuronidated) over diazepam, atenolol over hepatically-cleared β-blockers; avoid prodrugs needing hepatic activation (bacampicillin).
  • PD: ↑ brain sensitivity to morphine/barbiturates (can precipitate coma); diuretic-induced hypokalaemic alkalosis → hepatic encephalopathy; prolonged PT with oral anticoagulants; Child-Pugh score (bilirubin, albumin, PT/INR, ascites, encephalopathy) guides dose reduction — no creatinine-clearance equivalent exists for the liver.

▸★★★Chronopharmacology — definition, biological basis & clinical chronotherapy.

Definition: chronopharmacology studies the bidirectional interaction between biological (chiefly ~24-h circadian) rhythms and drugs — how administration time alters kinetics/effect/toxicity, and how drugs modify those rhythms. Deliberate dosing-time optimisation = chronotherapy/chronotherapeutics.

Key terminology

TermMeaning
ChronopharmacokineticsRhythmic (time-of-day) variation in ADME
ChronesthesyRhythmic variation in target/receptor sensitivity
ChronergyNet rhythmic effect = chronokinetics + chronesthesy
ChronotoxicityTime-dependent variation in drug toxicity

Rhythm classes: ultradian (<20h), circadian (~20–28h — sleep-wake, cortisol), infradian (>28h — menstrual, circannual). Circadian rhythms are endogenous (free-run ~24.2h), entrained by zeitgebers (mainly light-dark cycle, the strongest external "time-giver"). A rhythm is quantified by its mesor (rhythm-adjusted mean), amplitude (half the peak–trough swing) and acrophase (clock time of the peak).

Biological clock

Master clock = suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), entrained by light via retinohypothalamic tract, synchronising peripheral clocks (liver, heart, kidney, adrenal — the adrenal's own clock helps set the cortisol rhythm). Molecular clock = CLOCK-BMAL1 heterodimer drives PER/CRY transcription; accumulated PER/CRY feed back to inhibit CLOCK-BMAL1 (~24h loop). Melatonin (pineal, dark-secreted; synthesised from serotonin) and cortisol (peaks ~06:00–08:00) are the principal humoral "hands" of the clock. Melatonin acts on MT1 (inhibits SCN firing) and MT2 (phase-shifts SCN rhythm) — both Gi/Go-coupled — and is the body's main chronobiotic ("internal zeitgeber").

Tissue glucocorticoid sensitivity runs inverse to the cortisol rhythm — low in late morning, high in evening/early night — so the GR–NF-κB-mediated inflammatory response is enhanced at night, relevant to evening worsening of inflammatory conditions.

The bedtime-antihypertensive-dosing debate continues: a 2025 meta-analysis of 56 RCTs found bedtime dosing lowered nighttime/ambulatory SBP more in patients <60y, BMI≥30, female-majority cohorts and CCB users [PMID 40251431]; the OMAN RCT (n=720) found bedtime single-pill olmesartan-amlodipine cut nighttime SBP by an extra 3.0mmHg and restored dipping [PMID 40632538]; but a 2026 meta-analysis (5 RCTs, 46,477 patients) found dosing time did not significantly change MACE/death/stroke once the contested Hygia trial was set aside [PMID 41605370] — current stance: dose antihypertensives for adherence, not clock-time. Cancer chronotherapy has extended beyond 5-FU/cisplatin: the LungTIME-C01 phase-3 RCT found anti-PD-1 immunochemotherapy before 15:00 roughly halved progression and death vs later infusion in advanced NSCLC [PMID 41629425], concordant with a 2024 checkpoint-inhibitor meta-analysis favouring early-day infusion [PMID 38232612]. Afternoon (15:00–16:00) once-daily inhaled corticosteroid dosing outperformed morning/twice-daily dosing for overnight FEV1 in a crossover RCT, refining the asthma "morning-dip" concept [PMID 40234005]. Melatonin's hypnotic efficacy is timing- not purely dose-dependent (peaks ~4mg/day) [PMID 38888087]; an FDA analysis found paediatric OTC melatonin products contained 0–667% of labelled content amid ~11,000 paediatric ER visits (2019–22) [FDA 2024-Apr], while a melatonin–heart-failure safety signal (AHA 2025) was not replicated in NHANES analysis [PMID 42167796]. "Circadian medicine" is emerging with clock-modulating drugs (REV-ERB/ROR ligands, CK1/CRY modulators) under investigation, and a 2025 Chest consensus recommends embedding circadian principles (light cycling, timed nutrition/sedation) into ICU care [PMID 41429288].

Causality assessment

WHO-UMC categories (certain, probable, possible, unlikely, unclassified) & Naranjo scale (definite/probable/possible/doubtful). Tools rely on temporal relationship, prior knowledge/consistency, dechallenge (event subsides on stopping) & rechallenge (recurs on re-exposure — often unethical). Newer descriptive scheme: DoTS (Dose-relatedness, Time-course, Susceptibility — Aronson & Ferner 2003).

Signal detection & management

Signal = new/changing drug–event association warranting investigation. Detected by disproportionality analysis (PRR, ROR) in databases; assessed, then risk-minimisation in 3 escalating tiers: (1) Communication — Drug Safety Communications, "Dear Doctor" letters, label/boxed warnings; (2) REMS — medication guides, restricted distribution, registries, mandatory testing (e.g. isotretinoin pregnancy-prevention programme); (3) Withdrawal (e.g. rofecoxib, terfenadine, astemizole, cisapride — QT-prolongation deaths).

PSUR & DSMB

  • PSUR/PBRER — Periodic Safety Update Report: periodic benefit–risk reassessment submitted to regulators.
  • DSMB/DSMC — Data & Safety Monitoring Board: independent group reviewing interim safety/efficacy in trials, can recommend stopping.

Why pre-marketing trials miss ADRs

Trials are small (median ~467 patients in pivotal trials, 2015–17), short (median ~24 wk — misses delayed events like bisphosphonate jaw osteonecrosis), exclude special populations (children, pregnant women, elderly >75y), often use surrogate endpoints (~59% of pivotal trials — flecainide/encainide reduced ectopy yet ↑mortality in CAST), and rarely use active comparators (~29%). Classic contrast: terfenadine ~5,000 studied premarket vs ~7.5 million exposed before withdrawal. Consequently ~70% of drugs undergo safety-related label changes post-marketing.

Challenges in India & role in safety

Under-reporting, lack of awareness/training, vast population & informal drug use, OTC misuse. PV protects patient safety, supports regulatory decisions & rational prescribing.

Section 8 · Preclinical Studies, Ethics & Alternatives

▸★★Aims, methodology & limitations of acute & chronic toxicity studies; prerequisites/battery of safety studies before IND.

Definition & underlying assumptions

Drug toxicity (safety) studies — sometimes "hyperpharmacology" — characterise a candidate's adverse effects, dose, duration and target-organ relationships before/during human exposure; conducted under GLP in ≥2 species (1 rodent + 1 non-rodent) by the intended human route. Two assumptions: (i) properly dose-scaled animal effects apply to humans; (ii) high-dose testing of small groups validly uncovers hazards occurring at low human doses.

Acute toxicity

  • Aim: determine short-term toxicity of a single (or 24-h) dose; estimate LD₅₀/approximate lethal dose, target organs, dose for further studies.
  • Method: ≥2 rodent species, ≥5 animals/sex/group, ≥3 graded doses; oral limit dose 2g/kg or 10× intended human dose; observe 14 days; gross pathology + histopathology of affected organs. Modern: fixed-dose, up-and-down, ATC methods (replace classical LD₅₀ for ethical/3R reasons).

Sub-acute & chronic toxicity

  • Aim: toxicity on repeated dosing; NOAEL, cumulative & target-organ effects; reversibility.
  • Method: sub-acute/subchronic 14–180 days; chronic 6 months–2 years in 2 species (rat+dog), 3 dose levels (high = overt toxicity, mid = mild signs, low = NOAEL) + control; full haematology/biochemistry (liver, hepatocellular-leakage enzymes AST/ALT/SDH/LDH, pancreatic, lipid, renal panels) & histopathology of all viscera; non-rodents also get ECG & fundus exam. Duration tied to intended clinical-use duration.

LD50 estimation

LD50 (Trevan & Behrens) = dose lethal to 50% of animals, ideally the most sensitive species. Karber's method (mathematical, no curve needed) vs probit/graphical method (Bliss 1939, Miller–Tainter 1944, Litchfield–Wilcoxon 1949 — % mortality→probit, plotted against log-dose to linearise the sigmoid curve). Rule of thumb: i.p. LD50≈30%, i.v. LD50≈10% of oral LD50. Therapeutic index (TI) = LD50/ED50 (animals) or TD50/ED50 (humans); margin of safety = LD1/ED99.

Full safety battery before IND

  • Acute, sub-acute, chronic toxicity.
  • Safety pharmacology core battery (CV — hERG/Kv11.1/QT, CNS, respiratory; dependence liability for CNS-penetrant drugs).
  • Genotoxicity (Ames, micronucleus, chromosomal aberration, dominant-lethal).
  • Reproductive/developmental toxicity (Segment I, II teratogenicity — rodent+rabbit, III).
  • Carcinogenicity (whole-life, ~2yr, 2 species — for chronic-use drugs), local tolerance, toxicokinetics/ADME at toxic doses.

Limitations & clinical translation

Species differences → poor human prediction; high cost/time (2–6 yr); ethical concerns; cannot detect idiosyncratic/immune ADRs or rare effects (caught later by pharmacovigilance); high-dose extrapolation issues. Starting human (Phase 0/I) dose conventionally 1/100–1/10 of the animal no-effect dose; records (protocol, raw data, slides, blocks) retained ≥5 years post-marketing; IAEC clearance compulsory.

Recent advances: NAMs (New Approach Methodologies — in-vitro/ex-vivo/in-silico/in-chemico methods improving predictivity or enabling 3Rs) are reframing preclinical toxicology, including organ-on-chip/microphysiological systems [PMID 41231273]; iPSC-derived hepatocyte/cardiomyocyte/neuron organoids are positioned as human-relevant alternatives to the rodent+non-rodent paradigm, though clone-to-clone variability limits full replacement [PMID 38639829]. CiPA + ICH S7B/E14 shift cardiac safety toward integrated mechanism-based proarrhythmia assessment; ICH S5(R3)/OECD guidelines now sit alongside AOP/IATA frameworks for DART weight-of-evidence decisions [PMID 42021917]. AI/ML is emerging in safety pharmacology (proarrhythmia, seizure-liability, digital pathology) — a 2024 survey found 57% of Safety Pharmacology Society members not yet using AI but 84% intending to [PMID 42031077]. ▸★Mechanism of gene-expression regulation by glucocorticoids & rationale of their use in some cancers.

Molecular mechanism (genomic)

  1. Lipophilic glucocorticoid enters cell → binds cytoplasmic glucocorticoid receptor (GR) (bound to HSP90).
  2. HSP dissociates → GR–steroid complex dimerises → translocates to nucleus.
  3. Binds Glucocorticoid Response Elements (GRE) on DNA.
  4. Transactivation (↑ anti-inflammatory genes: lipocortin/annexin-1, IκB) & transrepression (↓ NF-κB/AP-1 → ↓ cytokines, COX-2).

Plus rapid non-genomic membrane effects.

Rationale in cancer

  • Lympholytic effect → apoptosis of lymphoid cells: used in ALL, lymphomas, multiple myeloma, CLL (part of CHOP, VAD regimens).
  • Antiemetic (with chemo), ↓ cerebral oedema (brain tumours/metastasis), hypercalcaemia of malignancy, appetite/palliation, prevent hypersensitivity to chemo.

▸★★★Concept of P-value, normal distribution, confounding factors & tests of significance — Student's t-test, chi-square test & non-parametric tests in research/clinical trials.

Normal distribution

Symmetric bell-shaped curve; mean = median = mode; ~68/95/99.7% data within ±1/2/3 SD. Basis of parametric tests.

P-value

Probability of obtaining the observed (or more extreme) result if the null hypothesis is true. P < 0.05 → statistically significant (reject null). Not the probability the hypothesis is true; complement with confidence intervals & effect size.

  • Type I error (α): false positive (reject true null).
  • Type II error (β): false negative; Power = 1−β.

Confounding factor

An extraneous variable associated with both exposure & outcome, distorting the true association (e.g. smoking confounding alcohol–lung cancer). Controlled by randomisation, matching, restriction, stratification, multivariable adjustment.

Tests of significance — overview

Student's t-test in detail

Statistic t = (mean difference)/(SE of the difference), compared against the t-distribution (df-dependent) to get a p-value.

TypeUse
Unpaired (independent)Compares means of two independent groups
PairedCompares means of matched/before–after observations in the same subjects
One-sampleCompares a sample mean to a known/hypothesised value

Assumptions: continuous data, approximately normal distribution, (unpaired) homogeneity of variance, independent observations. Sample-size formula (two-group comparison, 80% power, α=0.05): n ≈ 16·σ²/E² per group (σ = SD, E = clinically meaningful difference) — a classic exam one-liner.

Diagram: t-distribution curve (bell-shaped, heavier tails than normal) with two-tailed critical regions shaded beyond ±tcrit; df labelled.

Chi-square (χ²) test in detail

Used for categorical/nominal data (proportions) — tests whether observed frequencies differ from those expected under the null hypothesis.

Formula: χ² = Σ(O−E)²/E; degrees of freedom df = (rows−1)(columns−1).

  • Yates' continuity correction — applied in a 2×2 table to reduce overestimation of χ² with small samples.
  • Fisher's exact test — preferred over chi-square when expected cell frequency <5 (sparse/small-sample 2×2 tables).
  • McNemar's test — chi-square variant for paired categorical (before–after) data.
Diagram: 2×2 contingency table (exposure × outcome) with observed (O) and expected (E) cell counts annotated, alongside a χ² distribution curve with the critical region.

▸★★Discuss the placebo effect in clinical pharmacology — types of placebo, mechanism of placebo analgesia, its role in RCT design, and the ethical issues involved. (RGUHS Paper I, Sep 2025)

Definition & types

TypeMeaning
Pure placeboInert substance with no pharmacological activity
Active placeboMimics the side effects of the test drug to preserve blinding
Sham procedureSham surgery/sham acupuncture as a procedural control
NoceboNegative effect arising purely from negative expectation
Open-label placeboGiven with full disclosure — still produces measurable benefit

Mechanism of placebo analgesia

Expectation-driven descending pain modulation: prefrontal cortex → periaqueductal grey (PAG) → rostral ventromedial medulla → release of endogenous opioids that inhibit nociceptive transmission at the dorsal horn. The effect is naloxone-reversible, confirming an opioidergic substrate; dopaminergic reward/expectation pathways also contribute. Classical conditioning and verbal expectation both drive the response.

Role in RCT design

  • Blinding controls for both patient and assessor bias, separating true pharmacodynamic effect from natural history, regression to the mean, and expectation.
  • Placebo response magnitude is typically 30–40% for subjective endpoints (pain, mood) and far lower for objective/hard endpoints (tumour shrinkage, mortality).
  • Run-in periods are used to identify and exclude high placebo-responders before randomisation.

Ethical considerations

  • Declaration of Helsinki: placebo is permissible only when no proven effective intervention exists, or when scientifically necessary and patients face no additional risk of serious harm.
  • Requires clinical equipoise and explicit disclosure in informed consent of the possibility of receiving placebo.

The mouse is the workhorse — rapid breeding cycle, well-characterised genome, >99% conservation with humans, and the only species whose totipotent embryonic stem (ES) cells can be grown in vitro and re-injected to form a germ line.

Methods

  • Pronuclear microinjection of foreign DNA into a fertilised oocyte → random genomic integration → classic transgenic founder; pivotal in studying MHC-class-II-associated autoimmune disease (RA, MS, diabetes).
  • ES-cell-based homologous recombination → targeted knockout in ES cells → injected into a blastocyst → chimeric mouse → heterozygous F1 inter-mated → homozygous knockout; germline transmission confirmed by Southern blot/PCR + Mendelian inheritance (Mario Capecchi, Martin Evans, Oliver Smithies — Nobel Prize 2007).
  • Knock-down (RNAi) — short dsRNA silences the target gene without altering DNA, useful where deletion is embryonically lethal (Andrew Fire & Craig Mello — Nobel Prize 2006).

Applications in pharmacology

  • Target validation via receptor KO: M2-KO abolishes vagal/carbachol bradycardia; M3-KO eliminates muscarinic bronchoconstriction; M1-KO impairs forebrain plasticity & loses pilocarpine seizures; α1-KO defines cardiovascular/lower-urinary-tract roles, α2-KO neuronal/inhibitory roles; MAO-KO vs NET-KO dissect noradrenergic-transmission termination.
  • Disease models: α-synuclein (Parkinson's), APP/3xTg-AD (Alzheimer's), R6/2 huntingtin-exon-1 (Huntington's), mutant SOD1 (ALS), ob/ob & db/db (obesity/T2DM, leptin pathway), HBV-transgenic (hepatitis-B/HCC), humanised TNF-transgenic (RA).
  • Humanised mice for antibody/biologic testing; CFTR-KO cystic fibrosis; humanised CYP/nuclear-receptor mice for ADME/toxicology; transgenic animals as biologic "bioreactors".

Recent: CRISPR/Cas9 is now the workhorse for rapidly generating KO/knock-in animals across species (mouse, rat, rabbit, pig, dog, monkey — e.g. first DMD rabbit via zygote co-injection of SpCas9 mRNA+gRNAs), though line creation remains costly and genetic compensation can still mask phenotypes [PMID 37894981, 38786024]. Next-gen editors extend beyond simple deletion — base editors (single-nucleotide change, no double-strand break; ~60% of pathogenic point mutations theoretically correctable) and prime editors (precise insertions ≤44bp) — letting modellers install defined point mutations rather than crude nulls [PMID 38786024, 42173386]. ▸★★Discuss drug enantiomers and stereoselectivity in pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics, with examples of chiral switching. (RGUHS Paper I, May 2025)

Basic concepts

A chiral centre gives rise to non-superimposable mirror-image enantiomers. The more pharmacologically active isomer is the eutomer, the less active (or inactive) the distomer; the eudismic ratio = potency of eutomer/distomer.

Pharmacodynamic stereoselectivity

DrugStereoselective finding
WarfarinS-warfarin is the more potent anticoagulant enantiomer
PropranololS-propranolol has ~100× the β-blocking activity of R-propranolol
SalbutamolR-isomer is the bronchodilator (levosalbutamol); S-isomer may worsen bronchial hyperreactivity
KetamineS-(es)ketamine is the more potent NMDA-receptor antagonist

Pharmacokinetic stereoselectivity

Enantiomers can show different protein binding, metabolism (CYP isoforms metabolise enantiomers at different rates), renal transport, and even chiral inversion in vivo (R-ibuprofen converts to the active S-form).

Chiral switches

Omeprazole → esomeprazoleImproved potency/selectivity, reduced effective dose, fewer off-target effects, patent-life extension
Citalopram → escitalopram
Ketamine → esketamine

Recent: esketamine nasal spray remains FDA-approved for treatment-resistant depression (including as monotherapy in recent label updates); a large escitalopram-vs-donepezil Alzheimer's-prevention trial returned negative; CPIC pharmacogenomic guidelines now address CYP2B6-mediated stereoselective methadone metabolism.
▸★★★Discuss the WHO concept of Essential Medicines — definition, criteria for selection, and the National List of Essential Medicines (NLEM). (RGUHS Paper I, May 2025 · Paper IV, May 2022 — asked 2×)

Definition & rationale

WHO defines Essential Medicines as those that "satisfy the priority healthcare needs of the population," selected with regard to public-health relevance, efficacy/safety evidence, and comparative cost-effectiveness — to be available at all times, in adequate amounts, assured quality, and at an affordable price.

WHO selection criteria

  • (a) Adequate efficacy & safety evidence from clinical studies.
  • (b) Assured quality, including bioavailability and storage stability.
  • (c) Disease pattern, feasibility (facilities, trained personnel, resources).
  • (d) Comparative efficacy/safety/cost when alternatives exist.
  • (e) Favourable pharmacokinetics & local manufacturing feasibility.
  • (f) Single compounds preferred; FDCs only with proven added benefit.
  • (g) Dynamic, continuously revised process.
  • (h) Based on standard treatment guidelines.

WHO Model List vs NLEM (India)

FeatureWHO Model List (EML)NLEM (India)

CategorisationCore vs complementary listPrimary / secondary / tertiary level of care

Fixed-dose combinations (FDCs)

Included only when ingredients have matched PK profiles and a proven advantage in efficacy, safety, adherence, or reducing resistance (anti-TB, antimalarial, HIV regimens) — e.g. cotrimoxazole, carbidopa+levodopa, ORS.

LMIC access reviews confirm a national EML is a key facilitator against stock-outs/high private prices, though paediatric-cancer-drug lists align only 40-60% with the WHO EML for Children [PMID 41658422, 39891074].
▸★★★Classify the drug reservoirs in the body with their clinical significance, and explain redistribution using thiopental as an example. (RGUHS Paper I, Jan 2025 · Paper I, Nov 2022 — asked 2×)

Concept

A drug reservoir is any compartment — plasma protein, tissue, fat, or bone — into which drug binds reversibly and from which it slowly re-releases as free plasma concentration falls. Bound drug is inactive but in equilibrium with free drug, prolonging duration of action and raising apparent Vd.

Classification

ReservoirMechanismExamples
Plasma proteinsAlbumin (acidic drugs), α1-acid glycoprotein (basic drugs)Warfarin, phenytoin, NSAIDs (albumin); lidocaine, propranolol (α1-AG)
Cellular/tissueBinding to intracellular proteins/phospholipids; active transportDigoxin → muscle/heart; chloroquine, tetracyclines → liver
FatPhysical solution of lipophilic drugs in neutral fatThiopental, lipophilic toxins
Bone & teethAdsorption onto hydroxyapatiteTetracyclines, bisphosphonates, lead, radium
Transcellular/organ-specificSelective uptake/melanin bindingIodine → thyroid; chloroquine → retina; atropine → iris

Plasma protein binding

Reversible equilibrium — only free drug is active, filtered, and metabolised. High protein binding → small Vd, longer apparent action, not removed by haemodialysis. α1-acid glycoprotein is an acute-phase reactant (rises in cancer, MI, arthritis).

Redistribution — thiopental

A single IV dose of thiopental rapidly crosses the BBB (peak brain concentration within ~1 min) due to high lipid solubility, producing rapid anaesthesia onset; it then redistributes to muscle and finally fat, and plasma/brain levels fall — anaesthesia terminates by redistribution, not elimination (true elimination t½ is long, 3–8 h). With repeated dosing the low-perfusion depots saturate, and the drug becomes cumulative/longer-acting.

Diagram: Thiopental concentration–time curves for blood, brain, muscle and fat on a log scale — showing the early blood/brain peak and the cross-over as muscle/fat levels rise while blood/brain fall, terminating anaesthesia.
Recent: a DOLPHIN-trial sub-analysis confirmed the unbound (free) fraction is the pharmacologically active reservoir component — unbound ceftriaxone fraction ~15% in critically ill patients, varying with albumin and renal function; a 2021 review challenged "high protein binding causes clinically important displacement" as largely a myth; a 2026 critical update reframes Vd(area), not Vd(ss), as the truer measure of the distribution/reservoir volume.
▸★★★Describe the principle, methodology and clinical applications of radioimmunoassay (RIA). (RGUHS Paper I — asked 5× across Jan 2025, Dec 2023, Jul 2023, Nov 2021, Jul 2021)

Principle — competitive binding

A fixed, limiting amount of specific antibody and a fixed amount of radiolabelled antigen (typically 125I) compete with the unlabelled antigen in the test sample for the limited antibody-binding sites. As sample antigen rises, it progressively displaces the radiolabelled antigen, so bound radioactivity falls in proportion to the analyte present — the signal is inversely proportional to analyte concentration. Developed by Berson & Yalow (1960) for plasma insulin; Yalow received the Nobel Prize (1977).

Reagents & methodology

StepProcedure
1. LabellingAntigen labelled with 125I/131I (iodogen or chloramine-T oxidation)
2. ReactionConstant antibody + labelled antigen added to standard or unknown sample
3. CompetitionIncubate to equilibrium — labelled and unlabelled antigen compete for antibody
4. SeparationBound vs free antigen separated (double-antibody precipitation, PEG, charcoal, or coated tube)
5. CountingGamma counter measures bound (or free) radioactivity
6. Read-offUnknown concentration interpolated from a log/logit-linearised standard binding curve

Variants

AssayLabel/principle
RIARadiolabel on antigen; competitive (limited antibody) — original method
IRMARadiolabel on antibody; non-competitive, two-site "sandwich"
RRALabelled ligand binds a receptor instead of an antibody
ELISAEnzyme label (no radioactivity) — safer, cheaper, longer shelf-life

Clinical applications

  • Hormone assays — insulin, thyroid hormones, steroids, gonadotropins, hCG.
  • Therapeutic drug monitoring — serum digoxin (narrow therapeutic window).
  • Plasma renin concentration (unaffected by aliskiren, unlike the enzymatic PRA assay).
Diagram: Standard binding curve — % antibody-bound radioactivity (y) vs log antigen concentration (x), a descending sigmoid; alongside a competitive-binding schematic showing labelled vs unlabelled antigen competing for limited antibody sites.
Recent: LC-MS/MS is displacing RIA as the reference method for steroid hormones — RIA overestimated plasma aldosterone by ~29% versus LC-MS/MS in a primary-aldosteronism screening study; radioimmunoprecipitation (RIPA) remains the most sensitive myasthenia-gravis autoantibody assay but is being phased out for non-radioactive cell-based assays; femtomolar non-isotopic bioluminescence immunoassays (LUCAS) now rival RIA sensitivity without radiation hazard.
▸★★★Discuss drug therapy in pregnancy and lactation — placental transfer, teratogenesis, and neonatal pharmacokinetics. (RGUHS Paper I, Dec 2023 · Paper I, Jul 2023 — asked 2×)

Placental transfer determinants

Lipid solubility, molecular size, ionisation, placental blood flow, and transporter activity (P-glycoprotein limits transfer of some drugs) determine the rate and extent of transplacental passage — most drugs cross to some degree by simple diffusion.

Teratogenesis — three phases

PhaseTimingVulnerability
Pre-implantation0–2 weeks"All-or-none" — death or no effect (minimal teratogenicity)
Embryonic (organogenesis)3–8 weeksHighest risk of major structural malformations
Fetal period9 weeks–termGrowth restriction, functional/CNS deficits rather than gross malformation

Important human teratogens

  • Valproate — neural tube defects, neurodevelopmental impairment (avoid in pregnancy).
  • ACE inhibitors/ARBs — fetal renal agenesis, oligohydramnios (2nd/3rd trimester).
  • Warfarin — warfarin embryopathy (nasal hypoplasia, stippled epiphyses).
  • Isotretinoin, thalidomide — classic severe structural teratogens.
  • Tetracyclines — dental staining, bone growth effects.

Lactation & neonatal pharmacokinetics

Drug transfer into milk favours lipid-soluble, low-protein-bound, weakly basic drugs (milk is slightly acidic — ion trapping). Neonates have immature CYP/UGT enzymes, higher body water, lower protein binding, and immature renal function — prolonging drug half-lives and increasing toxicity risk (e.g. chloramphenicol "gray baby" syndrome).

▸★★★Discuss autoreceptors — concept, signal transduction mechanism, and therapeutic correlates. (RGUHS Paper I, Dec 2023 · Paper I, Jul 2023 — asked 2×)

Concept

An autoreceptor is a presynaptic receptor on a neuron that responds to the same neurotransmitter the neuron itself releases ("homotropic" feedback), providing negative-feedback inhibition of further release. (A heteroreceptor responds to a different neurotransmitter released by a neighbouring neuron — "heterotropic" modulation.)

Major autoreceptor systems

SystemAutoreceptorEffect
Noradrenergicα2↓ noradrenaline release (Gi-coupled)
DopaminergicD2↓ dopamine release/synthesis
Serotonergic5-HT1A (somatodendritic) / 5-HT1B (terminal)↓ serotonin firing/release
CholinergicM2↓ acetylcholine release
HistaminergicH3↓ histamine release

Signal transduction

Most autoreceptors (α2, D2, 5-HT1A/1B, M2, H3) are Gi/Go-coupled — activation ↓ adenylyl cyclase/cAMP and ↓ voltage-gated Ca²⁺ influx while ↑ K⁺ conductance, reducing vesicular neurotransmitter release.

Therapeutic correlates

  • Clonidine (α2-agonist) → activates autoreceptors → ↓ central sympathetic outflow → antihypertensive effect.
  • Mirtazapine (α2-antagonist) → blocks autoreceptor feedback → ↑ noradrenaline & serotonin release — antidepressant mechanism.
  • Buspirone (5-HT1A partial agonist) — anxiolytic via autoreceptor/heteroreceptor modulation.
  • SSRI delayed onset — initial ↑ synaptic 5-HT activates inhibitory 5-HT1A somatodendritic autoreceptors, which must desensitise (over 2–4 weeks) before full antidepressant effect emerges.
Diagram: Noradrenergic varicosity — released NA feeding back onto presynaptic α2 autoreceptor (Gi-coupled) → ↓cAMP, ↓Ca²⁺ influx → ↓ further NA release (negative-feedback loop).
Recent: gepirone ER (5-HT1A partial agonist) is FDA-approved for major depressive disorder; pitolisant (H3-receptor antagonist/inverse agonist) showed efficacy in narcolepsy in the HAROSA III trial; ongoing H3-antagonist research explores cognitive-enhancement potential via heteroreceptor modulation.
▸★★★Describe the principle, instrumentation and clinical applications of flame photometry. (RGUHS Paper I, Dec 2023 · Paper I, Jul 2023 — asked 2×)

Principle

A liquid sample is aspirated into a flame in four sequential steps — desolvation (solvent evaporates) → atomisation (solid residue vaporises to free atoms) → excitation (flame thermal energy promotes outer-shell electrons) → emission (electrons fall back, emitting light of a characteristic wavelength). Emission intensity at that wavelength is proportional to analyte concentration.

Analytical emission lines

ElementWavelength
Sodium (Na)589 nm (yellow)
Potassium (K)766/770 nm
Lithium (Li)671 nm
Calcium (Ca)622 nm

Instrumentation

Atomiser/burner with fuel-oxidant flame (propane/air or acetylene/air) → optical filter or monochromator selects the element-specific wavelength → photodetector → readout, calibrated against known standards (calibration curve).

Interferences & flame photometry vs AAS

FeatureFlame photometryAtomic absorption spectroscopy (AAS)
MeasuresEmission from thermally excited atomsAbsorption of light by ground-state atoms
SensitivityGood for easily-excited alkali/alkaline-earth metals (Na, K, Li, Ca)Higher sensitivity, broader element range
InterferencesSpectral overlap, ionisation interference, viscosity/flow-rate variationFewer spectral interferences (hollow-cathode lamp specificity)

Clinical applications

Estimation of serum/urine sodium, potassium, lithium (therapeutic drug monitoring) and calcium — long the standard clinical-laboratory method, now substantially replaced by ion-selective electrodes for Na⁺/K⁺.

▸🩺 CaseA 26-year-old woman on combined oral contraceptive pills is started on rifampicin for tuberculosis. Two months later she is pregnant. Explain the pharmacological basis & what advice was missed.
Clue: Which CYP process does rifampicin trigger, and how does that affect the pill?

Mechanism: Rifampicin is a potent CYP3A4 (and P-glycoprotein) inducer → ↑ metabolism of estrogen & progestin → ↓ contraceptive plasma levels → loss of efficacy → ovulation & pregnancy.

What should have happened: warn the patient and advise an additional/alternative non-hormonal method (e.g. copper IUD, barrier) during and for ~4 weeks after rifampicin.

Concept tested: enzyme induction & clinically important drug interactions (Type F ADR — therapeutic failure). Other inducers: phenytoin, carbamazepine, phenobarbitone, St John's wort.

▸🩺 CaseA patient with epilepsy is stable on phenytoin. The dose is increased by a small amount and he develops nystagmus, ataxia and drowsiness with a disproportionately high serum level. Why?
Clue: What order of kinetics does phenytoin follow at therapeutic doses?

Mechanism: Phenytoin shows zero-order (saturation / Michaelis-Menten) kinetics — the metabolising enzymes are saturated, so a small dose increase causes a large, non-linear rise in plasma concentration → toxicity.

Implication: titrate in small increments, monitor levels (TDM), and remember t½ lengthens as concentration rises. Other zero-order drugs: ethanol, aspirin (high dose), warfarin, theophylline.

▸🩺 CaseA patient on digoxin has a measured trough of 0.6 ng/mL but remains symptomatic; target is ~1.2 ng/mL. The current dose is 0.125 mg/day. Roughly what maintenance dose is needed, and what's the caveat?
Clue: Digoxin follows first-order kinetics — concentration is proportional to dose.

Calculation: For first-order drugs, new dose = current dose × (target / measured) = 0.125 × (1.2 / 0.6) = 0.25 mg/day.

Caveats: digoxin has a narrow therapeutic index — recheck level at steady state (after ~5 half-lives; sample ≥6–8 h post-dose), watch for toxicity (nausea, visual halos, arrhythmia), and reduce dose in renal impairment, hypokalaemia & the elderly. This linear rule does not apply to phenytoin.

▸🩺 CaseA breastfeeding mother is given codeine for post-partum pain. Her neonate becomes excessively sedated with poor feeding. What happened?
Clue: Codeine is a prodrug — which enzyme activates it, and what genetic variant is dangerous?

Mechanism: Codeine is converted to morphine by CYP2D6. An ultra-rapid metaboliser (gene duplication) produces excessive morphine, which is secreted in breast milk → neonatal opioid toxicity (sedation, respiratory depression).

Lesson: avoid codeine in breastfeeding/ultra-rapid metabolisers (regulatory warning); a poor metaboliser instead gets no analgesia. Classic pharmacogenomics example.

▸🩺 CaseA young man starts azathioprine for inflammatory bowel disease and develops profound bone-marrow suppression within two weeks. Which enzyme should have been checked first?
Clue: Thiopurine metabolism depends on a polymorphic enzyme.

Mechanism: Azathioprine → 6-mercaptopurine, inactivated by thiopurine methyltransferase (TPMT) (and NUDT15). TPMT-deficient patients accumulate cytotoxic thioguanine nucleotides → severe myelosuppression.

Lesson: test TPMT/NUDT15 activity or genotype before starting; reduce dose or avoid in deficiency. Also: never co-prescribe full-dose azathioprine with allopurinol (blocks xanthine oxidase → ↑ toxicity).

▸🩺 CaseA patient stable on warfarin (INR 2.5) is prescribed a course of an antibiotic and presents days later with bruising and an INR of 7. Name a likely culprit & the mechanism.
Clue: warfarin is metabolised by CYP2C9/3A4 — what would an enzyme inhibitor do?

Mechanism: Drugs such as metronidazole, ciprofloxacin, co-trimoxazole, macrolides, fluconazole inhibit warfarin metabolism (CYP2C9) → ↑ warfarin levels → ↑ INR → bleeding risk. Some also displace protein binding.

Management: hold warfarin, give vitamin K ± PCC/FFP if bleeding, monitor INR; anticipate interactions when adding any drug to warfarin.

▸🩺 CaseA 70-year-old with eGFR 25 mL/min is admitted; the team plans gentamicin and metformin. What pharmacokinetic principles guide dosing here?
Clue: both drugs are renally cleared — what changes in renal impairment?

Principles: renally-eliminated drugs accumulate when GFR falls → reduce dose and/or extend the dosing interval per eGFR/CrCl.

  • Gentamicin — extend interval, use TDM (trough to avoid nephro/ototoxicity); loading dose unchanged (Vd same).
  • Metformin — risk of lactic acidosis; avoid if eGFR <30 (contraindicated), caution 30–45.

Concept: dose individualisation in renal impairment + avoiding nephrotoxins.

▸🩺 CaseTwo weeks after starting a new drug, a patient develops a maculopapular rash. How would you decide whether the drug caused it?
Clue: there are formal causality-assessment tools.

Approach — causality assessment: use the WHO-UMC categories (certain, probable, possible, unlikely) or the Naranjo algorithm (definite/probable/possible/doubtful), assessing:

  • Temporal relationship (timing after drug start).
  • Dechallenge (improves on stopping) & rechallenge (recurs on restart).
  • Alternative causes excluded; known ADR profile; dose-relationship.

Action: stop suspected drug, manage rash, and report to PvPI (suspected-ADR form). Concept: pharmacovigilance & ADR causality.

▸🩺 CaseYou must start IV therapy that needs an immediate therapeutic level. The drug has Vd 40 L and target concentration 5 mg/L. What loading dose, and why is it independent of clearance?
Clue: loading dose depends on Vd, not clearance.

Calculation: Loading dose = Vd × target concentration = 40 L × 5 mg/L = 200 mg.

Why: the loading dose fills the apparent volume to reach the target immediately; clearance determines the maintenance dose (= Css × CL) and the time to steady state (~5 t½), not the loading dose. Useful when waiting 4–5 half-lives is unacceptable (e.g. status epilepticus, severe infection).

▸🩺 CaseA junior doctor must choose a first-line antihypertensive for a new clinic. How would the "P-drug" concept guide a rational choice?
Clue: WHO "Guide to Good Prescribing" — four selection criteria.

P-drug approach: pre-select a personal drug of choice by comparing groups on Efficacy, Safety, Suitability & Cost.

  1. Define the objective (lower BP, reduce CV risk).
  2. List effective groups (ACEi/ARB, CCB, thiazide).
  3. Choose by E-S-S-C → e.g. a thiazide or ACEi/ARB/CCB depending on comorbidity (diabetes/CKD → ACEi/ARB).
  4. Verify suitability for the individual patient, prescribe, inform & monitor.

Concept: rational prescribing reduces polypharmacy, ADRs & cost.
