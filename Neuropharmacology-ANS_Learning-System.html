🧠The Autonomic Nervous System
(an accelerated learning system)
  • Sympathetic (thoracolumbar, T1–L2) drives "fight or flight"; parasympathetic (craniosacral) drives "rest and digest"
  • Every preganglionic fiber releases ACh onto nicotinic receptors; postganglionic parasympathetic fibers release ACh onto muscarinic receptors; most postganglionic sympathetic fibers release NE onto adrenergic receptors
  • Two exceptions to remember: sweat glands are sympathetic but cholinergic (muscarinic), and the adrenal medulla acts like a ganglion, releasing epinephrine straight into the blood after a nicotinic signal
  • Nicotinic receptors are fast ion channels; muscarinic and adrenergic receptors are slower GPCRs
  • Drugs can act at any of six stages: making the transmitter, storing it, releasing it, binding the receptor, shutting off the signal, or autoreceptor feedback
  • Cholinergic transmission: choline + acetyl-CoA forms ACh (via ChAT) > loaded into vesicles by VAChT > released by calcium influx > broken down quickly by acetylcholinesterase (AChE)
  • Drug targets here: hemicholinium stops uptake, vesamicol stops storage, botulinum stops release, AChE inhibitors stop breakdown
  • Adrenergic transmission: tyrosine becomes DOPA (rate-limiting step) then dopamine then NE; signal is cleared mainly by reuptake (NET), not enzymes
  • Drug targets here: metyrosine stops synthesis, reserpine stops storage, guanethidine stops release, cocaine/TCAs block NET reuptake, MAO inhibitors stop breakdown
  • Agonists (parasympathomimetics): pilocarpine for glaucoma/dry mouth, bethanechol for urinary retention and GI atony ("bethanechol = bladder/bowel")
  • Agonist effects spell SLUDGE-M: Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI motility, Emesis, Miosis
  • Antagonists (antimuscarinics): atropine for bradycardia, organophosphate poisoning, drying secretions, pupil dilation; also ipratropium/tiotropium (COPD), oxybutynin (overactive bladder), scopolamine (motion sickness)
  • Antimuscarinic toxidrome mnemonic: "dry as a bone, red as a beet, blind as a bat, mad as a hatter, hot as a hare"
  • Receptor map: M1 in CNS/gastric acid, M2 (Gi) slows the heart, M3 (Gq) controls smooth muscle, glands, and the eye
  • Agonists act on M3 (Gq, drives contraction/secretion/pupil constriction) and M2 (Gi, slows heart at SA/AV node)
  • Atropine competes for the same receptor site rather than disabling it permanently — a surmountable block, so a flood of ACh (e.g. organophosphate poisoning) can overpower it
  • Atropine acts only at muscarinic sites — it fixes the "wet" symptoms of organophosphate poisoning but does nothing for nicotinic-driven muscle weakness, which needs pralidoxime instead
  • Reversible inhibitors: neostigmine (myasthenia gravis, reversing paralytics, post-op ileus — can't cross BBB), physostigmine (crosses BBB, used in antimuscarinic toxicity), donepezil/rivastigmine/galantamine (Alzheimer's), pyridostigmine/edrophonium (short-acting, diagnostic)
  • Irreversible organophosphates: insecticides (malathion, parathion) and nerve agents (sarin) — these "phosphorylate" AChE, and the bond becomes permanent once "aging" locks it in place
  • Poisoning looks like a cholinergic crisis (DUMBELS/SLUDGE plus bradycardia), with nicotinic effects progressing from muscle twitching to weakness to respiratory paralysis
  • Antidote: atropine for the muscarinic symptoms plus pralidoxime (2-PAM) to reactivate AChE and fix the nicotinic symptoms — but only before aging happens
  • Normally AChE destroys ACh within milliseconds; blocking it lets ACh build up and over-stimulate cholinergic synapses everywhere
  • Reversible inhibitors detach from the enzyme within minutes to hours; organophosphates bind permanently once "aging" occurs, so the body must make brand-new enzyme to recover
  • Pralidoxime can strip the phosphate off the enzyme, but only before aging has taken place
  • Overdosing anticholinesterase therapy in myasthenia gravis can look identical to worsening myasthenia itself (cholinergic crisis vs myasthenic crisis) — both cause weakness
  • Non-depolarizing (competitive) blockers end in "-curium/-curonium" (rocuronium, vecuronium, atracurium, pancuronium) — reversed with neostigmine or sugammadex
  • Depolarizing blocker succinylcholine causes sustained activation (Phase I block) and is broken down fast by plasma cholinesterase — NOT reversed by neostigmine, which would make it worse
  • Watch for hyperkalemia, malignant hyperthermia, and prolonged apnea with succinylcholine in patients with pseudocholinesterase deficiency
  • Ganglionic blockers (hexamethonium, trimethaphan) shut down both autonomic divisions at once by blocking Nn receptors
  • Nicotine has a two-phase effect: low doses stimulate ganglia, high doses cause a depolarizing block
  • Non-depolarizing agents simply occupy the receptor without triggering it — flooding the synapse with ACh (via neostigmine) outcompetes them and reverses the block
  • Succinylcholine instead activates the receptor and stays bound, so the muscle fires once (fasciculation) and then cannot fire again — adding more ACh only prolongs this
  • Succinylcholine's two serious dangers: a brief spike in serum potassium, and malignant hyperthermia (treated with dantrolene)
  • α1 (Gq): vasoconstriction, pupil dilation, raises blood pressure, contracts bladder sphincter — phenylephrine activates it, prazosin blocks it
  • α2 (Gi): presynaptic brake that lowers NE release; clonidine activates it centrally to lower blood pressure
  • β1 (Gs): raises heart rate, force of contraction, and conduction — dobutamine activates it, metoprolol blocks it
  • β2 (Gs): relaxes airway and blood vessel smooth muscle (bronchodilation) — salbutamol/albuterol activate it
  • β3 (Gs): breaks down fat and relaxes the bladder — mirabegron activates it for overactive bladder
  • Epinephrine hits α1, α2, β1, β2 — first choice for anaphylaxis; norepinephrine hits mostly α1, α2, β1 — used as a pressor in septic shock
  • Selective agonists: phenylephrine (α1), clonidine (α2), dobutamine (β1), salbutamol/terbutaline (β2)
  • Indirect agents (ephedrine, amphetamine, tyramine) trigger NE release; cocaine blocks NET reuptake instead
  • α-blockers: prazosin family blocks α1; phenoxybenzamine and phentolamine are used in pheochromocytoma
  • β-blockers: propranolol (non-selective), metoprolol/atenolol (β1-selective), carvedilol/labetalol (combined α and β blockade)
  • α1 = squeeze (vessels, sphincters); α2 = brake (presynaptic feedback); β1 = "one heart" — drive it harder; β2 = "two lungs" — open them
  • Epinephrine reversal: after an α-blocker, adrenaline's α1 pressor effect is gone, leaving only β2 vasodilation, so blood pressure falls instead of rising
  • In pheochromocytoma, giving a β-blocker first removes β2 vasodilation while α1 vasoconstriction stays unchecked, causing a hypertensive crisis — always α-block first
  • Na⁺-channel blockers (phenytoin, carbamazepine, lamotrigine) treat focal and generalised tonic-clonic seizures via use-dependent block of the inactivated channel
  • T-type Ca²⁺ blockers (ethosuximide, valproate) treat absence seizures by quieting the 3-Hz thalamocortical rhythm
  • GABA enhancers (benzodiazepines, barbiturates, vigabatrin, tiagabine) raise inhibitory tone
  • Broad-spectrum: valproate (multi-mechanism, most teratogenic), lamotrigine, levetiracetam (SV2A)
  • Never give carbamazepine/phenytoin for absence or myoclonic seizures — they worsen them
  • Status epilepticus: IV lorazepam (or buccal midazolam) → load phenytoin/valproate/levetiracetam → anaesthesia if refractory
  • Phenytoin shows zero-order kinetics and induces CYP enzymes; carbamazepine auto-induces its own metabolism
  • Valproate inhibits lamotrigine breakdown → doubles its level → Stevens-Johnson risk; start lamotrigine low and titrate slowly
  • 5-HT1 receptors are targeted by triptans for migraine; 5-HT2 is the antipsychotic target; 5-HT3 is targeted by ondansetron as an antiemetic
  • Dopamine pathways: nigrostriatal (movement, affected in Parkinson's), mesolimbic (psychosis), tuberoinfundibular (prolactin)
  • Watch out for serotonin syndrome when combining serotonergic drugs
  • Glutamate is the main excitatory transmitter (acts on NMDA/AMPA receptors); GABA is the main inhibitory transmitter (acts on the GABA-A chloride channel)
  • GABA-A is the shared target of benzodiazepines, barbiturates, alcohol, and anesthetics
  • Tight junctions plus efflux pumps (P-glycoprotein) keep most drugs out of the brain
  • Only small, lipophilic molecules that aren't P-glycoprotein substrates can get into the CNS
  • SSRIs (fluoxetine) are first-line and safest in overdose; SNRIs (venlafaxine); TCAs (amitriptyline, dangerous in overdose); MAOIs (risk of tyramine/"cheese" crisis)
  • Onset takes weeks because it depends on the brain adapting its receptors, not just blocking reuptake
  • Antipsychotics block D2 dopamine receptors; typicals (haloperidol) cause more movement side effects, atypicals (risperidone, olanzapine, clozapine) cause fewer
  • Clozapine carries a risk of agranulocytosis and needs blood monitoring; lithium is used for mania and has a narrow safety margin
  • Watch out for neuroleptic malignant syndrome
  • Drugs suppress excess firing by blocking sodium channels, enhancing GABA, or blocking calcium channels
  • Phenytoin/carbamazepine block Na+ channels; valproate is broad-spectrum; ethosuximide treats absence seizures via T-type calcium channels; benzodiazepines treat status epilepticus
  • Parkinson's: restore dopamine with levodopa + carbidopa, dopamine agonists, or MAO-B/COMT inhibitors
  • Alzheimer's: boost ACh with cholinesterase inhibitors, plus memantine (an NMDA antagonist)
  • Benzodiazepines increase how often the GABA-A chloride channel opens — safer, has a ceiling effect, reversed by flumazenil
  • Barbiturates increase how long the channel stays open — no ceiling effect, so overdose can cause fatal respiratory depression
  • Opioids act as agonists at mu opioid receptors, producing pain relief, euphoria, respiratory depression, pinpoint pupils, and constipation
  • Key drugs: morphine, fentanyl, codeine (a prodrug processed by CYP2D6), tramadol; naloxone is the antagonist used to reverse overdose
  • Overdose triad: coma, pinpoint pupils, and respiratory depression
  • General anesthesia works by enhancing GABA-A inhibition and reducing NMDA excitation
  • Inhaled agents (sevoflurane, nitrous oxide) are measured by MAC — the lower the MAC, the more potent the drug
  • IV agents: propofol (fast-acting), ketamine (an NMDA blocker with dissociative effects)
  • Local anesthetics block voltage-gated sodium channels from inside the axon, preventing the action potential that carries pain signals
  • Named with the suffix "-caine"; esters (procaine) versus amides (lidocaine, which has two "i"s in its name)
  • They work poorly in infected or acidic tissue due to ion trapping; adding adrenaline prolongs how long they act
  • THC acts on CB1 receptors in the CNS and CB2 receptors in the immune system; the endocannabinoid system affects appetite, pain, and mood
  • Therapeutic uses include nausea, appetite stimulation, spasticity, and seizures (cannabidiol)
  • Ethanol is a CNS depressant that enhances GABA and inhibits NMDA
  • Metabolized by alcohol dehydrogenase to acetaldehyde, then to acetate, via zero-order kinetics (about 10 g/hour)
  • Disulfiram blocks aldehyde dehydrogenase, causing an aversive reaction if alcohol is consumed
  • Addiction is a disorder of the mesolimbic dopamine reward pathway, involving tolerance, dependence, and withdrawal
  • Treatments: methadone/buprenorphine for opioids, naltrexone/acamprosate for alcohol, varenicline/nicotine replacement for nicotine

Neurotransmission & the Autonomic Nervous System

The wiring diagram every downstream drug acts on
Tier 1 · Foundation
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Virtually every drug acting on the autonomic system either copies, interferes with, or fine-tunes one of just two chemical messengers — acetylcholine and norepinephrine — acting on a limited number of receptors. Once the wiring is clear, you can forecast what hundreds of unfamiliar drugs will do.

The high-yield elements

  • Two divisions, opposite tone: the sympathetic branch (thoracolumbar, T1–L2) drives "fight or flight"; the parasympathetic branch (craniosacral — cranial nerves III, VII, IX, X plus S2–4) handles "rest and digest."
  • The transmitter pattern: every preganglionic fiber, regardless of division, lets go of ACh onto nicotinic receptors. Postganglionic parasympathetic fibers always release ACh onto muscarinic receptors. The bulk of postganglionic sympathetic fibers instead send NE onto adrenergic receptors.
  • Two exceptions worth memorizing: sympathetic innervation of sweat glands is unusually cholinergic (muscarinic) rather than adrenergic; and the adrenal medulla behaves like a repurposed ganglion — a nicotinic preganglionic ACh signal makes it dump epinephrine directly into the bloodstream.
  • Receptor categories: nicotinic receptors are fast ligand-gated ion channels, while muscarinic (M1–M5) and adrenergic (α1, α2, β1, β2, β3) receptors are slower-acting GPCRs.
  • Six stages, six drug targets: manufacture → storage → release → receptor engagement → signal shutdown (via reuptake or enzymatic degradation) → autoreceptor feedback. Any autonomic drug you encounter acts somewhere along this sequence.

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

The mechanism, step by step

Tracing cholinergic transmission from start to finish: choline combines with acetyl-CoA to form ACh, a reaction run by choline acetyltransferase (ChAT); VAChT then loads it into vesicles; a calcium influx triggers release; the transmitter latches onto a nicotinic or muscarinic receptor; and the signal is shut off quickly when acetylcholinesterase (AChE) cleaves ACh back into choline and acetate, with the choline recaptured for reuse. drug targets: hemicholinium stops uptake · vesamicol stops storage · botulinum stops release · AChE inhibitors stop breakdown

The adrenergic transmission cycle runs differently: tyrosine is converted to DOPA by tyrosine hydroxylase — the rate-limiting reaction — then to dopamine, which is packaged into a vesicle where dopamine β-hydroxylase converts it to NE; after release and receptor binding, the signal is cleared predominantly through reuptake via NET rather than enzymes, with leftover transmitter either repackaged or degraded by MAO and COMT. drug targets: metyrosine stops synthesis · reserpine stops storage · guanethidine stops release · cocaine/TCAs block NET reuptake · MAO-Is stop breakdown

💡 The Analogy — rival delivery services

Picture the autonomic system as two delivery firms serving the same streets but carrying opposite instructions. Each shipment passes first through a local depot (the ganglion), and every depot, no matter the firm, accepts incoming trucks with an identical greeting — a "nicotinic" handshake. That shared handshake is exactly why sabotaging the depots (a ganglionic blocker) takes down both firms at once.

Past the depot, the parasympathetic firm finishes deliveries with a "muscarinic" knock, while the sympathetic firm uses an "adrenergic" one. The cholinergic firm reclaims any undelivered parcel almost instantly — AChE shreds it right at the door — whereas the adrenergic firm mostly sucks the parcel back into the truck for later reuse (that's NET reuptake). That's also why a drug that jams the vacuum, like cocaine, leaves signal piling up at the doorstep.

⚠️ Warning — the classic mistake: It's tempting to treat "sympathetic" and "adrenergic" as synonyms, but two cases break that rule: sweat glands are innervated by cholinergic (muscarinic) sympathetic fibers, and the adrenal medulla receives a cholinergic nicotinic preganglionic fiber directly, skipping a second neuron entirely. Overlook these and you'll get sweating and circulating-epinephrine effects wrong.

Muscarinic Agonists & Antagonists

"Rest-and-digest" turned up or switched off
Tier 1
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Muscarinic pharmacology gives you a near-perfect set of opposites: an agonist switches on the full parasympathetic response (SLUDGE-M), while an antagonist such as atropine flips every one of those effects — so understanding one side hands you the other for free.

The high-yield elements

  • Agonists (parasympathomimetics): pilocarpine treats glaucoma and dry mouth; bethanechol treats urinary retention and GI atony (remember "bethanechol = bladder/bowel"). Together their effects spell SLUDGE-M: Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI motility, Emesis, Miosis.
  • Antagonists (antimuscarinics): atropine is the classic example, used for bradycardia, organophosphate poisoning, drying secretions before anesthesia, and pupil dilation. Other agents include ipratropium/tiotropium for COPD, oxybutynin for overactive bladder, scopolamine for motion sickness, and glycopyrrolate.
  • Antimuscarinic toxidrome mnemonic: "Dry as a bone, red as a beet, blind as a bat, mad as a hatter, hot as a hare."
  • Receptor map: M1 acts in the CNS and on gastric acid secretion; M2 (Gi) slows the heart; M3 (Gq) governs smooth muscle, glands, and the eye.

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism

Agonists act straight on muscarinic GPCRs: M3 (Gq) on smooth muscle, glands, and the eye drives contraction, secretion, and pupil constriction, while M2 (Gi) at the SA/AV node slows heart rate. Antagonists like atropine compete for the same site rather than disabling it permanently — this is a surmountable block, which explains why a flood of ACh from organophosphate poisoning can overpower it and why clinicians must dose atropine repeatedly and generously in that setting.

💡 The Analogy — the body's housekeeping dial

Think of parasympathetic tone as a "housekeeping mode" dial in the body — saliva runs, the gut keeps moving, pupils stay relaxed, the heartbeat ticks along slowly. A muscarinic agonist cranks that dial to maximum, so everything secretes and contracts at once. Atropine slams the dial down to zero — the housekeeping staff walk out: the mouth dries up, the gut stops, the pupils blow wide open, and the heart speeds up. The toxidrome's rhyming descriptions are simply what an unattended building looks like: parched, overheated, and a little chaotic.

⚠️ Warning: Keep nicotinic and muscarinic blockade separate in your head. Atropine acts exclusively at muscarinic sites, leaving the neuromuscular junction and ganglia untouched. So in organophosphate poisoning, atropine corrects the muscarinic ("wet") symptoms but does nothing for the nicotinic-driven muscle weakness or paralysis — that requires pralidoxime.

Anticholinesterases & Reactivators

Flooding the synapse by stopping the cleanup crew
Tier 1
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
These drugs don't supply extra acetylcholine — instead they prevent its breakdown, so the transmitter accumulates and amplifies signaling at every cholinergic synapse simultaneously, muscarinic, nicotinic, and central.

The high-yield elements

  • Reversible inhibitors: neostigmine (used for myasthenia gravis, reversing non-depolarizing blockade, and post-op ileus; cannot cross the BBB), physostigmine (a tertiary amine that crosses the BBB, useful in antimuscarinic toxicity), donepezil/rivastigmine/galantamine (Alzheimer's disease), and pyridostigmine/edrophonium (short-acting, used diagnostically).
  • Irreversible agents (organophosphates): this category covers insecticides (malathion, parathion) and nerve agents (sarin), which phosphorylate AChE in a way that becomes permanent once "aging" locks the bond in place.
  • Poisoning presents as cholinergic crisis: the DUMBELS/SLUDGE picture plus bradycardia, with nicotinic effects progressing from muscle fasciculations to weakness to respiratory paralysis.
  • Antidote strategy: give atropine to counter the muscarinic effects together with pralidoxime (2-PAM), which reactivates AChE and addresses the nicotinic signs — but only if given before aging occurs.

  • Why is timing critical for pralidoxime, and what exactly happens during enzyme "aging" at the molecular level?

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism

Under normal conditions AChE destroys ACh in a matter of milliseconds. Block that enzyme and ACh sticks around, amplifying cholinergic signaling throughout the body. Reversible inhibitors let go of the enzyme again within minutes to hours, whereas organophosphates attach covalently — and once "aging" occurs (an alkyl group is lost), that bond becomes permanent, so the body can only recover function by synthesizing fresh enzyme. Pralidoxime can strip the phosphate group off the enzyme, but only before aging has taken place.

💡 The Analogy — a jammed paper-shredder

Picture AChE as a paper shredder that destroys every ACh "memo" the instant it's read. A reversible inhibitor is like wedging a paperclip into the shredder — annoying, but easily cleared. An organophosphate instead fills it with superglue; pralidoxime acts as a solvent that can dissolve that glue, but only if applied before it fully sets ("aging"). Either way, unread memos keep piling up, and the whole office gets flooded with the same message.

⚠️ Warning: Overdosing a myasthenia patient on anticholinesterase therapy can mimic the appearance of worsening myasthenia itself — weakness either way. This is the cholinergic crisis vs myasthenic crisis trap. The (now largely historical) edrophonium test tells them apart: it briefly helps a myasthenic crisis but makes a cholinergic one worse.

NMJ, Ganglia, Nicotine & Muscle Relaxants

Pharmacology of the nicotinic synapse
Tier 1
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Neuromuscular blocking drugs paralyze skeletal muscle through two mechanistically opposite routes at the nicotinic-muscle (Nm) receptor — they either block it (non-depolarizing) or over-activate it (depolarizing) — and recognizing which is which is the single most important distinction in this chapter.

The high-yield elements

  • Non-depolarizing (competitive) blockers: recognizable by the "-curium/-curonium" suffix — rocuronium, vecuronium, atracurium, pancuronium. These compete with ACh for the receptor and are reversible with neostigmine (or sugammadex, specifically for rocuronium/vecuronium). Train-of-four testing shows fade with this type.
  • Depolarizing blocker: succinylcholine causes sustained depolarization (Phase I block) and is broken down extremely quickly by plasma cholinesterase. It is NOT reversible with neostigmine — that would worsen it. Watch for hyperkalemia, malignant hyperthermia, and prolonged apnea in patients with pseudocholinesterase deficiency.
  • Ganglionic blockers: hexamethonium and trimethaphan block Nn receptors at autonomic ganglia, shutting down both divisions at once (largely of historical interest now).
  • Nicotine has a two-phase effect: low doses stimulate ganglia, while high doses produce a depolarizing block.
🧠 Section 2 · Active Recall & Blurting Gate
An anesthetist administers a paralytic before intubating a patient. Recall the two opposite mechanisms by which a drug can paralyze that muscle, and explain — this is the key point — why neostigmine would reverse one type but deepen the other.

Guiding primers

  • Why does succinylcholine produce visible fasciculations before paralysis sets in, while a non-depolarizer produces smooth flaccid paralysis from the start?
  • Why does adding more ACh (via neostigmine) undo a competitive block yet make a depolarizing block worse?
  • A ganglionic blocker lowers blood pressure and dries the mouth — what receptor, common to both effects, explains this?
✓ I've blurted — reveal the plain-language notes
📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism

Non-depolarizing agents occupy the Nm receptor without triggering it, simply plugging the site competitively. Flooding the synapse with ACh via neostigmine outcompetes them and reverses the block. Succinylcholine works in reverse: it activates the receptor and stays bound, so the muscle fires once (the fasciculation) and then cannot repolarize enough to fire again, producing flaccid paralysis. Adding more ACh here only prolongs that depolarized state.

💡 The Analogy — a stuck doorbell

Think of the Nm receptor as a doorbell wired to the muscle. A non-depolarizer is like gum stuck over the button — no one can press it until the gum is cleared away, which is what extra ACh does. Succinylcholine instead jams the button down permanently: the bell rings once (fasciculation), then stays stuck "on" and useless. Pushing the button harder — adding more ACh — doesn't free it; it only makes things worse.

⚠️ Warning: Succinylcholine carries two serious dangers: it can briefly spike serum potassium (hazardous after burns, crush injury, or denervation), and it can set off malignant hyperthermia, treated with dantrolene. Neostigmine should never be used to try to "reverse" it.

Adrenergic Agonists & Antagonists

The sympathetic toolbox — the single biggest ANS chapter
Tier 1 · Highest yield
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Learn what each adrenergic receptor subtype (α1, α2, β1, β2, β3) actually does, and every sympathomimetic drug and every blocker becomes predictable from there — the drug is merely the key, while the receptor it fits is the lock that decides the outcome.

The drugs to anchor

  • Endogenous catecholamines: epinephrine acts on α1, α2, β1, and β2 and is the agent of choice in anaphylaxis; norepinephrine hits α1, α2, β1 with only weak β2 activity and serves as the pressor in septic shock; dopamine's effect shifts with dose (dopamine receptors, then β1, then α1 as dose climbs); isoproterenol works on β1 and β2 only.
  • Selective agonists: phenylephrine targets α1, clonidine targets α2, dobutamine targets β1, and salbutamol/terbutaline target β2.
  • Indirect/mixed agents: ephedrine, amphetamine, and tyramine work by triggering NE release; cocaine instead blocks NET reuptake.
  • α-blockers: prazosin and the rest of the "-zosin" family selectively block α1; phenoxybenzamine (irreversible) and phentolamine (reversible) are both used in pheochromocytoma.
  • β-blockers: the "-olol" family includes propranolol (non-selective), metoprolol/atenolol (β1-selective, "cardioselective"), and carvedilol/labetalol (combined α and β blockade).

Mechanism & the logic of selectivity

All adrenergic receptors are GPCRs. The trick is matching effect to receptor: α1 = squeeze (vessels, sphincters), α2 = brake (presynaptic "enough already" feedback; clonidine pulls this brake centrally to lower BP), β1 = "one heart" — drive it harder, β2 = "two lungs" — open them. A drug's clinical effect is just the sum of which locks it turns.

💡 The Analogy — a building's control panel

Think of the sympathetic system as the emergency control panel of a building. α1 is the master valve that narrows the water pipes (raises pressure). β1 is the dial on the furnace/pump in the basement — turn it up and the whole building's pump (the heart) runs faster and harder ("β1 = the one central pump"). β2 opens the air vents ("β2 = the two lungs"). α2 is the foreman radioing "ease off" back to the crew (presynaptic feedback). Adrenaline is the master override that grabs every switch at once — which is exactly why it's perfect for anaphylaxis: it pumps pressure (α1), opens airways (β2), and supports the heart (β1) simultaneously.

⚠️ Warning — two classic exam traps: (1) Epinephrine reversal — after an α-blocker, adrenaline's α1 pressor effect is gone, leaving only β2 vasodilation, so BP falls. (2) Unopposed α in pheochromocytoma — give a β-blocker first and you remove β2 vasodilation while leaving α1 vasoconstriction unchecked → hypertensive crisis. Always α-block first.

Serotonin & Dopamine

Two monoamines behind mood, psychosis, nausea, migraine and movement
Tier 1 · Foundation
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Serotonin (5-HT) and dopamine are the two monoamines you must know cold, because their receptor subtypes are the targets of antimigraine, antiemetic, antipsychotic and antiparkinsonian drugs. The trick is to attach one signature drug to each receptor subtype — then any clinical scenario decodes itself.

The high-yield elements

  • 5-HT is made from tryptophan (tryptophan → 5-HTP → 5-HT) and cleared by MAO; ~90% of body serotonin is in the gut (enterochromaffin cells), explaining its role in nausea and motility.
  • 5-HT₁ (Gi) → triptans for migraine (5-HT₁B/₁D agonists cause cranial vasoconstriction + block CGRP release); 5-HT₁A partial agonist buspirone for anxiety.
  • 5-HT₂ (Gq) → antipsychotic/antidepressant target; 5-HT₂A blockade underlies atypical antipsychotics; agonism causes hallucinations (LSD).
  • 5-HT₃ (ligand-gated cation channel) → ondansetron, the "-setron" antiemetics, blocking both the gut vagal afferents and the chemoreceptor trigger zone.
  • Four dopamine pathways: nigrostriatal (movement — lost in Parkinson's, blocked → EPS), mesolimbic (psychosis — overactive, the antipsychotic target), mesocortical (negative symptoms/cognition), tuberoinfundibular (inhibits prolactin — blockade → hyperprolactinaemia/galactorrhoea).
  • Dopamine receptors: D1-like (D1, D5 — Gs) and D2-like (D2, D3, D4 — Gi); antipsychotic potency tracks D2 affinity.
  • Serotonin syndrome (combining serotonergic drugs) = the clinical emergency of this chapter — hyperthermia, hyperreflexia, clonus, agitation.

  • Why does the same D2 blockade that relieves psychosis also cause a parkinsonian tremor and galactorrhoea?
  • Why is ondansetron such a clean antiemetic — what does targeting 5-HT₃ specifically achieve that a dopamine blocker doesn't?
  • Why might combining a triptan with an SSRI/SNRI raise a (small) flag, and what is the feared syndrome?
✓ I've blurted — reveal the plain-language notes
📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

The mechanism, step by step

Think of receptor subtypes as different locks that happen to accept the same key (the monoamine). A drug's job is to fit one specific lock. Triptans fit 5-HT₁B/₁D locks on cranial vessels and trigeminal nerve endings → vasoconstriction plus reduced release of the inflammatory peptide CGRP → the migraine aborts. Ondansetron plugs the 5-HT₃ lock — a fast cation channel found on vagal gut afferents and in the brainstem vomiting centre — so the nausea signal never fires. Antipsychotics jam the D2 lock; because the brain uses D2 in four separate circuits, you can't block it in one without blocking it in all.

Dopamine and serotonin are both swept up and broken down by monoamine oxidase. That single fact explains a web of interactions: MAO inhibitors raise both transmitters, so adding any further serotonergic drug risks tipping the system into excess.

💡 The Analogy — one master key, four doors

Dopamine is like a master key that opens four very different doors in the same building — the motor-control room, the reward lounge, the executive office, and the hormone valve. An antipsychotic is a wad of gum jammed into that master key's shape: push it into the building and it blocks every door at once. You wanted to lock only the reward lounge (psychosis), but you've also frozen the motor room (parkinsonism), dimmed the executive office (worse negative symptoms), and jammed the hormone valve open (prolactin rises).

⚠️ Warning — serotonin syndrome: the triad is neuromuscular excitation (clonus, hyperreflexia, tremor), autonomic instability (hyperthermia, tachycardia, diaphoresis), and altered mental state (agitation). Classic triggers: SSRI/SNRI + MAOI, or SSRI + tramadol/pethidine/linezolid/triptans. Distinguish from NMS, which has lead-pipe rigidity, hyporeflexia and slower onset. Treatment: stop the drug, supportive cooling, benzodiazepines, and cyproheptadine (a 5-HT₂ antagonist) in severe cases.

CNS Neurotransmission

Glutamate vs GABA — the brain's accelerator and brake
Tier 1 · Foundation
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Almost the entire CNS pharmacopoeia comes down to tipping one balance: the main excitatory transmitter glutamate (the accelerator) against the main inhibitory transmitter GABA (the brake). Learn the two receptor families and you've pre-loaded the mechanism of sedatives, anaesthetics, antiepileptics and alcohol.

The high-yield elements

  • Glutamate = excitation. Ionotropic receptors: NMDA (Ca²⁺-permeable, needs glycine co-agonist + relief of an Mg²⁺ block; the target of ketamine, memantine, alcohol), AMPA and kainate (fast Na⁺). Excess glutamate → excitotoxicity (Ca²⁺ overload → neuronal death in stroke/status).
  • GABA = inhibition. GABAA is a ligand-gated Cl⁻ channel (fast IPSP; target of benzodiazepines, barbiturates, alcohol, propofol, etomidate, neurosteroids). GABAB is a Gi GPCR (slow; target of baclofen).
  • Benzodiazepines vs barbiturates at GABAA: benzos ↑ frequency of channel opening (ceiling effect, need GABA present); barbiturates ↑ duration and at high dose open the channel directly (no ceiling → fatal).
  • Glycine is the main inhibitory transmitter in the spinal cord/brainstem (blocked by strychnine; impaired by tetanus toxin).
  • Other CNS transmitters worth a line: acetylcholine (memory — Alzheimer's), histamine (wakefulness — sedating antihistamines), and the monoamines from Module 15.

  • Why does NMDA receptor activation require both glutamate and glycine and a depolarisation to relieve the Mg²⁺ block — what does this triple-lock prevent?

  • Why does blocking the inhibitory transmitter glycine (strychnine, tetanus) cause violent muscle spasms rather than sedation?
✓ I've blurted — reveal the plain-language notes
📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

The mechanism, step by step

The NMDA receptor is a coincidence detector with a clever safety lock. At rest, a magnesium ion physically plugs its pore. To open, it needs three things simultaneously: glutamate binds, the co-agonist glycine binds, and the membrane must already be depolarised to expel the Mg²⁺ plug. Only then does Ca²⁺ flood in. This triple requirement is why NMDA receptors mediate learning (they fire only when inputs coincide) — and why their over-activation in stroke causes lethal Ca²⁺ excitotoxicity.

On the inhibitory side, GABAA is a chloride channel with several distinct binding sites. GABA opens it; benzodiazepines bind a separate allosteric site and make GABA more effective (so they do nothing without GABA — hence the ceiling and relative safety). Barbiturates not only prolong opening but, at high concentration, prise the channel open themselves with no GABA needed — no ceiling, so respiratory centres can be silenced completely.

💡 The Analogy — accelerator and brake

Picture the brain as a car with glutamate on the accelerator and GABA on the brake. A benzodiazepine doesn't press the brake itself — it just tightens the brake cable so the existing foot pressure stops the car faster (it needs GABA to act, which caps how far it can go). A barbiturate, at high dose, will stamp on the brake pedal for you — which is why it can bring the whole car, including breathing, to a dead stop.

⚠️ Warning: CNS depressants are additive and synergistic. Combining alcohol + benzodiazepine + opioid stacks GABA enhancement with respiratory depression and is a leading cause of fatal overdose. The classic exam point: flumazenil reverses benzodiazepines but NOT barbiturates or alcohol (different binding mechanism), and it can precipitate seizures in chronic benzodiazepine users.

The Blood-Brain Barrier

What decides whether a drug ever reaches the brain
Tier 2 · Important
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
The blood-brain barrier (BBB) is a wall of tight junctions plus efflux pumps that lets only small, lipophilic, non-pumped molecules into the CNS. Whether a drug is centrally active or peripherally restricted is usually decided right here.

The high-yield elements

  • Structure: continuous capillary endothelium sealed by tight junctions, wrapped by astrocyte foot-processes and pericytes — no fenestrations, minimal pinocytosis.
  • To cross passively a drug must be: small, lipophilic, un-ionised at pH 7.4, and not a substrate of efflux pumps.
  • P-glycoprotein (P-gp/MDR1) is the key efflux pump — it actively throws many drugs back into the blood (e.g. loperamide stays in the gut, second-generation antihistamines stay non-sedating).
  • Carrier-mediated entry: levodopa and baclofen hitch a ride on the LAT1 amino-acid transporter (dopamine itself can't cross — hence give the precursor).
  • The barrier breaks down at the circumventricular organs (CTZ/area postrema — why some antiemetics work centrally), and in meningitis/inflammation (why penicillins penetrate inflamed meninges far better).
🧠 Section 2 · Active Recall & Blurting Gate

Guiding primers — push for "why," not "what"

  • Why does adding carbidopa to levodopa help, given carbidopa itself cannot enter the brain?
  • Why does P-glycoprotein make loperamide a safe anti-diarrhoeal rather than a drug of abuse?
  • Why do clinicians say antibiotics "penetrate better when the meninges are inflamed"?
✓ I've blurted — reveal the plain-language notes
📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

The mechanism, step by step

Ordinary capillaries leak — they have gaps and pores. Brain capillaries don't: their endothelial cells are welded together by tight junctions, so a drug can't slip between cells and must go through them. Going through a cell means crossing two lipid membranes, which only small lipophilic molecules manage. On top of that, the endothelium is studded with P-glycoprotein, an ATP-powered pump that grabs many drugs that do dissolve in and ejects them straight back into the blood — a second line of defence.

Levodopa shows the elegant workaround: dopamine is too polar to cross, so you give its precursor, which is a near-twin of an amino acid and rides the LAT1 transporter into the brain, where it's converted to dopamine. Adding carbidopa — which can't cross the BBB — blocks that conversion only in the periphery, so more levodopa survives to reach the brain and fewer peripheral side effects occur.

💡 The Analogy — a nightclub with a bouncer

The BBB is a club with no side entrances (tight junctions), so everyone must pass the front door. The door only admits people who look the part — small and lipophilic. And even if you sneak in, a bouncer (P-glycoprotein) patrols and throws certain people right back out. Levodopa gets in by carrying a VIP pass (the amino-acid transporter) that dopamine doesn't have.

⚠️ Warning: P-glycoprotein interactions are easy marks to lose. A P-gp inhibitor (e.g. verapamil, ciclosporin) can let a normally-excluded drug into the CNS, causing unexpected central toxicity; a P-gp inducer (rifampicin) can keep a drug out and cause failure. The BBB is also immature in neonates — bilirubin crosses freely, causing kernicterus.

Depression & Anxiety

Raising synaptic monoamines — and the weeks-long lag that proves it's not just reuptake
Tier 1 · Foundation
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Every antidepressant raises synaptic monoamines (5-HT and/or noradrenaline) within hours, yet clinical benefit takes 2–4 weeks — proof the cure is downstream receptor adaptation and neuroplasticity, not the reuptake block itself. Know the five classes, their signature toxicities, and the two emergencies (serotonin syndrome, discontinuation).

The high-yield elements

  • SSRIs (fluoxetine, sertraline, citalopram, escitalopram) — first-line; safest in overdose; side effects: GI upset, sexual dysfunction, initial anxiety, hyponatraemia (SIADH, elderly), QTc (citalopram).
  • SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine) — 5-HT + NA; useful in pain/neuropathy; venlafaxine raises BP and is more toxic in overdose.
  • TCAs (amitriptyline, imipramine, clomipramine) — effective but cardiotoxic in overdose (Na⁺-channel block → wide QRS; treat with bicarbonate) plus antimuscarinic, antihistaminic, α-block effects.
  • MAOIs (phenelzine, tranylcypromine; moclobemide = reversible RIMA) — tyramine "cheese" crisis and serotonin syndrome risk; reserved for resistant cases; need washout periods.
  • Others: mirtazapine (α2/5-HT₂ antagonist — sedating, ↑appetite), bupropion (NDRI — no sexual dysfunction, lowers seizure threshold), buspirone (5-HT₁A partial agonist — non-sedating anxiolytic, no dependence, slow onset).
  • Anxiety pharmacology: SSRIs/SNRIs are first-line maintenance; benzodiazepines only for short-term/acute use (dependence); buspirone and pregabalin as alternatives.
  • Washout rule: ≥14 days between an irreversible MAOI and a serotonergic drug; 5 weeks after fluoxetine before an MAOI (long half-life of norfluoxetine).

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

The mechanism, step by step

An SSRI blocks the serotonin transporter (SERT), so serotonin lingers in the synapse from the first dose. But flooding the synapse immediately triggers inhibitory 5-HT₁A autoreceptors on the presynaptic neuron, which initially throttle back firing. Over two to four weeks these autoreceptors desensitise, firing recovers, postsynaptic receptors adapt, and BDNF-driven neuroplasticity increases — and only then does mood lift. That lag is the single most tested fact in the chapter.

TCAs block the same transporters but are "dirty" drugs: they also block muscarinic (dry mouth, constipation), histamine H1 (sedation), α1 (postural hypotension) and — critically — cardiac fast Na⁺ channels. In overdose the Na⁺ block widens the QRS and can cause fatal arrhythmias, which is why sodium bicarbonate (raises pH + Na⁺ gradient) is the antidote. MAOIs attack from the other direction — they stop monoamine breakdown, so levels everywhere rise, which is powerful but leaves the system dangerously exposed to tyramine and other serotonergic drugs.

💡 The Analogy — a sink with a thermostat

Picture serotonin as water in a sink and SERT as the drain. An SSRI plugs the drain, so water rises instantly — but the tap has a thermostat (the autoreceptor) that senses the high level and turns itself down at first. Only after a few weeks does the thermostat recalibrate and let the tap run normally on top of the plugged drain. That recalibration — not the plugging — is when the patient actually feels better.

⚠️ Warning: Don't stop SSRIs/SNRIs abruptly — a discontinuation syndrome (dizziness, "brain zaps", flu-like symptoms, anxiety) follows, worst with short-half-life drugs (paroxetine, venlafaxine), mildest with fluoxetine. Also: starting an antidepressant in a young person can transiently increase suicidal ideation in the first weeks (energy returns before mood) — review early. Never co-prescribe an MAOI with another serotonergic agent.

Psychosis & Mania

Blocking D2, balancing 5-HT₂ — and the mood stabilisers
Tier 1 · Foundation
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Antipsychotics work by blocking D2 dopamine receptors in the mesolimbic pathway (the dopamine-overactivity hypothesis). "Typicals" do this strongly and pay in extrapyramidal side effects (EPS); "atypicals" add 5-HT₂A blockade and cause fewer EPS but more metabolic harm. Layer on lithium and valproate for bipolar mania.

The high-yield elements

  • Typicals (1st-gen): haloperidol, chlorpromazine, fluphenazine — high D2 affinity → strong antipsychotic effect but EPS + hyperprolactinaemia.
  • Atypicals (2nd-gen): risperidone, olanzapine, quetiapine, aripiprazole (D2 partial agonist), clozapine — D2 + 5-HT₂A → fewer EPS, but weight gain, diabetes, dyslipidaemia.
  • EPS timeline: acute dystonia (hours–days) → akathisia (days) → parkinsonism (weeks) → tardive dyskinesia (months–years, may be irreversible).
  • Clozapine = most effective for treatment-resistant schizophrenia but causes agranulocytosis (mandatory FBC monitoring), myocarditis, seizures, severe constipation.
  • NMS (hyperthermia + lead-pipe rigidity + altered consciousness + autonomic instability + ↑CK) — stop drug, cool, dantrolene/bromocriptine.
  • Lithium for bipolar — narrow therapeutic index (0.6–1.0 mmol/L), renal/thyroid monitoring, toxicity with dehydration/NSAIDs/thiazides/ACEi.
  • Valproate & atypicals (olanzapine, quetiapine) for acute mania; avoid antidepressant monotherapy in bipolar (manic switch).

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

The mechanism, step by step

In the striatum, dopamine and acetylcholine sit in a balance. Block D2 with an antipsychotic and acetylcholine is left unopposed → the parkinsonian, dystonic movements of EPS. That's why an anticholinergic (procyclidine, benztropine) — not more dopamine, which would worsen psychosis — relieves acute dystonia and drug-induced parkinsonism. Serotonin normally inhibits dopamine release in the nigrostriatal pathway, so an atypical's 5-HT₂A blockade disinhibits dopamine there, partly restoring motor function while the mesolimbic D2 block keeps treating psychosis.

Lithium is the odd one out — not a receptor blocker but an ion that interferes with second-messenger systems (inositol monophosphatase, GSK-3β). It's handled by the kidney like sodium, so anything that makes the kidney avidly reabsorb sodium (dehydration, thiazides, NSAIDs reducing GFR) drives lithium up into the toxic range.

💡 The Analogy — a see-saw of dopamine and acetylcholine

The striatum runs on a see-saw between dopamine and acetylcholine. Psychosis is too much dopamine push; an antipsychotic shoves the dopamine end down. But push too far and the acetylcholine end flies up — that's EPS. You can rebalance it either by trimming the acetylcholine end (an anticholinergic) or by choosing an atypical that doesn't push the dopamine end down quite so hard in the motor circuit.

⚠️ Warning: Distinguish the look-alikes. Acute dystonia (young men, hours, oculogyric crisis) → anticholinergic. Akathisia (restlessness, often mistaken for worsening agitation — don't increase the dose) → reduce dose / propranolol. NMS (rigidity + fever + ↑CK) → stop drug, dantrolene. Tardive dyskinesia (late, orofacial) → may be irreversible; switch to clozapine, consider a VMAT2 inhibitor (tetrabenazine/valbenazine). And remember the dementia black-box: antipsychotics increase stroke and mortality in elderly dementia.

Epilepsy & Antiepileptic Drugs

Three mechanisms, one goal — stop runaway neuronal firing
Tier 1 · Foundation
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
A seizure is a sudden burst of excessive, synchronous neuronal firing. Every antiepileptic drug (AED) does one of three things to calm it: damp the "go" signal (block voltage-gated Na⁺ or Ca²⁺ channels), boost the "stop" signal (enhance GABA), or interrupt transmitter release (SV2A). Match the drug's mechanism to the seizure type and you can predict both efficacy and toxicity.

The high-yield elements

  • Two seizure families, two drug strategies: focal seizures (start in one area) and generalised tonic-clonic respond to Na⁺-channel blockers; absence seizures respond to T-type Ca²⁺ blockers (ethosuximide). Choosing the wrong class can worsen seizures.
  • Na⁺-channel blockers — phenytoin, carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, lamotrigine, lacosamide — bind the inactivated state and prolong it, selectively silencing rapidly-firing neurons (use-dependent block) while sparing normal traffic.
  • GABA enhancers — benzodiazepines & barbiturates (GABAA), tiagabine (GAT-1 reuptake), vigabatrin (GABA-transaminase). More GABA = more inhibitory Cl⁻ flux.
  • T-type Ca²⁺ blockers — ethosuximide and (broadly) valproate quiet the thalamocortical 3-Hz oscillation that drives absence seizures.
  • The broad-spectrum trio: valproate (multiple mechanisms — Na⁺, T-Ca²⁺, ↑GABA), lamotrigine, and levetiracetam (SV2A, novel mechanism) cover most seizure types — but valproate is the most teratogenic AED and levetiracetam causes behavioural/mood effects.
  • Status epilepticus is a 3-step emergency: IV lorazepam (or buccal midazolam) → load with IV phenytoin/fosphenytoin or valproate/levetiracetam → refractory: general anaesthesia (propofol, thiopental, midazolam infusion).
  • Pharmacokinetic landmines: phenytoin shows zero-order (saturable) kinetics and is a potent CYP inducer; carbamazepine auto-induces its own metabolism; valproate is an inhibitor that raises lamotrigine levels (rash risk).

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

The mechanism, step by step

Use-dependent Na⁺-channel block is the cleverest idea in the chapter. A voltage-gated Na⁺ channel cycles through three states: resting → open → inactivated. Phenytoin, carbamazepine and lamotrigine bind preferentially to the inactivated state and hold it there longer. A neuron firing at normal rates spends little time inactivated, so it's spared; a neuron firing in a seizure barrage spends lots of time inactivated, so it gets clamped down. selective for the cells that are misbehaving — that's why these drugs stop seizures without flattening all brain activity

Enhancing GABA works on the other side of the synapse. GABAA is a Cl⁻ channel; let more Cl⁻ in and the neuron hyperpolarises, raising the threshold to fire. Benzodiazepines increase the frequency of channel opening (a ceiling effect makes them safer); barbiturates increase the duration of opening (no ceiling → lethal respiratory depression in overdose). Vigabatrin irreversibly inhibits GABA-transaminase (the enzyme that destroys GABA), and tiagabine blocks GABA reuptake — both leave more GABA in the cleft. "Frequ-ENZ-y = bENZos; barbi-DURAT-es = duration"

T-type Ca²⁺ block targets a different rhythm entirely. Absence seizures arise from a 3-Hz oscillation in the thalamocortical loop, paced by low-threshold T-type Ca²⁺ channels. Ethosuximide blocks exactly these channels — which is why it works for absence and nothing else. Valproate hits Na⁺ channels, T-type Ca²⁺ channels and raises GABA, so it covers absence, myoclonic and tonic-clonic seizures alike. Levetiracetam binds the synaptic vesicle protein SV2A, modulating neurotransmitter release through a mechanism unlike any older AED.

💡 The Analogy — crowd control at a stadium

Picture a crowd starting to stampede (the seizure). You have three ways to restore order. Close some of the exit turnstiles that are spinning fastest — that's a Na⁺-channel blocker, which clamps down only on the gates being hammered (the over-firing neurons) and leaves the quiet ones alone. Send in more stewards waving "calm down" signs — that's boosting GABA, adding inhibitory pressure everywhere. Or, for one very specific kind of rhythmic chanting (the 3-Hz absence rhythm), jam the one drum keeping the beat — that's blocking T-type Ca²⁺ channels with ethosuximide.

Valproate is the security chief who does a bit of all three at once — versatile, but with the most baggage (it's the drug you must keep away from a pregnancy).

⚠️ Warning — the classic mistakes: (1) Never give carbamazepine, phenytoin, vigabatrin or gabapentin for absence or myoclonic seizures — they can dramatically worsen them. (2) Lamotrigine + valproate is a notorious pairing: valproate inhibits lamotrigine glucuronidation, doubling its level and sharply raising the risk of Stevens-Johnson syndrome — start lamotrigine at half dose and titrate very slowly. (3) Phenytoin's zero-order kinetics mean once the metabolising enzyme saturates, a small dose rise causes a disproportionate, sometimes toxic, jump in plasma level (nystagmus → ataxia → diplopia → confusion).

Neurodegenerative Disorders

Parkinson's and Alzheimer's — replace what the brain has lost
Tier 1 · Foundation
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Both diseases are about a transmitter running out. Parkinson's = loss of dopamine in the nigrostriatal pathway → restore it. Alzheimer's = loss of acetylcholine in cortical/hippocampal neurons → boost it (plus tame glutamate excitotoxicity). Neither current therapy stops the underlying neurodegeneration — they are symptomatic.

The high-yield elements — Parkinson's

  • Levodopa + carbidopa — the cornerstone; levodopa crosses the BBB (dopamine can't), carbidopa blocks peripheral conversion (↓nausea/hypotension, ↑CNS delivery). Long-term → motor fluctuations & dyskinesias.
  • Dopamine agonists (pramipexole, ropinirole, rotigotine) — delay levodopa; cause impulse-control disorders (gambling, hypersexuality) and sleep attacks.
  • MAO-B inhibitors (selegiline, rasagiline) and COMT inhibitors (entacapone) — block dopamine breakdown to smooth "wearing off".
  • Amantadine for levodopa-induced dyskinesia; anticholinergics (trihexyphenidyl) for tremor-predominant young patients.

The high-yield elements — Alzheimer's

  • Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine) — mild–moderate AD; cholinergic side effects (bradycardia, GI, vivid dreams).
  • Memantine — moderate–severe AD; an NMDA-receptor antagonist that dampens glutamate excitotoxicity; can combine with an AChEI.
  • Both are symptomatic only; newer anti-amyloid antibodies (lecanemab/donanemab) modestly slow decline but carry ARIA (oedema/microhaemorrhage) risk.

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

The mechanism, step by step

Dopamine can't cross the blood-brain barrier, so we give levodopa, which rides the amino-acid transporter into the brain and is converted to dopamine there. The catch: the enzyme that does this conversion exists in the periphery too, wasting most of the dose and causing nausea and hypotension. Carbidopa — deliberately unable to cross the BBB — switches off only the peripheral enzyme, so more levodopa reaches the brain. Early on, surviving neurons store and release dopamine smoothly; as they die off, the brain loses its buffer and dopamine levels swing with each dose, producing wearing-off and peak-dose dyskinesias.

In Alzheimer's, cholinergic neurons degenerate, so AChE inhibitors raise the acetylcholine that remains. Separately, chronic low-grade glutamate excitotoxicity damages neurons; memantine blocks the NMDA receptor just enough to filter out this pathological background "noise" while still allowing the sharp signals needed for memory — which is why a blocker, paradoxically, helps cognition.

💡 The Analogy — topping up a leaking tank vs filtering a noisy line

Parkinson's is a slowly draining fuel tank — levodopa keeps topping it up, but as the tank wall corrodes (neurons die) the fuel gauge swings wildly no matter how you refill it (motor fluctuations). Alzheimer's memantine is different: it's like a noise filter on a phone line — the constant glutamate hiss drowns out real calls, and turning the NMDA "volume" down a notch lets the genuine signals come through.

⚠️ Warning: Never stop levodopa or a dopamine agonist abruptly — it can precipitate a parkinsonism-hyperpyrexia syndrome resembling NMS. Dopamine agonists cause impulse-control disorders — ask specifically about gambling, shopping, hypersexuality. AChE inhibitors are contraindicated in significant bradyarrhythmia/heart block, and antimuscarinics (oxybutynin, TCAs) blunt their benefit — avoid the cholinergic/anticholinergic tug-of-war.

Sedatives & Hypnotics

Tuning the GABAA brake — safely or fatally
Tier 1 · Foundation
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Sedatives enhance the GABAA chloride channel — but how they enhance it determines whether they are safe or lethal. Benzodiazepines increase channel-opening frequency (a ceiling makes overdose rarely fatal alone); barbiturates increase duration and at high dose open the channel directly (no ceiling → respiratory arrest).

The high-yield elements

  • Benzodiazepines — anxiety, insomnia, seizures, alcohol withdrawal, muscle spasm. Classified by half-life: short (midazolam), intermediate (lorazepam, temazepam), long (diazepam, chlordiazepoxide).
  • "LOT" benzodiazepines (Lorazepam, Oxazepam, Temazepam) lack active metabolites and skip oxidative metabolism → safer in liver disease and the elderly.
  • Z-drugs (zolpidem, zopiclone, zaleplon) — bind the same benzodiazepine site selectively (α1) for insomnia; shorter action, still dependence-forming, sleep-driving/parasomnias.
  • Barbiturates (phenobarbital, thiopental) — now niche (epilepsy, induction); potent CYP inducers; lethal overdose; precipitate acute porphyria.
  • Flumazenil reverses benzodiazepines (not barbiturates/alcohol) — use cautiously: can precipitate seizures/withdrawal in dependent patients.
  • Tolerance & dependence develop within weeks; stop by slow taper (abrupt withdrawal → rebound anxiety, insomnia, seizures, delirium).

The mechanism, step by step

The GABAA receptor is a chloride gate. Benzodiazepines bind a site separate from GABA and act as a volume knob on GABA's own signal — they make each GABA molecule open the channel more often. Crucially, with no GABA there's nothing to amplify, so their effect plateaus once endogenous GABA is maxed out: the ceiling. Barbiturates hold the channel open longer and, pushed hard enough, force the channel open themselves without any GABA — there's no natural limit, so breathing can stop.

Metabolism explains the clinical choices. Most benzodiazepines undergo hepatic oxidation (CYP) into long-lived active metabolites — bad in the elderly and in liver disease, where these accumulate and cause falls and confusion. Lorazepam, Oxazepam and Temazepam skip oxidation and are cleared by simple glucuronidation, with no active metabolites — far more predictable in those patients.

💡 The Analogy — a dimmer switch vs ripping out the wiring

A benzodiazepine is a dimmer switch wired into the existing light — it can dim the room only as far as the bulb (GABA) already glows, so it can never plunge you into total darkness. A barbiturate at high dose bypasses the switch and feeds current straight to the bulb — it can take the room to pitch black, including the lights that keep you breathing.

⚠️ Warning: The danger with benzodiazepines is combination — with alcohol or opioids the respiratory depression becomes additive and the "ceiling" no longer protects. Dependence forms fast; withdrawal can cause seizures and delirium, so always taper. In the elderly, benzodiazepines and Z-drugs markedly increase falls, fractures and confusion (on the Beers list).

Opioid Analgesics

μ-receptor agonists — analgesia, euphoria, and the overdose triad
Tier 1 · Foundation
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Opioids are agonists at μ (mu) opioid receptors (a Gi GPCR) → they close Ca²⁺ channels presynaptically and open K⁺ channels postsynaptically, damping pain transmission. The same μ activation gives every effect you must know: analgesia, euphoria, respiratory depression, miosis, constipation.

The high-yield elements

  • Overdose triad: coma + pinpoint pupils + respiratory depression (the killer). Antidote: naloxone (short-acting → may need repeat dosing/infusion).
  • Strong agonists: morphine (standard), fentanyl (100× potent, patches/anaesthesia), oxycodone, hydromorphone, methadone (long t½, QT), diamorphine.
  • Codeine & tramadol are prodrugs activated by CYP2D6 → ultra-rapid metabolisers get toxicity, poor metabolisers get no analgesia; codeine contraindicated in children/breastfeeding.
  • Partial agonist/antagonists: buprenorphine (partial μ — ceiling on respiratory depression, used in addiction), naloxone (antagonist), naltrexone (oral, addiction).
  • Tolerance develops to most effects except miosis and constipation — these persist throughout treatment.
  • Tramadol also inhibits 5-HT/NA reuptake → serotonin syndrome risk and lowers seizure threshold.

The mechanism, step by step

A μ-opioid receptor is a Gi-coupled brake on neurons: when an opioid binds, it closes voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels on the pain-transmitting (presynaptic) terminal — so less transmitter is released — and opens K⁺ channels on the receiving (postsynaptic) neuron — so it's harder to fire. In the dorsal horn and brainstem this turns the volume down on pain. In the brainstem respiratory centre the same dampening reduces the drive to breathe — the dose-limiting, life-threatening effect.

Codeine is inert until CYP2D6 converts ~10% of it to morphine. Genetics scatter this enzyme's activity across the population: ultra-rapid metabolisers convert too much and risk overdose (notoriously, breastfed infants of such mothers), while poor metabolisers get essentially no pain relief. Tolerance arises from receptor desensitisation and adaptation, but the gut's μ receptors and the pupillary pathway adapt little — so constipation and miosis stay put.

💡 The Analogy — a master volume dial

The μ receptor is a master volume dial on the nervous system. Turn it down and the pain channel goes quiet (analgesia) — but the dial is wired to several channels at once, so the "breathing" channel goes quiet too. The body slowly learns to compensate on most channels (tolerance), but the "gut" and "pupil" channels never recalibrate — which is why a long-term user still has pinpoint pupils and needs a laxative.

⚠️ Warning: Naloxone's half-life is shorter than most opioids, so a patient can wake, then re-sedate — observe and re-dose or infuse, especially with long-acting opioids (methadone) or fentanyl. Pethidine's metabolite norpethidine accumulates → seizures (avoid in renal impairment); pethidine + an MAOI → severe serotonin syndrome. Buprenorphine, a partial agonist, can precipitate withdrawal if given to someone on a full agonist.

General Anaesthetics

Reversible unconsciousness — MAC, IV induction, and malignant hyperthermia
Tier 1 · Foundation
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
General anaesthetics produce reversible loss of consciousness by tipping the CNS balance toward inhibition — mostly enhancing GABAA and, for some, blocking NMDA. Two practical anchors dominate exams: MAC (potency of inhaled agents) and onset/offset kinetics driven by lipid solubility.

The high-yield elements

  • IV induction agents (fast on): propofol ("milk of amnesia" — GABAA; rapid, antiemetic, causes hypotension, propofol-infusion syndrome); thiopental (barbiturate); etomidate (cardiostable but adrenal suppression); ketamine (NMDA blocker — dissociative, maintains BP/airway, raises secretions, emergence hallucinations).
  • Inhaled maintenance agents: sevoflurane (smooth induction), isoflurane, desflurane, and nitrous oxide (weak anaesthetic, good analgesic).
  • MAC = minimum alveolar concentration preventing movement in 50% — lower MAC = more potent; MAC correlates with lipid solubility (Meyer-Overton).
  • Blood:gas partition coefficient — low solubility (desflurane, N₂O) = faster onset/offset; high solubility = slower.
  • Malignant hyperthermia — volatile agents + suxamethonium trigger uncontrolled SR Ca²⁺ release (RYR1 mutation) → rigidity, hyperthermia, ↑CO₂; antidote dantrolene.
  • Balanced anaesthesia = induction agent + volatile + opioid + neuromuscular blocker + antiemetic, each component doing one job.

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

The mechanism, step by step

MAC is just a potency yardstick: the alveolar concentration at which half of patients won't move to a surgical stimulus. A potent drug reaches that threshold at a tiny concentration, so its MAC number is small — that's why low MAC = high potency. Potency tracks lipid solubility because the more lipid-soluble the agent, the more of it partitions into neuronal membranes at a given pressure.

Speed is a separate property. An inhaled agent must build up in the blood before it can reach the brain. If the agent is very blood-soluble, the blood acts like a big sponge that must be saturated first — slow onset. If it's poorly blood-soluble (low blood:gas coefficient), the blood saturates almost instantly and brain levels rise fast — quick induction and quick recovery. Ketamine is the outlier among IV agents: by blocking NMDA it produces "dissociative" anaesthesia and actually stimulates the sympathetic system, preserving blood pressure and airway reflexes — ideal in shock, asthma and field/trauma settings.

💡 The Analogy — filling a pool with a sponge in the middle

Getting an inhaled anaesthetic into the brain is like filling a back garden (the brain) from a tap, where the hose runs through a sponge (the blood). A blood-soluble agent is a giant dry sponge that drinks up everything first — the garden fills slowly. A blood-insoluble agent is a tiny sponge that saturates at once, so water reaches the garden almost immediately. MAC, meanwhile, is a different question entirely: how strong the water needs to be.

⚠️ Warning — malignant hyperthermia: a genetic (RYR1) susceptibility triggered by volatile agents or suxamethonium. Earliest sign is often a rising end-tidal CO₂ and masseter spasm, then hyperthermia, rigidity, acidosis, hyperkalaemia. Treat immediately: stop trigger, 100% O₂, IV dantrolene (blocks SR Ca²⁺ release), active cooling, treat hyperkalaemia/arrhythmia. Also note propofol-infusion syndrome (prolonged high-dose → metabolic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, cardiac failure) and etomidate's adrenal suppression.

Local Anaesthetics

Blocking Na+ channels from the inside — esters, amides and toxicity
Tier 1 · Foundation
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Local anaesthetics (the "-caines") block voltage-gated Na⁺ channels from the inside of the axon, so no action potential can propagate and no pain signal reaches the brain — all without loss of consciousness. Two structural classes (esters vs amides) and one dangerous toxicity (CNS then cardiac) carry the chapter.

The high-yield elements

  • Mechanism: the un-ionised form crosses the membrane, then the ionised form binds the Na⁺ channel from inside — use-dependent block (prefers open/active channels), so rapidly-firing pain fibres are hit hardest.
  • Amides (two "i"s: lidocaine, bupivacaine, prilocaine, ropivacaine) — hepatic metabolism, rare allergy.
  • Esters (procaine, tetracaine, benzocaine, cocaine) — plasma cholinesterase metabolism; metabolite PABA → allergy more common.
  • Acidic/infected tissue reduces efficacy — low pH keeps the drug ionised so less crosses the membrane (ion trapping); this ties directly to pH-partitioning.
  • Adrenaline co-injection → vasoconstriction → longer action, less bleeding, lower systemic toxicity — but never in end-arteries (fingers, toes, nose, penis).
  • LA systemic toxicity (LAST): CNS first (perioral tingling, tinnitus, seizures) then cardiac (arrhythmia, arrest — bupivacaine is the most cardiotoxic). Treatment: stop, support, IV lipid emulsion ("lipid rescue").
  • Prilocaine/benzocaine → methaemoglobinaemia (treat with methylene blue).

The mechanism, step by step

A local anaesthetic is a weak base that exists as two forms in equilibrium: an un-ionised form that dissolves through the lipid axon membrane, and an ionised form that does the actual work — binding the Na⁺ channel from the inside. So the drug must be un-ionised to get in and ionised to act. The block is use-dependent: it prefers channels that are open or inactivated, so the busy pain fibres firing repeatedly are silenced preferentially. Small, unmyelinated, rapidly-firing fibres (pain, temperature) go first; large motor fibres last.

Tissue pH shifts the equilibrium. In infected, acidic tissue, more of the drug sits in the ionised form that can't cross the membrane — so little reaches the inside and the block is weak. Adrenaline constricts local vessels, keeping the drug at the site (longer block) and out of the bloodstream (less systemic toxicity) — but in an end-artery that vasoconstriction can cause ischaemic necrosis.

💡 The Analogy — a key that must be smuggled in, then assembled

The drug is like a key delivered in two parts: only the "smooth" (un-ionised) half can slip under the locked door (the membrane), but only once inside, in its "toothed" (ionised) form, can it actually turn the lock (block the channel). Acidic tissue jams too many keys in the toothed form on the wrong side of the door — they never get in, so the lock never turns.

⚠️ Warning — LA systemic toxicity (LAST): caused by inadvertent intravascular injection or overdose. Bupivacaine is the most cardiotoxic (binds cardiac Na⁺ channels avidly, hard to resuscitate). 📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

The mechanism, step by step

Endocannabinoids are unusual: they're made on demand by the postsynaptic neuron and travel backwards across the synapse to CB1 receptors on the presynaptic terminal, where they dial down further transmitter release. This "retrograde brake" lets a neuron tell its inputs "that's enough" — the basis for cannabis's effects on memory, appetite and pain. THC mimics this but floods the whole system indiscriminately, producing euphoria and appetite stimulation but also impaired short-term memory and, at high doses or in vulnerable people, anxiety and psychosis.

💡 The Analogy — a "talk to the hand" signal

An endocannabinoid is the receiving neuron putting up a "that's enough — ease off" hand signal back to the sender. THC is like jamming that hand-signal on permanently across the whole brain: appetite circuits, memory circuits and mood circuits all get told to ease off at once.

⚠️ Warning: Heavy, early and high-potency cannabis use is associated with increased risk of psychotic illness in susceptible individuals, and with cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome (paradoxical cyclical vomiting relieved by hot showers). Synthetic cannabinoids ("spice") are full agonists and far more dangerous than THC.

Ethanol

Zero-order metabolism, withdrawal, and disulfiram
Tier 2 · Important
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Ethanol is a CNS depressant that enhances GABAA and inhibits NMDA. Two facts dominate exams: its zero-order metabolism (constant rate, ~one unit/hour) and the GABA/glutamate rebound that drives a potentially fatal withdrawal.

The high-yield elements

  • Metabolism: ethanol → (alcohol dehydrogenase) → acetaldehyde → (aldehyde dehydrogenase, ALDH) → acetate. The first step follows zero-order kinetics (enzyme saturated).
  • Disulfiram blocks ALDH → acetaldehyde accumulates → flushing, nausea, headache (aversive); the same reaction occurs with metronidazole, some cephalosporins, chlorpropamide.
  • Withdrawal (GABA down-regulated, glutamate up-regulated by chronic use): tremor/anxiety (6–12 h) → seizures (24–48 h) → delirium tremens (48–72 h, mortality). Treat with benzodiazepines (long-acting: chlordiazepoxide/diazepam) + thiamine.
  • Give thiamine BEFORE glucose to prevent precipitating Wernicke's encephalopathy (ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, confusion).
  • Relapse-prevention drugs: naltrexone (opioid antagonist — reduces craving/reward), acamprosate (modulates glutamate/GABA), disulfiram (aversive).

  • Why does abruptly stopping alcohol cause CNS hyperexcitability and seizures (think GABA/glutamate adaptation)?

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

The mechanism, step by step

Because the enzyme that breaks down alcohol is saturated at ordinary drinking levels, it works flat-out at a constant rate — so blood alcohol falls in a straight line (zero-order), not proportionally to how much is present. Chronically, the brain adapts to alcohol's GABA-boosting, glutamate-blocking depression by down-regulating GABAA and up-regulating NMDA. Remove the alcohol suddenly and that compensation is unmasked: an under-braked, over-excited brain — hence tremor, seizures and delirium tremens. Benzodiazepines re-supply the GABA tone while the brain readjusts.

💡 The Analogy — leaning into a strong wind

A chronic drinker's brain is like someone leaning hard into a constant gale (the alcohol's depressant push) to stay upright. Take the wind away abruptly and they pitch forward violently — that lurch is withdrawal. Benzodiazepines are a hand on the back, easing the wind down gradually so they can straighten up safely.

⚠️ Warning: Always give thiamine before glucose — a glucose load in a thiamine-deficient drinker consumes the last thiamine and can precipitate Wernicke's encephalopathy. Untreated, it progresses to irreversible Korsakoff's psychosis. Delirium tremens is a medical emergency with real mortality — treat withdrawal proactively, don't wait.

Drug Use Disorders & Addiction

The mesolimbic reward pathway and medication-assisted treatment
Tier 2 · Important
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Addiction is a chronic disorder of the mesolimbic dopamine "reward" pathway (ventral tegmental area → nucleus accumbens). Nearly all drugs of abuse converge on raising dopamine there. Treatment ("medication-assisted treatment", MAT) is organised by substance.

The high-yield elements

  • Core concepts: tolerance (need more for same effect), dependence (physiological need), withdrawal (substance-specific), and addiction (compulsive use despite harm).
  • Reward circuit: dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens reinforces the behaviour; repeated surges drive neuroadaptation and craving.
  • Opioid use disorder: methadone (full agonist) or buprenorphine (partial agonist, ceiling — safer) for substitution; naltrexone for relapse prevention; naloxone for overdose.
  • Alcohol use disorder: naltrexone, acamprosate, disulfiram (from Module 27).
  • Nicotine use disorder: nicotine replacement therapy, varenicline (α4β2 nicotinic partial agonist), bupropion.
🧠 Section 2 · Active Recall & Blurting Gate

Guiding primers — push for "why," not "what"

  • Why do chemically unrelated drugs (heroin, alcohol, nicotine) all produce the same core "reward"?
  • Why does a partial agonist like buprenorphine or varenicline both relieve craving and limit overdose risk?
  • Why is substitution (methadone/buprenorphine) often more effective than abrupt abstinence?
✓ I've blurted — reveal the plain-language notes
📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

The mechanism, step by step

Every addictive drug, by its own route, ends up raising dopamine in the nucleus accumbens — the brain's "that was good, do it again" signal. Repeated artificial surges blunt the system's normal responses, so ordinary pleasures fade and ever more drug is needed (tolerance), while cues trigger powerful craving. Partial agonists are the elegant therapeutic move: buprenorphine (opioid) and varenicline (nicotine) occupy the receptor enough to ease craving and withdrawal, but their ceiling caps the high and the overdose risk, and by sitting on the receptor they block the full agonist from acting.

💡 The Analogy — a thermostat stuck on "reward"

The reward pathway is a thermostat that learns what "normal" feels like. Drugs blast the heat far above normal again and again, so the thermostat resets its baseline upward — now ordinary life feels cold (anhedonia) and only the drug feels warm (craving). A partial agonist is like a low, steady pilot light: enough to stop the shivering of withdrawal, but it can never blast the room hot, so the cycle cools down.

⚠️ Warning: Give buprenorphine only once a patient is in early withdrawal — given too soon to someone on a full agonist, it displaces the agonist and precipitates acute withdrawal. Methadone prolongs the QT interval and has a long, variable half-life (accumulation risk early in treatment). Varenicline requires monitoring for mood changes/neuropsychiatric symptoms.

01 A patient on phenelzine (MAOI) for depression eats aged cheese at a party and collapses with a hypertensive crisis, severe headache, and diaphoresis. Explain the full pharmacological chain of events.
The cheese reaction (tyramine crisis): aged cheese is rich in tyramine — normally metabolised by MAO-A in intestinal epithelium and liver (first-pass), preventing systemic absorption. Phenelzine irreversibly inhibits MAO-A and MAO-B → tyramine bypasses first-pass metabolism → enters systemic circulation → taken up into presynaptic terminals by NET → displaces noradrenaline from vesicles → massive NA release into synapse → intense α1/β1 adrenoceptor stimulation → hypertensive crisis (NA + adrenaline surge), tachycardia, severe occipital headache, diaphoresis, risk of intracerebral haemorrhage. Treatment: IV phentolamine (non-selective α-blocker) or nitroprusside.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Moclobemide is a reversible MAO-A inhibitor (RIMA) — tyramine competitively displaces it, making the cheese reaction far less severe. Dietary restrictions less strict than irreversible MAOIs. Selegiline (low dose) is a selective MAO-B inhibitor — does NOT require tyramine dietary restriction at standard doses (MAO-B does not metabolise tyramine in the gut).
⚠️ Exam Trap: MAOIs + SSRIs/SNRIs/tramadol/pethidine → serotonin syndrome (not cheese reaction). MAOIs + sympathomimetics (pseudoephedrine, amphetamine) → tyramine-like crisis. Minimum 14-day washout after stopping an irreversible MAOI before starting any serotonergic drug; 5 weeks after fluoxetine (long half-life) before starting an MAOI.
02 A 70-year-old man with Parkinson's disease on levodopa/carbidopa develops sudden uncontrollable leg movements, fluctuating responsiveness, and periods of freezing. What are these phenomena called and what causes them?
These are motor complications of long-term levodopa therapy: (1) Dyskinesias — involuntary choreiform movements, typically at peak levodopa plasma levels (peak-dose dyskinesia). Caused by sensitisation of dopamine receptors (especially D1) in the striatum from pulsatile dopamine stimulation → aberrant corticostriatal plasticity. (2) Wearing off — return of Parkinsonian symptoms before next dose (as levodopa plasma levels fall) — due to progressive loss of striatal dopaminergic neuron terminals that previously buffered dopamine levels. (3) On-off fluctuations — unpredictable switches between mobile (on) and frozen (off) states. Management: continuous dopaminergic stimulation — COMT inhibitors (entacapone), MAO-B inhibitors (selegiline, rasagiline), dopamine agonists, controlled-release levodopa, deep brain stimulation.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Carbidopa is a peripheral DOPA decarboxylase inhibitor — it does NOT cross the blood-brain barrier, preventing peripheral conversion of levodopa to dopamine (reducing nausea, vomiting, hypotension) and allowing lower levodopa doses. The standard ratio is 4:1 or 10:1 levodopa:carbidopa.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Dietary protein competes with levodopa for transport across the gut (via LAT1 amino acid transporter) — a high-protein meal significantly delays levodopa absorption and onset. Advise patients to take levodopa 30–60 minutes before meals and consider low-protein diet during daytime.
03 A patient with schizophrenia on haloperidol develops severe muscle rigidity, hyperthermia (40.5°C), altered consciousness, and autonomic instability with diaphoresis over 24 hours. What is this and how is it managed?
Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS) — a life-threatening idiosyncratic reaction to dopamine antagonists (antipsychotics, metoclopramide). Mechanism: abrupt central D2 receptor blockade → disruption of dopaminergic pathways in hypothalamus (thermoregulation) and basal ganglia (motor control) → hyperthermia, lead-pipe muscle rigidity (causing rhabdomyolysis → AKI), altered consciousness, autonomic instability. Tetrad: Hyperthermia + Rigidity + Altered consciousness + Autonomic instability. Management: (1) Stop the offending drug immediately; (2) Aggressive cooling, IV fluids, supportive care; (3) Dantrolene (directly blocks SR Ca²⁺ release → reduces muscle rigidity and heat generation); (4) Bromocriptine (D2 agonist → restores dopaminergic tone).
💎 Clinical Pearl: NMS is NOT dose-dependent — it can occur after a single dose or after years of stable treatment. CK is dramatically elevated (rhabdomyolysis). Distinguish from serotonin syndrome: NMS has lead-pipe rigidity + slower onset (days) + hyporeflexia; serotonin syndrome has hyperreflexia + clonus + faster onset (hours) + myoclonus. Both can have hyperthermia and autonomic instability.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Antipsychotic rechallenge after NMS: wait ≥2 weeks after full resolution; switch to a lower-potency or atypical antipsychotic (e.g., quetiapine — lowest D2 affinity among atypicals). Clozapine has the lowest NMS risk. Avoid high-potency typical antipsychotics (haloperidol, fluphenazine) in patients with prior NMS.
04 A 35-year-old woman with generalised tonic-clonic epilepsy takes phenytoin. She is started on oral contraceptives. Six months later she has a breakthrough seizure. What happened?
Phenytoin is a potent inducer of CYP3A4, CYP2C9, and P-glycoprotein. Combined oral contraceptives (COC) contain ethinylestradiol and progestogen — both metabolised by CYP3A4. Phenytoin induction → dramatically accelerated oestrogen/progestogen metabolism → sub-therapeutic OCP levels → contraceptive failure (unwanted pregnancy OR, as here, a hormonal trigger for catamenial epilepsy). Additionally, phenytoin itself has narrow therapeutic index — drug interactions can alter seizure control. Management: use non-hormonal contraception (copper IUD — not affected by enzyme induction) or a combined pill with higher oestrogen dose (50 µg EE). Do NOT use progestogen-only pill or implant (too reliant on progestogen levels).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Enzyme-inducing AEDs (phenytoin, carbamazepine, phenobarbital, primidone, topiramate at high doses, oxcarbazepine) all reduce effectiveness of hormonal contraception. Non-enzyme-inducing AEDs (lamotrigine, levetiracetam, valproate, gabapentin, pregabalin) are safe with hormonal contraception. Lamotrigine itself is affected by OCP — OCP induces lamotrigine glucuronidation → lamotrigine levels fall 50% when OCP started.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Phenytoin has zero-order (saturable) kinetics at therapeutic doses — small dose changes cause disproportionately large changes in plasma levels. Phenytoin also causes concentration-dependent toxicity: nystagmus (early), ataxia, diplopia (intermediate), altered consciousness (high). Monitor levels carefully, especially with any drug interaction.
05 An elderly man with Alzheimer's disease is started on donepezil. His wife calls 2 weeks later reporting vivid nightmares and the patient waking with bradycardia (HR 48). Explain both effects.
Donepezil is a reversible acetylcholinesterase inhibitor (AChEI) — inhibits AChE in CNS synapses → increased synaptic acetylcholine → enhances cholinergic transmission in the hippocampus and cortex (modest cognitive benefit). Both effects are cholinergic: (1) Vivid dreams/nightmares — increased central ACh during REM sleep → intensified dreaming (parasympathetic CNS effects on sleep architecture). Mitigate by taking donepezil in the morning (not at night). (2) Bradycardia — increased peripheral ACh → enhanced M2 muscarinic receptor activation at the SA node → reduced heart rate. Other GI effects: nausea, diarrhoea, urinary urgency (all cholinergic).
💎 Clinical Pearl: AChEIs for Alzheimer's: donepezil (all stages), rivastigmine (mild-moderate, also available as patch — fewer GI side effects), galantamine (mild-moderate). Memantine (NMDA receptor antagonist) is used for moderate-severe AD — can be combined with an AChEI. None modify disease progression — they provide modest symptomatic benefit.
⚠️ Exam Trap: AChEIs are contraindicated in patients with sick sinus syndrome or heart block — severe bradycardia risk. They worsen peptic ulcers (cholinergic → increased gastric acid secretion) and should be used cautiously with NSAIDs. AChEIs also lower the seizure threshold — caution in epilepsy.
06 A patient with bipolar disorder on lithium presents with coarse tremor, vomiting, diarrhoea, and confusion. Lithium level is 2.8 mmol/L (therapeutic range 0.6–1.0). What precipitated toxicity and what is the treatment?
Lithium toxicity — therapeutic range is very narrow (0.6–1.0 mmol/L). Likely precipitant: volume depletion → increased proximal tubular reabsorption of Na⁺ and Li⁺ together (Li⁺ is handled like Na⁺ in the kidney — reabsorbed proximally, competes with Na⁺). Volume depletion → avid proximal Na⁺ reabsorption → Li⁺ reabsorption rises → toxicity. Common causes: vomiting/diarrhoea (this patient), dehydration, NSAIDs (reduce GFR → reduce Li⁺ excretion — most common drug interaction), thiazide diuretics (proximal Na⁺ loss → compensatory proximal Li⁺ retention), ACE inhibitors/ARBs (reduce GFR → reduce Li⁺ clearance). Treatment: IV normal saline (restore volume → increase Li⁺ clearance), hold lithium, consider haemodialysis if level >2.5 mmol/L or severe symptoms.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Lithium toxicity signs by level: 1.0–1.5 (fine tremor, thirst, polyuria — therapeutic range upper limit); 1.5–2.0 (coarse tremor, nausea, confusion); >2.0 (severe tremor, ataxia, seizures, coma, arrhythmias). Chronic toxicity can occur at lower levels than acute toxicity. Neurological effects (cerebellar ataxia, cognitive impairment) can be irreversible.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Loop diuretics (furosemide) do NOT significantly increase lithium levels — they increase distal Na⁺ delivery which does NOT enhance proximal Li⁺ reabsorption. In fact, furosemide with adequate Na⁺ replacement is relatively safe. Thiazides are the dangerous diuretic for lithium (proximal tubule effect). Amiloride is used to treat lithium-induced nephrogenic DI — by blocking ENaC in collecting duct, it reduces aquaporin-2 downregulation.
07 A 25-year-old woman presents with absence seizures. Sodium valproate is started. After 3 months she has gained 8 kg, has tremor, and reports hair loss. Explain each adverse effect.
All three are well-recognised valproate adverse effects: (1) Weight gain — valproate increases appetite and reduces fatty acid β-oxidation (inhibits mitochondrial carnitine transport) → adipogenesis; also stimulates neuropeptide Y. Common (~50% of patients). (2) Tremor — valproate-induced postural/action tremor (dose-related) — due to GABA-A enhancement → inhibition of cerebellar pathways that stabilise limb movement. (3) Hair loss (alopecia) — zinc/selenium chelation by valproate → impaired hair follicle enzyme function → telogen effluvium. Supplementation with zinc and selenium may help; hair often regrows even with continued therapy.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Most important valproate adverse effects to know: hepatotoxicity (idiosyncratic, most common in children <2 on polytherapy — monitor LFTs; mitochondrial disease is a contraindication); teratogenicity — highest rate of any AED (neural tube defects, neurodevelopmental impairment, cardiac/limb defects — avoid in women of childbearing age; requires PREVENT programme registration in UK). Hyperammonaemia — valproate inhibits carbamoyl phosphate synthetase → raised ammonia even with normal LFTs.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Valproate is highly teratogenic — highest rates of neural tube defects (2–3% vs 0.1% background) AND neurodevelopmental harm (autism, reduced IQ). Despite this, valproate remains the most effective AED for idiopathic generalised epilepsy (absence, myoclonic, tonic-clonic). High-dose folic acid (5 mg/day) before conception reduces NTD risk but does NOT eliminate it. Consider lamotrigine or levetiracetam as alternatives in women of reproductive age.
08 A patient with treatment-resistant depression on clomipramine (TCA) takes an overdose of 30 tablets. In A&E, ECG shows QRS duration 160 ms, QTc 550 ms, and BP 80/50. What is the immediate treatment?
TCA overdose — the major life-threatening effects are: (1) QRS widening (fast Na⁺ channel blockade → slowed ventricular conduction → risk of VT/VF); (2) QTc prolongation (K⁺ channel blockade → Torsades de Pointes risk); (3) Hypotension (α1-blockade → vasodilation + negative inotropy). Immediate treatment: IV sodium bicarbonate (1–2 mmol/kg bolus, repeat to maintain pH 7.45–7.55) — two mechanisms: (a) alkalinisation increases the fraction of Na⁺ channels in the non-ionised state, reducing TCA binding; (b) increased plasma Na⁺ concentration increases the Na⁺ gradient across the cardiac membrane → partially reverses Na⁺ channel blockade → narrows QRS. Target: QRS <120 ms. Vasopressors for hypotension (noradrenaline — avoid adrenaline which can worsen arrhythmias).
💎 Clinical Pearl: QRS >100 ms in TCA overdose predicts significant toxicity; >160 ms predicts risk of seizures and VT. Seizures in TCA overdose → IV benzodiazepines (GABA-A agonist — first-line). Do NOT use flumazenil in TCA overdose (can precipitate seizures by removing any benzodiazepine-mediated seizure protection).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Do NOT use class Ia or class Ic antiarrhythmics (procainamide, flecainide) in TCA overdose — they also block Na⁺ channels → additive toxicity. Amiodarone is also contraindicated (QT prolongation). The antidote is sodium bicarbonate — the only pharmacological intervention with proven benefit in TCA-induced QRS widening.
09 A 55-year-old with Parkinson's disease starts pramipexole (dopamine agonist). His wife reports he has developed compulsive gambling, hypersexuality, and binge eating. What is this syndrome?
Impulse control disorders (ICDs) — a class effect of dopamine agonists (pramipexole, ropinirole, rotigotine) acting on D3 receptors in the mesolimbic reward circuit (nucleus accumbens, ventral striatum). D3 receptor stimulation in the reward pathway → dysregulated reward-seeking behaviour → pathological gambling, hypersexuality, compulsive shopping, binge eating, dopamine dysregulation syndrome. Risk factors: male sex, younger age at PD diagnosis, prior history of addictive behaviour, higher dopamine agonist doses. Management: reduce or stop the dopamine agonist — ICDs generally resolve. Switch to levodopa (lower ICD risk) if dopaminergic replacement still required.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Dopamine agonists are also used for: restless legs syndrome (RLS) — first-line; hyperprolactinaemia (cabergoline, bromocriptine — D2 agonists reduce prolactin secretion from pituitary lactotrophs); acromegaly (cabergoline as adjunct). ICD risk applies across all these uses, though highest in PD patients on long-term high-dose therapy.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Dopamine agonist withdrawal syndrome — abrupt cessation after long-term use → dopamine agonist withdrawal syndrome (DAWS): severe anxiety, depression, diaphoresis, nausea, fatigue — resembles opioid withdrawal. Taper slowly. Also: daytime somnolence and sudden-onset sleep (sleep attacks) are class effects of dopamine agonists — warn patients not to drive until established on stable dose.
10 An 18-year-old with generalised anxiety disorder is prescribed diazepam. After 6 weeks she has developed tolerance and wants to stop abruptly. Explain why this is dangerous and the correct approach.
Benzodiazepines (BZDs) act as positive allosteric modulators of GABA-A receptors — they bind the α/γ subunit interface → increase frequency of Cl⁻ channel opening in response to GABA → enhanced inhibitory tone. Chronic BZD use → downregulation of GABA-A receptors (reduced receptor number and sensitivity) AND upregulation of excitatory systems (glutamate/NMDA). Abrupt cessation → rebound CNS hyperexcitability → BZD withdrawal syndrome: anxiety, insomnia, tremor, diaphoresis, tachycardia → seizures (potentially fatal, as in alcohol withdrawal — both target GABA-A). Correct approach: gradual tapering over weeks to months — switch to diazepam (long-acting) if on short-acting BZD, reduce by 5–10% every 1–2 weeks.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Flumazenil (BZD antagonist — competitive antagonist at the BZD binding site on GABA-A) reverses BZD sedation acutely. BUT: in BZD-dependent patients, flumazenil can precipitate acute withdrawal seizures. Also: flumazenil has a short half-life (1 hour) — re-sedation occurs with long-acting BZDs. NOT a substitute for supportive care in BZD overdose.
⚠️ Exam Trap: BZDs are relatively SAFE in overdose alone (respiratory depression is rare unless combined with other CNS depressants). The dangerous combinations are BZD + alcohol, BZD + opioid, BZD + other sedatives — synergistic respiratory depression. Z-drugs (zolpidem, zopiclone) act at the same BZD binding site (α1-selective) — same dependence risk, same flumazenil reversal.
11 A patient is given suxamethonium for rapid-sequence intubation. After 15 minutes they have not recovered from paralysis and are still apnoeic. What genetic condition explains this, and how is it managed?
Pseudocholinesterase (butyrylcholinesterase) deficiency — suxamethonium (succinylcholine) is a depolarising neuromuscular blocker (activates nAChRs at NMJ → persistent depolarisation → flaccid paralysis). Normally hydrolysed within 3–5 minutes by plasma pseudocholinesterase (BChE) → action terminates. In BChE deficiency (autosomal recessive, dibucaine-resistant variants) → suxamethonium is not hydrolysed → prolonged apnoea (hours). Management: continue mechanical ventilation until paralysis resolves naturally (BChE-independent metabolism via spontaneous Hofmann elimination is minimal for suxamethonium — must wait). Fresh frozen plasma (contains BChE) can accelerate recovery. Test with dibucaine number (BChE activity assay) after recovery; genetic testing; screen family members.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Suxamethonium also causes hyperkalaemia (K⁺ released from depolarised muscle — usually mild) — dangerous (cardiac arrest) in burns >24h old, crush injuries, prolonged immobilisation, upper motor neuron injuries (proliferation of extrajunctional nAChRs → massive K⁺ release). Contraindicated in these conditions. Also causes malignant hyperthermia in susceptible individuals (RYR1 mutation — suxamethonium ± volatile anaesthetics → uncontrolled SR Ca²⁺ release → hyperthermia, rigidity).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Suxamethonium is NOT reversed by neostigmine (AChEI) — it is a depolarising blocker; AChEI would worsen or prolong paralysis by increasing ACh at the already-depolarised NMJ. Non-depolarising blockers (rocuronium, vecuronium, atracurium) ARE reversed by neostigmine (or sugammadex for rocuronium/vecuronium — encapsulates the drug).
12 A patient with acute alcohol withdrawal is treated with chlordiazepoxide. He also receives thiamine before any glucose. Why is thiamine given first, and what syndrome does glucose-before-thiamine risk precipitating?
Glucose administration in a thiamine-deficient patient → rapidly consumes the remaining cellular thiamine stores (thiamine pyrophosphate is an essential cofactor for pyruvate dehydrogenase, α-KG dehydrogenase, and transketolase — all required for glucose metabolism). Depleting thiamine further → acute Wernicke's encephalopathy (thiamine-deficient neurons cannot oxidise pyruvate → lactate accumulates → selective necrosis of high-metabolic-rate areas: mammillary bodies, medial thalamus, periaqueductal grey). Triad: Confusion + Ophthalmoplegia/nystagmus + Ataxia. Treatment: parenteral thiamine (Pabrinex IV). Untreated → Korsakoff's psychosis (irreversible anterograde amnesia + confabulation — mammillary body and thalamic damage).
💎 Clinical Pearl: In any malnourished, alcohol-dependent, or vomiting patient presenting to A&E — give IV thiamine (Pabrinex) BEFORE glucose. Always. This is a critical clinical reflex. Oral thiamine supplementation is insufficient if there is any suspicion of Wernicke's — GI absorption is unreliable in malnourished patients.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Wernicke's can also occur in: hyperemesis gravidarum, prolonged TPN without thiamine supplementation, bariatric surgery patients, cancer patients with poor oral intake, dialysis patients. Not exclusively an alcohol-related diagnosis. Any patient with unexplained ophthalmoplegia + confusion should receive empirical IV thiamine immediately.
13 A 65-year-old man is on warfarin post-AF ablation. He is prescribed carbamazepine for trigeminal neuralgia. His INR drops from 2.5 to 1.3. Explain, and what alternative AED would be safer?
Carbamazepine is a potent CYP3A4, CYP2C9, and CYP1A2 inducer. Warfarin (S-warfarin primarily CYP2C9; R-warfarin CYP3A4/1A2) → dramatically accelerated metabolism → reduced warfarin plasma levels → reduced anticoagulant effect → subtherapeutic INR → increased stroke risk. This is one of the most important drug interactions in clinical practice. Safer AED alternatives for trigeminal neuralgia: oxcarbazepine (weaker CYP inducer — less effect on warfarin), gabapentin, pregabalin (not CYP inducers — renally excreted — no significant warfarin interaction), lamotrigine (not a CYP inducer). Carbamazepine also induces its own metabolism (autoinduction) — requires dose escalation over the first 4–6 weeks as CYP3A4 is induced.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Carbamazepine mechanism of action: blocks voltage-gated Na⁺ channels (use-dependent — preferentially blocks rapidly firing neurons) → reduces repetitive neuronal firing. Effective in: focal epilepsy, trigeminal neuralgia (drug of choice), bipolar disorder (mood stabiliser). Carbamazepine also causes SIADH (hyponatraemia — enhances ADH action at V2 receptor — especially in elderly).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Carbamazepine hypersensitivity: Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS) and toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN) — strongly associated with HLA-B*1502 allele (Han Chinese, South-East Asian populations). Screen for HLA-B*1502 before prescribing carbamazepine in these ethnic groups. Cross-reactivity with oxcarbazepine and phenytoin exists but is incomplete.
14 A patient is started on clozapine for treatment-resistant schizophrenia. After 3 weeks, she develops a sore throat and fever. FBC shows neutrophils 0.3 ×10⁹/L. What must happen immediately?
Clozapine-induced agranulocytosis — occurs in ~1–3% of patients. Clozapine's metabolite (norclozapine) is believed to directly damage neutrophil precursors in bone marrow (immune-mediated and/or direct toxicity). Agranulocytosis is potentially fatal (sepsis) but is prevented by mandatory blood monitoring. Immediate action: (1) STOP CLOZAPINE IMMEDIATELY — neutrophils <0.5 ×10⁹/L = absolute contraindication; (2) Infective screening (blood cultures, CXR); (3) Broad-spectrum antibiotics for febrile neutropenia; (4) G-CSF to stimulate neutrophil recovery; (5) Report to the clozapine monitoring service (mandatory). Clozapine is permanently contraindicated in this patient after agranulocytosis.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Clozapine monitoring schedule (UK): weekly FBC for 18 weeks → fortnightly for 12 months → monthly thereafter. Must be registered with the CPMS (Clozapine Patient Monitoring Service). Dispensing is linked to blood results — pharmacy will not dispense if overdue monitoring. Clozapine is the only antipsychotic with proven superior efficacy in treatment-resistant schizophrenia.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Clozapine's other serious adverse effects: myocarditis (1st 4–8 weeks — check troponin and ECG at baseline and if symptoms); metabolic syndrome (weight gain, dyslipidaemia, T2DM — greatest metabolic risk of any antipsychotic); seizures (dose-dependent — plasma level monitoring crucial); hypersalivation (M4 muscarinic agonism paradoxically → excess saliva); constipation/bowel obstruction (anticholinergic).
15 A patient with severe chronic pain on morphine 30 mg/day develops constipation despite laxatives. What opioid-specific drug should be added, and what is the mechanism?
Methylnaltrexone (or naloxegol) — peripherally-acting mu-opioid receptor antagonists (PAMORAs). Morphine activates μ-opioid receptors in myenteric plexus → reduced propulsive peristalsis, increased sphincter tone, delayed gastric emptying → opioid-induced constipation (OIC). PAMORAs block peripheral GI μ-receptors to reverse OIC WITHOUT crossing the BBB → do not reverse central analgesia. Methylnaltrexone is a quaternary ammonium compound (charged → cannot cross BBB). Naloxegol is a PEGylated naloxone derivative (PEGylation makes it a P-gp substrate → actively excluded from CNS). Both specifically treat OIC while preserving pain control.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Standard laxatives should be prescribed with every opioid prescription — lactulose, senna, or macrogol. However, unlike most opioid side effects, tolerance does NOT develop to opioid constipation (peripheral GI receptors do not desensitise like CNS receptors) — OIC persists indefinitely and requires ongoing laxative/PAMORA treatment.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Naloxone (oral) in naloxegol/Targinact (oxycodone/naloxone combination) undergoes extensive first-pass hepatic metabolism → very low systemic bioavailability (2%) → blocks GI opioid receptors without reversing analgesia. This first-pass mechanism fails if liver function is severely impaired → systemic naloxone absorption → opioid withdrawal. Avoid oral naloxone-containing formulations in severe hepatic impairment.
16 A 45-year-old with migraine takes sumatriptan and within 30 minutes has complete relief. Two days later she takes another dose but develops chest tightness during the dose. Explain the mechanism and contraindication.
Sumatriptan is a 5-HT1B/1D agonist. 5-HT1D agonism → inhibits trigeminal nerve terminal release of CGRP and substance P → reduces neurogenic inflammation around meningeal blood vessels → aborts migraine. 5-HT1B agonism → vasoconstriction of intracranial blood vessels (the therapeutic target — reverses meningeal vasodilation of migraine). However, 5-HT1B receptors are also present on coronary artery smooth muscle → coronary vasoconstriction → chest tightness, and in patients with underlying CAD, risk of myocardial ischaemia or MI. Contraindications: ischaemic heart disease, Prinzmetal angina, uncontrolled hypertension, stroke/TIA history, peripheral vascular disease.
💎 Clinical Pearl: CGRP is now the major therapeutic target in preventive migraine treatment: erenumab (anti-CGRP receptor monoclonal antibody), fremanezumab, galcanezumab (anti-CGRP monoclonal antibodies) — monthly SC injections. Also: gepants (CGRP receptor antagonists — rimegepant, ubrogepant) for acute treatment — do NOT cause vasoconstriction, safe in cardiovascular disease, no overuse headache risk.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Triptans + SSRIs/SNRIs → theoretical serotonin syndrome risk (5-HT1 agonism + serotonin reuptake inhibition). However, FDA advisory (2006) was largely based on weak case reports — major neurological societies state the actual risk of clinically significant serotonin syndrome from this combination is very low. More clinically relevant serotonin syndrome risks: MAOIs + any serotonergic drug.
17 A patient with myasthenia gravis is treated with pyridostigmine. Despite adequate doses, she remains weak. What would happen if you accidentally gave too much pyridostigmine?
Pyridostigmine is a reversible AChE inhibitor → increases ACh at the NMJ → enhances transmission in MG (where autoantibodies against nAChRs reduce functional receptor numbers). Excessive pyridostigmine → too much ACh at NMJ → depolarisation block (same mechanism as suxamethonium — persistent receptor depolarisation → flaccid paralysis = cholinergic crisis). Both myasthenic crisis (too little AChE inhibitor) and cholinergic crisis (too much) cause muscle weakness. Distinguish by muscarinic signs: cholinergic crisis → DUMBELS (Diarrhoea, Urination, Miosis, Bradycardia/Bronchospasm, Emesis, Lacrimation, Salivation/Sweating) — present in cholinergic crisis, absent in myasthenic crisis. Edrophonium (Tensilon) test: brief improvement → myasthenic crisis; worsening → cholinergic crisis.
💎 Clinical Pearl: MG treatment pyramid: (1) Pyridostigmine (symptomatic); (2) Prednisolone (immunosuppression); (3) Azathioprine or mycophenolate (steroid-sparing); (4) Rituximab or eculizumab (anti-C5, for MuSK-Ab or complement-mediated MG); (5) Thymectomy (if thymoma present — mandatory; also improves outcomes in generalised AChR-Ab MG even without thymoma in patients <65).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Drugs that can precipitate or worsen myasthenic crisis: aminoglycosides (block pre-synaptic Ca²⁺ channels → reduced ACh release), fluoroquinolones, macrolides, neuromuscular blocking agents, beta-blockers, iodinated contrast, magnesium (blocks NMJ transmission — caution in eclampsia treatment). Avoid or use with extreme caution in MG patients.
18 A patient with treatment-resistant depression fails three SSRIs and one SNRI. She is started on ketamine infusions. What is the rapid antidepressant mechanism, distinct from all other antidepressants?
Ketamine (and its S-enantiomer esketamine/Spravato) produces rapid antidepressant effects within hours — compared to weeks for conventional antidepressants. Mechanism: NMDA receptor antagonism (non-competitive, open-channel block of glutamate NMDA receptors). This rapidly increases synaptic glutamate (by blocking inhibitory interneuron NMDA receptors → disinhibition) → activates AMPA receptors → BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) release → TrkB receptor activation → mTOR signalling → rapid synaptogenesis in prefrontal cortex and hippocampus. Depression is associated with synaptic loss in these areas; ketamine reverses this within hours. This mechanism is completely distinct from monoamine-based antidepressants (SSRIs/SNRIs/TCAs/MAOIs).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Esketamine (Spravato) nasal spray is FDA/EMA-approved for treatment-resistant depression (+ standard antidepressant) and for major depression with acute suicidal ideation. It must be administered in a certified medical setting with 2-hour post-dose monitoring (dissociation, haemodynamic effects, abuse potential). Effects are NOT permanent — repeated dosing required (twice-weekly × 4 weeks, then weekly, then fortnightly).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Ketamine at anaesthetic doses causes the 'dissociative anaesthesia' state: profound analgesia + amnesia + cataleptic immobility, but patient maintains airway reflexes and spontaneous breathing — unlike other general anaesthetics. Ketamine increases sympathetic tone (raises BP and HR) — useful in haemodynamically compromised patients but avoid in hypertensive emergencies. Contraindicated in psychosis (can worsen psychotic symptoms through NMDA blockade in schizophrenia-relevant pathways).
19 A 72-year-old man with Parkinson's disease on levodopa develops acute prostatic symptoms (urinary retention, dry mouth, blurred vision). The A&E doctor gives him procyclidine for his tremor. Was this appropriate?
No — procyclidine is a centrally-acting antimuscarinic (anticholinergic) drug used for drug-induced parkinsonism (D2 blockade from antipsychotics → relative cholinergic excess in basal ganglia → tremor/rigidity — corrected by anticholinergic). In idiopathic Parkinson's disease, antimuscarinics are now largely avoided — poor evidence in PD, and they worsen: (1) urinary retention (this patient's presenting symptom — M3 block → detrusor relaxation); (2) Cognitive impairment/delirium (elderly are especially sensitive to central anticholinergic effects); (3) Glaucoma (M3 block → reduced aqueous humour drainage); (4) Constipation, dry mouth, tachycardia. Procyclidine is contraindicated here — the appropriate management of this patient's PD tremor would be adjustment of levodopa or addition of a beta-blocker for tremor.
💎 Clinical Pearl: The anticholinergic burden concept is important in elderly patients — many drugs have anticholinergic activity (TCAs, antihistamines, oxybutynin, procyclidine, promethazine, some antipsychotics) and their combined effect → confusion, falls, urinary retention, constipation. Use the Anticholinergic Cognitive Burden (ACB) scale to identify polypharmacy anticholinergic risk.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Distinguish: procyclidine/benztropine (anticholinergics) are used for DRUG-INDUCED parkinsonism (antipsychotic-induced), NOT for idiopathic PD. For idiopathic PD: levodopa, dopamine agonists, MAO-B inhibitors, COMT inhibitors, amantadine. Amantadine (NMDA antagonist + anticholinergic + dopamine-releasing) is used for levodopa-induced dyskinesias — effective but causes livedo reticularis and ankle oedema.
20 A patient with severe Alzheimer's develops agitation. A colleague proposes haloperidol. What specific risk in this population makes all antipsychotics a serious concern, and what is the only MHRA/FDA-approved non-antipsychotic alternative?
Antipsychotics (both typical and atypical) carry a black-box warning for increased mortality in elderly patients with dementia — primarily from cerebrovascular events (stroke, TIA) and cardiovascular death (arrhythmia, heart failure). Risk is ~1.6–1.7× higher than placebo. Additionally, antipsychotics worsen extrapyramidal symptoms in DLB (Lewy body dementia) — where dopamine neurons are depleted even more than in Parkinson's — potentially causing severe, life-threatening antipsychotic sensitivity reactions (rigidity, aspiration, rapid cognitive decline). Non-antipsychotic alternative: brexpiprazole is now approved (FDA 2023) for agitation in Alzheimer's — partial D2 agonist with lower EPS risk. Also: citalopram/escitalopram for agitation, non-pharmacological approaches (structured activity, sensory stimulation, caregiver training) are first-line.
💎 Clinical Pearl: In DLB specifically: antipsychotics (especially typicals and risperidone) are absolutely contraindicated — severe antipsychotic sensitivity in ~50% → acute parkinsonism, autonomic instability, impaired consciousness → death in some cases. If an antipsychotic is unavoidable in DLB, quetiapine or clozapine (lowest D2 affinity) are relatively safer options.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Memantine (NMDA antagonist) has some evidence for agitation in moderate-severe AD. Donepezil/rivastigmine may reduce BPSD (behavioural and psychological symptoms of dementia) modestly. Valproate is sometimes used for agitation off-label but evidence is weak and toxicity risk (falls, hepatotoxicity, cognitive effects) is significant in this population.
21 A 30-year-old man is brought to A&E with a generalised tonic-clonic seizure that has continued for 8 minutes. Set out the stepwise pharmacological management of status epilepticus and the timing of each step.
Status epilepticus = a seizure lasting ≥5 minutes, or recurrent seizures without recovery of consciousness between them. It is a neurological emergency (prolonged seizures → excitotoxic neuronal death, rhabdomyolysis, hyperthermia). Stepwise management: (1) 0–5 min — supportive: ABC, oxygen, IV access, check glucose (give glucose ± thiamine if hypoglycaemic). (2) 5–20 min — first-line benzodiazepine: IV lorazepam 4 mg (or IV diazepam, or buccal midazolam / rectal diazepam if no IV access) — enhances GABAA; may repeat once. (3) 20–40 min — second-line AED load: IV levetiracetam, valproate, or (fos)phenytoin as an infusion. (4) >40 min — refractory status: rapid-sequence induction with general anaesthesia (propofol, midazolam infusion, or thiopental) + intubation + EEG monitoring in ICU.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Fosphenytoin is a water-soluble phenytoin prodrug — it can be infused faster and causes less injection-site reaction (no "purple glove syndrome") and less hypotension than IV phenytoin, which must be given slowly (≤50 mg/min) with cardiac monitoring because of the risk of bradycardia and hypotension (partly from the propylene glycol diluent).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Do not keep redosing benzodiazepines beyond two doses — escalating benzodiazepine boluses cause respiratory depression without controlling refractory status; escalate to a second-line agent instead. Always check and correct glucose early — hypoglycaemia is a readily reversible cause of seizures.
22 A child of Han Chinese ancestry is about to be started on carbamazepine for focal epilepsy. Which pre-prescription test is recommended and why? What two other characteristic adverse effects does carbamazepine carry?
Test for the HLA-B*1502 allele before starting carbamazepine in patients of Han Chinese, Thai, or other Southeast Asian ancestry. Carriers have a markedly increased risk of Stevens-Johnson syndrome / toxic epidermal necrolysis (SJS/TEN) — a severe, potentially fatal mucocutaneous reaction. (HLA-A*3101 predicts a broader range of carbamazepine hypersensitivity reactions across ethnicities.) Two other characteristic effects: (1) Hyponatraemia / SIADH — carbamazepine has an ADH-like action on the collecting duct → water retention → dilutional hyponatraemia (monitor Na⁺, especially in the elderly). (2) Autoinduction — carbamazepine induces CYP3A4, including its own metabolism, so serum levels fall over the first few weeks and the dose often needs upward adjustment.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Carbamazepine can also cause aplastic anaemia and agranulocytosis (idiosyncratic — baseline and periodic FBC) and is a potent enzyme inducer (reduces efficacy of warfarin, oral contraceptives, and many other AEDs). Oxcarbazepine, a structural analogue, causes less autoinduction and fewer interactions but more hyponatraemia.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Carbamazepine is excellent for focal seizures and trigeminal neuralgia, but it worsens absence and myoclonic seizures — never use it in idiopathic generalised epilepsy until the syndrome is confirmed. The same caution applies to phenytoin, gabapentin, and vigabatrin.
23 A woman with epilepsy stabilised on valproate is started on lamotrigine. Two weeks later she develops a widespread blistering rash with mucosal involvement. What is the pharmacological basis of this dangerous interaction?
Lamotrigine is cleared almost entirely by hepatic glucuronidation (UGT). Valproate is a potent inhibitor of glucuronidation → it roughly doubles lamotrigine's plasma half-life and concentration. A standard lamotrigine starting dose therefore produces toxic levels, and the risk of a serious hypersensitivity rash — Stevens-Johnson syndrome / TEN — rises sharply, especially with rapid titration. Management of this interaction: when adding lamotrigine to valproate, halve the lamotrigine starting dose and titrate very slowly (the slow titration itself is the single biggest protection against rash). Conversely, enzyme-inducing AEDs (carbamazepine, phenytoin) lower lamotrigine levels and require higher doses.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Lamotrigine is one of the preferred AEDs in women of childbearing age (low teratogenicity, doesn't reduce contraceptive efficacy). But oestrogen-containing contraceptives induce lamotrigine glucuronidation → levels fall ~50% when the pill is started and rise again in the pill-free week — seizure control can fluctuate across the cycle.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Any new rash in the first 8 weeks of lamotrigine must be taken seriously — stop the drug and assess for SJS/TEN/DRESS. The risk is highest with high starting doses, rapid escalation, and concomitant valproate. A slow, low titration schedule is the definitive preventive measure.
24 A 7-year-old has brief episodes of staring and eyelid fluttering many times a day; EEG shows 3-Hz spike-and-wave. What is the first-line drug, its mechanism, and why might a clinician choose valproate instead?
This is childhood absence epilepsy (3-Hz generalised spike-and-wave). First-line drug: ethosuximide, which blocks T-type (low-threshold) Ca²⁺ channels in thalamic relay neurons — these channels pace the abnormal thalamocortical 3-Hz oscillation, so blocking them abolishes the absence rhythm. Ethosuximide is the preferred first choice for pure absence epilepsy because it is at least as effective as valproate for absences and has fewer cognitive/attentional and systemic side effects (a head-to-head trial favoured ethosuximide and valproate over lamotrigine). Valproate is chosen instead when the child also has generalised tonic-clonic or myoclonic seizures, because ethosuximide only covers absences whereas valproate is broad-spectrum and covers all three.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Ethosuximide's main side effects are GI upset and, less commonly, idiosyncratic reactions; it has no significant enzyme-inducing activity. The mnemonic: "ethosuximide = absence only" — it has essentially no role in any other seizure type.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Carbamazepine, phenytoin, vigabatrin, gabapentin and tiagabine can all exacerbate absence seizures — a classic exam trap is the child "treated" with carbamazepine whose staring spells get worse. Lamotrigine is an acceptable alternative for absence but can occasionally worsen myoclonus.
25 An infant with West syndrome (infantile spasms) is treated with vigabatrin and improves. What irreversible adverse effect mandates monitoring, and what is vigabatrin's mechanism?
Vigabatrin is an irreversible inhibitor of GABA-transaminase (the enzyme that degrades GABA) → raises CNS GABA → enhanced inhibitory tone ("vigabatrin = vinyl-GABA"). Its defining toxicity is irreversible peripheral visual field constriction (concentric tunnel-vision field defects), affecting up to a third of patients and not reversing on stopping the drug — so regular visual field perimetry (and, in young children, ERG) is mandatory. Because of this, vigabatrin is reserved for specific indications where benefit outweighs the risk: infantile spasms / West syndrome (especially due to tuberous sclerosis, where it is first-line) and refractory focal seizures.
💎 Clinical Pearl: West syndrome triad = infantile spasms + hypsarrhythmia on EEG + developmental regression. First-line treatments are vigabatrin (first choice if tuberous sclerosis) and ACTH / corticosteroids. Vigabatrin can also cause MRI signal changes in infants, usually asymptomatic and reversible.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Don't confuse the GABA-acting drugs: vigabatrin inhibits GABA-transaminase (breakdown), tiagabine inhibits GABA reuptake (GAT-1), benzodiazepines/barbiturates act at the GABAA receptor. All raise GABAergic tone but by different steps in the GABA cycle.
26A migraine patient already on an SSRI is prescribed a triptan and a week later develops agitation, tremor, clonus and a temperature of 39°C. What is happening and how is it managed? (Ch 15)
Serotonin syndrome from additive serotonergic activity (SSRI raises synaptic 5-HT; triptans are 5-HT₁ agonists). Triad: neuromuscular excitation (clonus, hyperreflexia, tremor — lower-limb predominant), autonomic instability (hyperthermia, tachycardia, diaphoresis), and altered mental state (agitation). Management: stop the serotonergic drugs, supportive cooling and IV fluids, benzodiazepines, and cyproheptadine (5-HT₂ antagonist) in severe cases.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Distinguish from NMS — serotonin syndrome has hyperreflexia and clonus with rapid onset (hours); NMS has lead-pipe rigidity, hyporeflexia and slower onset (days). Other potent triggers: SSRI/SNRI + MAOI, tramadol, pethidine, linezolid, methylene blue.
27A young woman on an antipsychotic develops galactorrhoea and amenorrhoea. Which dopamine pathway explains this, and which agent would minimise it? (Ch 15)
Blockade of the tuberoinfundibular dopamine pathway — dopamine normally inhibits prolactin release, so D2 blockade disinhibits prolactin → hyperprolactinaemia (galactorrhoea, amenorrhoea, gynaecomastia, sexual dysfunction). Risperidone and typical antipsychotics raise prolactin most; switching to a prolactin-sparing agent (aripiprazole — a D2 partial agonist, or quetiapine) minimises it.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Aripiprazole's partial agonism actually lowers prolactin and is sometimes added specifically to treat antipsychotic-induced hyperprolactinaemia.
28A patient on chemotherapy is given ondansetron for nausea. What receptor does it block, and where? (Ch 15)
Ondansetron blocks 5-HT₃ receptors — ligand-gated cation channels — at two sites: peripheral vagal afferents in the gut (chemotherapy/radiation release serotonin from enterochromaffin cells) and centrally at the chemoreceptor trigger zone. This dual action makes it highly effective for chemotherapy-induced and post-operative nausea.
⚠️ Exam Trap: 5-HT₃ antagonists prolong the QT interval (especially IV) and cause constipation and headache. They do nothing for motion sickness (that's a vestibular/muscarinic-histaminergic problem — use hyoscine or antihistamines).
29Why is the NMDA receptor described as a "coincidence detector", and what protects it from firing inappropriately? (Ch 16)
The NMDA receptor opens only when three conditions coincide: glutamate binds, the co-agonist glycine binds, and the membrane is already depolarised enough to expel the Mg²⁺ ion plugging its pore. Only then does Ca²⁺ enter. Requiring simultaneous presynaptic release (glutamate) and postsynaptic activity (depolarisation) makes it a detector of coincident activity — the molecular basis of synaptic plasticity and learning (LTP).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Over-activation floods neurons with Ca²⁺ → excitotoxicity (stroke, status epilepticus, traumatic brain injury). Memantine and ketamine are therapeutic NMDA blockers; alcohol also inhibits NMDA.
30A patient with severe spasticity from multiple sclerosis is started on baclofen. What receptor does it act on, and what is the danger of stopping it abruptly? (Ch 16)
Baclofen is a GABAB receptor agonist (a Gi-coupled GPCR) — it reduces excitatory transmission in the spinal cord, relieving spasticity. Abrupt withdrawal (especially of intrathecal baclofen) can precipitate a severe syndrome of rebound spasticity, hyperthermia, autonomic instability, seizures and even death — taper rather than stop suddenly.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Don't confuse the GABA receptors: GABAA is an ionotropic Cl⁻ channel (benzodiazepines, barbiturates, alcohol, propofol); GABAB is a metabotropic GPCR (baclofen). Different structure, different drugs.
31Why can dopamine not be used to treat Parkinson's disease, while levodopa can — and what does carbidopa add? (Ch 17)
Dopamine is too polar to cross the blood-brain barrier. Its precursor levodopa resembles an amino acid and is carried across by the LAT1 transporter, then decarboxylated to dopamine in the brain. Carbidopa inhibits peripheral DOPA decarboxylase but cannot itself cross the BBB — so it blocks wasteful peripheral conversion (reducing nausea and hypotension) and lets more levodopa reach the CNS.
💎 Clinical Pearl: The four requirements to cross the BBB: small, lipophilic, un-ionised, and not a P-glycoprotein substrate. Levodopa breaks the "small lipophilic" rule but gets in by hijacking an amino-acid carrier.
32A patient on loperamide for chronic diarrhoea takes a large dose hoping for a "high" but feels nothing centrally. Why? (Ch 17)
Loperamide is an opioid agonist that acts on gut μ-receptors to slow motility, but it is a P-glycoprotein substrate — the efflux pump at the blood-brain barrier ejects it back into the blood, so it never reaches central reward circuits. Hence it relieves diarrhoea without euphoria or dependence at normal doses.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Massive overdose (or co-ingestion of a P-gp inhibitor) can overwhelm the pump and cause CNS opioid effects — and loperamide also blocks cardiac channels, causing QT prolongation and fatal arrhythmias in abuse.
33A patient newly started on an SSRI for depression reports no improvement after 5 days and asks if it has failed. How do you respond and why? (Ch 18)
Reassure: antidepressants take 2–4 weeks to lift mood. The reuptake block raises synaptic serotonin immediately, but this initially activates inhibitory 5-HT₁A autoreceptors that suppress firing. Benefit appears only after these autoreceptors desensitise and downstream neuroplasticity (BDNF) develops. Continue at the same dose and review at 2–4 weeks before judging response.
⚠️ Exam Trap: In the first weeks, energy and drive can return before mood does, transiently increasing suicide risk (especially in young people) — review early and counsel the patient/family.
34A patient on phenelzine eats a mature cheese platter and develops a severe throbbing headache with BP 220/120. Mechanism and treatment? (Ch 18)
The tyramine ("cheese") reaction. Phenelzine irreversibly inhibits MAO, so dietary tyramine escapes gut/hepatic first-pass metabolism, enters nerve terminals via NET, and displaces stored noradrenaline → massive sympathetic surge → hypertensive crisis. Treatment: IV phentolamine (α-blocker) or sodium nitroprusside.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Moclobemide (reversible MAO-A inhibitor, RIMA) is displaced by tyramine, so dietary restriction is far less strict; low-dose selegiline (selective MAO-B) doesn't require tyramine restriction either.
35A young man started on haloperidol develops a sudden painful upward deviation of both eyes and neck twisting 6 hours after the first dose. Name it and treat it. (Ch 19)
Acute dystonia (oculogyric crisis + torticollis) — an early extrapyramidal effect of D2 blockade, leaving striatal acetylcholine unopposed. Treat with an anticholinergic: IV/IM procyclidine or benztropine (or IV diphenhydramine). It resolves rapidly.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Do not treat with dopamine — that would worsen psychosis. The EPS timeline: acute dystonia (hours–days) → akathisia (days) → parkinsonism (weeks) → tardive dyskinesia (months–years, often irreversible).
36A 62-year-old man with Parkinson's on ropinirole has lost his savings gambling online and his wife reports compulsive shopping. What is the cause? (Ch 21)
Impulse-control disorder caused by the dopamine agonist — direct stimulation of mesolimbic D3 receptors in the reward circuit produces pathological gambling, hypersexuality, compulsive shopping and binge eating. It is dose-related and reversible: reduce or switch off the agonist (and screen for it proactively, as patients rarely volunteer it).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Always ask specifically about gambling, hypersexuality, shopping and eating when prescribing dopamine agonists — patients feel ashamed and won't report it spontaneously. Sudden agonist withdrawal can cause a dopamine-agonist withdrawal syndrome.
37Why is memantine, a receptor blocker, useful in Alzheimer's when the disease involves loss of acetylcholine? (Ch 21)
Memantine targets a different problem: chronic low-level glutamate excitotoxicity. As an NMDA-receptor antagonist, it blocks the pathological background "noise" of excess glutamate (which damages neurons and degrades the signal-to-noise ratio of memory) while still permitting the larger, physiological signals needed for learning. So a blocker improves cognition by filtering noise, not by raising acetylcholine — it's used in moderate–severe AD and can be combined with an AChE inhibitor.
💎 Clinical Pearl: AChE inhibitors (donepezil etc.) suit mild–moderate AD and provide modest symptomatic benefit; none halt progression. Newer anti-amyloid antibodies (lecanemab) slow decline modestly but risk ARIA (cerebral oedema/microhaemorrhage).
38An elderly patient with insomnia and mild liver impairment needs a short course of a benzodiazepine. Which agents are preferred and why? (Ch 22)
Prefer Lorazepam, Oxazepam or Temazepam ("LOT") — they are cleared by glucuronidation rather than hepatic oxidation and have no active metabolites, so they don't accumulate in liver disease or the elderly (more predictable, less risk of prolonged sedation and falls).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Benzodiazepines and Z-drugs markedly increase falls, fractures and confusion in the elderly (Beers list) — use the lowest dose for the shortest time. Long-acting oxidised agents (diazepam, chlordiazepoxide) accumulate dangerously in this group.
39A patient takes an overdose of 50 phenobarbital tablets and stops breathing, whereas a similar number of diazepam tablets would likely only cause deep sleep. Explain the difference. (Ch 22)
Both enhance GABAA, but differently. Benzodiazepines increase the frequency of channel opening and require endogenous GABA — once GABA's effect is maximised there is a ceiling, so overdose alone rarely kills. Barbiturates increase the duration of opening and, at high concentration, open the channel directly without GABA — there is no ceiling, so the brainstem respiratory centres can be silenced completely.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Flumazenil reverses benzodiazepines but not barbiturates. The real danger of benzodiazepines is co-ingestion with alcohol/opioids — the ceiling no longer protects when respiratory depression becomes additive.
40A patient is found unresponsive with a respiratory rate of 5 and pinpoint pupils. After naloxone he wakes, but 30 minutes later becomes drowsy again. Explain. (Ch 23)
Opioid overdose (the triad: coma, pinpoint pupils, respiratory depression). Naloxone, a competitive μ-antagonist, reversed it — but its half-life is shorter than most opioids, so as it wears off the opioid re-occupies the receptor and re-sedation occurs. Manage with repeat doses or a naloxone infusion and prolonged observation, especially with long-acting opioids (methadone) or fentanyl.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Tolerance develops to analgesia, euphoria and respiratory depression but never to miosis or constipation — pinpoint pupils stay diagnostic even in chronic users, and laxatives must be co-prescribed from day one.
41A breastfeeding mother is given codeine for post-caesarean pain; her infant becomes lethargic and apnoeic. What is the mechanism? (Ch 23)
Codeine is a prodrug converted to morphine by CYP2D6. If the mother is an ultra-rapid metaboliser, she produces excessive morphine that passes into breast milk, causing opioid toxicity (sedation, respiratory depression) in the infant. For this reason codeine is contraindicated in breastfeeding and in children — use a safer analgesic.
💎 Clinical Pearl: CYP2D6 activity varies widely: poor metabolisers get no analgesia from codeine/tramadol; ultra-rapid metabolisers risk toxicity. The same genetics affect tramadol.
42During general anaesthesia with sevoflurane and suxamethonium, the patient's end-tidal CO₂ rises sharply, with masseter spasm and a rising temperature. Diagnosis and treatment? (Ch 24)
Malignant hyperthermia — a genetic (RYR1) susceptibility triggered by volatile anaesthetics and suxamethonium, causing uncontrolled sarcoplasmic-reticulum Ca²⁺ release → sustained muscle contraction, heat, and CO₂ production. Earliest sign is often rising end-tidal CO₂. Treat immediately: stop the trigger, 100% O₂, IV dantrolene (blocks SR Ca²⁺ release), active cooling, and treat hyperkalaemia/acidosis/arrhythmias.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Dantrolene is the specific antidote (it also treats neuroleptic malignant syndrome). Etomidate causes adrenal suppression; propofol can cause propofol-infusion syndrome — don't confuse these with MH.
43A hypotensive trauma patient with active bronchospasm needs rapid anaesthetic induction. Which IV agent is preferred and why? (Ch 24)
Ketamine — an NMDA-receptor antagonist producing "dissociative" anaesthesia. Unlike propofol/thiopental (which drop blood pressure), ketamine stimulates the sympathetic nervous system, maintaining blood pressure and heart rate, and is a bronchodilator — ideal in shock and asthma. It also preserves airway reflexes.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Ketamine raises secretions (consider an antisialagogue) and causes emergence phenomena/hallucinations (mitigate with a benzodiazepine). Avoid where a rise in blood pressure or intracranial/intraocular pressure is hazardous.
44A dentist injects lidocaine into an inflamed, abscessed tooth but achieves poor anaesthesia. Why does infection reduce efficacy? (Ch 25)
Local anaesthetics must be un-ionised to cross the nerve membrane, then ionised inside to block the Na⁺ channel. Infected tissue is acidic, which shifts the weak-base drug toward its ionised form that cannot cross the membrane (ion trapping) — so little reaches the intracellular site and the block is weak.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Adding adrenaline causes vasoconstriction → prolongs action, reduces bleeding and systemic toxicity — but never in end-arteries (fingers, toes, nose, penis). This is the same pH-partitioning principle as aspirin absorption.
45During a regional block, a patient develops perioral tingling and tinnitus, then a seizure and cardiac arrest. Which agent is most implicated and what is the specific treatment? (Ch 25)
Local-anaesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST) from inadvertent intravascular injection. The sequence is CNS first (circumoral numbness, metallic taste, tinnitus, agitation, seizures) then cardiac (arrhythmia, arrest). Bupivacaine is the most cardiotoxic (avidly binds cardiac Na⁺ channels, hard to resuscitate). Specific treatment: stop injection, ABC, benzodiazepine for seizures, and IV 20% lipid emulsion ("lipid rescue").
⚠️ Exam Trap: Always know the maximum safe mg/kg dose before injecting. Prilocaine and benzocaine instead cause methaemoglobinaemia (treat with methylene blue) — a different toxicity.
46Which cannabinoid receptor mediates the "high" and appetite stimulation, and how does THC differ from CBD? (Ch 26)
CB1 (mainly CNS, presynaptic) mediates euphoria, appetite ("the munchies"), memory impairment, analgesia and antiemesis. THC is the psychoactive partial CB1 agonist; CBD is non-intoxicating with anticonvulsant and anxiolytic properties. CB2 (immune cells) handles anti-inflammatory/immunomodulatory effects with little psychoactivity.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Endocannabinoids (anandamide, 2-AG) are retrograde messengers — released by the postsynaptic neuron to dampen presynaptic transmitter release.
47A child with Dravet syndrome has seizures refractory to standard drugs. Which cannabinoid is licensed for this? (Ch 26)
Cannabidiol (CBD) is licensed as an adjunct for the severe childhood epilepsies Dravet syndrome and Lennox-Gastaut syndrome (and seizures in tuberous sclerosis). It is non-psychoactive.
⚠️ Exam Trap: CBD inhibits CYP enzymes and raises levels of clobazam's active metabolite — watch for sedation when co-prescribed. Don't confuse the antiepileptic CBD with psychoactive THC.
48A long-term heavy cannabis user has recurrent severe vomiting relieved only by very hot showers. What is this? (Ch 26)
Cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome — paradoxical cyclical nausea and vomiting in chronic heavy users, classically relieved by hot bathing/showers. The only definitive treatment is cannabis cessation; topical capsaicin and supportive antiemetics/fluids help acutely.
💎 Clinical Pearl: It is easily mistaken for cyclical vomiting syndrome — the hot-shower relief and chronic cannabis history are the diagnostic clues.
49Why are synthetic cannabinoids ("spice") far more dangerous than herbal cannabis? (Ch 26)
Synthetic cannabinoids are full CB1 agonists (and often far more potent), whereas THC is only a partial agonist. Full agonism produces much more intense and unpredictable effects — severe agitation, psychosis, seizures, tachyarrhythmias and acute kidney injury.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Heavy, early, high-potency cannabis use is associated with increased risk of later psychotic illness in susceptible individuals.
50A patient on disulfiram for alcohol dependence drinks a beer and develops flushing, throbbing headache, vomiting and palpitations. Explain the mechanism. (Ch 27)
Disulfiram inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH), so ethanol metabolism stalls at acetaldehyde, which accumulates and causes a deliberately aversive reaction: flushing, throbbing headache, nausea/vomiting, tachycardia and hypotension. The unpleasantness is the therapeutic principle (deterrence).
⚠️ Exam Trap: The same disulfiram-like reaction occurs with metronidazole, some cephalosporins and chlorpropamide — warn patients to avoid alcohol with these drugs.
51A chronic alcohol user is admitted; 36 hours after his last drink he has a tonic-clonic seizure, then becomes tremulous, sweaty and hallucinating. What is happening and how is it treated? (Ch 27)
Alcohol withdrawal progressing toward delirium tremens. Chronic alcohol down-regulates GABAA and up-regulates NMDA; removing the alcohol unmasks CNS hyperexcitability → tremor/anxiety (6–12 h), seizures (24–48 h), delirium tremens (48–72 h, with mortality). Treat with long-acting benzodiazepines (chlordiazepoxide or diazepam) to restore GABA tone, plus thiamine and supportive care.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Give thiamine before any glucose — a glucose load in a thiamine-deficient patient can precipitate Wernicke's encephalopathy (ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, confusion), which may progress to irreversible Korsakoff's psychosis.
52Why does a person's blood alcohol fall by roughly the same amount each hour regardless of how much they drank? (Ch 27)
Because alcohol dehydrogenase is saturated at ordinary drinking concentrations, it works at a constant maximal rate — so ethanol is eliminated by zero-order kinetics (a fixed amount per unit time, ~one unit/hour), not proportionally to concentration.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Phenytoin and high-dose aspirin share this saturable, zero-order behaviour — small dose increases can cause disproportionate rises in plasma level.
53A patient on long-term opioid therapy wants to stop. They are started on buprenorphine but immediately develop sweating, agitation and abdominal cramps. Why? (Ch 28)
Buprenorphine is a partial μ-agonist with very high receptor affinity. Given while a full agonist still occupies the receptor, it displaces the full agonist and, because its intrinsic activity is lower, the net opioid effect drops — precipitating acute withdrawal. It must be started only once the patient is already in early withdrawal.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Buprenorphine's partial agonism gives a ceiling on respiratory depression, making it safer than methadone (a full agonist) in overdose.
54What single neural pathway is common to addiction to heroin, alcohol and nicotine, and how does this shape treatment? (Ch 28)
All converge on the mesolimbic dopamine reward pathway (ventral tegmental area → nucleus accumbens) — each drug, by its own receptor route, raises dopamine there. But pharmacological treatment is substance-specific: opioids → methadone/buprenorphine/naltrexone; alcohol → naltrexone/acamprosate/disulfiram; nicotine → NRT/varenicline/bupropion.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Partial agonists (buprenorphine for opioids, varenicline for nicotine) ease craving while capping the high, then block the full agonist — an elegant common strategy.
55A patient wants to quit smoking and is offered varenicline. What is its mechanism and a key monitoring point? (Ch 28)
Varenicline is a partial agonist at the α4β2 nicotinic receptor: it relieves craving and withdrawal (partial stimulation) while blocking nicotine's reinforcing effect if the patient smokes. Monitor for neuropsychiatric symptoms / mood changes and unusual dreams; nausea is the commonest side effect.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Bupropion is an alternative cessation aid (and antidepressant) that lowers the seizure threshold — avoid in epilepsy, eating disorders and alcohol withdrawal.

1How do benzodiazepines modify the GABAA channel?
They increase the frequency of chloride channel opening (need GABA present).
2How do barbiturates differ?
They increase the duration of opening and, at high dose, open the channel directly — no ceiling.
3Which benzodiazepines avoid oxidative metabolism (safer in liver disease/elderly)?
Lorazepam, Oxazepam, Temazepam ("LOT").
4What are the Z-drugs and their use?
Zolpidem, zopiclone, zaleplon — insomnia (selective benzodiazepine-site agonists).
5Antidote for benzodiazepine overdose?
Flumazenil.
6Why is flumazenil used cautiously in chronic users?
It can precipitate seizures/acute withdrawal.
7Why is benzodiazepine overdose rarely fatal alone?
The ceiling effect (they require endogenous GABA to act).
8What makes benzodiazepines dangerous in overdose in practice?
Co-ingestion with other CNS depressants (alcohol, opioids) — additive respiratory depression.
9How should chronic benzodiazepines be stopped?
By slow taper (abrupt withdrawal → rebound anxiety, insomnia, seizures).
10Two further barbiturate hazards besides overdose?
Potent CYP enzyme induction and precipitation of acute porphyria.
14Opioid AnalgesicsCh 23 · 10 Q
1Receptor mediating opioid analgesia and its G-protein?
μ (mu) receptor, coupled to Gi.
2Classic opioid overdose triad?
Coma, pinpoint pupils, respiratory depression.
3Opioid antidote and a pitfall of its use?
Naloxone — short half-life, so re-sedation can occur (re-dose/infuse).
4Two opioid effects to which tolerance does NOT develop?
Miosis and constipation.
5Which enzyme activates codeine, and why does it matter?
CYP2D6 — ultra-rapid metabolisers risk toxicity, poor metabolisers get no analgesia.
6Why is codeine contraindicated in breastfeeding/children?
Ultra-rapid CYP2D6 metabolism can produce dangerous morphine levels.
7Why is buprenorphine relatively safer in overdose?
It is a partial μ-agonist with a ceiling on respiratory depression.
8Two extra hazards of pethidine?
Norpethidine accumulation → seizures (renal impairment); with MAOIs → serotonin syndrome.
9Why does tramadol carry a serotonin-syndrome and seizure risk?
It also inhibits 5-HT/noradrenaline reuptake and lowers seizure threshold.
10Why must constipation be managed prophylactically?
Tolerance never develops to it — laxatives are co-prescribed from the start.
15General AnaestheticsCh 24 · 10 Q
1What does MAC measure, and does low MAC mean more or less potent?
Minimum alveolar concentration preventing movement in 50%; low MAC = more potent.
2What property determines speed of onset/recovery of an inhaled agent?
The blood:gas partition coefficient — low solubility = faster on/off.
3Main molecular target of most general anaesthetics?
GABAA enhancement (ketamine instead blocks NMDA).
4Induction agent of choice in a shocked/asthmatic trauma patient, and why?
Ketamine — maintains blood pressure and airway tone (sympathomimetic, bronchodilator).
5Which IV agent causes adrenal suppression?
Etomidate.
6Triggers of malignant hyperthermia?
Volatile anaesthetics and suxamethonium (RYR1 susceptibility).
7Earliest sign and treatment of malignant hyperthermia?
Rising end-tidal CO₂ (± masseter spasm); treat with IV dantrolene + cooling.
8What is propofol-infusion syndrome?
Prolonged high-dose propofol → metabolic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, cardiac failure.
9Which inhaled agent is a weak anaesthetic but good analgesic?
Nitrous oxide (high MAC).
10What is balanced anaesthesia?
Combining agents (induction + volatile + opioid + muscle relaxant + antiemetic), each doing one job.
16Local AnaestheticsCh 25 · 10 Q
1Molecular mechanism of local anaesthetics?
Block voltage-gated Na⁺ channels from inside the axon (use-dependent).
2Which form crosses the membrane and which form binds the channel?
Un-ionised crosses; ionised binds the channel from inside.
3Sort: lidocaine, procaine, bupivacaine, tetracaine into esters/amides.
Amides: lidocaine, bupivacaine. Esters: procaine, tetracaine. (Amides have two "i"s.)
4Which class causes more allergy and why?
Esters — metabolite PABA is allergenic.
5Why do local anaesthetics fail in infected tissue?
Acidic pH keeps the drug ionised so less crosses the membrane (ion trapping).
6Why add adrenaline, and where is it forbidden?
Vasoconstriction prolongs action and reduces toxicity; avoid in end-arteries (fingers, toes, nose, penis).
7Give the sequence of local-anaesthetic systemic toxicity.
CNS first (perioral tingling, tinnitus, seizures) then cardiac (arrhythmia, arrest).
8Which local anaesthetic is most cardiotoxic?
Bupivacaine.
9Treatment of severe LA systemic toxicity?
Supportive care plus IV 20% lipid emulsion ("lipid rescue").
10Which agents cause methaemoglobinaemia, and its antidote?
Prilocaine and benzocaine; treat with methylene blue.
17CannabinoidsCh 26 · 10 Q
1Which cannabinoid receptor is mainly in the CNS?
CB1.
2Which receptor is mainly on immune cells?
CB2.
3Which constituent is psychoactive, and its receptor action?
THC — a partial CB1 agonist.
4Is CBD intoxicating?
No — non-psychoactive (anticonvulsant, anxiolytic).
5Name the two main endocannabinoids.
Anandamide and 2-AG (made on demand).
6How do endocannabinoids signal relative to the synapse?
Retrogradely — from postsynaptic neuron back to presynaptic CB1, reducing transmitter release.
7Licensed cannabinoid for chemotherapy nausea/appetite?
Nabilone or dronabinol.
8Cannabinoid licensed for specific epilepsies (Dravet/Lennox-Gastaut)?
Cannabidiol (CBD).
9What is cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome?
Paradoxical cyclical vomiting in heavy users, classically relieved by hot showers.
10Why are synthetic cannabinoids ("spice") more dangerous than THC?
They are full CB1 agonists (THC is only partial).
18EthanolCh 27 · 10 Q
1Two molecular actions of ethanol in the CNS?
Enhances GABAA and inhibits NMDA.
2What kinetic order describes ethanol elimination, and why?
Zero-order — alcohol dehydrogenase is saturated at usual levels.
3Give the metabolic pathway of ethanol.
Ethanol → (ADH) acetaldehyde → (ALDH) acetate.
4How does disulfiram work?
Inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase → acetaldehyde accumulates → aversive flushing reaction.
5Name a drug causing a disulfiram-like reaction.
Metronidazole (also some cephalosporins, chlorpropamide).
6Why does abrupt alcohol cessation cause seizures?
Chronic use down-regulates GABAA and up-regulates NMDA; removal unmasks CNS hyperexcitability.
7First-line treatment of alcohol withdrawal?
Long-acting benzodiazepines (chlordiazepoxide/diazepam) plus thiamine.
8Why give thiamine before glucose?
Glucose without thiamine can precipitate Wernicke's encephalopathy.
9Triad of Wernicke's encephalopathy?
Ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, confusion.
10Two relapse-prevention drugs besides disulfiram?
Naltrexone and acamprosate.
19Drug Use Disorders & AddictionCh 28 · 10 Q
1Which brain pathway underlies addiction/reward?
The mesolimbic dopamine pathway (VTA → nucleus accumbens).
2What do nearly all drugs of abuse have in common functionally?
They raise dopamine in the nucleus accumbens.
3Define tolerance.
Needing more drug to achieve the same effect.
4Two substitution treatments for opioid use disorder?
Methadone (full agonist) and buprenorphine (partial agonist).
5Why is buprenorphine safer than methadone?
Partial agonism gives a ceiling on respiratory depression.
6When can buprenorphine precipitate withdrawal?
If given while a full agonist still occupies the receptor (give in early withdrawal).
7Relapse-prevention drugs for alcohol use disorder?
Naltrexone, acamprosate, disulfiram.
8Partial nicotinic agonist for smoking cessation?
Varenicline (α4β2 partial agonist).
9A non-nicotine, non-varenicline aid to smoking cessation?
Bupropion.
10Cardiac caution with methadone?
QT-interval prolongation (plus long, variable half-life → accumulation).
