Natural vs acquired resistance

Natural (intrinsic) resistance is a genetically determined species/group trait — the organism lacks the metabolic process or target site (e.g. Gram-negatives to penicillin G, anaerobes to aminoglycosides, M. tuberculosis to tetracyclines) — and is not a clinical problem. Acquired resistance — a previously sensitive organism becoming resistant on drug exposure — is the major clinical/public-health threat; essentially every major antibiotic class now shows significant acquired resistance.

More than one mechanism may act concurrently against a single drug. Cross-resistance (resistance to one agent conferring resistance to a related, unexposed agent) is usually complete within a class (all sulfonamides, all tetracyclines) but may be partial/absent across drugs (gentamicin-resistant strains may still respond to amikacin). A striking modern example of plasmid-mediated spread is the mcr-1 gene conferring colistin resistance — alarming because colistin is a last-resort agent.

Measures to reduce/prevent resistance

  • Rational, restrained prescribing — avoid indiscriminate, inadequate or unduly prolonged use; shortest effective, symptom-determined course for acute localised infection in healthy patients — minimises selection pressure.
  • Prefer narrow-spectrum, rapidly acting agents; reserve broad-spectrum drugs for when a specific organism/drug cannot be determined; de-escalate/streamline once culture & sensitivity return (24–48 h).
  • Combination therapy for prolonged treatment to prevent emergence of resistance to monotherapy — anti-TB multidrug regimens, combination ART for HIV/AIDS, SABE.
  • Treat resistance-prone organisms intensively (S. aureus, E. coli, M. tuberculosis, Proteus).
  • Surgical/empiric prophylaxis discipline — shortest possible course (ideally a single dose), avoid unnecessarily broad coverage.
  • Infection control & stewardship — accurate culture-based diagnosis, hand hygiene/barrier nursing, vaccination, restrict OTC sale (Schedule H1), avoid agricultural/veterinary misuse (One Health); develop new antibiotics & β-lactamase inhibitors; surveillance.

Antibiotic Stewardship Programme (ASP) — definition & goals

A coordinated, institution-wide set of interventions to optimise antimicrobial use — right drug, dose, route and duration for the right indication — maximising clinical cure while minimising four unintended consequences: emergence of resistance, adverse drug events, superinfection (e.g. C. difficile) and cost. It is the clinical counterpart of infection-prevention and a cornerstone of national/global AMR containment. Useful mnemonic — the "5 D's": right Diagnosis → right Drug → right Dose → right Duration → De-escalation.

Core ASP interventions/strategies

  • Front-end (restrictive): formulary restriction & pre-authorisation before release of reserve/restricted drugs.
  • Back-end (persuasive): prospective audit & feedback — real-time, non-coercive review of ongoing prescriptions.
  • De-escalation/"streamlining": the single most emphasised principle — start broad empirically, narrow to a targeted agent once culture/sensitivity return; switch to monotherapy where one drug suffices.
  • IV-to-oral (step-down) conversion when: (a) overall clinical improvement, (b) afebrile 8–24 h, (c) falling WBC, (d) functioning GI tract — suited agents: fluoroquinolones, clindamycin, doxycycline, metronidazole, linezolid, co-trimoxazole.
  • PK/PD-guided dosing: concentration-dependent killers (aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones, daptomycin) optimise Cmax/MIC or AUC/MIC (high once-daily dosing); time-dependent killers (β-lactams) optimise T>MIC (frequent/extended infusions).
  • Clinical order-sets/guidelines (e.g. IDSA pneumonia/meningitis protocols); rational combination only for defined indications (prevent resistance, broaden empiric cover, synergy, dose-sparing toxicity reduction) — never as a substitute for accurate diagnosis; antibiotic policy/cycling.

Measuring an ASP

Consumption: Defined Daily Doses (DDD)/1000 patient-days, Days of Therapy (DOT). Process: de-escalation rate, IV-to-oral conversion rate, guideline adherence. Outcome: antibiogram/resistance trends, C. difficile incidence, length of stay, mortality, cost savings.

WHO AWaRe classification & Indian context

  • WHO AWaRe: Access (first-line, narrow-spectrum, low resistance potential — e.g. amoxicillin; target ≥60% of total use), Watch (higher resistance potential — fluoroquinolones, 3rd-gen cephalosporins, carbapenems), Reserve (last-resort — colistin, linezolid, ceftazidime-avibactam).
  • India: ICMR & NCDC national AMS/treatment guidelines; National Action Plan on AMR (NAP-AMR); Schedule H1 of the Drugs & Cosmetics Rules (restricts OTC antibiotic sale); "Red Line" campaign (red line on prescription-only antibiotic packs).
Recent advances: CDC 2019 Core Elements update reframed "Drug expertise" as "Pharmacy expertise" — by 2018 ~85% of US acute-care hospitals met all 7 elements. The WHO Access target was raised from ≥60% to a globally binding ≥70% by 2030 at the Sept 2024 UN General Assembly High-Level Meeting on AMR; amoxicillin-clavulanate is promoted as the Access-group "cornerstone." An ICMR need-assessment found very high antibiotic-prescribing rates at primary/secondary Indian hospitals (~60–64%), showing AMS is still largely confined to tertiary centres — driving calls for AMSP at lower tiers. COVID-19 shifted prescribing toward higher "Watch"-category antibiotics, reinforcing the need for sustained stewardship vigilance.

Mechanisms of β-lactam resistance

  • β-lactamase production — hydrolyse β-lactam ring (penicillinase, ESBL, AmpC, carbapenemases — KPC, NDM-1, OXA).
  • Altered PBPs — e.g. PBP2a in MRSA (mecA gene); altered PBP in penicillin-resistant pneumococci.
  • ↓ Permeability (porin loss) & efflux in gram-negatives.

Treatment principles

  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa is intrinsically resistant to many drugs & develops resistance rapidly.

▸★★★Definitive therapy & management of drug-resistant / MDR-TB; WHO drug grouping (A/B/C); newer all-oral regimens (BPaL/BPaLM).

Definitions

Drug-resistant TB (DR-TB) arises when M. tuberculosis acquires spontaneous chromosomal point mutations, selected by inadequate/irregular/single-drug therapy — resistance is not plasmid-mediated. Because mutation rates for different drugs are independent (~1 in 10⁶ for rifampicin, ~1 in 10⁸ for isoniazid), a single bacillus resistant to two drugs simultaneously is vanishingly rare (~10⁻¹⁴) — the rationale for multidrug therapy.

Injectables (kanamycin, capreomycin) are no longer recommended — the modern era is all-oral, bedaquiline-anchored.

Key second-line drug mechanisms

  • Bedaquiline: diarylquinoline inhibiting the c-subunit of mycobacterial ATP synthase (human enzyme ~20,000-fold less sensitive); active vs dividing & dormant bacilli; CYP3A4-metabolised; very long t½ (~5.5 months); resistance via atpE or Rv0678 efflux mutation (causes cross-resistance with clofazimine). Toxicity: QT prolongation (serial ECG), hepatotoxicity. Dose: 400 mg OD × 2 wk → 200 mg thrice-weekly × 22 wk.
  • Linezolid: oxazolidinone, inhibits ribosomal protein synthesis, ~100% oral bioavailability; mitochondrial toxicity on prolonged use → myelosuppression, peripheral/optic neuropathy, lactic acidosis; weak MAO inhibitor (avoid SSRIs).
  • Pretomanid & delamanid: bicyclic nitroimidazole prodrugs activated by F420-dependent nitroreductase; dual action — inhibit mycolic-acid synthesis (aerobic) and generate reactive nitrogen species killing dormant persisters (anaerobic); both prolong QT modestly.
  • Fluoroquinolones (levofloxacin/moxifloxacin): inhibit DNA gyrase — the pivotal oral bactericidal Group A backbone.

▸★★Clofazimine — mechanism, pharmacokinetics & place in anti-tubercular treatment (with its use in leprosy).

Introduction

Clofazimine is a lipophilic riminophenazine dye with both antimycobacterial and anti-inflammatory properties. First evaluated as an anti-TB agent in 1954, but owing to inconsistent early results it was instead licensed for leprosy (1969), becoming a pillar of WHO multidrug therapy (MDT). It has since been repurposed as a key oral companion drug in MDR/RR/XDR-TB regimens, and is also used in non-tuberculous mycobacteria (NTM) and M. ulcerans (Buruli ulcer).

Mechanism of action (multifactorial, incompletely understood)

  • Membrane disruption — alters mycobacterial cell-membrane structure & transport function.
  • Redox cycling/ROS generation — reduced by NADH dehydrogenase (NDH-2), reoxidised by O₂, generating H₂O₂/ROS that interfere with the electron-transport chain — lethal to the bacillus.
  • Inhibition of mycobacterial phospholipase A₂ and microbial K⁺ transport; interference with DNA template function; efflux-pump inhibition contributing to anti-persister activity.
  • Anti-inflammatory/immunomodulatory action — inhibits macrophages, T cells, neutrophils, complement — the basis of its use in lepra reactions.

Against M. leprae it is slowly bactericidal (weaker/slower than dapsone); against M. tuberculosis it suppresses multiplication with notable sterilising activity on dormant/persister bacilli — explaining its value in shortening DR-TB regimens.

Diagram (labelled description): Clofazimine redox cycling at the mycobacterial membrane — clofazimine → reduced by NADH dehydrogenase (NDH-2) → reduced clofazimine → reoxidised by O₂ → H₂O₂/ROS → membrane disruption → bacterial death.

Resistance

Mutations in the transcriptional repressor Rv0678 (mmpR) up-regulate the MmpL5 efflux pump, conferring cross-resistance between clofazimine and bedaquiline — clinically critical since both are mainstays of all-oral DR-TB regimens. pepQ mutations also confer cross-resistance to both; Rv1979c mutations affect clofazimine only. Resistance in leprosy remains rare.

Place in DR-TB therapy

Clofazimine is a WHO Group B companion drug (added to the three Group A drugs — fluoroquinolone, bedaquiline, linezolid). It is a core component of the 9-month shorter all-oral MDR-TB regimen (with bedaquiline, FQ, high-dose isoniazid, ethambutol, pyrazinamide, ethionamide/linezolid) and a preferred Group B add-on in longer individualised regimens for its sterilising activity. The RNTCP/NTEP 2016 XDR-TB regimen used clofazimine 200 mg/day as one of 7 intensive-phase drugs continued into the continuation phase. Also an alternative agent for M. avium complex and rapidly-growing NTM (synergy with amikacin).

Adverse effects

  • Skin/eye pigmentation (hallmark): dose-dependent reddish-black to violaceous discolouration of skin, conjunctiva, hair, and all body secretions; reversible but fades over months–years — major cause of non-adherence/stigma. Also ichthyosis, itching, phototoxicity.
  • GI: very common (nausea, anorexia, abdominal pain, weight loss) in 40–50% on higher TB doses; crystal deposition can cause rare fatal "clofazimine enteropathy" — take with meals.
  • Cardiac: QT prolongation — additive with bedaquiline, fluoroquinolones, delamanid → mandatory baseline + serial ECG/electrolyte monitoring.

Caution: avoid in early pregnancy and significant hepatic/renal impairment; caution with pre-existing QT prolongation/electrolyte derangement. Antacids ↓ absorption; high-fat meals ↑ absorption. In leprosy, clofazimine treats lepra reactions itself (unlike rifampicin).

Recent advances: A 2024 multicentre RCT (China) of a 9-month all-oral regimen (bedaquiline + linezolid + levofloxacin + clofazimine + cycloserine) was non-inferior to longer therapy (unfavourable outcomes 16.5% vs 22.4%), confirming clofazimine's role in short regimens, though QTcF prolongation occurred in ~22.6% — underscoring ECG monitoring. Programmatic use of shorter clofazimine-containing regimens achieved high success rates (e.g. 83.7% in Pakistan). Clofazimine exemplifies host-directed drug repurposing in TB, sparing the 20–30 year cost of novel drug discovery. The newer clofazimine-sparing BPaLM/BPaL regimens are preferred where applicable, but clofazimine remains central where BPaLM is unsuitable (extensive prior exposure, intolerance) — with ongoing vigilance for Rv0678/mmpL5-mediated cross-resistance with bedaquiline.
▸★HIV protease inhibitors — mechanism, pharmacokinetic boosting (ritonavir/cobicistat), uses & adverse effects.

Introduction

Protease inhibitors (PIs) are peptidomimetic antiretrovirals that competitively block the HIV-1 aspartyl protease, the enzyme performing the final maturation step of the virion. By preventing cleavage of viral polyproteins they cause release of immature, non-infectious virions. They act at a late (maturation) step, so are effective in both newly and chronically infected cells, and need no intracellular phosphorylation/activation (unlike NRTIs).

Mechanism of action

HIV protease is an aspartyl-protease homodimer; it cleaves the gag and gag-pol polyproteins into the structural proteins and enzymes (reverse transcriptase, integrase) of the mature virion. PIs bind the active site, blocking this cleavage, so defective, immature, non-infectious progeny are produced, preventing further rounds of infection.

Diagram (labelled description): Late HIV life cycle — gag-pol polyprotein → HIV aspartyl protease (blocked by PI) → cleavage prevented → immature, non-infectious virion released.

Ritonavir/cobicistat boosting

Most PIs are extensively CYP3A4-metabolised. Ritonavir, itself a weak PI, is used at sub-therapeutic dose (100 mg) purely as a potent CYP3A4 inhibitor — raising the companion PI's bioavailability and t½, reducing pill burden, and raising the genetic barrier to resistance ("/r" boosted regimen). Cobicistat is a ritonavir analogue with no antiviral activity — a pure PK enhancer, more CYP3A4-selective, better tolerated, no enzyme induction, but blocks tubular creatinine secretion (small reversible rise in serum creatinine). Nelfinavir is not boosted (cleared by CYP2C19, unaffected by ritonavir).

Clinical uses

  • Component of combination ART (typically 2 NRTIs + 1 boosted PI); reserved largely for second-line/salvage regimens (WHO/NACO) since PI use restricts later options.
  • Boosted darunavir or atazanavir preferred in pregnancy (lopinavir/r not preferred — preterm-birth signal); also used in post-exposure prophylaxis.

Adverse effects

  • Class: GI intolerance (often dose-limiting), asthenia, headache, circumoral/peripheral paraesthesias.
  • Metabolic syndrome/lipodystrophy: central obesity, "buffalo hump", peripheral/facial wasting, dyslipidaemia, insulin resistance/hyperglycaemia.
  • Agent-specific: atazanavir → indirect hyperbilirubinaemia (UGT1A1 inhibition), PR prolongation; indinavir → nephrolithiasis/crystalluria; lopinavir/r → PR/QT prolongation, pancreatitis; darunavir/fosamprenavir (sulfonamide moiety) → rash/hypersensitivity; darunavir → hepatotoxicity.

Drug interactions

As CYP3A4 inhibitors, PIs raise levels of narrow-margin CYP3A4 substrates — contraindicated with ergot alkaloids, cisapride, simvastatin/lovastatin, oral midazolam/triazolam. Enzyme inducers (rifampin, carbamazepine, phenytoin, St John's wort) lower PI levels → use rifabutin instead of rifampin. Atazanavir absorption is acid-dependent — PPIs are contraindicated.

Recent advances: Ritonavir's CYP3A4-inhibiting property is now exploited beyond HIV — as the booster in nirmatrelvir/ritonavir (Paxlovid) for COVID-19. Cobicistat increasingly replaces ritonavir as booster (fixed-dose with atazanavir/darunavir/elvitegravir). PIs have largely moved to second-line roles as integrase inhibitors (dolutegravir-based) now dominate first-line ART. The HCV protease (NS3/4A) "-previrs" (simeprevir, paritaprevir, grazoprevir, glecaprevir, voxilaprevir) extend the protease-inhibition concept to hepatitis C within all-oral DAA combinations.
▸★Classification of systemic antifungal drugs by molecular target/mechanism.

Rationale

Systemic antifungals treat invasive/deep mycoses and recalcitrant superficial infections. The clinically useful classes exploit fungus-specific targets — chiefly membrane ergosterol and cell-wall β-(1,3)-glucan — both absent from mammalian cells, giving selective toxicity. ▸★Current status of HIV treatment & latest HAART/cART guidelines (drug classes, preferred first-line regimen, long-acting therapy).

Introduction

HAART/cART is combination therapy with ≥3 drugs from ≥2 classes that maximally and durably suppresses HIV replication. Goal: plasma HIV-RNA <50 copies/mL, CD4 restoration, and prevention of transmission ("U=U" — undetectable = untransmissible). Therapy controls but does not eradicate integrated provirus, so it is life-long; monotherapy is contraindicated (rapid resistance).

▸★★Antimicrobial use in pregnancy and lactation — placental/lactational transfer principles, safe vs avoided agents, and timing-related foetal risks.

Introduction

Untreated maternal infection itself harms the foetus, so antimicrobials cannot simply be withheld in pregnancy — the challenge is dual safety, since most antimicrobial agents (AMAs) cross the placenta and many enter breast milk. Guiding rule: use only AMAs with an established safety record, at the lowest effective dose for the shortest duration.

General principles of placental transfer & pregnancy pharmacokinetics

  • Lipid-soluble, un-ionised, low-molecular-weight drugs cross readily (high foetal levels); large molecules (e.g. heparin) do not — placental P-glycoprotein partly pumps some drugs back to the mother.
  • Pregnancy PK changes: delayed gastric emptying, ↑ plasma volume (~30%, ↑ Vd/haemodilution), progesterone-induced hepatic enzyme induction (faster metabolism), ↑ renal blood flow → faster clearance (e.g. aminoglycosides) — some AMA doses need upward adjustment.
  • Timing matters: 1st trimester = organogenesis/teratogenesis risk; near-term/3rd trimester = functional/neonatal toxicity risk (kernicterus, grey-baby syndrome).

▸★★Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) in HIV — risk assessment, timing, preferred regimens & follow-up; distinction from PrEP.

Introduction

Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) is the short-course administration of antiretroviral (ARV) drugs, begun as soon as possible after accidental exposure to potentially HIV-infected material, to abort establishment of systemic infection by suppressing local viral replication before dissemination/integration. PEP is classified as occupational (oPEP) — e.g. a needle-stick in a healthcare worker — or non-occupational (nPEP) — condomless intercourse, sexual assault, shared injection equipment, other community exposures.

First-aid (immediate)

Wounds/skin: wash immediately with soap and running water (do not scrub/squeeze; allow free bleeding). Mucous membranes/eyes: irrigate copiously with water/saline (avoid caustic antiseptics). Then report, document, risk-assess, and start drugs without delay.

Timing & duration

Start ASAP — ideally within 1–2 h, and not beyond 72 h of exposure (efficacy falls sharply with delay). Duration: 28 days (4 weeks) of the full regimen.

▸★★Recent advances in the treatment of Hepatitis C — direct-acting antivirals (DAA classification, mechanisms, pangenotypic regimens).

Introduction — the paradigm shift

HCV (a hepatotropic, positive-sense RNA flavivirus, genotypes 1–6) causes chronic hepatitis progressing to cirrhosis/HCC. The toxic, prolonged, low-cure peginterferon-α (PegIFNα) + ribavirin era (24–48 weeks, SVR only ~50%, flu-like illness, cytopenias, depression, haemolytic anaemia) has been replaced by short, all-oral, well-tolerated direct-acting antiviral (DAA) regimens achieving sustained virological response (SVR12) in >95% of patients over just 8–12 weeks, IFN-free and increasingly ribavirin-free. HCV is now the only chronic viral infection reliably curable with drugs.

Rationale for combination: HCV NS5B polymerase lacks proofreading → enormous daily viral turnover and pre-existing resistance-associated substitutions (RASs); single agents have a low genetic barrier, so ≥2 DAAs on different targets suppress replication and prevent resistance (analogous to HIV/ART).

Clinical & public-health impact

Short oral course, >95% cure, minimal monitoring; SVR reduces cirrhosis, HCC and liver-related mortality and can reverse fibrosis. ▸★★Status of metronidazole in different diseases — mechanism of selective toxicity, spectrum, disease-wise dosing, and current shift away from first-line use in C. difficile colitis.

Introduction

Metronidazole, the prototype 5-nitroimidazole (introduced 1959 for trichomoniasis, later shown to be a highly active amoebicide), is a broad-spectrum cidal agent against anaerobic protozoa and anaerobic/microaerophilic bacteria — a workhorse across protozoal, anaerobic-surgical, gastrointestinal and genital infections. Its therapeutic "status" has shifted in several diseases (notably C. difficile colitis) with current guidelines.

Mechanism / basis of selective toxicity

Metronidazole is a prodrug. After diffusing into the cell, its nitro group is reduced by low-redox-potential electron-transport proteins found only in anaerobes (the pyruvate:ferredoxin oxidoreductase, PFOR, pathway). The resulting highly reactive nitro radical acts as an electron sink, competing with the organism's biological electron acceptors, generating reactive compounds that damage DNA and disrupt the energy metabolism of anaerobes (which lack mitochondria). It is selectively toxic to anaerobic/microaerophilic organisms because an aerobic environment attenuates cytotoxicity (O₂ inhibits reductive activation and competes for free electrons) — hence no activity vs aerobes. Additional actions: inhibits cell-mediated immunity, mutagenic, radiosensitising.

Diagram (labelled description): Reductive activation cartoon — metronidazole (NO₂ group) → PFOR/ferredoxin reduction → reactive nitro radical → DNA damage; side note: "O₂ blocks activation (aerobes spared)".

Spectrum

Anaerobic protozoa: Entamoeba histolytica, Giardia lamblia, Trichomonas vaginalis. Anaerobic/microaerophilic bacteria: Bacteroides fragilis, Fusobacterium, Clostridium perfringens, C. difficile, peptococci, anaerobic streptococci, spirochetes, H. pylori, Campylobacter (Gram-positive anaerobes — Actinomyces, Propionibacterium, Lactobacillus — are typically resistant).

Pharmacokinetics

Almost completely absorbed from the small intestine (little reaches the colon unabsorbed → poor luminal cyst clearance); wide distribution — therapeutic levels in vaginal secretion, semen, saliva and CSF (treats brain abscess); hepatic metabolism + renal excretion; t½ ≈ 8 h; CYP inhibitor.

Adverse effects

Common: anorexia, nausea, metallic taste, abdominal cramps. Less frequent: headache, glossitis, dry mouth, impaired concentration, allergic rash/urticaria. Prolonged/high dose: peripheral neuropathy, CNS effects, seizures, leucopenia with repeated courses; thrombophlebitis if poorly diluted IV.

Contraindications & interactions

Caution in neurological disease, blood dyscrasias, 1st trimester pregnancy (mutagenic potential), chronic alcoholics. Disulfiram-like reaction with alcohol; enzyme inducers (phenobarbitone, rifampin) reduce efficacy; cimetidine raises levels; potentiates warfarin (inhibits metabolism); reduces renal lithium clearance → lithium toxicity.

Resistance

Clinically significant resistance has not emerged in E. histolytica; reduced responsiveness reported in some T. vaginalis and Giardia; resistance is a real clinical problem mainly with H. pylori. Mechanism: deficient generation of the reactive nitro radical/lower PFOR activity; in Bacteroides, nim resistance genes encode a nitroimidazole reductase converting the drug to an inactive 5-aminoimidazole.

Recent advances: For C. difficile infection, metronidazole is no longer first-line for adults — oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin are now preferred for initial non-severe CDI (2017–18 IDSA update); IV metronidazole is retained only as an adjunct to oral vancomycin in fulminant disease/ileus. For H. pylori, high background metronidazole resistance (~27% treatment-naïve, ~41% treatment-experienced) undermines metronidazole-containing triple therapy — bismuth quadruple therapy and the 3-in-1 bismuth/metronidazole/tetracycline capsule retain efficacy even against resistant strains; concomitant therapy and 10–14 day courses are favoured. Congeners broaden the picture: tinidazole (t½ ~12h, better tolerated, single-dose) and secnidazole (t½ 17–29h, single 2 g dose for amoebiasis/giardiasis/trichomoniasis/BV) share metronidazole's spectrum with more convenient dosing.

Section 4 · Corticosteroids — Uses & Safety

▸★★★Corticosteroids — MOA, therapeutic uses (endocrine & non-endocrine), ADRs; life-saving uses, role in malignancy/COVID-19/dermatology; note on long-term & topical therapy.

Mechanism of action

  1. Lipophilic steroid enters cell → binds cytoplasmic glucocorticoid receptor (HSP90-bound) → complex translocates to nucleus.
  2. Binds Glucocorticoid Response Elements (GRE) → transactivation of anti-inflammatory genes (annexin-1/lipocortin, IκB) & transrepression of NF-κB/AP-1 → ↓ cytokines, COX-2, phospholipase A2.
  3. Net: anti-inflammatory, immunosuppressive, metabolic (gluconeogenesis), plus rapid non-genomic effects.

Therapeutic uses

Endocrine (replacement)Non-endocrine (pharmacological)
Adrenal insufficiency (Addison's), CAH, acute adrenal crisisInflammatory/allergic disease, autoimmune disease, asthma/COPD, dermatoses, IBD, transplant immunosuppression, malignancy, cerebral oedema, shock

Life-saving uses

  • Anaphylaxis (adjunct), acute severe asthma, adrenal crisis, septic shock (refractory), cerebral oedema, severe COVID-19, status asthmaticus, severe drug reactions (SJS/TEN), autoimmune crises.

Role in malignancy

Lympholytic → apoptosis of lymphoid cells: ALL, lymphoma, multiple myeloma, CLL (CHOP/VAD regimens); antiemetic with chemo; ↓ cerebral oedema (brain tumours/metastases); hypercalcaemia of malignancy; appetite/palliation.

Role in COVID-19

Dexamethasone (RECOVERY trial) reduced mortality in patients needing oxygen/ventilation by dampening the cytokine-mediated inflammatory/hyperimmune response.

Role in dermatology

Topical (eczema, psoriasis, dermatitis) & systemic (pemphigus, severe drug reactions). Topical potency graded; ADR — skin atrophy, striae, tachyphylaxis.

Adverse effects (esp. long-term)

  • Iatrogenic Cushing's, weight gain, moon face, truncal obesity.
  • Hyperglycaemia/diabetes, hypertension, osteoporosis, avascular necrosis, myopathy.
  • Peptic ulcer, immunosuppression/infection (TB reactivation), cataract & glaucoma, growth retardation (children), mood/psychosis, skin thinning.
  • HPA-axis suppression → never stop abruptly (taper to avoid adrenal crisis).

Precautions for long-term & topical therapy

  • Lowest effective dose, shortest duration; alternate-day dosing; morning dosing (mimic circadian); taper gradually.
  • Calcium + vitamin D ± bisphosphonate (bone protection); monitor BP, glucose, weight, eyes; PPI if ulcer risk; screen/treat latent TB.
  • Topical: use appropriate potency for site, avoid prolonged use on face/flexures, beware systemic absorption in children/large areas.

Section 5 · Ecopharmacology & One Health

▸★★★Ecopharmacology — pharmaceuticals in the environment, ecopharmacovigilance & the One Health concept; recent advances & regulatory aspects.

Definitions

  • Ecopharmacology: study of the entry, fate, distribution and effects of pharmaceuticals (and their metabolites) in the environment.
  • Ecopharmacovigilance (EPV): the science & activities concerning detection, assessment, understanding and prevention of adverse effects of pharmaceuticals in the environment (the environmental analogue of pharmacovigilance).

Pharmaceuticals in the environment — sources & examples

  • Sources: patient excretion (urine/faeces), manufacturing effluent, improper disposal of unused/expired drugs, hospital & livestock waste.
  • Examples of harm: diclofenac → catastrophic decline of Indian vultures (renal failure); synthetic estrogens → feminisation/altered reproduction in fish; antibiotics → environmental antimicrobial resistance; antineoplastics, NSAIDs, hormones & psychoactive drugs detected in surface/drinking water.

One Health concept

A collaborative, multisectoral approach recognising that human, animal and environmental health are interconnected. Crucial for AMR — antibiotic use in humans, animals/agriculture & environmental contamination all drive resistance, requiring coordinated action.

Mitigation & regulatory aspects

  • Environmental Risk Assessment (ERA) now part of drug approval (EMA/FDA).
  • "Green pharmacy"/eco-friendly drug design (benign-by-design, biodegradable molecules).
  • Drug take-back/disposal programmes; improved sewage & effluent treatment; manufacturing discharge norms.
  • Surveillance & classification of environmentally hazardous drugs; ban on veterinary diclofenac (India).
Recent advances: One Health AMR action plans (NAP-AMR), antibiotic discharge limits for manufacturers, environmental monitoring of waterways, and incorporation of EPV into pharmacovigilance frameworks.

Section 6 · Poisoning Management & Chelating Agents

▸★★★General principles of poisoning management; role of chelating agents in heavy-metal poisoning (examples); note on paracetamol poisoning, iron overload & atropine poisoning.

General principles of poisoning management

  1. Resuscitation & stabilisation (ABCDE) — airway, breathing, circulation, disability, exposure.
  2. Identification — history, examination, toxidromes, lab/levels.
  3. Decontamination: activated charcoal (within 1 h), gastric lavage (selected early presentations), skin/eye irrigation, whole-bowel irrigation (iron, packets, SR drugs).
  4. Enhanced elimination: urine alkalinisation (salicylate, phenobarbitone), multiple-dose charcoal, haemodialysis (methanol, lithium, salicylate, ethylene glycol).
  5. Specific antidotes.
  6. Supportive & symptomatic care, monitoring, psychiatric referral.

Toxic metabolite NAPQI (via CYP2E1) depletes glutathione → hepatic necrosis. Use the Rumack-Matthew nomogram; antidote N-acetylcysteine (replenishes glutathione) — most effective within 8 h; activated charcoal if early.

Iron overload

Acute iron poisoning or chronic transfusional overload (thalassaemia). Chelation: deferoxamine (IV/IM, "vin rosé" urine), oral deferasirox / deferiprone; supportive care, whole-bowel irrigation in acute ingestion.

Atropine (anticholinergic) poisoning

Toxidrome: "hot, dry, red, blind, mad" (hyperthermia, dry skin, flushing, mydriasis, delirium). Treatment: supportive cooling, benzodiazepines for agitation, and physostigmine (reversible anticholinesterase crossing BBB) for severe central toxicity.

Section 7 · Other Important Topics (Overlaps)

▸★★Current status & issues with COX-2 inhibitors; note on non-endocrine uses of corticosteroids & their therapeutic status in malignancy.

COX-2 inhibitors

  • Rationale: selectively inhibit inducible COX-2 (inflammation), sparing constitutive COX-1 (gastric mucosa, platelets) → fewer GI ulcers/bleeds, no antiplatelet effect. Drugs: celecoxib, etoricoxib, parecoxib.
  • Issues: ↑ cardiovascular thrombotic risk (↓ vascular prostacyclin, unopposed thromboxane) → rofecoxib & valdecoxib withdrawn; celecoxib/etoricoxib available with CV/renal cautions; renal effects & hypertension as with NSAIDs.
  • Uses: osteoarthritis, RA, acute pain, ankylosing spondylitis (esp. high GI-bleed risk); lowest dose, shortest duration.
Recent (PRECISION trial): moderate-dose celecoxib CV safety comparable to naproxen/ibuprofen — partly rehabilitating the class.

Non-endocrine uses of corticosteroids

Inflammatory/allergic disorders, autoimmune disease (SLE, RA, vasculitis), asthma/COPD, dermatoses, IBD, nephrotic syndrome, transplant immunosuppression, cerebral oedema, anaphylaxis (adjunct), septic/refractory shock, COVID-19 (dexamethasone), and malignancy.

Corticosteroids in malignancy

Lympholytic → apoptosis of lymphoid cells: used in ALL, lymphomas, multiple myeloma, CLL (CHOP, VAD regimens); also antiemetic with chemotherapy, ↓ cerebral oedema (brain tumour/metastasis), hypercalcaemia of malignancy, appetite stimulation & palliation, prevent hypersensitivity reactions to chemotherapy.

▸★★Pharmacotherapy with recent advances of: Congestive Heart Failure (ARNI & SGLT-2), Type-2 Diabetes (SGLT-2 & DPP-4), and acid-peptic disease & PPIs (use & misuse).
1. Congestive Heart Failure

Four pillars of HFrEF: ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan — neprilysin inhibition ↑ natriuretic peptides; superior to enalapril, PARADIGM-HF) ≫ ACEi/ARB + β-blocker (carvedilol/bisoprolol/metoprolol) + MRA (spironolactone/eplerenone) + SGLT2 inhibitor (dapagliflozin/empagliflozin — ↓ HF hospitalisation & mortality even without diabetes). Plus loop diuretics (symptoms), ivabradine, vericiguat.

Recent: SGLT2 inhibitors benefit across the EF spectrum (HFpEF — EMPEROR-Preserved, DELIVER); start all four pillars early & together.
2. Type-2 Diabetes Mellitus

Metformin first-line; choose add-ons by comorbidity. SGLT2 inhibitors — ↓ renal glucose reabsorption, weight/BP reduction, cardio-renal protection (used in HF/CKD even without diabetes); ADR genital infection, euglycaemic DKA. DPP-4 inhibitors — ↑ incretins, weight-neutral, low hypoglycaemia, CV-neutral (saxagliptin — ↑HF signal). GLP-1 RAs & tirzepatide give potent glucose + weight benefit.

3. Acid-peptic disease & PPIs (use & misuse)
  • PPIs (omeprazole, pantoprazole, esomeprazole) irreversibly inhibit the gastric H⁺/K⁺-ATPase → potent acid suppression. Uses: peptic ulcer, GERD, H. pylori eradication, NSAID-ulcer prophylaxis, Zollinger-Ellison, stress-ulcer prophylaxis.
  • Misuse: over-prescription & long-term continuation without indication; lack of de-prescribing.
  • Long-term ADRs: ↓ B12/Mg/Ca/iron (fracture risk), C. difficile & pneumonia, hypomagnesaemia, ?CKD/interstitial nephritis, rebound hyperacidity, clopidogrel interaction (omeprazole-CYP2C19).
Recent: P-CABs (vonoprazan, tegoprazan) — K⁺-competitive acid blockers giving faster, stronger, longer, food-independent acid suppression than PPIs; promising for refractory GERD & H. pylori.
▸★DMARDs in rheumatoid arthritis — classification, methotrexate pharmacology, biologic & targeted-synthetic agents, treat-to-target strategy.

Introduction

Disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs (DMARDs), also called slow-acting antirheumatic drugs (SAARDs), suppress the underlying rheumatoid process, induce remission and retard radiographic progression (cartilage damage, bony erosions) — unlike NSAIDs/glucocorticoids, which give only symptomatic relief and do not arrest disease. Onset of benefit is delayed weeks-to-months, and relapse occurs a few months after stopping. Current recommendation is to start a DMARD as soon as RA is diagnosed (early aggressive therapy), because available drugs slow but do not reverse existing joint damage.

Goals of therapy

Ameliorate pain, swelling and stiffness; prevent cartilage damage and bony erosions; preserve joint function and prevent deformity; achieve/maintain remission or low disease activity (treat-to-target).

Methotrexate (MTX) — the anchor drug

  • DMARD of first choice for most patients (incl. juvenile RA); used as monotherapy or as the backbone of combination regimens.
  • Mechanism: dihydrofolate reductase inhibitor → inhibits DNA synthesis/cellular replication; benefit in RA relates to inhibition of cytokine production, chemotaxis and proliferation of immune-inflammatory cells.
  • PK: once-weekly oral/SC/IM; oral bioavailability ~60% and falls at higher doses; ~50% protein-bound; 80–90% excreted renally unchanged — avoid/reduce in renal impairment. SC route gives higher bioavailability, fewer GI effects.
  • Dose: low-dose 7.5–15 (up to 20) mg weekly; onset of relief 3–6 weeks; co-prescribe folic acid 1–5 mg/day to reduce toxicity.
  • ADRs: oral ulceration, GI upset, hepatotoxicity → cirrhosis, interstitial pneumonitis, myelosuppression, increased infection risk.
  • Contraindications: pregnancy/lactation (teratogenic), liver disease/alcoholism, active infection, leucopenia, peptic ulcer, immunodeficiency.
  • Interactions: probenecid & aspirin raise MTX levels/toxicity; trimethoprim adds to antifolate marrow suppression.

Other conventional synthetic DMARDs

  • Leflunomide: inhibits dihydro-orotate dehydrogenase (pyrimidine synthesis); efficacy ≈ MTX, faster onset (~4 wk); long-lived active metabolite (t½ ~2 wk) → cholestyramine washout needed for toxicity/pregnancy. ADRs: diarrhoea, alopecia, ↑BP, hepatotoxicity; contraindicated in pregnancy.
  • Sulfasalazine: splits to sulfapyridine + 5-ASA; second-line/mild disease; neutropenia/thrombocytopenia in 5–10% (monitor CBC); avoid in sulfa/salicylate allergy.
  • Hydroxychloroquine: reduces monocyte IL-1; low toxicity but low efficacy (does not prevent erosions) — used in mild non-erosive disease or combinations; major risk = retinal/corneal toxicity (annual ophthalmologic exam); safe in pregnancy.

Biologic & targeted synthetic DMARDs

  • TNF inhibitors: neutralise TNF-α, the driver of the inflammatory cascade via TNFR1/TNFR2; usually added to MTX (improves response, reduces immunogenicity). Etanercept (TNF-receptor–Fc fusion, 50 mg SC weekly); infliximab (chimeric mAb, IV q4–8wk, infusion reactions, ~15% anti-drug antibodies); adalimumab (human mAb, 40 mg SC q2wk).
  • Abatacept: CTLA4-Ig fusion — binds CD80/CD86, blocking the CD28 costimulatory signal → inhibits T-cell activation.
  • Tocilizumab/sarilumab: anti-IL-6 receptor mAbs. Rituximab: anti-CD20 → depletes B cells; used for inadequate response to a TNF inhibitor.
  • JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib, baricitinib, upadacitinib): oral; inhibit JAK-STAT signalling, blocking multiple pro-inflammatory cytokines.

Treat-to-target strategy & monitoring

ACR/EULAR recommend MTX-anchored csDMARD first-line; escalate to combination csDMARDs, a biologic, or a JAK inhibitor (± MTX) if moderate/high activity persists; reassess ≤3 months; taper (not stop) on sustained remission. Glucocorticoids are used short-term as "bridge" therapy for flares only. Monitoring: MTX/leflunomide/sulfasalazine — CBC, ALT/AST, renal function every 2–4 wk ×3 mo, then 8–12 wk, then 12-weekly. Before any biologic/JAK inhibitor, screen for latent TB (TST/IGRA) and hepatitis B; counsel re: serious/opportunistic infection.

Recent advances: The ORAL Surveillance RCT (tofacitinib vs TNF inhibitor) led to an FDA boxed warning (2021) for major cardiovascular events, malignancy and thrombosis with JAK inhibitors, now restricted to patients with inadequate response/intolerance to ≥1 TNF inhibitor. Biosimilars of infliximab, etanercept and adalimumab are now widely available, improving access to biologic therapy.

▸🩺 CaseA patient has a wound infection growing methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus. Drug of choice and the resistance mechanism?
Clue: Altered penicillin-binding protein.

MRSA resists beta-lactams via PBP2a (mecA gene). Treat with vancomycin (or linezolid, daptomycin, ceftaroline); community MRSA may respond to co-trimoxazole/doxycycline/clindamycin.

▸🩺 CaseA ventilated ICU patient develops pneumonia with Pseudomonas. Antibiotic strategy?
Clue: Intrinsic resistance.

Anti-pseudomonal cover, often a combination (e.g. piperacillin-tazobactam or ceftazidime/cefepime/meropenem plus an aminoglycoside or ciprofloxacin); de-escalate per sensitivity. MDR strains → ceftolozane-tazobactam, ceftazidime-avibactam, colistin, cefiderocol.

▸🩺 CaseA patient has a painful red eye, a fixed mid-dilated pupil and very high intraocular pressure after sitting in a dark cinema. Emergency management?
Clue: Lower the IOP fast.

Acute angle-closure glaucoma. IV acetazolamide + mannitol, topical pilocarpine + timolol (with brimonidine) to lower IOP → definitive laser iridotomy. Avoid mydriatics/anticholinergics.

▸🩺 CaseA patient on prednisolone for several months stops it abruptly and collapses with hypotension. What happened, and the rule?
Clue: HPA-axis suppression.

Adrenal crisis from HPA-axis suppression. Long-term steroids must be tapered, not stopped abruptly; give stress doses during illness/surgery; treat a crisis with IV hydrocortisone and fluids. Add bone and gastric protection for chronic use.

▸🩺 CaseA patient presents 6 hours after a large paracetamol overdose. Antidote and how the need is assessed?
Clue: Glutathione replenishment.

N-acetylcysteine (replenishes glutathione and detoxifies NAPQI) — most effective within 8 hours; plot the level on the Rumack-Matthew nomogram; activated charcoal if the patient presents early.

▸🩺 CaseA child with anaemia, abdominal pain and developmental regression has a high blood-lead level. Treatment?
Clue: Chelation.

Lead poisoning — chelate with DMSA (succimer) or CaNa2-EDTA; add dimercaprol (BAL) for encephalopathy; remove the source of exposure.

▸🩺 CaseWhy was veterinary diclofenac banned in India, and what does this illustrate?
Clue: The vulture collapse.

Diclofenac residues in cattle carcasses caused fatal renal failure in vultures, collapsing their populations — a landmark ecopharmacology / One Health example of a drug harming the environment. (Clinically, diclofenac is also nephrotoxic — avoid in CKD and the elderly.)

▸🩺 CaseBefore starting an anti-TNF biologic for severe psoriasis, which screening is essential, and why?
Clue: Latent infection.

Screen for latent tuberculosis (and hepatitis B/C). TNF-alpha inhibitors impair granuloma maintenance → reactivation of TB. Treat latent TB before or alongside the biologic.

▸🩺 CaseA patient develops profuse diarrhoea after broad-spectrum antibiotics; stool tests positive for Clostridioides difficile toxin. Management?
Clue: Stop and treat.

Antibiotic-associated C. difficile colitis. Stop the offending antibiotic; treat with oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin (metronidazole if mild/unavailable); avoid antimotility agents; recurrent disease → faecal microbiota transplant.
