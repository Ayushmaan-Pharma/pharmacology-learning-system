Drugs Affecting Renal Excretory Function — Diuretics

🎯 High-yield · the nephron as a drug map
High-yield core
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Each diuretic class can be traced back to a single transporter at a single nephron segment — once you know which part of the tubule handles which share of Na⁺ reabsorption, the drug's strength, its electrolyte fallout, and its side-effect pattern all follow logically, with no need to rote-learn separate lists.

Key anchors — loop diuretics

  • Furosemide works from inside the tubule, not from the blood — it has to be pumped into the urine (via OAT) before it can reach NKCC2. That's the reason the amount of furosemide showing up in the urine, rather than the plasma level, tells you whether the drug is actually getting to its site of action.
  • Diuretic resistance in heart failure arises from several overlapping problems: the kidneys are underperfused, less drug gets secreted into the tubule, Na⁺ rebounds back up once the dose wears off ("braking"), and downstream segments enlarge to take over for the blocked NKCC2. This is why adding a second agent further down the nephron (a thiazide or an MR antagonist) makes sense — sequential blockade.
  • Torsemide vs furosemide: torsemide is absorbed orally more reliably and at a higher rate, and it stays in the body longer. conversion ≈ 2:1 (furosemide:torsemide), not 4:1
  • Electrolyte signatures: both loops and thiazides drive K⁺ down and push the blood toward alkalosis; loops also drop Ca²⁺ (lost in the urine), while thiazides do the opposite and raise Ca²⁺ (retained); K⁺-sparing agents, true to their name, push K⁺ up.

  • Why does the same dose of a loop diuretic gradually become less effective with long-term use (what change is the distal tubule undergoing)?
  • Why does one segment cause Ca²⁺ to fall with loop diuretics, while the very next segment causes Ca²⁺ to rise with thiazides?

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism

NKCC2, sitting in the thick ascending limb, is the target loop diuretics shut down. Under normal conditions this transporter pulls Na⁺, K⁺, and 2Cl⁻ out of the tubular fluid, and in doing so it also maintains the countercurrent gradient the kidney relies on to concentrate urine. Once it's blocked, a large amount of salt is left behind in the tubule and washes out in the urine, water following along with it. Losing that medullary gradient also means the kidney can no longer concentrate urine effectively. Since Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ reabsorption depend on the same gradient, their reabsorption falls too — which is why hypocalcemia results.

💡 The Analogy — bouncers on a river of salt

Think of the nephron as a river returning salt to the body, lined with reabsorption "pump stations" along its bank. Of all the stations you can actually reach with a drug, the thick ascending limb runs the biggest one — loop diuretics act as bouncers who shut that particular station down, sending a wave of salt rushing past it and out into the urine.

Diuretic resistance shows up when (a) the bouncers never make it inside the building in the first place — in heart failure, poor renal blood flow means the drug barely gets secreted into the tubule, the place it has to act (which is exactly why checking urinary furosemide, rather than the plasma level, tells you about delivery to the site); or (b) the stations further downstream bulk up to pick up the slack (distal tubule hypertrophy). Sequential nephron blockade simply means stationing a second bouncer — a thiazide — at that downstream spot as well.

⚠️ Warning — the torsemide conversion trap: the furosemide-to-torsemide ratio is approximately 2:1 (so, for example, 40 mg furosemide ≈ 20 mg torsemide) — not 4:1. It's torsemide's more reliable, higher oral bioavailability that makes it the better choice when gut absorption can't be trusted — a point that ties directly back into managing resistance.

Renin & Angiotensin — the RAAS

🎯 High-yield · the master volume hormone axis
High-yield
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Think of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system as the body's built-in thermostat for blood pressure and fluid volume; four separate drug classes each break this circuit at their own step, which makes RAAS the single most frequently targeted pathway anywhere in pharmacology.

The cascade & its blockers

  • The cascade: a drop in BP or Na⁺ triggers the kidney to release renin, which cleaves angiotensinogen into Ang I; ACE then converts this to Ang II, which acts on the AT1 receptor to cause vasoconstriction and stimulate aldosterone release (driving Na⁺/water retention) — the net effect being a rise in BP/volume.
  • ACE inhibitors ("-pril": enalapril, ramipril) — prevent Ang II from being formed; as a side effect they also let bradykinin build up, which is responsible for the dry cough and angioedema.
  • ARBs ("-sartan": losartan, valsartan) — instead block the AT1 receptor directly; since bradykinin is untouched, cough is much less common.
  • MR antagonists (spironolactone, eplerenone) — work by blocking aldosterone (this is the same mechanism covered in Ch 29; they confer a survival benefit in HF).
  • Direct renin inhibitor (aliskiren); ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan) — combines neprilysin inhibition with an ARB, representing a significant advance in HF therapy.
🧠 Section 2 · Active Recall & Blurting Gate
A patient taking an ACE inhibitor starts having a persistent dry cough. Try to blurt out: which molecule other than angiotensin is to blame, why ACE blockade causes its levels to climb, and which single drug-class swap eliminates the cough while preserving the same treatment benefit.

Guiding primers

  • Why can ACE inhibitors and ARBs both raise serum potassium — what downstream hormone falls?
  • Why are these drugs dangerous in bilateral renal artery stenosis (what holds up filtration there)?
  • Why combine an ARB with a neprilysin inhibitor (sacubitril) rather than with an ACE inhibitor?

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism

ACE does two jobs: it builds Ang II and destroys bradykinin. Inhibit it and you lose the vasoconstrictor (good for BP) but let bradykinin accumulate (cough, angioedema). ARBs sidestep this by blocking only the AT1 receptor, so bradykinin is untouched. Less aldosterone means the kidney holds onto K⁺ — hence hyperkalemia risk.

💡 The Analogy — the building's pressure regulator

The RAAS is a smart pressure regulator that senses when the building's water pressure drops and responds by both narrowing the pipes (Ang II) and telling the tank to hold more water (aldosterone). An ACE inhibitor is a worker who shuts off the regulator's factory — but that same worker was also sweeping up an irritant dust (bradykinin), so now dust piles up and everyone coughs. An ARB instead just tapes over the pressure sensor's output socket (AT1) — the factory still runs, no dust piles up, no cough.

⚠️ Warning: Never combine an ACE inhibitor and an ARB routinely — double RAAS blockade raises hyperkalemia and acute kidney injury risk without added benefit. And both are contraindicated in pregnancy (fetal renal damage).

Treatment of Ischemic Heart Disease

Rebalancing myocardial oxygen supply & demand
Tier 1
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Angina is a supply-demand mismatch in the heart muscle, so every anti-anginal drug works by one of two moves: cut the heart's oxygen demand or improve its supply.
  • Nitrates (glyceryl trinitrate, isosorbide) → release NO → venodilation → ↓preload → ↓demand. Fast relief; tolerance with continuous use (need a nitrate-free interval).
  • β-blockers → ↓heart rate & contractility → ↓demand (first-line for chronic stable angina; survival benefit post-MI).
  • Calcium-channel blockers → coronary vasodilation + ↓afterload (dihydropyridines: amlodipine) or ↓rate (verapamil, diltiazem).
  • Antiplatelets & statins → prevent the underlying clot/plaque (disease-modifying).
🧠 Section 2 · Active Recall & Blurting Gate
A patient gets crushing chest pain on exertion. You have three drug classes that relieve angina. Blurt: for each one, state whether it works by cutting demand or boosting supply, and which single physiological variable it changes to do so.

Guiding primers

  • Why does lying down or raising the legs sometimes worsen angina — what does it do to preload that nitrates undo?
  • Why is continuous nitrate dosing self-defeating (what is tolerance doing)?
  • Why pair a dihydropyridine CCB with a β-blocker but avoid verapamil + β-blocker?
✓ I've blurted — reveal the plain-language notes
📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism

Nitrates donate nitric oxide, relaxing veins most of all; less blood returns to the heart (↓preload), so the heart does less work and needs less oxygen. β-blockers slow and soften each beat. CCBs widen coronary arteries and (the non-DHP ones) also slow the heart.

💡 The Analogy — an overworked engine on a hill

The ischemic heart is an engine straining uphill with a clogged fuel line. Nitrates lighten the load in the trailer (less blood to pump = less preload). β-blockers ease off the accelerator (slower, gentler revs). CCBs widen the fuel line (dilate coronaries). You can't unclog the line instantly, so you reduce how hard the engine must work until it stops knocking (the pain stops).

⚠️ Warning: Nitrates are absolutely contraindicated with PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil) — the combined vasodilation causes catastrophic hypotension. And don't combine verapamil/diltiazem with a β-blocker: both slow the AV node → risk of severe bradycardia/heart block.

Treatment of Hypertension

Lowering pressure by attacking its determinants
Tier 1
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Because blood pressure is just cardiac output multiplied by peripheral resistance, every antihypertensive works by pulling down one of those two factors — and the "first-line four" between them hit both levers.
  • The first-line classes: thiazide diuretics (↓volume), ACE-I / ARB (↓RAAS), and calcium-channel blockers (↓resistance) make up the core options. β-blockers have been bumped to second-line for uncomplicated HTN, but they move back to first-line when comorbidities are present (post-MI, HF).
  • Specific patient factors steer the choice: diabetes/CKD → ACE-I/ARB (renoprotective); pregnancy → labetalol, methyldopa, nifedipine; in Black patients, a CCB or thiazide is generally favored as monotherapy.
  • For resistant HTN, spironolactone gets added as the 4th-line agent — bringing aldosterone back into the picture.
  • In a hypertensive emergency, IV agents (labetalol, nicardipine, sodium nitroprusside) are used to bring BP down in a controlled, not abrupt, fashion.

Picture two patients with identical blood pressure readings: one has diabetes plus protein showing up in their urine, the other is an otherwise-healthy 60-year-old of African descent. Without looking anything up, say out loud which first-line agent you'd reach for in each case, and pinpoint exactly why the optimal choice isn't the same for both.

Guiding primers

  • A thiazide's antihypertensive effect persists well past the point where its diuresis has worn off — what's happening to vascular resistance over those intervening weeks that explains this?
  • What makes an ACE-I particularly protective for kidneys damaged by diabetes (hint: think about glomerular pressure)?
  • What's the rationale for pairing agents from separate classes instead of pushing a single drug to its ceiling dose?
✓ I've blurted — reveal the plain-language notes
📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism

There are three basic routes to a lower BP: pull fluid off (diuretics), open up the arteries (CCBs, ACE-I/ARB), or ease the heart's pumping effort (β-blockers). ACE-I/ARBs have a bonus trick — they widen the efferent arteriole leaving the glomerulus, which takes pressure off the kidney's filtering units and is the basis of their renoprotective effect in diabetics.

💡 The Analogy — a garden hose under pressure

Picture the circulatory system as a garden hose with an adjustable nozzle. Water pressure climbs either because you're pushing more water through (cardiac output) or because you've cranked the nozzle tighter (resistance). Diuretics work by letting some water out of the system; CCBs and ACE-I/ARBs act by opening up the nozzle; β-blockers ease off how hard the pump is working. Pairing a "less water" agent with a "wider nozzle" agent, each at a moderate dose, controls the pressure just as well — with fewer side effects — than pushing one drug all the way to its max.

⚠️ Warning: In a hypertensive emergency, resist the urge to crash the BP down quickly — the brain and kidneys have adapted their autoregulation to the elevated pressure, so a sudden drop can trigger stroke or acute kidney injury. The goal is a gradual, partial correction, not normalization on the spot.

Therapy of Heart Failure

🎯 High-yield · breaking the neurohormonal vicious cycle
High-yield core
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
The key to modern heart-failure treatment is breaking the neurohormonal vicious cycle (RAAS plus excess sympathetic drive) — a response the failing heart switches on to compensate, which ends up wrecking it further over time. Agents that interrupt this cycle extend survival, whereas agents that merely ease symptoms do not.

Guiding primers

  • If a potent positive inotrope makes the heart contract more forcefully, why might it actually cut short survival in chronic HF?
  • Why do β-blockers in HF always need to be initiated at a low dose and titrated up slowly, rather than started at full strength?
  • From a clinical standpoint, why does diuretic resistance carry so much weight if diuretics aren't even improving survival to begin with?

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism

When the heart weakens, the body reads it as "low blood pressure" and switches on the same emergency systems it would for bleeding — RAAS and the sympathetic nerves. Those retain salt and whip the heart faster and harder. Helpful for a day; catastrophic for years, because they make the heart remodel and fail further. The four survival pillars each switch off part of this maladaptive response.

💡 The Analogy — whipping a tired horse

The failing heart is a exhausted horse pulling a cart uphill. The body's reflex is to whip it harder (sympathetic drive) and load more cargo it thinks it needs (salt/water retention) — which works for a minute and then breaks the horse. Diuretics unload some cargo so the horse can breathe (it feels relief — but the horse is still being whipped). β-blockers and RAAS blockers stop the whipping — counterintuitively the horse may stumble at first, but it's the only way it survives the journey. That's why symptom relief and survival are different goals.

⚠️ Warning — why diuretic resistance matters: diuretic resistance is clinically dangerous precisely because diuretics are the main tool for congestion. When loops fail, patients flood and get hospitalised — even though no diuretic changes mortality. Newer rescue strategies (add-on dapagliflozin or spironolactone) target exactly this gap: an SGLT2 inhibitor and an MR antagonist each break a different limb of the cycle while aiding decongestion.

Tier 1 · Hard but high-yield

✓ I've blurted — reveal the plain-language notes
📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism

Each heartbeat is an electrical wave: Na⁺ rushes in (phase 0 spike), then a Ca²⁺ plateau, then K⁺ leaves to reset (phase 3). Arrhythmias are this wave misfiring or looping. Each drug class dampens one part of the wave to restore order.

💡 The Analogy — an orchestra losing the beat

The heart's electrical system is an orchestra keeping perfect time. An arrhythmia is musicians rushing or echoing each other into chaos. Class I quiets the loud opening drumbeat (Na⁺ phase 0); Class II tells the conductor to slow the tempo (β-blockade); Class III stretches out the rests between notes (K⁺, longer refractory period); Class IV softens the sustained string section (Ca²⁺ plateau). Amiodarone is the conductor who does all four at once — superb control, but it slowly wears out every section of the orchestra (multi-organ toxicity).

⚠️ Warning: Antiarrhythmics can be proarrhythmic — drugs that prolong the QT (Class Ia, III) can trigger torsades de pointes, especially with hypokalemia. The CAST trial famously showed Class Ic drugs increased mortality post-MI. More suppression is not always safer.

Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension

Three vasodilator pathways for the lung circulation
Tier 2
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Managing PAH comes down to manipulating three vasodilator pathways in the lung arteries: amplify the two pathways that relax vessels (NO and prostacyclin) while shutting down the one that constricts them (endothelin).
  • NO pathway: PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil) + sGC stimulators (riociguat) → ↑cGMP → vasodilation.
  • Prostacyclin pathway: epoprostenol, iloprost → ↑cAMP → vasodilation.
  • Endothelin pathway: endothelin receptor antagonists (bosentan, ambrisentan) → block the constrictor.
🧠 Section 2 · Active Recall & Blurting Gate
There's a mechanistic overlap between PAH drugs and erectile-dysfunction drugs. Before reading on, try to recall which mechanism they share, why that same enzyme is relevant in both the pulmonary vasculature and elsewhere in the body, and why this shared target makes combining these drugs with nitrates so risky.

Guiding primers

  • If your goal is to dilate only the pulmonary vessels, why does using a vasodilator that acts throughout the body threaten to crash systemic blood pressure too?
  • What makes endothelin a sensible drug target — what is its normal effect on blood vessels?
  • What's the reasoning behind checking liver function in patients on bosentan?
✓ I've blurted — reveal the plain-language notes
📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism

In PAH, the pulmonary arteries become excessively narrowed and undergo structural remodeling. All three pathways ultimately act on the same target — smooth-muscle tone: NO and prostacyclin work to relax it (through cGMP and cAMP, respectively), while endothelin works to tighten it. Treatment nudges all three pathways in the direction of dilation.

💡 The Analogy — three thermostats on one room

Think of three thermostats governing how tight or loose the pulmonary artery stays: two are set toward "relax" (NO, prostacyclin), and one is set toward "tighten" (endothelin). In PAH, the room is jammed on too tight. PDE5 inhibitors work by keeping the "relax" signal from fading out (cGMP sticks around longer); prostacyclin analogues crank up the other relax dial; endothelin blockers disable the "tighten" thermostat. Used together, the vessel can finally loosen up.

⚠️ Warning: The same danger that applies to nitrates elsewhere applies here too — PDE5 inhibitors + nitrates = severe hypotension. Separately, endothelin antagonists carry teratogenic and hepatotoxic risk (LFTs must be monitored, and contraception is mandatory).

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism

Heparin dramatically boosts antithrombin activity (the body's own braking protein), so its effect is immediate and is tracked with aPTT. Warfarin interferes with vitamin K recycling, which cuts off the liver's supply of clotting factors II, VII, IX, and X — but the factors already circulating have to clear out first, so onset takes days; it's tracked with INR. DOACs directly block a single factor and need no routine monitoring. Aspirin permanently acetylates COX-1 inside platelets, and since platelets cannot synthesize fresh enzyme, the effect persists for that platelet's entire lifespan. tPA activates plasmin, which breaks down fibrin.

💡 The Analogy — fighting a fire

Picture a clot as a fire that can be fought in three different ways. Antiplatelets clear away the kindling (the platelets that get the process started). Anticoagulants choke off the fuel supply (the fibrin cascade) — heparin closes that valve immediately, while warfarin only gradually halts new fuel production, so existing fuel keeps the fire going for a day or two more. Fibrinolytics act like a hose, dousing a fire that's already burning out of control (a clot that has already formed, as in stroke or STEMI).

⚠️ Warning: There's a brief window early in warfarin therapy where clotting risk actually goes up — proteins C and S (the body's own anticoagulant proteins) decline more quickly than the pro-clotting factors do, so days 1–3 can paradoxically favor clot formation (warfarin skin necrosis). This is exactly why heparin is used as a bridge until the INR reaches a therapeutic level.

Drug Therapy for Dyslipidemias

Lowering LDL to stop atherosclerosis
Tier 1
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Nearly everything in this topic serves a single aim — lowering LDL cholesterol — and statins are the dominant drug class because they achieve this by making the liver pull in more LDL from the blood.
  • Statins ("-statin") — inhibit HMG-CoA reductase (rate-limiting step of cholesterol synthesis) → liver upregulates LDL receptors → pulls LDL from blood. First-line; biggest mortality benefit. ⚠ myopathy/rhabdomyolysis, ↑LFTs
  • Ezetimibe — blocks intestinal cholesterol absorption (NPC1L1); add-on to statin.
  • PCSK9 inhibitors (evolocumab) — prevent LDL-receptor degradation → very potent LDL lowering (injectable).
  • Fibrates (fenofibrate) — PPAR-α agonists → mainly lower triglycerides.
  • Bile-acid sequestrants, niacin, omega-3 — adjuncts.

  • How can PCSK9 inhibitors still be effective in patients whose livers respond poorly to statins?
  • For the shorter-acting statins, what's the rationale for dosing at night (think about when cholesterol synthesis peaks)?
✓ I've blurted — reveal the plain-language notes
📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism

Statins shut down the liver's own cholesterol-producing machinery (HMG-CoA reductase). Because the liver cell now senses a shortfall of internal cholesterol, it responds by displaying more LDL receptors on its surface so it can pull cholesterol in from the bloodstream instead — and this removal of circulating LDL is where the real clinical benefit comes from. PCSK9 is normally responsible for breaking down those same receptors; blocking PCSK9 allows them to remain active for longer.

💡 The Analogy — a factory that starts importing

Picture the liver as a factory that can either manufacture cholesterol or import it. A statin shuts down the factory's own assembly line; now running low on supply, the factory builds extra loading docks (LDL receptors) so it can unload cholesterol from passing delivery trucks (blood LDL) — which clears LDL off the streets. PCSK9 acts like a manager whose job is tearing down those loading docks; a PCSK9 inhibitor essentially removes that manager from the job, so the docks remain open and pull in even more LDL.

⚠️ Warning: The adverse effect to remember is statin-induced myopathy progressing to rhabdomyolysis; the odds go up when fibrates are added (gemfibrozil in particular), with CYP3A4 inhibitors (grapefruit, macrolides — tying back to the metabolism chapter), and at higher doses. Watch for muscle pain and check CK.

🃏 Clinical Vignette Flashcards

Tap a card to reveal the answer, mechanism, clinical pearl, and exam trap. Twenty high-yield scenarios across Cardiovascular & Renal Pharmacology.

01 A 62-year-old man with hypertension develops a dry, persistent cough 3 weeks after starting lisinopril. What is the mechanism and the correct management?
ACE inhibitors block the breakdown of bradykinin (normally degraded by ACE/kininase II). Accumulating bradykinin stimulates pulmonary sensory C-fibres → dry, non-productive cough in ~15–20% of patients (higher in Asian populations). Management: switch to an ARB (e.g., losartan, valsartan) — ARBs block the AT₁ receptor without affecting bradykinin metabolism, so no cough.
💎 Clinical Pearl: ACE inhibitor cough is the #1 reason patients discontinue the drug. Always document it as 'ACE-inhibitor cough' (not 'allergy') — the patient can safely receive an ARB.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Do NOT switch to antihistamines — the cough is bradykinin-mediated, not histaminergic. Do NOT re-challenge with a different ACE inhibitor — the cough will recur with any ACE inhibitor.
02 A patient with HFrEF (EF 28%) on furosemide and digoxin has K⁺ 2.9 mEq/L. He reports nausea, yellow-green halos, and a heart rate of 44 bpm. Diagnose and explain the mechanism.
Digoxin toxicity precipitated by hypokalaemia. K⁺ and digoxin compete for the same binding site on Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase; hypokalaemia enhances digoxin binding and inhibition of the pump → intracellular Na⁺ rises → Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger reversal → ↑ intracellular Ca²⁺ → increased automaticity + AV node block. Classic triad: GI (nausea/vomiting), visual (yellow-green halos/xanthopsia), and cardiac (bradycardia, heart block, VT).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Therapeutic digoxin range in HF is 0.5–0.9 ng/mL (lower than old toxicity threshold). Loop diuretics cause hypokalaemia which dramatically lowers the toxic threshold. Add a K⁺-sparing agent (spironolactone) or supplement K⁺.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Treatment of digoxin toxicity: correct K⁺, give Digibind (digoxin immune Fab) for life-threatening toxicity. However, if the patient is already hyperkalaemic (digoxin blocks Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase → K⁺ exits cells), giving Digibind releases K⁺ back into cells — monitor carefully.
03 A 55-year-old woman with gout is started on hydrochlorothiazide for hypertension. Three weeks later she has an acute gout flare. Explain the mechanism.
Thiazide diuretics competitively inhibit uric acid secretion at the proximal tubule via the OAT1/OAT3 transporters → reduced renal urate excretion → hyperuricaemia → gout flare. Thiazides are relatively contraindicated in gout. Preferred alternatives: losartan (ARB with mild uricosuric effect) or amlodipine (CCB, uric-acid neutral).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Loop diuretics also raise uric acid (less consistently than thiazides). Among antihypertensives, losartan uniquely lowers uric acid (blocks URAT1 reabsorption transporter) — ideal for hypertensive patients with gout or hyperuricaemia.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Low-dose aspirin also inhibits uric acid secretion (same OAT transporters) and can precipitate gout. Do NOT use aspirin to treat gout-associated pain — use colchicine or a full-dose NSAID (noting that NSAIDs raise BP and impair renal function in HTN).
04 A post-MI patient (EF 35%) is started on carvedilol 3.125 mg BID, but her BP drops to 88/58 mmHg and she feels dizzy. Why is carvedilol used, and how do you manage the hypotension?
Carvedilol blocks β1, β2, AND α1 receptors → negative inotropy + chronotropy + vasodilation. Short-term it can worsen haemodynamics; long-term (weeks–months) it reverses adverse cardiac remodelling and reduces mortality in HFrEF. Management: start at the lowest dose (3.125 mg BID), up-titrate slowly every 2 weeks. Do NOT stop — abrupt withdrawal causes rebound adrenergic surge. Ensure patient is euvolaemic (not overloaded or dehydrated) before starting.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Only three beta-blockers have proven mortality benefit in HFrEF: carvedilol, bisoprolol, metoprolol succinate (CR/XL). Atenolol and metoprolol tartrate are NOT proven for HF mortality reduction.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Short-term drop in EF after beta-blocker initiation in HF is EXPECTED and does NOT mean the drug is harmful. The long-term effect is EF improvement ('reverse remodelling'). Never stop a beta-blocker in HF just because the EF appears worse at 4 weeks.
05 A patient on amiodarone for AF is also taking warfarin (INR 2.5). Her INR rises to 5.8 over six weeks without any warfarin dose change. Explain.
Amiodarone and its active metabolite desethylamiodarone are potent inhibitors of CYP2C9 (which metabolises S-warfarin, the more potent enantiomer) and CYP3A4 → increased warfarin plasma levels → supratherapeutic INR. The interaction is delayed because amiodarone has an extremely long half-life (~40–55 days) and takes weeks to reach steady-state inhibitory levels in plasma.
💎 Clinical Pearl: When initiating amiodarone in a warfarin patient, reduce warfarin dose by 30–50% prophylactically and monitor INR weekly for 4–6 weeks. The interaction persists for months after stopping amiodarone due to its very long t½ and massive tissue distribution.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Amiodarone also inhibits CYP3A4 → increases levels of digoxin, statins, and cyclosporine. It inhibits the renal elimination of digoxin — digoxin dose must be halved when amiodarone is started.
06 The CAST trial showed that flecainide and encainide increased mortality in post-MI patients despite suppressing PVCs. What class are these, and why are they harmful?
Class IC agents (Na⁺ channel blockers with slow dissociation kinetics). They reduce conduction velocity markedly in ischaemic/scarred myocardium → create unidirectional block in reentrant circuits → promote sustained VT/VF. The lesson: suppressing an arrhythmia surrogate (PVCs) ≠ mortality benefit. Post-MI arrhythmia prophylaxis: use Class II (beta-blockers) — the only antiarrhythmic class proven to reduce post-MI mortality.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Class IC drugs (flecainide, propafenone) ARE safe for AF termination or rhythm control in patients with structurally normal hearts — they are NOT contraindicated in all patients, only in structural heart disease and post-MI.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Class IA (quinidine) prolongs QT → Torsades. Class IB (lidocaine, mexiletine) — fast dissociation, safe post-MI. Class IC — slow dissociation, proarrhythmic in structural disease. Class III (amiodarone, sotalol) — prolong action potential duration (QT risk). Class IV (verapamil, diltiazem) — AV node block.
07 A patient develops a polymorphic VT with twisting of the QRS complex around the isoelectric line on ECG. Labs show K⁺ 3.0 and Mg²⁺ 0.5 mg/dL. What is the immediate treatment?
Torsades de pointes (TdP) — triggered by QT prolongation + electrolyte derangement. Immediate treatment: IV magnesium sulphate 2 g over 5–10 minutes, regardless of serum Mg²⁺ level. Then correct K⁺ (target >4.5 mEq/L). If refractory: overdrive pacing or isoproterenol infusion (increases heart rate → shortens QT). Identify and remove QT-prolonging drugs.
💎 Clinical Pearl: TdP is pause-dependent — it initiates after a long RR interval (bradycardia or post-ectopic pause extends QT). Acquired (drug-induced) QT prolongation is more common and dangerous than congenital long-QT syndrome.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Do NOT treat TdP with amiodarone, sotalol, or other Class III agents — they further prolong QT. Magnesium is the answer even in normomagnesaemic patients. Lidocaine (Class IB) is relatively safe if a Na-channel blocker is absolutely needed.
08 Nitroglycerin tolerance develops after 24 hours of continuous transdermal patch use. Explain the mechanism and how to prevent it.
Nitrate tolerance (Needleman effect): continuous nitroglycerin exposure depletes mitochondrial aldehyde dehydrogenase 2 (ALDH2), which converts GTN to its active NO-releasing form. Additionally, reactive oxygen species generated by nitrate metabolism inactivate guanylate cyclase. Result: loss of vasodilatory effect with continuous exposure. Prevention: maintain an 8–12 hour nitrate-free interval daily (e.g., remove patch at night). Oral ISDN: twice-daily asymmetric dosing (e.g., 8 AM and 2 PM).
💎 Clinical Pearl: During the nitrate-free interval, angina risk increases — use a beta-blocker or CCB for background protection. Never rely on nitrates alone for angina prevention without a nitrate-free period.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Nitrates + PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil, vardenafil) = absolute contraindication. Both increase cGMP → synergistic, potentially fatal hypotension. Sildenafil t½ ~4 h (hold nitrates 24 h); tadalafil t½ ~17 h (hold nitrates 48 h).
09 IV sodium nitroprusside is infused for 72 hours in a hypertensive emergency. The patient develops confusion and a rising lactate. What toxic metabolite is responsible, and how is it treated?
Cyanide toxicity. Nitroprusside releases nitric oxide (vasodilation) + cyanide ions (CN⁻) which accumulate during prolonged/high-dose infusions. CN⁻ binds cytochrome c oxidase → blocks mitochondrial respiration → tissue hypoxia + lactic acidosis (high anion gap). Treatment: hydroxocobalamin (binds CN⁻ to form cyanocobalamin — excreted renally) or sodium thiosulphate (converts CN⁻ to thiocyanate).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Nitroprusside must be light-protected (foil-wrapped bag). Cyanide risk increases with: doses >2 µg/kg/min, infusion >72 h, hepatic failure (impaired conversion to thiocyanate), and renal failure (thiocyanate accumulation). Limit to ≤3 days.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Thiocyanate (from CN⁻ detoxification) also accumulates in renal failure → thiocyanate toxicity: nausea, tremor, psychosis, seizures. Monitor thiocyanate levels in prolonged infusions or CKD. The antidote for thiocyanate toxicity is dialysis.
10 Sacubitril/valsartan (Entresto) is superior to enalapril in HFrEF. A trainee asks why you can't just add sacubitril to the patient's existing ACE inhibitor. What is the critical safety concern?
Sacubitril inhibits neprilysin, which degrades bradykinin (among other peptides). ACE inhibitors ALSO impair bradykinin degradation. Combining both → profound bradykinin accumulation → life-threatening angioedema. Therefore: never combine sacubitril/valsartan with an ACE inhibitor. A mandatory 36-hour washout after stopping ACE inhibitor before starting Entresto is required.
💎 Clinical Pearl: PARADIGM-HF trial: sacubitril/valsartan reduced CV death + HF hospitalisation by 20% vs enalapril. Indicated for HFrEF (EF ≤40%). Also the first therapy to show benefit in HFpEF (PARAGON-HF — modest effect at EF ~45–57%).
⚠️ Exam Trap: The 36-hour washout is from ACE inhibitor → Entresto (bradykinin danger). No washout is needed when switching from an ARB to Entresto (ARBs don't affect bradykinin metabolism). Entresto IS an ARB-containing product (valsartan).
11 A 70-year-old man with AF on warfarin switches to apixaban. Two months later he needs an urgent colonoscopy. How long should apixaban be held?
Apixaban (direct factor Xa inhibitor) t½ ≈ 12 hours. For low-bleeding-risk procedures: hold ≥24 h before. For high-bleeding-risk procedures (colonoscopy with polypectomy): hold ≥48 hours before. No bridging anticoagulation needed (unlike warfarin). Restart 24–48 h after procedure depending on haemostasis. Reversal if urgent: andexanet alfa (specific reversal agent for factor Xa inhibitors).
💎 Clinical Pearl: DOAC monitoring: no routine INR equivalent. Renal function determines dosing — apixaban dose-reduce if 2 of 3: age ≥80, weight ≤60 kg, creatinine ≥1.5 mg/dL. Dabigatran relies most on renal excretion (~80%) — most affected by CKD, and uniquely dialysable.
⚠️ Exam Trap: DOACs do NOT show reliably on standard coagulation tests (PT/aPTT can be normal despite therapeutic anticoagulation). Anti-Xa levels (for Xa inhibitors) or dilute thrombin time (for dabigatran) are used in emergency situations.
12 Spironolactone is added to an HFrEF patient already on ACE inhibitor and furosemide. Three days later K⁺ is 6.2 mEq/L. Why, and what factors predict this?
Spironolactone (aldosterone receptor antagonist) blocks K⁺ excretion at the collecting duct. Combined with ACE inhibitor (which reduces angiotensin II → reduces aldosterone → reduces K⁺ excretion), the additive K⁺-sparing effect causes hyperkalaemia. Risk factors: CKD (reduced K⁺ excretion), high-dose spironolactone (>25 mg/day in HF), concurrent NSAIDs, trimethoprim, or potassium supplements.
💎 Clinical Pearl: RALES trial proved spironolactone 25 mg/day reduces HF mortality by 30% in NYHA III–IV. Eplerenone (selective aldosterone antagonist) has fewer anti-androgenic side effects (gynaecomastia, breast pain — from spironolactone's cross-reactivity with androgen receptors).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Spironolactone also inhibits testosterone synthesis and blocks androgen receptors → gynaecomastia (10%), erectile dysfunction in men. This is NOT seen with eplerenone (selective for aldosterone receptor). Ask about breast tenderness in male HF patients on spironolactone.
13 Furosemide 80 mg is infused rapidly over 2 minutes to a patient in pulmonary oedema. Twenty minutes later she complains of tinnitus and cannot hear clearly. What happened?
Loop diuretic ototoxicity from rapid IV infusion. Furosemide inhibits Na⁺/K⁺/2Cl⁻ co-transporter (NKCC2) in the stria vascularis of the cochlea → disrupts endolymph ionic gradient → hearing loss and tinnitus. Usually reversible with loop diuretics (vs permanent with aminoglycosides). Risk: rapid infusion, high doses, concurrent aminoglycosides, renal failure.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Safe infusion rate: ≤4 mg/min for furosemide. Ethacrynic acid has the highest ototoxicity risk of all loop diuretics and is the only non-sulfonamide loop diuretic (use in true sulfa allergy, though rare — cross-reactivity with furosemide/bumetanide is very low).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Furosemide and bumetanide are sulfonamide derivatives. In a true sulfa allergy, ethacrynic acid is the alternative. However, cross-reactivity between sulfonamide diuretics and sulphonamide antibiotics is NOT well established — withholding furosemide based on antibiotic sulfa allergy is often unnecessary.
14 ACE inhibitor is started for a patient with undiagnosed bilateral renal artery stenosis. Creatinine jumps from 1.1 to 3.2 mg/dL in 5 days. Explain.
In bilateral RAS (or RAS in a solitary kidney), glomerular filtration is maintained by angiotensin II–mediated efferent arteriolar constriction (maintains intraglomerular pressure despite reduced perfusion). ACE inhibitor blocks this → efferent arteriolar dilation → sharp fall in intraglomerular pressure → acute decline in GFR → AKI. Same risk with ARBs.
💎 Clinical Pearl: A modest creatinine rise (<30%) after starting ACE inhibitor is expected and acceptable in most patients — it reflects reduced intraglomerular hypertension (the renoprotective mechanism). Halt if rise >30–35% or K⁺ rises significantly. Investigate for RAS if rise is severe or bilateral.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Unilateral RAS: the contralateral kidney compensates → ACE inhibitor is safe and actually beneficial (reduces proteinuria). Bilateral RAS: ACE inhibitor can precipitate AKI — this is a contraindication (relative in unilateral, absolute in bilateral/single kidney with RAS).
15 A patient on clopidogrel after coronary stenting is also started on omeprazole for GI protection. The cardiologist requests a change. Why?
Clopidogrel is a prodrug requiring CYP2C19 for activation to its active thiol metabolite (irreversible P2Y12 blocker). Omeprazole and esomeprazole are CYP2C19 inhibitors → reduced clopidogrel activation → reduced antiplatelet effect by up to 45% → potential risk of stent thrombosis. Switch to pantoprazole (weakest CYP2C19 inhibition among PPIs).
💎 Clinical Pearl: CYP2C19 poor metabolisers (10–15% Caucasians, 15–25% Asians) have inherently poor clopidogrel response — consider genotype-guided therapy. Ticagrelor and prasugrel are direct P2Y12 inhibitors that do NOT require metabolic activation — unaffected by CYP2C19 or PPIs.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Prasugrel is contraindicated in patients with prior TIA or stroke (increased intracranial bleeding risk) and age >75 years or weight <60 kg (excess bleeding). Ticagrelor is contraindicated if prior haemorrhagic stroke or severe hepatic impairment.
16 A patient with combined hypertension and stable angina is started on amlodipine. Another patient with the same conditions is given verapamil. Weeks later, the verapamil patient develops ankle swelling AND bradycardia. Explain the differences between these two CCBs.
Both block L-type Ca²⁺ channels, but differ in tissue selectivity: Amlodipine (dihydropyridine) → predominantly vascular smooth muscle → arteriolar vasodilation (↓ afterload) + reflex tachycardia; no significant cardiac conduction effects. Ankle oedema from arteriolar vasodilation (not fluid retention). Verapamil (non-dihydropyridine, phenylalkylamine) → predominantly cardiac (SA node, AV node) → bradycardia, AV block, negative inotropy; less potent vasodilation.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Verapamil and diltiazem are CONTRAINDICATED in HFrEF (negative inotropy worsens systolic function) and with beta-blockers (additive AV block risk → complete heart block). Dihydropyridines (amlodipine, nifedipine) can be used in HFrEF.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Nifedipine (short-acting DHP) causes reflex tachycardia — contraindicated in stable angina without a beta-blocker. Amlodipine (long-acting DHP) has minimal reflex tachycardia due to its very slow onset — safe as monotherapy for angina and HTN.
17 A patient with HFrEF and NYHA Class II symptoms is started on digoxin. Six months later, an echo shows unchanged EF. Has digoxin failed?
No. Digoxin does NOT improve EF or reverse cardiac remodelling in HF. Its benefits: (1) positive inotropy (inhibits Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase → ↑ intracellular Na⁺ → Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger reversal → ↑ Ca²⁺ → increased contractility); (2) reduces neurohormonal activation (increases parasympathetic tone → reduces HR). DIG trial: digoxin reduces HF hospitalisations and symptoms but does NOT reduce mortality. Current indication: symptom control in patients remaining symptomatic on optimal therapy, and rate control in AF with HF.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Digoxin has a very narrow therapeutic index (therapeutic: 0.5–0.9 ng/mL for HF; toxic: >2 ng/mL). Factors increasing toxicity: hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia, hypercalcaemia, hypothyroidism, amiodarone, verapamil (reduce dose by 50% with each).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Digoxin is renally cleared — dose-reduce in CKD. Hypothyroidism reduces digoxin clearance → toxicity. Hyperthyroidism increases clearance → subtherapeutic levels. Always check TSH when digoxin is not working as expected.
18 A patient with dyslipidaemia on simvastatin 40 mg is started on gemfibrozil for elevated triglycerides. Two weeks later he has severe myalgia and CK >10× ULN. What interaction caused this?
Rhabdomyolysis from statin + gemfibrozil combination. Gemfibrozil inhibits UGT1A3 (glucuronidation of statin acid forms) and OATP1B1 (hepatic uptake transporter) → reduced statin clearance → elevated plasma statin levels → myotoxicity. Gemfibrozil is the most dangerous fibrate for statin interactions. Fenofibrate is the preferred fibrate when combination with a statin is necessary.
💎 Clinical Pearl: The statin most prone to rhabdomyolysis: simvastatin > lovastatin > atorvastatin. Pravastatin and rosuvastatin have lower myopathy risk. Risk also increased by: CYP3A4 inhibitors (azoles, macrolides), high statin doses, hypothyroidism, renal failure, Asian ethnicity.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Dark urine in this context = myoglobinuria → risk of acute tubular necrosis. Stop statin immediately; aggressive IV hydration; monitor CK, creatinine, and urine output. Even if creatinine normalises, do NOT restart the same statin + gemfibrozil combination.
19 Adenosine is given as a rapid IV push for SVT. The patient immediately feels flushing, chest tightness, and dyspnoea — then converts to sinus rhythm in 15 seconds. Explain the mechanism and the required administration technique.
Adenosine activates A₁ receptors on AV nodal cells → activates IK,ACh → membrane hyperpolarisation → transient AV block → interrupts AV-nodal reentrant tachycardia (most SVTs). Half-life <10 seconds (inactivated by adenosine deaminase in erythrocytes and vascular endothelium). Technique: rapid IV bolus via a large antecubital vein (6 mg first, then 12 mg if no response), immediately followed by a fast normal saline flush to propel drug to the heart before degradation.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Dipyridamole (blocks adenosine uptake) potentiates its effect — reduce dose. Methylxanthines (caffeine, theophylline) are competitive adenosine antagonists — higher doses may be needed in caffeine users. The transient symptoms (chest tightness, dyspnoea) are universal and self-limiting.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Adenosine is CONTRAINDICATED in WPW with pre-excited AF — preferential AV nodal block may accelerate conduction down the accessory pathway → VF. In WPW, use procainamide (IV) or electrical cardioversion.
20 A patient with bilateral leg oedema is on amlodipine 10 mg for hypertension. The GP adds furosemide. Three months later the oedema persists. What is the most likely reason?
Amlodipine-induced pedal oedema is caused by arteriolar vasodilation without corresponding venodilation → increased hydrostatic pressure in capillaries → fluid shift into interstitium. This is a haemodynamic (non-fluid-retention) mechanism — not due to excess total body fluid. Therefore, diuretics are largely ineffective. Correct approach: reduce amlodipine dose or switch to another antihypertensive class. Adding an ACE inhibitor or ARB can also attenuate CCB-induced oedema (venodilatory effect).
💎 Clinical Pearl: DHP CCB oedema is dose-dependent and position-dependent (worse at end of day, improves with leg elevation). It is NOT a sign of worsening cardiac or renal function — reassure the patient and address the mechanism.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Do not assume new oedema in a cardiac patient = HF decompensation without checking the medication list. Amlodipine-induced oedema is the most common cause of new ankle oedema in hypertensive patients on CCBs.
21 A woman with bilateral renal artery stenosis, T2DM, and chronic HF is being optimised for hypertension. Her medications include lisinopril and metformin. Which of her existing drugs is potentially most dangerous, and why?
Lisinopril (ACE inhibitor) in bilateral RAS: blocks angiotensin II–mediated efferent arteriolar tone → precipitous GFR fall → AKI. Metformin in AKI: if ACE inhibitor triggers AKI, creatinine rises → metformin accumulates (renally cleared) → lactic acid clearance impaired → metformin-associated lactic acidosis (MALA). Both drugs interact dangerously in this vascular anatomy. Image the renal arteries; if bilateral RAS confirmed, stop ACE inhibitor and consider revascularisation.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Suspected bilateral RAS: duplex ultrasound (screening), then CT angiography or MR angiography (definitive). Classic presentation: flash pulmonary oedema (bilateral RAS + ACE inhibitor or diuretic), resistant hypertension, or bilateral small kidneys on imaging.
⚠️ Exam Trap: The ACE inhibitor itself causes the AKI that then makes metformin dangerous — it is an indirect metformin risk, not a direct drug–drug interaction. Hold metformin whenever AKI risk is high (contrast, surgery, dehydration, ACEI initiation in high-risk patients).

1Diuretics 🎯Ch 29 · 20 Q
1Which transporter do loop diuretics block, and where?
Na⁺-K⁺-2Cl⁻ (NKCC2) in the thick ascending limb of the loop of Henle.
2Why are loop diuretics the most powerful ("high-ceiling")?
The thick ascending limb reabsorbs ~25% of filtered Na⁺ — the largest accessible fraction.
3From which side of the tubule does furosemide act, and what's the practical implication?
The luminal (urine) side — it must be secreted into the tubule to reach NKCC2, which is why urinary (not plasma) furosemide reflects delivery to its site.
4Which transporter carries furosemide into the tubular lumen?
Organic anion transporters (OATs) in the proximal tubule.
5Name four mechanisms of diuretic resistance in heart failure.
Reduced renal perfusion/secretion, post-diuretic Na⁺ retention ("braking"), distal tubule hypertrophy, and reduced/erratic oral absorption.
6What is sequential nephron blockade?
Adding a diuretic acting at a different segment (e.g., a thiazide) to overcome distal compensation for loop blockade.
7Furosemide-to-torsemide dose conversion ratio?
Approximately 2:1 (not 4:1).
8Why is torsemide sometimes preferred over furosemide?
Higher and more predictable oral bioavailability and a longer half-life.
9Which transporter do thiazides block, and where?
Na⁺-Cl⁻ cotransporter (NCC) in the distal convoluted tubule.
10Effect of loops vs thiazides on serum calcium?
Loops cause hypocalcemia (↑Ca²⁺ excretion); thiazides cause hypercalcemia (↑Ca²⁺ reabsorption).
11Shared electrolyte/acid-base disturbance of loops and thiazides?
Hypokalemia and metabolic alkalosis.
12Mechanism and example of a potassium-sparing diuretic acting on ENaC?
Amiloride blocks the epithelial Na⁺ channel in the collecting duct.
13Mechanism of spironolactone?
Competitive mineralocorticoid (aldosterone) receptor antagonist in the collecting duct.
14Main risk of potassium-sparing diuretics?
Hyperkalemia (especially with ACE-I/ARB).
15Where do carbonic anhydrase inhibitors act, and a clinical use?
Proximal tubule; used in glaucoma, altitude sickness, metabolic alkalosis.
16Mechanism of mannitol?
Osmotic diuretic — holds water in the tubule by osmosis; used for ↑ICP/cerebral edema.
17Why does chronic loop use lose potency at the same dose ("braking")?
Compensatory distal tubule hypertrophy and post-diuretic sodium retention.
18Why do loops cause metabolic alkalosis?
Increased distal Na⁺ delivery enhances H⁺ and K⁺ secretion; volume contraction concentrates bicarbonate.
19What does a spot urinary sodium at 2 hours assess (your protocol)?
Natriuretic response to the diuretic — a marker of diuretic efficacy/resistance.
20Why might plasma furosemide look adequate while the drug fails?
It is highly protein-bound and acts from the lumen; if tubular secretion is impaired, plasma levels don't reflect delivery to NKCC2.
2RAAS & HypertensionCh 30 & 32 · 20 Q
1Trace the RAAS cascade from renin to aldosterone.
Renin → angiotensinogen to Ang I → ACE converts to Ang II → AT1 receptor → vasoconstriction + aldosterone release.
2Two actions of ACE (and why it matters for side effects)?
It forms Ang II and degrades bradykinin; inhibiting it raises bradykinin → cough/angioedema.
3Suffix and example of ACE inhibitors?
"-pril" — enalapril, ramipril, lisinopril.
4Why do ARBs cause less cough than ACE inhibitors?
They block the AT1 receptor without affecting bradykinin metabolism.
5Why can ACE-I/ARBs raise serum potassium?
Reduced aldosterone → less renal K⁺ excretion.
6Why are ACE-I/ARBs contraindicated in pregnancy?
Fetal renal damage/teratogenicity.
7Why are they risky in bilateral renal artery stenosis?
Ang II maintains glomerular filtration via efferent constriction there; blocking it drops GFR → AKI.
8What is an ARNI, and a key example?
Angiotensin receptor–neprilysin inhibitor — sacubitril/valsartan.
9The first-line antihypertensive classes?
Thiazide diuretics, ACE-I/ARBs, and calcium-channel blockers.
10Equation that frames all antihypertensive action?
BP = cardiac output × systemic vascular resistance.
11Preferred antihypertensive in diabetic kidney disease, and why?
ACE-I/ARB — lowers intraglomerular pressure (efferent dilation) → renoprotection.
12Antihypertensives of choice in pregnancy?
Labetalol, methyldopa, nifedipine.
134th-line add-on for resistant hypertension?
Spironolactone.
14Dihydropyridine vs non-dihydropyridine CCB — key difference?
Dihydropyridines (amlodipine) act on vessels; non-DHPs (verapamil, diltiazem) also slow the heart/AV node.
15Why avoid rapid BP correction in hypertensive emergency?
Auto-regulation has shifted; a sudden drop risks cerebral/renal hypoperfusion (stroke, AKI).
16"CAPTOPRIL" — name three ACE-I adverse effects.
Cough, Angioedema, Potassium ↑, Taste disturbance, hypotension, Pregnancy contraindication, Renal impairment.
17Why combine antihypertensives from different classes?
Greater efficacy with fewer dose-related side effects than maximising one drug.
18Mechanism of aliskiren?
Direct renin inhibitor.
19Why does a thiazide's long-term antihypertensive effect persist beyond its diuresis?
Chronically it reduces peripheral vascular resistance, not just volume.
20Why avoid routine ACE-I + ARB combination?
Double RAAS blockade increases hyperkalemia and AKI risk without added benefit.
3Ischemic Heart Disease & Heart Failure 🎯Ch 31 & 33 · 20 Q
1Angina is fundamentally what kind of imbalance?
A myocardial oxygen supply–demand mismatch.
2How do nitrates relieve angina?
Release NO → venodilation → ↓preload → ↓myocardial oxygen demand.
3Why must nitrates be dosed with a nitrate-free interval?
Continuous exposure causes tolerance.
4Lethal nitrate interaction?
PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil) → severe hypotension.
5How do β-blockers reduce angina?
↓Heart rate and contractility → ↓oxygen demand.
6Why avoid verapamil + β-blocker together?
Both depress the AV node → risk of severe bradycardia/heart block.
7Core principle of modern heart-failure therapy?
Interrupt the neurohormonal vicious cycle (RAAS + sympathetic overactivity).
8Name the four mortality-reducing pillars in HFrEF.
ACE-I/ARB/ARNI, β-blockers, MR antagonists, and SGLT2 inhibitors.
9Do loop diuretics improve survival in heart failure?
No — they relieve congestion/symptoms but don't reduce mortality.
10Which HF drugs are symptom-only?
Loop diuretics and digoxin.
11Why can a strong positive inotrope worsen long-term survival?
It increases myocardial oxygen demand and arrhythmia risk, accelerating decline.
12Why start β-blockers low and slow in HF?
Acute negative inotropy can transiently worsen failure before long-term benefit.
13Three evidence-based β-blockers in HF?
Carvedilol, bisoprolol, metoprolol succinate.
14Mechanism of SGLT2 inhibitors relevant to HF?
Glucosuric/natriuretic with cardiorenal protection (e.g., dapagliflozin).
15Mechanism of digoxin?
Inhibits Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase → ↑intracellular Ca²⁺ → positive inotropy; also vagotonic (↓AV conduction).
16Why is hypokalemia dangerous with digoxin?
Low K⁺ potentiates digoxin binding → increased toxicity.
17Two thesis rescue agents and their classes?
Dapagliflozin (SGLT2 inhibitor) and spironolactone (MR antagonist).
18Why does spironolactone help HF beyond diuresis?
Aldosterone blockade reduces cardiac remodeling/fibrosis → mortality benefit.
19What compensatory systems does the failing heart switch on?
RAAS and the sympathetic nervous system.
20Why is diuretic resistance clinically critical even though diuretics don't cut mortality?
They are the main tool for relieving congestion; resistance → fluid overload and hospitalisation.
4Antiarrhythmic DrugsCh 34 · 20 Q
1Name the Vaughan-Williams classes and their targets.
I = Na⁺ channels, II = β-receptors, III = K⁺ channels, IV = Ca²⁺ channels.
2Which phase of the action potential does Class I affect?
Phase 0 (the Na⁺ upstroke).
3Subclass and example of a Na⁺ blocker used for post-MI ventricular arrhythmias?
Class Ib — lidocaine.
4Why does lidocaine prefer ischemic tissue?
It binds preferentially to depolarised/inactivated Na⁺ channels, common in ischemia.
5Class Ia example and its dual channel effect?
Quinidine — blocks Na⁺ and K⁺ channels (prolongs QT).
6What did the CAST trial show about Class Ic drugs?
They increased mortality post-MI (proarrhythmia).
7Class III mechanism and main risk?
Block K⁺ channels → prolong repolarisation/QT → risk of torsades de pointes.
8Why is amiodarone uniquely effective?
It has properties of all four classes.
9List five organ toxicities of amiodarone.
Thyroid (hypo/hyper), pulmonary fibrosis, hepatotoxicity, corneal/skin deposits, neuropathy.
10Class IV drugs and their nodal effect?
Verapamil, diltiazem — slow AV-node conduction.
11Drug used to terminate SVT by transient AV block?
Adenosine.
12What predisposes to torsades with QT-prolonging drugs?
Hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, bradycardia, congenital long QT.
13Which class flattens the phase-4 pacemaker slope?
Class II (β-blockers).
14Which ion drives the plateau (phase 2)?
Calcium (inward Ca²⁺).
15Treatment of torsades de pointes?
IV magnesium (correct K⁺; stop offending drug).
16Why are antiarrhythmics described as "proarrhythmic"?
By altering conduction/repolarisation they can themselves provoke new arrhythmias.
17Class II benefit beyond rhythm control?
Mortality reduction post-MI and in heart failure.
18Which classes reuse mechanisms from earlier chapters?
II = β-blockade (adrenergic), IV = Ca²⁺ blockade (also antihypertensive/anti-anginal).
19How does hypokalemia from a loop diuretic interact with antiarrhythmic safety?
It heightens torsades risk with QT-prolonging drugs.
20Why monitor thyroid and lung function on long-term amiodarone?
It commonly causes thyroid dysfunction and pulmonary fibrosis.
5PAH & CoagulationCh 35 & 36 · 20 Q
1Name the three drug-targeted pathways in PAH.
Nitric oxide (cGMP), prostacyclin (cAMP), and endothelin.
2How do PDE5 inhibitors help PAH?
They prevent cGMP breakdown → sustained pulmonary vasodilation.
3Mechanism of bosentan?
Endothelin receptor antagonist (blocks a vasoconstrictor).
4Key monitoring on endothelin antagonists?
Liver function (hepatotoxicity); also teratogenic.
5Prostacyclin analogue example?
Epoprostenol (or iloprost).
6The three jobs of clot-directed drugs?
Antiplatelet, anticoagulant, fibrinolytic.
7Mechanism of aspirin as an antiplatelet?
Irreversibly inhibits COX-1 → blocks thromboxane A₂ for the platelet's lifespan.
8Mechanism of clopidogrel?
Blocks the platelet ADP P2Y12 receptor (prodrug activated by CYP2C19).
9How does heparin work, and how fast?
Activates antithrombin to inhibit thrombin (IIa) and Xa; acts within minutes.
10Lab test to monitor unfractionated heparin?
aPTT (activated partial thromboplastin time).
11Reversal agent for heparin?
Protamine sulfate.
12Mechanism of warfarin?
Inhibits vitamin-K epoxide reductase → ↓functional factors II, VII, IX, X.
13Lab test and reversal for warfarin?
INR; reverse with vitamin K (± PCC/FFP).
14Why does warfarin take days to act?
Existing clotting factors must decay; effect depends on reduced new synthesis.
15Why bridge warfarin with heparin initially?
Early fall in proteins C and S causes transient hypercoagulability (warfarin skin necrosis risk).
16Direct thrombin inhibitor (DOAC) example and reversal?
Dabigatran; reversed by idarucizumab.
17Direct factor Xa inhibitors and their reversal?
Rivaroxaban/apixaban; reversed by andexanet alfa.
18Mechanism of alteplase (tPA)?
Activates plasminogen to plasmin, which dissolves fibrin.
19Drug that opposes fibrinolysis (antifibrinolytic)?
Tranexamic acid.
20Why might clopidogrel fail in some patients?
CYP2C19 poor metabolisers cannot activate the prodrug.
6Dyslipidemia & Integrated CardiovascularCh 37 + mixed · 20 Q
1Enzyme inhibited by statins?
HMG-CoA reductase (rate-limiting step of cholesterol synthesis).
2How do statins lower blood LDL indirectly?
Low hepatic cholesterol upregulates LDL receptors, which clear LDL from blood.
3Main adverse effect of statins?
Myopathy/rhabdomyolysis (and ↑LFTs).
4Mechanism of ezetimibe?
Blocks intestinal cholesterol absorption (NPC1L1).
5Mechanism of PCSK9 inhibitors?
Prevent LDL-receptor degradation → more receptors → potent LDL lowering.
6Fibrates' main lipid effect and mechanism?
Lower triglycerides via PPAR-α activation.
7Why does statin + fibrate raise myopathy risk?
Additive muscle toxicity (especially gemfibrozil, which also impairs statin clearance).
8Which transporter mediates hepatic statin uptake?
OATP1B1.
9Why does clarithromycin raise statin toxicity risk?
It inhibits CYP3A4, raising statin levels.
10Which "crossover" drug appears in diuretics, RAAS, HTN and HF chapters?
Spironolactone (and broadly, ACE-I/ARBs and thiazides).
11Why is spironolactone useful across so many cardiovascular conditions?
Aldosterone blockade aids diuresis, lowers BP, and reduces cardiac remodeling.
12How does a loop diuretic's hypokalemia tie into antiarrhythmic safety?
It increases the risk of torsades with QT-prolonging drugs and digoxin toxicity.
13Which second messengers underlie the NO and prostacyclin pathways?
cGMP (NO) and cAMP (prostacyclin).
14How does a PDE inhibitor amplify a GPCR signal without binding the receptor?
It blocks breakdown of the second messenger (cAMP/cGMP), prolonging the signal.
15Why give short-acting statins at night?
Cholesterol synthesis peaks overnight.
16Which β1 action links β-blocker use in angina, HTN and HF?
Reduced heart rate/contractility (and renin); the same receptor block serves all three.
17Why is the ACE-I/ARB renoprotective effect a double-edged sword?
Efferent dilation lowers harmful glomerular pressure but can drop GFR in low-flow states (stenosis).
18How does the same pharmacogenetic concept cause opposite problems for clopidogrel and warfarin?
CYP2C19 PM → too little active clopidogrel; CYP2C9/VKORC1 variants → too much warfarin effect.
19Which classes of antihypertensive overlap with anti-anginal therapy?
β-blockers and calcium-channel blockers.

Most cardiovascular drugs either offload volume, block the RAAS/sympathetic cycle, or relax/clear vessels — a handful of levers reused across every chapter.
