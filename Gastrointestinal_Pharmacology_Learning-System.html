🍽️GI Pharmacology
Chapters 53–55
  • ACh (from the vagus nerve), histamine (from nearby ECL cells), and gastrin (from G cells) all push the parietal cell to make acid.
  • Gastrin is the single strongest trigger, but mostly works indirectly — by telling ECL cells to dump histamine, not by acting on the acid cell itself.
  • D cells release somatostatin once the stomach gets acidic enough, which shuts gastrin down — this brake fails in H. pylori infection because the bacteria kill off the D cells.
  • Both signaling paths (cAMP from histamine, calcium from ACh/gastrin) end up at the same final pump, which is why blocking that one pump works no matter which signal is driving things.
  • PPIs are prodrugs that need acid to activate, then permanently glue themselves to the acid pump — that's why effect outlasts the drug's short time in the blood (cell has to build fresh pumps).
  • Take PPIs 30-60 minutes before breakfast, since fasting means more pumps are sitting there ready to be hit.
  • PPI downsides: low B12/magnesium, higher fracture and infection risk, and omeprazole specifically can blunt clopidogrel.
  • H2 blockers (like cimetidine) work reversibly and lose effectiveness within about 3 days; cimetidine alone also blocks CYP enzymes and can cause breast enlargement/impotence.
  • P-CABs (vonoprazan) block the pump reversibly but don't need acid to activate, so they work fast, aren't tied to meal timing, and skip the CYP2C19 pathway.
  • Misoprostol mimics a natural protective hormone but also triggers uterine contractions — never give it in pregnancy.
  • Sucralfate physically coats the ulcer once stomach pH is low enough; take on an empty stomach, can cause constipation, avoid in kidney failure.
  • Antacids work fast but trade off diarrhea (magnesium) against constipation (aluminum), and can block absorption of other drugs.
  • Bismuth coats the ulcer and helps fight H. pylori, but turns stool and tongue black (harmless).
  • PPIs heal reflux esophagitis and stomach/duodenal ulcers better than H2 blockers.
  • NSAIDs cause ulcers by cutting the prostaglandins that normally protect the stomach lining.
  • Zollinger-Ellison syndrome (a gastrin-producing tumor) needs high-dose PPIs, with octreotide added if that's not enough.
  • Metoclopramide speeds up the upper gut and also blocks nausea, but it enters the brain and can cause dystonia or permanent tardive dyskinesia — keep use under 12 weeks.
  • Domperidone does the same job at the gut but mostly stays out of the brain, so movement side effects are rare — its risk instead is a dangerous heart rhythm at higher doses or in older patients.
  • Prucalopride is a more selective, longer-acting drug for chronic constipation; cisapride (an older one) was pulled from the market for causing dangerous heart rhythms.
  • Erythromycin can kick-start a sluggish stomach by mimicking a gut hormone, but it stops working after about a month as the body adapts.
  • Laxatives range from gentle fiber and stool softeners, to osmotic agents (polyethylene glycol, lactulose), to stronger stimulant laxatives (senna, bisacodyl) that irritate the gut into moving.
  • Newer constipation drugs (lubiprostone, linaclotide) work by pulling water into the gut and barely get absorbed into the body.
  • Drugs for opioid-caused constipation are built to stay out of the brain so they don't reverse the opioid's pain relief.
  • Loperamide treats diarrhea by acting like an opioid only in the gut — but high doses carry a serious heart-rhythm warning, so stay under 16 mg per day.
  • Never give anti-diarrheal or antibiotic treatment when a dangerous bacterial infection (like the one causing bloody diarrhea/HUS, or C. difficile) is suspected — slowing the gut down can make things worse.
  • Alosetron treats diarrhea-predominant IBS in women but can cause a dangerous loss of blood flow to the bowel, so it requires special monitoring.
  • Eluxadoline works on opioid-type receptors in the gut but should be avoided, or dosed lower, in patients without a gallbladder.
  • Constipation-predominant IBS uses the same gut-water drugs as regular constipation (lubiprostone, linaclotide).
  • 5HT3 blockers (ondansetron) are best for chemo- and surgery-related nausea but don't help much with ordinary nausea or motion sickness.
  • NK1 blockers (aprepitant) specifically target the delayed phase of chemo-induced vomiting and are always combined with a 5HT3 blocker plus a steroid.
  • Dopamine blockers (metoclopramide, droperidol, haloperidol) cover a range of nausea causes but bring movement-disorder risk.
  • Antihistamines and scopolamine are the go-to choice specifically for motion sickness and inner-ear-related nausea.
  • Pregnancy-related nausea is treated with doxylamine plus vitamin B6 (pyridoxine).
  • Pancreatic enzyme replacement (pancrelipase) helps digestion when the pancreas can't make enough enzymes itself.
  • Ursodiol dissolves certain gallstones and treats a chronic bile-duct disease (primary biliary cholangitis).
  • Simethicone treats gas/bloating; rifaximin (not absorbed into the body) treats small-intestine bacterial overgrowth.
  • Crohn's disease affects the full thickness of the gut wall, can hit any part of the GI tract, and is driven by IL-12/23, IFN-gamma and TNF-alpha.
  • Ulcerative colitis stays in the surface lining, starts at the rectum and spreads continuously, and is driven by a different set of immune chemicals (IL-13).
  • Ulcerative colitis can be surgically cured by removing the colon; Crohn's disease tends to come back even after surgery.
  • Mesalamine (5-ASA) is the first choice for mild-to-moderate ulcerative colitis and works directly on the gut lining.
  • Sulfasalazine releases its active drug only once colon bacteria break a special bond — its side effects mostly come from the other half of the molecule, and it can lower folate and sperm count.
  • Steroids (prednisone, budesonide) work well to calm a flare-up but should never be used long-term — they always need to be tapered off slowly.
  • Budesonide is processed heavily by the liver before reaching the rest of the body, so it acts mainly on the gut with fewer whole-body steroid side effects.
  • Azathioprine needs a TPMT enzyme test before starting, since low TPMT activity can cause dangerous bone marrow suppression.
  • Combining azathioprine with allopurinol requires cutting the azathioprine dose to about a quarter of normal.
  • Methotrexate is strongly teratogenic (causes birth defects) and absolutely cannot be used in pregnancy.
  • Cyclosporine and tacrolimus are reserved for severe disease that hasn't responded to steroids, and both can harm the kidneys.
  • Anti-TNF drugs (infliximab, adalimumab) block a key inflammation chemical, but patients must be tested for hidden TB infection first.
  • Certolizumab is the anti-TNF drug considered safest in pregnancy because it doesn't cross into the placenta.
  • Natalizumab blocks immune cells from entering tissue but carries a risk of a serious brain infection (PML), so its use is tightly restricted.
  • Vedolizumab is more gut-selective than natalizumab, lowering that brain-infection risk.
  • Tofacitinib (a JAK inhibitor pill) and ozanimod are newer non-biologic options, but both are unsafe in pregnancy.

Pharmacotherapy for Gastric Acidity, Peptic Ulcers & GERD Tier 1

PPIs · H₂RAs · P-CABs · mucosal defence · H. pylori eradication · special acid-peptic disorders
🧭 Stage 1 — 80/20 High-Yield Map
Almost every drug used for acid-peptic disease tips a single scale — acid attack against mucosal protection — and the single strongest tool for doing so is permanently and covalently disabling the parietal cell's H⁺,K⁺-ATPase (the proton pump), since every secretory pathway converges on this one final step.
  • Three signals push the parietal cell to secrete: ACh → M3 (from the vagus/enteric nerves, during the cephalic phase), histamine → H2 (released locally by ECL cells — this is the biggest contributor, working through Gs–cAMP), and gastrin → CCK2 (from antral G cells — the single strongest trigger, though it works mostly indirectly by stimulating ECL cells to release histamine).
  • The counterbalance: antral D cells release somatostatin; once luminal pH drops below 3, SST is triggered and shuts gastrin down (a negative-feedback loop). H. pylori wipes out D cells, so SST falls and gastrin runs unchecked.
  • Where the pathways converge: cAMP (downstream of H2) and Ca²⁺ (downstream of M3/CCK2) both switch on the H⁺,K⁺-ATPase, which trades H⁺ for K⁺ across the largest ion gradient found anywhere in vertebrate physiology (roughly pH 7.3 inside the cell versus pH 0.8 in the canaliculus).
  • PPIs only work once activated: they're prodrugs that need acid to convert into a tetracyclic sulfenamide, which then attaches permanently and covalently to cysteine residues on the pump. This knocks out 80–95% of acid output. Even though plasma half-life is just 0.5–3 hours, the suppression persists 24–48 hours — until the cell builds fresh pumps. That's why dosing 30–60 minutes before breakfast matters: fasting means more pumps are available to hit. All six PPIs work about equally well.
  • PPI drug interactions and downsides: metabolised through CYP2C19 and 3A4, and people of Asian/Pacific Islander descent are more likely to be poor metabolisers. Omeprazole alone among the PPIs blocks CYP2C19 (potentially blunting clopidogrel activation, though this is debated; pantoprazole is considered the safer partner drug). Chronic use can drop B12 and Mg²⁺ (magnesium loss is worse alongside diuretics), raise fracture risk, raise infection risk (C. difficile, pneumonia, SBP), and push gastrin levels up — which can drive ECL hyperplasia and rebound acid hypersecretion.
  • H₂ antagonists: block H2 reversibly and competitively, cutting acid output by roughly 70% (strongest effect on nocturnal/basal secretion). Cleared by the kidneys, so doses need lowering if renal function is impaired. Cimetidine stands out by also inhibiting CYP enzymes and acting as an antiandrogen (causing gynaecomastia and impotence). Effectiveness wanes within about three days as gastrin rises (tachyphylaxis). Ranitidine and nizatidine have mostly been pulled from the market (NDMA contamination).
  • Potassium-competitive acid blockers (P-CABs): vonoprazan, tegoprazan, and revaprazan block the pump's K⁺-binding site reversibly. As weak bases, they accumulate in the canaliculus and can bind the pump whether it's active or not, giving them quicker onset (full effect by day one), a longer-lasting effect, and better control of nocturnal acid. They don't need acid to become active, so dosing isn't tied to meals, and they bypass CYP2C19 entirely. Use is concentrated in Asia.
  • Drugs that bolster mucosal defence: Misoprostol is a PGE₁ analogue that signals through EP3/Gi to lower cAMP (reducing acid) while also protecting the mucosa; it prevents NSAID-induced ulcers but causes diarrhoea, and is strictly forbidden in pregnancy because it can trigger uterine contractions and abortion. Sucralfate (a combination of sucrose octasulfate and aluminum hydroxide) polymerizes when pH drops below 4 and physically coats the ulcer; it must be taken on an empty stomach, causes constipation, and should be avoided in renal failure since aluminum can build up. Antacids trade off magnesium-driven diarrhea against aluminum-driven constipation and can bind other drugs, so dosing should be separated by two hours.
  • GERD and peptic ulcer disease: PPIs outperform H₂RAs at healing erosive esophagitis and gastric/duodenal ulcers. NSAIDs trigger ulcers by cutting mucosal PGE₂/PGI₂ production. Barrett's esophagus tends to resist treatment. Zollinger-Ellison syndrome (a gastrin-secreting tumor) calls for high-dose PPI therapy, adding octreotide if needed.

📝 Stage 3 — Plain-Language Notes

Think of acid output as water controlled by three taps draining into one pipe

Imagine the parietal cell as a basin with three separate taps feeding it. There's a nerve-controlled tap (ACh acting on M3, switched on by the sight or smell of food during the cephalic phase), a locally-controlled tap (histamine acting on H2, seeping over from neighboring ECL cells — this is the heavyweight contributor), and a hormone-controlled tap (gastrin acting on CCK2, called out from the antrum). Gastrin shouts the loudest of the three, yet most of what it accomplishes is by prompting the ECL cell to dump histamine rather than opening the parietal cell's tap on its own — which is exactly why it's described as both "the strongest trigger" and "indirect." Nearby, a D cell behaves like a float switch: once the basin grows acidic enough (pH under 3), it releases somatostatin, which quietly closes off the gastrin tap.

Regardless of which tap is running, the water all flows into a single drain — the H⁺,K⁺-ATPase. The histamine pathway flows through cAMP; the ACh and gastrin pathways flow through Ca²⁺; but both streams converge on the same pump. This is why PPIs are so elegant — rather than wrestling with three separate taps, you simply plug the drain. A PPI behaves like a stealth weapon: harmless while circulating in the blood, it only activates in the one place acidic enough to trigger it — the parietal cell's secretory canaliculus. Once triggered, it forms a bond that can't be undone, so the pump stays disabled long after the drug itself has cleared from circulation, until the cell builds replacement pumps (a process taking a day or two). Dosing before breakfast works because an overnight fast replenishes the supply of pumps, meaning more freshly built, fully functional targets are present when the drug shows up.

Picture squirting fast-setting adhesive into a warehouse's single loading bay right as the morning crew arrives. The adhesive itself is used up within minutes (mirroring the drug's short half-life), yet the bay stays sealed for roughly two days until a replacement door gets installed (mirroring new pump synthesis). An H₂ blocker, on the other hand, is more like a guard simply standing in one of three doorways — he can be pushed aside (reversible binding), and before long deliveries get rerouted around him entirely (tolerance).

  • Gastrin's effect comes chiefly through ECL-derived histamine, not from acting on the parietal cell directly — resist labeling it a direct stimulant.
  • PPIs attach covalently and irreversibly. It would be incorrect to describe them as "competitive" — that description fits H₂ blockers and P-CABs instead.
  • Pregnancy is an absolute contraindication for misoprostol (it stimulates the uterus and can cause abortion) — a frequently tested single-best-answer scenario.
  • Cimetidine uniquely combines CYP inhibition with antiandrogenic effects (gynecomastia, impotence) — don't generalize these to every H₂ blocker.
  • Ranitidine and nizatidine were pulled from shelves (NDMA contamination) — treat any exam option naming ranitidine as "first-line" with suspicion.
  • Among the PPIs, omeprazole is the only one that inhibits CYP2C19 (raising clopidogrel concerns); pantoprazole is regarded as the safest to pair with it.

GI Motility, Water Flux, Emesis & Biliary/Pancreatic Disease Tier 1

ENS · prokinetics · laxatives · antidiarrhoeals · IBS · antiemetics · ursodiol · pancreatic enzymes
🧭 Stage 1 — 80/20 High-Yield Map
The enteric nervous system functions as the gut's own independent brain — peristalsis is driven by excitatory ACh and inhibitory NO motor neurons, while serotonin (5HT) released from enterochromaffin cells sets off both the gut's reflexes and the brain's vomiting machinery; essentially every drug for motility, constipation, diarrhea, or nausea works by nudging one specific neurotransmitter–receptor pairing.

Prokinetics

  • Metoclopramide works as a D2 antagonist plus a 5HT4 agonist plus a 5HT3 antagonist, raising LES tone and boosting contractions in the upper gut (it does not affect the large bowel); it's first-line for gastroparesis and doubles as an antiemetic via D2 blockade at the CTZ. Because it crosses the BBB, it brings extrapyramidal risk — acute dystonia early on, and potentially permanent tardive dyskinesia with prolonged use — plus elevated prolactin and QT prolongation, so treatment should stay under 12 weeks.
  • Domperidone blocks D2 only in the periphery (it barely enters the brain), so extrapyramidal symptoms are rare, though prolactin elevation and antiemetic effects still occur since the CTZ sits outside the BBB; it carries a risk of ventricular arrhythmia and sudden death, particularly past age 60 or above 30 mg/day.
  • 5HT4 agonists include cisapride (pulled from the market after causing torsades via hERG blockade), prucalopride (a selective agent for chronic constipation with a 24-hour half-life), and tegaserod (a partial agonist for constipation-predominant IBS in women under 65).
  • Erythromycin activates motilin receptors, kicking off phase III of the migrating motor complex; it's used short-term for diabetic gastroparesis, though its benefit fades after roughly 28 days as motilin receptors downregulate (tachyphylaxis).

Laxatives and antidiarrheals (opposite ends of the same spectrum)

  • Bulk-forming agents: psyllium and methylcellulose, which can cause bloating. Osmotic agents: PEG (for bowel cleansing or chronic use), magnesium/phosphate saline solutions (which cause diarrhea and should be avoided in renal failure or with diuretics), and lactulose/sorbitol (which doubles as hepatic encephalopathy treatment by trapping ammonia as ammonium). Stool softeners: docusate (modest benefit) and mineral oil (which lowers fat-soluble vitamin absorption).
  • Stimulant laxatives: bisacodyl and senna/anthraquinones (which can cause melanosis coli), plus castor oil (active component ricinoleic acid). Prosecretory agents: lubiprostone (acting on ClC-2), linaclotide and plecanatide (GC-C agonists), and tenapanor (an NHE3 inhibitor).
  • For opioid-induced constipation: peripherally restricted MOR antagonists — methylnaltrexone, naloxegol, and naldemedine — stay out of the CNS because they're P-glycoprotein substrates. Alvimopan is reserved strictly for postoperative ileus, with restricted access due to MI risk.
  • Antidiarrheal agents: loperamide (a peripherally restricted MOR agonist with minimal CNS entry, carrying a black-box cardiac warning at high doses — cap at 16 mg/day), diphenoxylate/difenoxin (combined with atropine, schedule V), bismuth subsalicylate (turns stool and tongue black; its salicylate component carries Reye's syndrome risk), octreotide (for secretory or carcinoid-related diarrhea and variceal bleeding), clonidine (an α2 agonist for diabetic diarrhea), racecadotril (which inhibits enkephalinase), cholestyramine (for bile-salt diarrhea after ileal resection), crofelemer (a CFTR/CaCC inhibitor for HIV-related diarrhea), and rifaximin (which isn't absorbed systemically).
  • Empiric antibiotic choices: a fluoroquinolone for traveler's diarrhea, with azithromycin generally preferred in children. Antibiotics and antimotility agents should both be withheld when EHEC (risking HUS) or C. difficile is suspected.

IBS

  • Options include TCAs for visceral pain, alosetron (a 5HT3 antagonist for diarrhea-predominant IBS in women, carrying ischemic colitis risk and requiring a REMS program), eluxadoline (acting as a MOR agonist, DOR antagonist, and KOR agonist, with pancreatitis and sphincter-of-Oddi risk — the lower 75 mg dose is used if the patient lacks a gallbladder), antispasmodics such as dicyclomine and hyoscyamine, lubiprostone or linaclotide for constipation-predominant IBS, and rifaximin for the diarrhea-predominant form.

Antiemetics — choose the receptor that matches the trigger

  • 5HT3 antagonists (ondansetron, granisetron, palonosetron) work best for chemotherapy-induced nausea/vomiting and PONV, though they carry QT risk (especially the older agents; IV dolasetron is contraindicated for prophylaxis); palonosetron stands apart with its long half-life, benefit against delayed emesis, and lack of QT effect. None of these are particularly effective against ordinary nausea or motion sickness.
  • NK1 antagonists (aprepitant/fosaprepitant, rolapitant, netupitant) are the go-to choice for delayed-phase chemotherapy-induced emesis, always paired with a 5HT3 antagonist and dexamethasone; they're metabolized through CYP3A4.
  • Dopamine antagonists span several classes: phenothiazines (prochlorperazine), butyrophenones (droperidol for PONV, haloperidol for cannabinoid hyperemesis), benzamides (metoclopramide, amisulpride), and olanzapine (useful for delayed CINV).
  • H1 antihistamines and the anticholinergic scopolamine are the choice for motion sickness and vestibular causes of nausea. Cannabinoids (dronabinol, nabilone) are reserved for refractory CINV, while dexamethasone and benzodiazepines serve as adjuncts (the latter for anticipatory nausea). For nausea and vomiting of pregnancy, doxylamine combined with pyridoxine is standard.

Biliary, pancreatic, and other agents

  • Pancreatic enzyme replacement (pancrelipase) is dosed according to its lipase content, starting around 500 USP units/kg per meal and titrating up to 2500. Ursodiol treats gallstones and primary biliary cholangitis. Obeticholic acid, an FXR agonist used for PBC, carries a black-box warning for hepatic decompensation. Simethicone addresses flatulence, teduglutide (a GLP-2 analogue) treats short-bowel syndrome, and rifaximin is used for SIBO.

📝 Stage 3 — Plain-Language Notes

The brain's vomiting control room, and the gut's built-in regulator

Picture the medulla's emetic center as a control room monitoring five separate incoming feeds: the gut (relaying through the vagus, activated by 5HT3 when chemotherapy floods enterochromaffin cells with serotonin), the bloodstream, the inner ear, the cortex above (responsible for anticipation), and a dedicated outpost called the CTZ. The CTZ occupies the area postrema, a region the brain intentionally leaves unshielded by the blood–brain barrier so it can continuously screen blood and spinal fluid for harmful substances. This isn't an oversight — it's how the body senses ingested poisons and triggers their expulsion. It also explains why drugs targeting CTZ receptors (5HT3, D2) can act there despite being technically "outside" the brain's protective barrier. From there, drug selection becomes a matter of identifying which feed is active and blocking it accordingly. Chemotherapy activates both the gut and CTZ feeds (countered with 5HT3 blockade, NK1 blockade for the slower delayed wave, and dexamethasone); motion sickness activates the vestibular feed (countered with antihistamines or antimuscarinics); pregnancy-related nausea responds to doxylamine paired with pyridoxine.

On the motility side, the gut largely manages itself: excitatory ACh neurons contract it, inhibitory NO neurons relax it, and serotonin sets the whole reflex in motion. Prokinetic drugs gently coordinate these existing signals, unlike older cholinomimetics that simply force uncoordinated contractions throughout the gut. Erythromycin gets around this by disguising itself as motilin at its receptor — highly effective at first, but the receptor eventually stops responding to the constant stimulation and downregulates, so benefit fades after about a month (tachyphylaxis). Loperamide, meanwhile, acts only in the gut: it activates peripheral μ-receptors on enteric nerves to slow transit, but gets pumped back out of the brain before it can cause euphoria, giving the constipating effect without the high.

Think of the CTZ as an exposed weather sensor mounted outside on the roof rather than inside the climate-controlled building — deliberately left unsheltered so it can detect the rain (blood-borne toxins) that the sealed interior never would. Erythromycin is like a visitor who keeps repeating the motilin password to the doorman; it works until the doorman simply tunes him out (receptor downregulation).

  • Tardive dyskinesia from metoclopramide can be permanent; keep treatment under 12 weeks. Domperidone and ondansetron both carry QT risk.
  • 5HT3 antagonists provide little benefit against delayed emesis or motion sickness — pair with NK1 blockade and dexamethasone for delayed CINV.
  • Loperamide carries a black-box cardiac warning at doses above therapeutic range (torsades risk). Stay under 16 mg/day.
  • Antimotility drugs should be withheld in invasive/bloody diarrhea, EHEC infection (HUS risk), and C. difficile — they keep the pathogen in place and raise the risk of toxic megacolon.
  • Eluxadoline carries pancreatitis and sphincter-of-Oddi spasm risk, particularly in patients lacking a gallbladder (the lower 75 mg dose should be used; some such patients shouldn't receive it at all).
  • Don't overlook that lactulose has a second major use — hepatic encephalopathy (via ammonia trapping) — beyond its role as a laxative.

Pharmacotherapy of Inflammatory Bowel Disease Tier 2

5-ASA · glucocorticoids · thiopurines/MTX · anti-TNF & other biologics · JAK · S1P · microbiome
🧭 Stage 1 — 80/20 High-Yield Map
IBD arises when the mucosal immune system mounts an inappropriate response against the gut's normal bacterial residents in someone genetically predisposed to it — Crohn's disease involves transmural TH1/TH17 activity (IL-12/23, IFN-γ, TNF-α), while ulcerative colitis involves mucosal-only TH2-like activity (IL-13) — and treatment escalates step by step, starting with topical 5-ASA and rising to targeted biologics and small molecules that each disrupt one specific point in that immune cascade.
  • 5-ASA (mesalamine): the first-choice agent for mild-to-moderate UC, working locally on the mucosa. Sulfasalazine links 5-ASA to sulfapyridine through an azo bond that colonic bacteria break apart (which targets drug delivery to the colon); the sulfapyridine portion is responsible for most adverse effects, lowers folate (requiring supplementation), and causes reversible reductions in sperm count. Olsalazine pairs two 5-ASA molecules together (causing diarrhea), while balsalazide uses an inert carrier molecule; delayed-release and pH-coated formulations exist for delivery to distal segments. This class has largely fallen out of use for maintaining Crohn's remission.
  • Glucocorticoids: prednisone, methylprednisolone, hydrocortisone, and budesonide all induce remission in moderate-to-severe disease, but none should be used for maintenance due to toxicity; tapering is always required to prevent HPA-axis suppression and relapse. Budesonide undergoes extensive first-pass metabolism, keeping systemic exposure low while concentrating its effect on the ileum and colon; its multimatrix formulation releases the drug once pH exceeds 7 (in the colon). Patients fall into categories based on response: steroid-responsive, steroid-dependent, or steroid-resistant.
  • Thiopurines: azathioprine converts to 6-MP, which is then converted to 6-thioguanine nucleotides. TPMT status must be checked beforehand, since low TPMT activity diverts metabolism toward 6-TGN and causes marrow suppression. Allopurinol inhibits xanthine oxidase, raising active metabolite levels, so the thiopurine dose must be cut to 25%. Roughly 5% of patients develop pancreatitis, lymphoma risk rises fourfold, onset is slow (weeks to months), and the drugs allow steroid tapering.
  • Methotrexate: inhibits DHFR and is used for maintaining Crohn's remission, given subcutaneously alongside folate supplementation; it is strongly teratogenic and contraindicated in pregnancy, and NSAIDs, PPIs, and penicillins reduce its renal clearance, raising toxicity risk. Cyclosporine and tacrolimus, both calcineurin inhibitors, are reserved for severe steroid-resistant disease and bridge therapy, and both carry nephrotoxic risk.
  • Anti-TNF biologics: infliximab (a chimeric antibody given IV, prone to antidrug antibody formation that's mitigated by combining with azathioprine; contraindicated in severe CHF), adalimumab and golimumab (humanized antibodies given subcutaneously), and certolizumab pegol (a pegylated Fab fragment — the safest option in pregnancy since it doesn't cross the placenta). Latent TB must be ruled out before starting any of these.
  • Other biologics: vedolizumab (targets α4β7, making it selective for the gut), natalizumab (targets α4 broadly — carrying PML/JC virus risk and requiring enrollment in the restricted TOUCH program), etrolizumab (targets β7/αEβ7), and ustekinumab (targets the p40 subunit shared by IL-12/23, with a long half-life around 120 days meaning live vaccines must be held for 15 weeks afterward).
  • Small-molecule agents: tofacitinib (a JAK1/3 inhibitor taken orally, used for moderate-to-severe UC once biologics have failed, carrying a boxed warning for cardiac events, clotting, and malignancy, and is teratogenic). Ozanimod (an S1P1/5 modulator that sequesters lymphocytes within lymph nodes; its metabolite inhibits MAO-B, raising the risk of hypertensive interactions with tyramine or SSRIs, and contraception is required during treatment).
  • Microbiome-directed approaches: antibiotics (metronidazole, ciprofloxacin, rifaximin) benefit Crohn's disease — particularly perianal and fistulizing forms — but offer little benefit in UC; probiotics are also used, and fecal transplant has its strongest evidence base in recurrent C. difficile infection.

📝 Stage 3 — Plain-Language Notes

Fighting a fire the body itself keeps reigniting

IBD happens when the immune system turns against the bacteria that normally live peacefully in a gut that's genetically prone to this reaction. The damage spreads in stages: bacteria breach a compromised epithelial barrier, antigen-presenting cells sound the alarm via IL-12 and IL-23, T cells then differentiate down a particular path (TH1/TH17 in Crohn's, something closer to TH2 in UC), and these cells release TNF-α, IFN-γ, and related cytokines that damage tissue. Each drug class represents a different way of fighting this fire. 5-ASA only dampens the surface — a purely local effect, which explains why it works well in mucosal-only UC but falls short in deep transmural Crohn's. Steroids act like a powerful hose that quickly extinguishes the whole blaze (excellent for inducing remission), but you can't leave them running indefinitely — long-term steroid use damages bone, skin, glucose control, and the HPA axis, making them unsuitable for maintenance. Thiopurines and methotrexate work more slowly, gradually clearing the fuel that feeds the fire (sparing the need for steroids, though taking weeks to act). Biologics take a more targeted approach — infliximab neutralizes TNF-α, vedolizumab blocks the integrin lymphocytes use to reach the gut in the first place, and ustekinumab disables IL-12/23. JAK inhibitors and S1P modulators either jam the internal signaling machinery or keep lymphocytes confined within lymph nodes.

Two mechanistic details are worth remembering well. Sulfasalazine functions like a disguised delivery system: its azo bond survives passage through the stomach and small intestine, broken open only by bacterial azoreductases that exist in the colon — ensuring the 5-ASA payload is released exactly where the inflammation is happening. And thiopurine metabolism pivots on the enzyme TPMT: when TPMT activity is low, the drug gets shunted down an alternate pathway that produces bone-marrow-toxic 6-thioguanine — which is why TPMT status must be checked beforehand, and why the dose must be reduced if the patient is also taking allopurinol, since allopurinol blocks the other clearance route (xanthine oxidase).

Sulfasalazine works like a package labeled "do not open until reaching the colon." Neither stomach acid nor small-bowel enzymes can break its azo seal; only colonic bacteria possess the right tool (azoreductase) to open it, releasing active 5-ASA right at the site of disease.

  • Steroids can induce remission but should never be used to maintain it — and must always be tapered to avoid HPA-axis suppression and relapse. Budesonide's extensive first-pass metabolism is what keeps its systemic toxicity lower.
  • Methotrexate is teratogenic and absolutely contraindicated during pregnancy. The same applies to JAK inhibitors and ozanimod; certolizumab is the anti-TNF agent considered safe in pregnancy.
  • Latent TB screening is required before starting any anti-TNF agent due to reactivation risk; infliximab specifically should be avoided in severe CHF.
  • TPMT must be checked before starting azathioprine; if the patient is also on allopurinol, cut the dose to roughly 25% (since blocking xanthine oxidase diverts metabolism toward toxic products).
  • Natalizumab carries PML risk (via JC virus reactivation) — it requires the restricted TOUCH program and should never be combined with other immunosuppressants.
  • Antimotility agents and anticholinergics should be avoided in severe IBD due to toxic megacolon risk, and NSAIDs can trigger flares.

01 A 62-year-old man on long-term omeprazole for GERD sustains a hip fracture after a minor fall. What is the pharmacological link between his PPI and bone loss?
Long-term PPI use causes hypochlorhydria → impaired ionisation and absorption of calcium carbonate (requires acid for dissolution; calcium citrate does not). Additionally, PPIs may inhibit osteoclast vacuolar H⁺-ATPase, disrupting bone remodelling. Net result: reduced bone mineral density and increased fracture risk, especially hip, spine, and wrist with >1 year of continuous use.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Use the lowest effective PPI dose for the shortest duration. If long-term PPI is unavoidable, supplement with calcium citrate (acid-independent absorption) and vitamin D rather than calcium carbonate.
⚠️ Exam Trap: PPIs also cause hypomagnesaemia by impairing intestinal Mg²⁺ absorption — can present as refractory hypokalaemia or hyponatraemia (Mg²⁺ depletion impairs K⁺ and Na⁺ regulation). Check Mg²⁺ in long-term PPI patients with arrhythmias or muscle cramps.
02 H. pylori triple therapy (amoxicillin + clarithromycin + PPI × 14 days) fails. Urea breath test remains positive. What is the most likely cause, and what is the next regimen?
Clarithromycin resistance — now 15–40% in many countries, the leading cause of triple therapy failure. Next regimen: bismuth quadruple therapy (PPI + bismuth subcitrate + tetracycline + metronidazole × 14 days) — does not rely on clarithromycin. Alternatively, levofloxacin-based triple therapy. Culture and susceptibility testing should guide retreatment when available.
💎 Clinical Pearl: The urea breath test and stool antigen test are the preferred non-invasive tests for confirming eradication — perform at least 4 weeks after completing antibiotics and 2 weeks after stopping PPI (PPIs suppress H. pylori without eradicating, giving false-negative results).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Serology (IgG anti-H. pylori antibodies) remains positive for years after eradication — do NOT use serology to confirm cure. Use urea breath test or stool antigen test only.
03 A 32-year-old pregnant woman (12 weeks) with an NSAID-induced gastric ulcer is considered for misoprostol therapy. Why is this absolutely contraindicated?
Misoprostol is a PGE1 analogue that replaces the cytoprotective prostaglandins depleted by NSAID-mediated COX-1 inhibition → protects gastric mucosa. However, PGE1 stimulates uterine smooth muscle contraction → uterotonic effect → risk of miscarriage, preterm labour, or uterine rupture. Misoprostol is used therapeutically for medical abortion (with mifepristone) and cervical ripening — thus absolutely contraindicated in ongoing pregnancy for GI indications.
💎 Clinical Pearl: For NSAID-induced ulcer prophylaxis in pregnancy: use a PPI (omeprazole — Category C, generally considered safe) or switch to paracetamol (acetaminophen). Sucralfate is also safe (minimal systemic absorption).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Misoprostol IS used intentionally in obstetrics: ripening the cervix before induction of labour, management of postpartum haemorrhage (uterotonic), and missed/incomplete miscarriage — the same uterotonic property that makes it dangerous in early pregnancy is therapeutic in these contexts.
04 An oncology patient develops nausea and vomiting within 2 hours of cisplatin infusion (acute phase) and again on day 3 (delayed phase). What antiemetic targets each phase?
Acute phase (0–24 h): primarily serotonin-mediated — enterochromaffin cells release 5-HT₃ in response to cisplatin → vagal afferent activation → vomiting centre. Target: 5-HT₃ antagonists (ondansetron, granisetron). Delayed phase (24–120 h): primarily substance P/neurokinin-1 mediated. Target: NK1 antagonists (aprepitant, fosaprepitant). Both phases benefit from dexamethasone (enhances efficacy via unknown mechanism). Triple therapy (5-HT₃ + NK1 + dexamethasone) is standard for highly emetogenic chemotherapy.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Palonosetron (second-generation 5-HT₃ antagonist, t½ ~40 h) has superior activity against delayed emesis compared to ondansetron — it allosterically blocks 5-HT₃ receptors and inhibits SP/NK1 crosstalk signalling.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Anticipatory emesis (before chemotherapy, conditioned response from prior cycles) does NOT respond to 5-HT₃ or NK1 antagonists — it requires benzodiazepines (lorazepam) for anxiolysis and behavioural conditioning therapy.
05 A 38-year-old woman with gastroparesis takes metoclopramide for 8 months. She develops involuntary repetitive lip-smacking and tongue protrusion that persists after stopping the drug. What has happened?
Tardive dyskinesia (TD) — caused by chronic central D2 receptor blockade (metoclopramide crosses the BBB). Prolonged D2 blockade → dopamine receptor supersensitivity/upregulation in the nigrostriatal pathway → involuntary stereotyped orofacial movements. May be irreversible. FDA black-box warning: metoclopramide should not be used for >12 weeks. Risk is higher in elderly patients and women.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Domperidone is a D2 antagonist that does NOT cross the BBB — effective prokinetic without central extrapyramidal/TD risk. Available in most countries but not FDA-approved in the USA. Preferred over metoclopramide for long-term gastroparesis management.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Acute dystonic reactions (oculogyric crisis, torticollis, jaw spasm) can occur within hours of starting metoclopramide — these are REVERSIBLE and treated with IV diphenhydramine or benztropine. Do not confuse with tardive dyskinesia (weeks–months of exposure, often irreversible).
06 A patient with Crohn's disease on azathioprine develops severe pancytopenia. TPMT genotyping reveals he is a poor metaboliser. Explain the pathway.
Azathioprine → 6-mercaptopurine (6-MP) → via HPRT → 6-thioguanine nucleotides (6-TGNs, cytotoxic, active). Parallel pathway: 6-MP → via TPMT → methylated (inactive) metabolites. In TPMT poor metabolisers (homozygous low activity, ~0.3% of population), 6-MP is not methylated → diverted entirely to 6-TGN production → profound myelosuppression. TPMT testing (or NUDT15 in Asian populations) before starting azathioprine/6-MP is now standard of care.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Allopurinol (xanthine oxidase inhibitor) also increases 6-TGN by blocking 6-MP catabolism — combining allopurinol with azathioprine without dose reduction (by 75%) causes life-threatening pancytopenia. This combination can be used therapeutically ('allopurinol shunting') under expert supervision in selected IBD patients.
⚠️ Exam Trap: TPMT testing predicts myelosuppression risk, NOT hepatotoxicity from azathioprine. Hepatotoxicity can occur independently of TPMT status. Monitor CBC monthly for the first 3 months regardless of TPMT genotype.
07 Before starting infliximab for ulcerative colitis, a patient has a positive QuantiFERON-TB Gold test and a normal chest X-ray. What is the management?
Latent TB must be treated before starting any anti-TNF agent. TNF-α is essential for granuloma integrity — anti-TNF therapy reactivates latent TB, often as extrapulmonary/disseminated disease. Management: start isoniazid × 9 months (or rifampin × 4 months) and wait at least 4 weeks before initiating infliximab. Also screen for hepatitis B (reactivation risk) and update all live vaccines before starting.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Anti-TNF-induced TB reactivation classically presents as miliary or extrapulmonary TB (lymph nodes, meninges, peritoneum) — different from typical post-primary pulmonary reactivation. A high index of suspicion is needed since chest X-ray may be normal.
⚠️ Exam Trap: IGRA (QuantiFERON) can be falsely negative in severely immunosuppressed patients (anergy). If clinical suspicion for latent TB is high despite negative IGRA (endemic region, prior exposure), treat empirically for latent TB before starting anti-TNF. Do NOT rely on IGRA alone.
08 A patient with hepatic encephalopathy is started on lactulose. His family asks why a laxative would help his confusion.
Lactulose is a synthetic, non-absorbable disaccharide cleaved by colonic bacteria → lactic acid + acetic acid → acidifies the colon (pH drops from ~7 to ~5). In the acidic colon, ammonia (NH₃) is trapped as ammonium ion (NH₄⁺) which cannot be absorbed → faecal excretion of ammonia. Reduced systemic ammonia → less CNS toxicity → improved hepatic encephalopathy. Secondary effect: osmotic laxation → shorter GI transit → less ammonia production and absorption.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Target: 2–3 soft stools per day. Over-laxation causes dehydration → worsens encephalopathy (pre-renal azotaemia → more ammonia). Rifaximin (non-absorbable antibiotic targeting gut ammonia-producing bacteria) is added for recurrent or refractory HE.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Lactulose is NOT lacTOSE. Lactose intolerance is a completely separate condition (brush-border lactase deficiency). Lactulose is synthetic and non-absorbable by design. Also: lactulose enema is used for obtunded patients who cannot take oral medication.
09 A patient with active peptic ulcer has negative H. pylori testing. She takes naproxen daily for rheumatoid arthritis. Explain the dual mechanism of NSAID-induced ulceration.
Mechanism 1 (systemic — dominant): NSAIDs inhibit COX-1 → reduce PGE₂ and PGI₂ (prostacyclin) in gastric mucosa → decreased mucus secretion, decreased bicarbonate secretion, decreased mucosal blood flow → barrier breakdown. Mechanism 2 (topical/local): NSAIDs are weak acids; in the acidic stomach they are protonated → lipophilic → penetrate epithelial cells → intracellular acidification → direct cytotoxicity. This is why enteric-coated NSAIDs reduce topical injury but do NOT eliminate systemic (COX-1) damage.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Risk stratification for NSAID GI toxicity — HIGH risk: age >65, H. pylori co-infection, history of ulcer/GI bleed, anticoagulant use, high-dose NSAIDs. In high-risk patients: use COX-2 selective NSAID (celecoxib) + PPI, or switch to paracetamol.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Enteric coating does NOT reduce ulcer risk — it only moves the topical damage from stomach to small intestine. The major mechanism (COX-1 systemic inhibition) is unaffected by formulation. Only a PPI or misoprostol truly reduces ulcer risk in NSAID users.
10 A patient takes bismuth subsalicylate for H. pylori therapy and calls alarmed about black, tarry-looking stools. How do you differentiate this from melaena?
Bismuth-black stool: bismuth reacts with hydrogen sulphide from gut bacteria → bismuth sulphide (jet-black, non-odorous, formed stool). This is expected, harmless, and reversible on stopping the drug. Melaena: upper GI bleeding → Hb oxidised by gut bacteria and acid → haematin (black, sticky, tarry, foul-smelling 'coffee-ground' consistency). Key differentiators: melaena has a characteristic foul odour and sticky tarry consistency; also check for concomitant symptoms (haemodynamic instability, vomiting blood, anaemia).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Bismuth also causes black tongue (bismuth sulphide on tongue bacteria) — equally harmless. Bismuth has weak antimicrobial effects against H. pylori (inhibits urease, disrupts bacterial adherence to gastric mucosa), cytoprotective effects, and anti-diarrhoeal (antisecretory) properties.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Bismuth subsalicylate contains salicylate — contraindicated in children with viral illness (Reye's syndrome risk) and in patients with aspirin hypersensitivity, bleeding disorders, or taking anticoagulants (additive antiplatelet effect from salicylate).
11 Sucralfate is prescribed alongside a fluoroquinolone antibiotic for a patient with a peptic ulcer. The antibiotic level is undetectable at 2 hours. Explain.
Sucralfate (aluminium sucrose octasulphate) chelates many drugs in the GI tract: fluoroquinolones, tetracyclines, digoxin, phenytoin, warfarin, levothyroxine, ketoconazole — reducing their absorption by 30–90%. Sucralfate must be given at least 2 hours before or after other medications. Additionally, sucralfate requires an acidic pH to activate (polymerises at pH <4) — simultaneous antacid or PPI use impairs its gastroprotective effect.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Sucralfate works entirely locally — it binds positively charged proteins in the ulcer base (necrotic tissue and albumin) → forms a protective physical barrier over the ulcer surface lasting 4–6 hours. Systemic absorption is <5%. Safe in pregnancy and renal failure (unlike aluminium-containing antacids in CKD).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Sucralfate contains aluminium — can accumulate in CKD (dialysis patients) → aluminium toxicity (encephalopathy, osteomalacia, anaemia). Use with caution or avoid in advanced CKD.
12 Cholestyramine is prescribed for hypercholesterolaemia. The patient notes that her warfarin is less effective. Explain.
Cholestyramine is a bile acid sequestrant (anion exchange resin) — binds bile acids in the gut → prevents reabsorption → increased hepatic bile acid synthesis from cholesterol → lowers LDL. The same binding mechanism non-selectively traps co-administered drugs including warfarin, digoxin, levothyroxine, fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K), some statins, and thiazides → reduced absorption. Give cholestyramine at least 4 hours after other medications.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Cholestyramine is used therapeutically to interrupt enterohepatic recirculation of drugs that need rapid elimination — e.g., leflunomide washout (give cholestyramine 8 g TID × 11 days to accelerate active metabolite elimination, as required before pregnancy).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Long-term cholestyramine use causes fat-soluble vitamin malabsorption (vitamins A, D, E, K) → vitamin K deficiency can independently prolong PT/INR, compounding the warfarin interaction. Supplement fat-soluble vitamins.
13 Erythromycin 125 mg is prescribed at subantimicrobial doses for gastroparesis. The patient asks why an antibiotic is being used for a stomach motility problem.
At low (sub-antimicrobial) doses, erythromycin and other macrolides are potent motilin receptor agonists. Motilin is a gut peptide hormone that triggers the migrating motor complex (MMC) — the 'housekeeper' wave that clears the stomach of undigested contents between meals. Erythromycin mimics motilin → stimulates gastric antral contractions → accelerates gastric emptying. The antibiotic effect requires much higher concentrations than the prokinetic effect.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Tachyphylaxis (motilin receptor downregulation) is a major limitation — efficacy typically wanes after 4 weeks of continuous use. Cycling the drug (2 weeks on / 2 weeks off) or using it intermittently may preserve the prokinetic response.
⚠️ Exam Trap: All macrolides (including azithromycin, clarithromycin) prolong the QT interval via hERG K⁺ channel blockade — risk of Torsades de Pointes. Avoid combining erythromycin with other QT-prolonging drugs (ondansetron, haloperidol, antiarrhythmics). This is especially relevant given that erythromycin inhibits CYP3A4, which can raise levels of QT-prolonging co-medications.
14 Mesalamine (5-ASA) is started for mild UC. Two weeks later the patient returns with significantly worse bloody diarrhoea. Could mesalamine be the cause?
Yes — paradoxical mesalamine-induced exacerbation of colitis occurs in approximately 2–3% of patients. Mechanism unclear (possibly hapten-mediated immune reaction). The presentation is indistinguishable from IBD flare, making it a diagnosis of exclusion. Key clue: onset within 2–4 weeks of starting or increasing mesalamine dose, rapid resolution within days of stopping the drug. Do not rechallenge.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Mesalamine mechanism: inhibits NF-κB → reduces TNF-α, IL-6, COX-2 in colonic epithelium; also inhibits 5-lipoxygenase → reduces LTB4. Key long-term toxicity: interstitial nephritis → check renal function annually in all patients on mesalamine.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Sulfasalazine (the original 5-ASA prodrug) is split by gut bacteria into 5-ASA (active) + sulphapyridine (largely responsible for side effects): haemolytic anaemia (G6PD-dependent), agranulocytosis, hepatotoxicity, reversible oligospermia, folate deficiency, and hypersensitivity. Mesalamine formulations eliminate sulphapyridine and have a much better side-effect profile.
15 Vedolizumab is chosen over infliximab for a 72-year-old woman with Crohn's disease and recurrent pneumonias. What is the mechanism and why is it preferred in this patient?
Vedolizumab is an anti-α4β7 integrin monoclonal antibody. The α4β7 integrin on gut-homing lymphocytes binds MAdCAM-1 (mucosal addressin cell adhesion molecule-1), expressed exclusively on gut endothelium → lymphocyte trafficking into intestinal mucosa is selectively blocked. Because the target is gut-specific, vedolizumab does not suppress systemic immunity — no increased risk of systemic infections, TB reactivation, or PML. Ideal for elderly patients with recurrent respiratory infections who cannot tolerate systemic immunosuppression.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Vedolizumab has a slow onset (weeks–months for Crohn's; faster for UC) — bridge with corticosteroids if rapid control is needed. It does not cross the BBB, so it never causes PML (unlike natalizumab — anti-α4 integrin, NOT gut-selective, associated with JC virus reactivation).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Natalizumab (anti-α4 integrin) targets BOTH gut α4β7 AND CNS α4β1 (VLA-4) → blocks lymphocyte entry into CNS → risk of progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML) from JC virus. Vedolizumab targets only α4β7 (gut) → NO PML risk. This gut-selectivity is the critical differentiator.
16 A patient is treated with polyethylene glycol (PEG) for constipation. Another patient on long-term opioids is given methylnaltrexone. Explain why PEG would not work for the second patient.
PEG (osmotic laxative): large polymer draws water osmotically into the colon → softens stool + stimulates peristalsis. This addresses mechanical stool hardening but does NOT address the underlying cause of opioid-induced constipation (OIC), which is peripheral µ-opioid receptor activation in the enteric nervous system → decreased GI motility + decreased secretion + increased sphincter tone. Methylnaltrexone is a peripherally acting µ-opioid receptor antagonist (PAMORA) — blocks enteric opioid receptors without crossing the BBB, restoring GI motility without reversing analgesia.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Other PAMORAs: naloxegol (oral, PEGylated naloxone — too large to cross BBB), naldemedine (oral). Lubiprostone (ClC-2 activator) also has an FDA indication specifically for OIC in non-cancer chronic pain patients.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Oral naloxone (unmodified) does have some intestinal effect for OIC, but is absorbed and undergoes partial hepatic first-pass — systemic exposure can occur in liver disease, reversing analgesia. PAMORAs are specifically designed to avoid CNS penetration and are safer for reliable OIC treatment without reversing pain control.
17 Lubiprostone is prescribed for chronic idiopathic constipation. A colleague says it 'works like a laxative.' What is the more precise mechanism?
Lubiprostone specifically activates ClC-2 chloride channels (type-2 chloride channels) in intestinal epithelial cells → Cl⁻ secretion into the lumen → passive Na⁺ and water follow → fluid accumulates in the lumen → softer stool + increased GI transit. Unlike osmotic laxatives (which draw water passively), lubiprostone actively secretes fluid via a targeted channel. It is also approved for IBS-C and OIC (at higher doses).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Linaclotide activates guanylate cyclase-C (GC-C) receptor → cGMP → CFTR Cl⁻ secretion (different channel) + analgesic effect on subepithelial afferent neurons. Preferred for IBS-C (addresses both constipation AND visceral pain). Plecanatide is a newer GC-C agonist.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Lubiprostone, linaclotide, and plecanatide are contraindicated in patients <6 years old (risk of severe diarrhoea and dehydration). Lubiprostone causes nausea in up to 30% of patients — take with food and water to reduce GI side effects.
18 A 40-year-old woman with primary biliary cholangitis (PBC) is started on ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA). How does a bile acid treat a liver disease?
UDCA (ursodiol) is a hydrophilic, non-toxic bile acid that, when given orally, replaces the endogenous pool of hydrophobic, cytotoxic bile acids (chenodeoxycholic, lithocholic, deoxycholic acids) via competitive inhibition of their intestinal reabsorption. Mechanisms in PBC: (1) replaces toxic bile acids → reduces hepatocyte injury; (2) stabilises cholangiocyte membranes; (3) has immunomodulatory and anti-apoptotic effects; (4) stimulates biliary HCO₃⁻ secretion ('bicarbonate umbrella'). Slows disease progression, improves LFTs, reduces need for transplant.
💎 Clinical Pearl: UDCA is also used for cholesterol gallstone dissolution (takes 6–24 months; only works for small, cholesterol-rich, radiolucent stones in functioning gallbladder) and to prevent gallstones during rapid weight loss or octreotide therapy.
⚠️ Exam Trap: UDCA does NOT cure PBC — it slows progression. For patients with inadequate response to UDCA, add obeticholic acid (FXR agonist → reduces bile acid synthesis, improves cholestasis) — but it can cause dose-dependent pruritus and is contraindicated in decompensated cirrhosis.
19 Ondansetron is given for post-operative nausea in a patient on fluoxetine. The anaesthetist is concerned about a drug interaction. What is it, and how significant is the risk?
Potential serotonin syndrome: fluoxetine (SSRI) increases synaptic serotonin by blocking reuptake; ondansetron is primarily a 5-HT₃ antagonist. The risk is real but low with ondansetron alone — however, in the context of polypharmacy (SSRI + opioids with serotonergic activity like tramadol/fentanyl + antiemetics like metoclopramide), cumulative serotonin burden increases. The more clinically significant concern: ondansetron QT prolongation in combination with other QT-prolonging perioperative drugs (haloperidol, droperidol, some antibiotics).
💎 Clinical Pearl: True serotonin syndrome risk with ondansetron monotherapy + SSRI is low because ondansetron ANTAGONISES 5-HT₃ (it doesn't activate serotonin receptors). The interaction concern is greatest when multiple serotonergic agents are used simultaneously — applies more to granisetron in some studies.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Max single IV ondansetron dose is 16 mg (the old 32 mg dose was removed due to QT prolongation risk). FDA warning: avoid in patients with congenital long-QT syndrome and concurrent QT-prolonging drugs. Oral 24 mg single dose for highly emetogenic chemotherapy is still acceptable.
20 A patient with Clostridioides difficile infection (CDI) fails a first course of oral metronidazole. What is the correct treatment escalation, and why has treatment guidance changed?
Current IDSA/ESCMID guidelines no longer recommend metronidazole as first-line for CDI of any severity. Vancomycin oral (125 mg QID × 10 days) or fidaxomicin (200 mg BID × 10 days) are now first-line. Fidaxomicin has a narrower spectrum → preserves Bacteroides and Lactobacillus colonisation resistance → significantly lower recurrence rate (~40% reduction vs oral vancomycin). Metronidazole is acceptable only for mild cases when cost is prohibitive.
💎 Clinical Pearl: For recurrent CDI: second recurrence — fidaxomicin (if first treatment was vancomycin) or vancomycin taper/pulse. Third or more recurrences: bezlotoxumab (anti-toxin B monoclonal antibody) as adjunct, or faecal microbiota transplant (FMT) — 85–90% cure rate for multiply recurrent CDI.
⚠️ Exam Trap: IV vancomycin does NOT treat CDI — it does not reach the colon lumen in therapeutic concentrations. Only oral vancomycin achieves adequate colonic levels. Fidaxomicin also has minimal systemic absorption — both act locally in the colon.

▸Quiz Set 53 — Acid–Peptic Disease & GERD18 Q
1
Which single cell type, via which receptor, is the dominant paracrine amplifier of parietal-cell acid secretion?
Reveal answer
The ECL cell releasing histamine, acting on the parietal-cell H2 receptor (Gs → cAMP). This is why H₂ blockers and PPIs are so effective.
2
Gastrin is the most potent inducer of acid yet acts "largely indirectly." Explain.
Reveal answer
Gastrin (from antral G cells, via CCK2) stimulates acid mainly by releasing histamine from ECL cells; its direct action on parietal cells is comparatively minor.
3
What luminal change triggers somatostatin release, and what is its downstream effect?
Reveal answer
Luminal pH < 3 stimulates antral D cells → somatostatin → suppresses gastrin (negative feedback). H. pylori depletes D cells, removing this brake.
4
Why is the PPI's duration of acid suppression (24–48 h) so much longer than its plasma half-life (0.5–3 h)?
Reveal answer
PPIs bind the pump covalently and irreversibly; acid secretion only resumes once the cell synthesises new pumps — decoupling effect duration from plasma clearance.
5
Why should a PPI be taken 30–60 minutes before the first meal of the day?
Reveal answer
Pump numbers are highest after fasting; dosing before the meal means the maximum number of freshly inserted, actively secreting pumps are available to be irreversibly inhibited.
6
Where exactly is a PPI prodrug activated, and into what active species?
Reveal answer
In the acidic secretory canaliculus of the parietal cell, it forms a tetracyclic sulfenamide that covalently binds pump cysteines.
7
Which PPI uniquely inhibits CYP2C19 and may impair clopidogrel activation, and which is the safest co-prescription?
Reveal answer
Omeprazole inhibits CYP2C19 (controversial clopidogrel interaction); pantoprazole is the safest pairing.
8
List four recognised long-term adverse associations of PPIs.
Reveal answer
↓ vitamin B12, hypomagnesaemia (worse with diuretics), ↑ fracture risk, and ↑ infections (C. difficile, pneumonia, SBP). Also hypergastrinaemia → ECL hyperplasia/rebound.
9
By what type of binding do H₂ antagonists act, and roughly what % acid suppression do they achieve?
Reveal answer
Reversible competitive antagonism at H2; ~70% suppression (especially nocturnal/basal). Tolerance develops within ~3 days.
10
Which H₂ antagonist causes gynaecomastia/impotence and inhibits CYP enzymes? Which two were withdrawn and why?
Reveal answer
Cimetidine (antiandrogen + CYP inhibitor). Ranitidine and nizatidine were largely withdrawn due to NDMA contamination.
11
How do potassium-competitive acid blockers differ mechanistically from PPIs, and what clinical advantages follow?
Reveal answer
P-CABs reversibly block the K⁺ site of the pump, need no acid activation, and bind both active and inactive pump → faster onset (day 1), longer duration, better nocturnal control, meal-independence, CYP2C19-independence.
12
Misoprostol's mechanism and its single most important contraindication?
Reveal answer
PGE₁ analogue → EP3/Gi → ↓cAMP ↓acid + cytoprotection (prevents NSAID ulcers). Absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy (uterotonic/abortifacient).
13
Why must sucralfate be taken on an empty stomach, and in whom is it risky?
Reveal answer
It needs acid (pH < 4) to cross-link into its protective gel. Avoid in renal failure (aluminium accumulation); it also binds other drugs (space dosing).
14
Contrast the bowel effects of magnesium- vs aluminium-containing antacids.
Reveal answer
Mg²⁺ → diarrhoea; Al³⁺ → constipation. Combinations aim to balance the two.
15
State a standard 10–14-day triple therapy for H. pylori.
Reveal answer
PPI + clarithromycin 500 mg + amoxicillin 1 g, all twice daily (metronidazole can replace amoxicillin if penicillin-allergic).
16
What is the quadruple regimen, and when is it preferred?
Reveal answer
PPI + bismuth + tetracycline + metronidazole — preferred where clarithromycin/metronidazole resistance is common.
17
In GERD during pregnancy, outline the stepwise drug progression.
Reveal answer
Start with antacids / alginates / sucralfate → then H₂ antagonist (cimetidine best track record; avoid nizatidine) → reserve PPI (lansoprazole/pantoprazole) for intractable/complicated disease.
18
Drug of choice for Zollinger-Ellison syndrome, and the rescue if uncontrolled?
Reveal answer
High-dose PPI (≈ twice the usual ulcer dose); if acid still uncontrolled, add the somatostatin analogue octreotide.
▸Quiz Set 54 — Motility, Emesis & Biliary/Pancreatic22 Q
1
Name the primary excitatory and inhibitory neurotransmitters of enteric motor neurons.
Reveal answer
Excitatory = acetylcholine; inhibitory = nitric oxide (with VIP, ATP, PACAP as contributors).
2
Which cell releases serotonin to ignite the peristaltic reflex, and which cells set the gut's electrical pacemaker rhythm?
Reveal answer
Enterochromaffin cells release 5HT; interstitial cells of Cajal set the pacemaker/slow-wave rhythm.
3
Give metoclopramide's full receptor profile and its most feared chronic adverse effect.
Reveal answer
D2 antagonist + 5HT4 agonist + 5HT3 antagonist. Chronic use → tardive dyskinesia (may be irreversible); also acute dystonia, hyperprolactinaemia, QT. Limit to < 12 weeks.
4
How does domperidone avoid extrapyramidal effects yet still cause prolactin elevation and antiemesis?
Reveal answer
It's a peripheral D2 antagonist that poorly crosses the BBB; but it still acts at sites outside the BBB (CTZ for emesis, pituitary for prolactin). Caution: ventricular arrhythmia/sudden death.
5
Why was cisapride withdrawn?
Reveal answer
Fatal QT prolongation / torsades via blockade of the hERG K⁺ channel.
6
Erythromycin's prokinetic mechanism and why its effect fades within ~28 days.
Reveal answer
It mimics motilin (triggers phase III MMC). Tachyphylaxis from motilin-receptor downregulation after ~28 days.
7
Lactulose has an indication beyond constipation — what, and by what mechanism?
Reveal answer
Hepatic encephalopathy: colonic fermentation drops luminal pH, trapping ammonia as non-absorbable NH₄⁺, plus faster transit lowers blood ammonia.
8
Match each prosecretory laxative to its target: lubiprostone, linaclotide/plecanatide, tenapanor.
Reveal answer
Lubiprostone → ClC-2 chloride channel; linaclotide/plecanatide → guanylate cyclase-C (GC-C); tenapanor → NHE3 inhibition.
9
Which laxative class should be avoided in renal failure and in patients on diuretics, and why?
Reveal answer
Magnesium (saline) laxatives — risk of hypermagnesaemia and electrolyte disturbance.
10
Why does loperamide relieve diarrhoea without central opioid effects, and what is its black-box warning?
Reveal answer
It's a peripheral MOR agonist that penetrates the CNS poorly (40–50× morphine as an antidiarrhoeal). Black box: cardiac events / torsades at overdose (max 16 mg/day).
11
Why is atropine co-formulated with diphenoxylate/difenoxin?
Reveal answer
A subtherapeutic atropine dose discourages abuse/overdose (unpleasant anticholinergic effects at high doses).
12
In which two diarrhoeal scenarios must antibiotics AND antimotility agents be avoided?
Reveal answer
Enterohaemorrhagic E. coli (HUS risk) and C. difficile. Antimotility risks toxic megacolon and prolonged organism clearance.
13
Bismuth subsalicylate: two harmless signs it causes, and one systemic caution.
Reveal answer
Black stools and black tongue (bismuth sulfide). Caution: the absorbed salicylate → Reye's risk, tinnitus, CNS effects.
14
Which IBS drug carries an ischaemic-colitis risk and a REMS, and in whom is it used?
Reveal answer
Alosetron (5HT3 antagonist) — for women with severe diarrhoea-predominant IBS, under a restricted prescribing programme.
15
Eluxadoline's receptor profile and the special dosing rule for patients without a gallbladder.
Reveal answer
MOR agonist / DOR antagonist / KOR agonist. Without a gallbladder use 75 mg twice daily (lower) to reduce sphincter-of-Oddi spasm/pancreatitis risk.
16
Why is the CTZ uniquely positioned to detect blood-borne emetics?
Reveal answer
It lies in the area postrema, outside the blood–brain barrier, sampling blood and CSF for toxins.
17
Which antiemetic class is the key addition for delayed-phase chemotherapy emesis, and with what two partners is it given?
Reveal answer
NK1 antagonists (aprepitant/fosaprepitant, rolapitant), given with a 5HT3 antagonist + dexamethasone.
18
Best drug classes for motion sickness?
Reveal answer
H1 antihistamines (cyclizine, meclizine) and the antimuscarinic scopolamine (patch). 5HT3 antagonists are ineffective for motion sickness.
19
First-line agent for nausea/vomiting of pregnancy?
Reveal answer
Doxylamine (H1 antihistamine) + pyridoxine (vitamin B6).
20
Which 5HT3 antagonist helps with delayed emesis and spares the QT interval, and why?
Reveal answer
Palonosetron — long half-life (~40 h) and highest receptor affinity; does not appear to prolong QT.
21
Ursodiol and obeticholic acid both treat primary biliary cholangitis — how do they differ mechanistically, and what is obeticholic acid's black-box risk?
Reveal answer
Ursodiol = hydrophilic bile acid that displaces toxic bile acids & reduces lithogenicity; obeticholic acid = FXR agonist that suppresses bile-acid synthesis. Black box: hepatic decompensation / liver failure if mis-dosed.
22
How is pancreatic enzyme replacement dosed, and on which enzyme is it titrated?
Reveal answer
Titrated on lipase content: start ~500 USP units/kg per meal, up to ~2500 USP units/kg per meal (≤ 10,000 USP units/kg/day).
▸Quiz Set 55 — Inflammatory Bowel Disease16 Q
1
Contrast the cytokine profiles of Crohn's disease vs ulcerative colitis.
Reveal answer
Crohn's = TH1/TH17 (IL-12, IL-23, IFN-γ, TNF-α); UC = TH2-like (IL-4, IL-13). Crohn's is transmural with skip lesions; UC is mucosal and continuous.
2
Which is the active moiety of sulfasalazine, and how is it released specifically in the colon?
Reveal answer
5-ASA (mesalamine). The azo bond survives stomach/small bowel and is cleaved by colonic bacterial azoreductases; sulfapyridine is the inert (toxic) carrier.
3
Two adverse effects attributable to the sulfapyridine moiety, and the supplement routinely given with sulfasalazine.
Reveal answer
Headache/nausea/fatigue, rash, reversible ↓ sperm count/motility, marrow suppression. Give folate (sulfasalazine inhibits folate absorption).
4
Why are glucocorticoids excellent for induction but not maintenance of IBD remission?
Reveal answer
They rapidly damp inflammation (induction) but chronic use carries serious toxicity (bone, skin, metabolic, HPA suppression). Always taper to avoid adrenal insufficiency/relapse.
5
What makes budesonide gentler systemically than prednisone?
Reveal answer
Extensive first-pass hepatic metabolism (CYP3A4) keeps systemic levels low while delivering steroid locally to ileum/colon.
6
Trace the activation pathway of azathioprine to its active moiety.
Reveal answer
Azathioprine → 6-mercaptopurine → (via HGPRT) → 6-thioguanine nucleotides, the active immunosuppressive species.
7
Why must TPMT be assessed before azathioprine, and what is the allopurinol interaction rule?
Reveal answer
Low TPMT shunts drug to 6-TGN → bone-marrow suppression. Allopurinol blocks xanthine oxidase → more active metabolite → reduce dose to ~25%.
8
Two notable thiopurine toxicities beyond marrow suppression.
Reveal answer
Pancreatitis (~5%) and a 4-fold increased risk of (non-Hodgkin) lymphoma.
9
Methotrexate's role in IBD, its main contraindication, and drugs that raise its toxicity.
Reveal answer
Maintenance of Crohn's remission (SC). Absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy (teratogen). NSAIDs, PPIs, penicillins, probenecid, ciprofloxacin reduce its renal clearance → toxicity.
10
Distinguish infliximab from adalimumab/certolizumab in structure and antidrug-antibody risk.
Reveal answer
Infliximab = chimeric (mouse/human), IV, more antidrug antibodies → often combined with azathioprine. Adalimumab/golimumab = humanised; certolizumab = pegylated Fab — both fewer antibodies, given SC.
11
What must be screened for before starting any anti-TNF agent, and which cardiac condition contraindicates infliximab?
Reveal answer
Screen for latent tuberculosis (reactivation risk). Infliximab is contraindicated in severe congestive heart failure.
12
Which biologic is gut-selective by blocking α4β7, and which carries a PML risk under a restricted programme?
Reveal answer
Vedolizumab (anti-α4β7, gut-selective). Natalizumab (anti-α4) → PML/JC virus, available only via the TOUCH programme.
13
Ustekinumab's target, and a practical vaccination caution from its long half-life.
Reveal answer
Targets the p40 subunit of IL-12/IL-23. Half-life up to ~120 days → stop 15 weeks before a live vaccine.
14
Tofacitinib's mechanism, key indication, and boxed warnings.
Reveal answer
JAK1/3 inhibitor (oral) for moderate–severe UC after biologic failure. Boxed warnings: serious cardiac events, thromboembolism, malignancy; teratogenic.
15
Ozanimod's mechanism and the dietary/drug interaction its metabolite creates.
Reveal answer
S1P1/5 receptor modulator — traps lymphocytes in lymph nodes. A metabolite inhibits MAO-B → hypertensive interactions with tyramine, SSRIs/SNRIs, TCAs, MAO inhibitors.
16
In a pregnant IBD patient, name the safest anti-TNF and the three drug categories that are absolutely contraindicated.
Reveal answer
Safest anti-TNF = certolizumab pegol (no placental transfer). Contraindicated: methotrexate, JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib), and ozanimod.
