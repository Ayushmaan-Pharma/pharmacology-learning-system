👁️Special Systems
Pharmacology
  • Prostaglandin analogues (latanoprost, travoprost, bimatoprost, tafluprost) act on FP receptors in the ciliary muscle to open up a second drainage route for eye fluid (uveoscleral outflow).
  • This route normally carries only 5–20% of outflow, but loosening the tissue around it lets a lot more fluid drain out, cutting eye pressure by 25–35%.
  • Big advantages: once-daily dosing, and the drug is broken down locally in the cornea so little reaches the rest of the body.
  • β-blockers instead just reduce fluid production, but they get absorbed through the nose and can slow the heart or trigger asthma — which is why prostaglandins overtook them as first choice.
  • Eye drops drain through a tear duct into the nose, where the lining is full of blood vessels — so the drug bypasses the liver's usual filtering (first-pass metabolism).
  • One timolol drop can deliver enough β-blocker into the blood to slow the heart — so pulse should be checked in patients using these drops.
  • Brimonidine (an α2 drug) can cross into a baby's brain and cause breathing to slow or stop (apnea) in neonates.
  • The cornea has three layers a drug must cross: an oily (hydrophobic) outer layer, a watery (hydrophilic) middle layer, then another oily layer.
  • A purely oil-loving drug gets stuck in the first layer; a purely water-loving drug can't even enter it — only a molecule that is both ("amphipathic") gets all the way through.
  • Latanoprost is designed as an oily prodrug to get in, then the eye's own enzymes convert it to its active, water-friendlier form once inside.
  • Echothiophate permanently blocks the enzyme that breaks down acetylcholine, so its pupil-constricting effect never lets up.
  • This nonstop pulling on the lens's support fibers slowly damages how the lens feeds itself, eventually causing cataracts.
  • Pilocarpine causes the same kind of pupil constriction but is reversible and milder, so it doesn't carry the same cataract risk.
  • Horner syndrome means a break somewhere in the nerve pathway that normally widens the pupil, causing droopy eyelid, small pupil, and dry skin on one side.
  • Cocaine drops normally widen a healthy pupil; in Horner syndrome there's no widening because the damaged nerve isn't releasing its chemical messenger.
  • Apraclonidine causes strong widening specifically in the Horner eye, because the eye becomes oversensitive to that chemical messenger after the nerve damage.
  • Hydroxyamphetamine tells you whether the damage is before or after the last nerve junction — no widening means the very last nerve segment is the damaged one.
  • Retinoids attach to receptors inside the cell (RAR and RXR) that turn certain genes on or off.
  • RAR-acting retinoids (tretinoin, adapalene, tazarotene, acitretin) mainly fix how skin cells grow and mature — used for acne, psoriasis, sun-damaged skin.
  • RXR-acting retinoids (bexarotene, alitretinoin) mainly push abnormal cells toward self-destruction — used for certain skin cancers.
  • Isotretinoin works differently and somewhat mysteriously — it shrinks oil glands and reduces acne-causing bacteria, and can cure severe acne in about 70% of patients after one course.
  • Acitretin itself clears the body in about 50 hours, which would normally be safe quickly after stopping.
  • But if the patient drinks any alcohol while on it, the drug converts into etretinate, which gets stored in body fat and lingers for months (80–160 days half-life).
  • Because etretinate causes birth defects just like acitretin, doctors require 3 years of contraception after stopping, just in case alcohol was ever consumed during treatment.
  • Facial skin is thin and absorbs much more steroid than skin elsewhere on the body.
  • Long-term use of strong (fluorinated) steroids on the face disturbs its normal bacterial balance, causing pimple-like bumps around the mouth and flushed, rosacea-like skin.
  • Stopping the steroid can temporarily make this worse before it gets better (a "rebound" effect), so only mild, non-fluorinated steroids should be used on the face.
  • IL-17 normally helps the skin fight off Candida yeast, so drugs that block IL-17 directly (secukinumab, ixekizumab, bimekizumab, brodalumab) raise the risk of yeast infections.
  • IL-23 sits one step earlier in the chain that produces IL-17, so blocking IL-23 (guselkumab, risankizumab, tildrakizumab) doesn't fully remove IL-17's protective effect, leaving fewer Candida infections.
  • Brodalumab blocks the receptor for all forms of IL-17, including effects in the brain, and trials noticed some patients reporting suicidal thoughts.
  • It's not proven that the drug actually causes this — psoriasis patients already have higher rates of depression — but regulators still required a special safety program (REMS) before the drug can be prescribed.
  • CaNa₂EDTA grabs lead well and helps the body excrete it, but it can stir lead loose from tissues and accidentally push some of it into the brain.
  • Succimer (DMSA) is more selective — it mostly leaves essential metals (zinc, copper, iron) alone, doesn't redistribute lead to the brain, and can be taken as a pill.
  • For a child with a moderate lead level who can be treated as an outpatient, succimer is the safer, preferred choice.
  • For iron, cadmium, and selenium, the dimercaprol-metal combination is actually more toxic than the metal alone, especially to the kidneys — so it should never be used for these.
  • Cadmium poisoning specifically gets worse with any chelation, so it's treated with supportive care only, not antidotes.
  • Dimercaprol is dissolved in peanut oil and given as a painful injection into the muscle, so it cannot be used in patients with peanut allergy.
  • Methyl mercury disguises itself as an amino acid (methionine), so the body's transporters actively carry it across the placenta and into the brain.
  • A developing fetal brain is still building its structure and is extremely sensitive to this kind of damage, while an adult's fully-formed brain tolerates it much better.
  • This mismatch explains Minamata disease — babies born with severe brain damage to mothers who had only mild symptoms themselves.
  • Aflatoxin (from moldy grains/nuts) damages liver cell DNA; hepatitis B's HBx protein blocks the cell's normal DNA-repair protein (p53), so that damage doesn't get fixed.
  • Hepatitis B also makes the liver produce more of the enzyme that turns aflatoxin into its cancer-causing form, while reducing the enzyme that detoxifies it.
  • Hepatitis B kills liver cells, forcing the survivors to multiply quickly — spreading any aflatoxin-caused mutations faster.
  • Result: aflatoxin alone raises liver cancer risk about 3-fold, hepatitis B alone about 7-fold, but together the risk rises 59-fold — far more than simply adding the two.
  • Fat-soluble A, D, E, K are stored → toxicity risk and deficiency in fat malabsorption; water-soluble B/C → deficiency syndromes (beriberi, pellagra, scurvy, megaloblastic anaemia).
  • GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide) & GIP/GLP-1 (tirzepatide): insulin secretion, slowed gastric emptying, appetite suppression; orlistat = lipase inhibitor (supplement A, D, E, K).
  • Paediatric: immature metabolism (grey-baby), bilirubin displacement (kernicterus); geriatric: reduced clearance, Beers list, "start low go slow".
  • Pregnancy teratogens (warfarin, ACEi, valproate, isotretinoin, tetracyclines); hepatic (Child-Pugh) and renal (eGFR) dose adjustment; pharmacogenomics (TPMT, HLA-B*57:01).
  • Five steps: resuscitate → identify (toxidrome) → decontaminate → antidote → enhance elimination; charcoal binds most toxins but not metals/alcohols/hydrocarbons/caustics/lithium/iron.
  • Key antidotes with mechanisms (NAC, naloxone, atropine+pralidoxime, fomepizole, deferoxamine, digoxin-Fab); dialysable toxins (salicylate, lithium, methanol, valproate); PIC duties.
  • Dale & Loewi (neurotransmission), James Black (β-blocker + H₂-blocker, rational design), Sutherland (cAMP), Gilman & Rodbell (G-proteins).
  • Yalow (radioimmunoassay), Allison & Honjo (checkpoint inhibitors); Ram Nath Chopra — father of Indian pharmacology.

Chapter 74: Ocular Pharmacology

Autonomic pharmacology of the eye, glaucoma therapy, antimicrobials, anti-VEGF agents, the visual cycle

🧭 80/20 High-Yield Map
Ocular autonomic pharmacology simply mirrors what's already known about the peripheral ANS elsewhere in the body — any agent that widens or narrows the pupil, or shifts intraocular pressure, is acting through the familiar α, β, or M receptor set.
Figure 74-A: Ocular autonomic receptor map — which receptor subtype is engaged dictates the drug response seen.

Key high-yield points:

  • Aqueous humor pathway: Fluid is produced by the ciliary processes, flows into the posterior chamber, passes through the pupil into the anterior chamber, then drains mainly via the trabecular meshwork (80–95%) with the remainder via the uveoscleral route into episcleral veins. Cholinergic agents act on the trabecular route; prostaglandin analogues act on the uveoscleral route.
  • Drug-melanin binding: Heavily pigmented irides dilate more slowly with α-agonists; chloroquine builds up in the RPE, producing "bull's-eye" maculopathy.
  • Cornea favors amphipathic drugs — a molecule must traverse a hydrophobic epithelium, then a hydrophilic stroma, then a hydrophobic endothelium again (the "fat-water-fat" sandwich).
  • Nasolacrimal drainage lets eye drops dodge first-pass metabolism, so meaningful systemic effects can follow topical dosing (β-blockers causing bradycardia; brimonidine causing apnea in neonates).

Vitamin A & Visual cycle key points:

  • 11-cis-retinal serves as the chromophore in both rod and cone photoreceptors. A photon converts it to all-trans, which activates transducin, which in turn activates PDE6; cGMP then falls, CNG channels shut, the cell hyperpolarizes, and transmitter release drops.
  • PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil) have weak off-target PDE6 inhibition, producing a bluish visual haze or light sensitivity at higher doses.
  • Vitamin A deficiency: Progresses from night blindness to xerophthalmia (Bitot spots, keratomalacia) and, if unchecked, corneal perforation with permanent blindness.
  • Hydroxychloroquine toxicity: Builds up in the RPE and produces "bull's-eye" maculopathy; the dose ceiling is 5 mg/kg/day, and yearly eye exams are needed once treatment passes the 5-year mark.

Why PG Analogues Won the First-Line Battle

Prostaglandin F₂α analogues (latanoprost, travoprost, bimatoprost, tafluprost) take an entirely separate route compared with the rest of the glaucoma armamentarium — they act on FP receptors (Gq/11-PLC-IP3-Ca²⁺ signaling) in the ciliary muscle, with the end result being a rise in uveoscleral outflow. This secondary drainage route (ordinarily handling 5–20% of flow) runs through the ciliary muscle into the suprachoroidal space. Release of matrix metalloproteinases breaks down extracellular matrix there, effectively widening the channel. The benefit: once-a-day dosing and few systemic effects (the prodrug is broken down locally by corneal esterases), plus a 25–35% drop in IOP.

β-blockers work differently, cutting aqueous production (β2 blockade lowers cAMP and thus secretion), but timolol gets absorbed nasally and can trigger systemic bradycardia and bronchospasm, making it a poor fit for asthmatics. That tradeoff is why PG analogues came out ahead.

Think of PG analogues as cutting a second drain into an overflowing bathtub — rather than just easing off the tap the way β-blockers do, you're widening the uveoscleral outlet. Adding outflow capacity controls IOP better than restricting inflow alone.

Nasolacrimal Systemic Absorption — The Hidden Route

An instilled eye drop largely drains through the nasolacrimal duct into the nasal passage. Because the nasal mucosa is richly vascularized, whatever is absorbed there skips hepatic first-pass metabolism entirely. A single timolol drop can therefore deliver a meaningfully bioavailable dose of β-blocker into the bloodstream. That's the reasoning behind: (a) checking pulse in patients using β-blocker drops; (b) brimonidine (a lipophilic α₂ agonist that crosses into the CNS) producing respiratory depression and apnea in neonates — they're effectively getting a systemic CNS depressant.

Corneal Drug Penetration — The "Fat-Water-Fat" Rule

Three layers stand between a topical drug and the eye's interior: a hydrophobic epithelium, a hydrophilic stroma, then a hydrophobic endothelium. A drug that's purely lipophilic gets trapped in the epithelium; one that's purely hydrophilic can't even enter it. Only an amphipathic molecule, with both properties, can move through all three layers. Latanoprost is built as an isopropyl ester prodrug — lipophilic enough to get in — which corneal esterases then convert to PGF₂α, the active acid form, once inside the eye.

Echothiophate → Cataracts: The Mechanism

Echothiophate, an organophosphate that inhibits AChE irreversibly, carries a quaternary ammonium group that keeps it charged and confined to the anterior chamber. This produces sustained, intense parasympathetic drive and miosis. That ongoing miotic pull on the zonules and lens can disturb lens metabolism over time, leading to posterior subcapsular cataracts. Pilocarpine also causes miosis, but being a reversible direct agonist its effect is milder and varies between doses, whereas echothiophate's irreversibility keeps receptors continuously saturated.

The Horner Diagnostic Logic

Horner syndrome reflects lost sympathetic outflow (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis). Pharmacologic testing probes the three-neuron chain (hypothalamus → cilio-spinal center → superior cervical ganglion → iris dilator) at different points:

  • Cocaine 10% — ordinarily produces dilation by blocking NE reuptake. With Horner present, there's no dilation since the damaged terminal neuron isn't releasing NE in the first place.
  • Apraclonidine 0.5% — normally produces little dilation since α₂ effects dominate. In Horner syndrome, denervation supersensitivity upregulates α₁ receptors, so dilation becomes pronounced — this test has largely taken over from cocaine.
  • Hydroxyamphetamine 1% — triggers NE release from the postganglionic terminal. Dilation occurs if that neuron is intact (1st- or 2nd-order Horner); absence of dilation points to a postganglionic lesion (3rd-order Horner), since there's no NE left to release.
  • Pilocarpine 0.05–0.1% — dilute enough to act only on supersensitive M receptors, as seen in Adie's pupil where parasympathetic denervation upregulates M₃. A normal pupil shows no response at this concentration.
Betaxolol's β₁ selectivity makes it less powerful for IOP control, since the eye's β receptors are mostly β₂ subtype — yet that same selectivity is exactly why it's chosen for patients with asthma, sparing pulmonary β₂ receptors. Efficacy and receptor selectivity are separate issues here, not interchangeable.
PG-analogue-induced iris darkening (most noticeable in hazel or mixed-color irides) is PERMANENT, driven by increased melanin output from stromal melanocytes. Keep this distinct from conjunctival hyperemia (which resolves) and orbital fat atrophy (a structural change, not a pigment change).
Topical steroids carry three signature risks: posterior subcapsular cataracts, secondary open-angle glaucoma (in those with a family history, IOP can spike markedly in up to 90%), and opportunistic infection. Loteprednol was designed to lower (though not eliminate) the glaucoma risk, since the eye's own 11β-HSD rapidly inactivates it.

01 A 65-year-old man with open-angle glaucoma is started on latanoprost eye drops. After 6 weeks he notices his iris has turned from grey-green to brown, and his eyelashes are longer and darker. Explain.
Latanoprost, a prostaglandin F2α (PGF2α) analogue, lowers intraocular pressure (IOP) by boosting uveoscleral outflow, the alternative aqueous-drainage route. Its effect on pigmentation: FP receptors on iris melanocytes get stimulated, ramping up melanin production in the stromal melanocytes, which produces irreversible darkening of iris color (heterochromia if only one eye is treated). The lash changes follow a similar path: FP receptors on follicular melanocytes and dermal papilla cells drive extra melanin production plus a longer anagen phase, giving hypertrichosis — lashes that are longer, thicker, and darker (this same property is exploited cosmetically with bimatoprost, a related prostamide). Neither change reverses once the drug is stopped.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Glaucoma drug ranking by first-line preference: prostaglandin analogues (latanoprost, bimatoprost, travoprost — once daily, very effective) rank above beta-blockers (timolol), which rank above alpha-2 agonists (brimonidine), which rank above carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (dorzolamide). Prostaglandins lead the list because of their single daily dose, strong efficacy, and lack of cardiovascular or pulmonary systemic effects.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Prostaglandin analogues should be avoided in uveitis and cystoid macular oedema — stimulating FP receptors promotes prostaglandin-driven inflammation, and they're also unsuitable for aphakic/pseudophakic eyes prone to CMO. Beta-blockers or carbonic anhydrase inhibitors become the first-line choice in these patients instead.
02 Timolol eye drops are prescribed for glaucoma in a 70-year-old man with mild COPD. Two weeks later he is admitted with acute bronchospasm. How did topical timolol cause systemic effects?
Timolol blocks both β1 and β2 receptors non-selectively. Drops applied to the eye drain through the nasolacrimal duct into the nose, get absorbed by the nasal mucosa, and reach the systemic circulation while skipping hepatic first-pass metabolism — roughly 80% of the topical dose ends up systemically bioavailable. β2 blockade in the airway smooth muscle then triggers bronchoconstriction, causing acute bronchospasm in patients already prone to it (COPD, asthma). Other systemic consequences include bradycardia, heart block, hypotension, masked hypoglycaemia, and fatigue. Occluding the nasolacrimal punctum (pressing the inner corner of the eye for 1–2 minutes after dosing) cuts systemic absorption by about half.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Timolol works by cutting aqueous humour production: β2 receptors on the ciliary body epithelium are blocked, cAMP falls, and secretion drops accordingly — outflow is unaffected. Avoid it in asthma, severe COPD, heart block, bradycardia, or decompensated heart failure. Betaxolol, being β1-selective, is a reasonable substitute in mild COPD with less bronchospasm risk.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Systemic effects from eye drops get overlooked far too often. Anyone presenting with bradycardia, bronchospasm, or fatigue should be asked specifically about eye drop use, since patients rarely think to mention them. Because of nasolacrimal drainage, a "just eye drops" history can still translate into clinically significant systemic drug exposure.
03 A patient with acute angle-closure glaucoma presents with severe eye pain, vomiting, and IOP of 58 mmHg. After pilocarpine and IV acetazolamide, IOP falls to 35 mmHg. Explain the mechanisms of both drugs.
Pilocarpine, a direct M3 muscarinic agonist, contracts the ciliary muscle, which tugs open the trabecular meshwork and Schlemm's canal, boosting conventional outflow. It also produces miosis via sphincter pupillae contraction; in acute angle-closure, this pulls the iris away from the trabecular meshwork and reopens the drainage angle. IV acetazolamide, a carbonic anhydrase inhibitor, blocks CA-II and CA-IV in the ciliary epithelium, which curbs HCO₃⁻ generation and thereby Na⁺/water secretion into the posterior chamber — aqueous production drops sharply and IOP falls within roughly 30 minutes. The definitive fix is Nd:YAG laser peripheral iridotomy, which punches a hole through the peripheral iris to equalize pressure across the chambers and eliminate the pupillary block.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Systemic acetazolamide produces metabolic acidosis by blocking renal tubular CA, impairing HCO₃⁻ reabsorption and causing bicarbonate loss (a non-anion-gap acidosis). It also brings sulfonamide-type effects — nephrolithiasis, paraesthesiae, hypokalaemia — and should be avoided with sulfonamide allergy or severe renal impairment.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Pilocarpine's miosis makes it unsuitable as standalone therapy for open-angle glaucoma — constricted pupils worsen dim-light vision and ciliary spasm causes headache. Its systemic cholinergic effects (the DUMBELS pattern) also mean caution in asthma or bowel/urinary obstruction. The drug is appropriate for ACUTE angle-closure but in CHRONIC open-angle disease, prostaglandins and beta-blockers are favored.
04 A dermatology patient with severe plaque psoriasis on cyclosporin develops new hypertension and rising creatinine. What is the mechanism, and what topical immunosuppressant avoids these systemic effects?
Cyclosporin works by inhibiting calcineurin, which blocks NFAT activity, lowers IL-2 output, and dampens T-cell activation — the net effect being less Th1/Th17-driven keratinocyte overgrowth in psoriasis. Its systemic downside is nephrotoxicity: the drug constricts the afferent arteriole (via increased TXA2 and ET-1, reduced NO/PGI2), which lowers GFR and, with chronic use, drives progressive CKD. The same vascular effect explains the hypertension; hyperkalaemia, hypomagnesaemia, gingival hyperplasia, and hirsutism round out its toxicity profile. A topical option that sidesteps this is tacrolimus (Protopic) or pimecrolimus (Elidel) — both calcineurin inhibitors applied directly to skin, where they suppress local T-cell cytokine output and inflammation. Because intact skin absorbs so little of either drug, nephrotoxicity and systemic immunosuppression are minimal.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Topical tacrolimus and pimecrolimus both carry a black-box warning for a theoretical malignancy risk (lymphoma, skin cancer), extrapolated from systemic immunosuppression data — yet topical exposure levels are far below what oral tacrolimus produces, and actual evidence linking topical use to malignancy is weak. Even so, they shouldn't be applied to malignant or pre-malignant lesions.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Cyclosporin-induced kidney injury is dose-related and partly reversible if the dose is reduced, but prolonged use leaves behind irreversible interstitial fibrosis — the same mechanism and monitoring needs apply to tacrolimus. For psoriasis, cyclosporin is capped at 2 years of use given this cumulative renal risk, with creatinine, blood pressure, and drug levels tracked throughout.
05 A 28-year-old woman with acne vulgaris is prescribed isotretinoin. Before dispensing, what pregnancy-related obligations must be fulfilled, and what monitoring is required during therapy?
Because isotretinoin (13-cis-retinoic acid) is so strongly teratogenic — retinoic acid signaling is critical to embryonic development, and excess produces the fetal retinoid syndrome (craniofacial defects, cardiac malformations, CNS abnormalities such as hydrocephalus, thymic hypoplasia) — strict safeguards apply before any prescription is filled: (1) two pregnancy tests come back negative (one before starting, a second 19–37 days afterward, timed to day 2–3 of the next cycle); (2) the patient uses two contraceptive methods spanning 1 month before, the entire treatment course, and 1 month after (equivalent to the US iPLEDGE program — termed the Pregnancy Prevention Programme in the UK); (3) informed consent is documented; (4) each prescription is valid for only 7 days, requiring reassessment before any refill. Ongoing monitoring includes monthly pregnancy testing, liver function tests (hepatotoxicity), fasting lipids (since marked hypertriglyceridaemia risks pancreatitis), and a full blood count (leucopenia).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Isotretinoin acts through several overlapping effects: shrinking sebaceous glands and cutting sebum output (the most important action, since sebum feeds C. acnes); correcting abnormal follicular keratinisation to reduce comedones; reducing inflammation; and indirectly limiting C. acnes colonisation. It stands alone among acne treatments in offering a shot at lasting remission — about 80% of patients never need a second course.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Because it's a vitamin A analogue that interferes with rhodopsin regeneration, isotretinoin can cause night blindness — patients should be warned in advance. Its most frequent side effect is actually cheilitis (dry, cracked lips), alongside dry eyes, myalgia, and epistaxis. Combining it with tetracyclines risks benign intracranial hypertension (pseudotumour cerebri), so this pairing must always be avoided.
06 A farmer presents with pinpoint pupils, bradycardia, copious secretions, bronchospasm, urinary incontinence, and muscle fasciculations. He has been spraying organophosphate pesticides. What is the treatment sequence?
This is the classic organophosphate (OP) toxidrome, driven by irreversible inhibition of acetylcholinesterase, which lets ACh build up at every cholinergic synapse. The muscarinic features fit the DUMBELS pattern — Diarrhoea/Defaecation, Urination, Miosis, Bradycardia/Bronchospasm/Bronchorrhoea, Emesis, Lacrimation, Salivation/Sweating — while nicotinic effects at the neuromuscular junction produce fasciculations progressing to paralysis (respiratory muscle failure is the leading cause of death), and CNS involvement brings seizures and coma. Management proceeds in order: (1) IV atropine in large, repeated doses titrated to drying of secretions (not pupil size) to reverse muscarinic effects including bronchospasm; (2) IV pralidoxime (2-PAM), which reactivates AChE by knocking the phosphate group loose before the bond 'ages' into an irreversible state — this must happen within hours; (3) benzodiazepines if seizures occur; (4) supportive measures, including ventilation for respiratory failure.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Pralidoxime only works within a narrow window — the OP-AChE bond 'ages' into an oxime-resistant form over a timeframe that varies by agent (minutes for nerve agents, hours for malathion, days for chlorpyrifos). Once ageing occurs, atropine alone manages the muscarinic symptoms. Nerve agents (sarin, VX, novichok) produce an identical toxidrome but age much faster.
⚠️ Exam Trap: OP poisoning calls for far larger atropine doses than typical clinical use — 2–4 mg IV boluses every 5–10 minutes until secretions dry up, sometimes totaling 20–100 mg. The goal is dry secretions and resolved bronchospasm, never pupil size or heart rate. Underdosing atropine is one of the most common causes of death in these cases.
07 A patient ingests a large quantity of paracetamol (acetaminophen) 4 hours ago. LFTs are normal, INR is 1.0. Should you start N-acetylcysteine (NAC) now, or wait for toxicity?
NAC should be started right away if the 4-hour paracetamol level sits above the Rumack-Matthew nomogram treatment line (or the UK risk line) — waiting for liver damage to appear defeats the purpose, since NAC works best before hepatotoxicity sets in. The toxicity itself stems from CYP2E1/CYP3A4 converting paracetamol into NAPQI (N-acetyl-p-benzoquinone imine), a reactive metabolite that's normally neutralized by conjugation with hepatic glutathione (GSH) into a harmless mercapturate. In overdose, GSH reserves run out, leaving free NAPQI to bind hepatocyte proteins covalently and cause necrosis, concentrated in zone 3 where CYP2E1 activity peaks. NAC counters this by supplying cysteine for new GSH synthesis (scavenging NAPQI directly), while also donating cysteine/GSH itself and improving microvascular flow.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Certain factors lower the threshold at which treatment becomes necessary — enzyme inducers like rifampin, carbamazepine, or chronic alcohol use (which boost CYP2E1 and thus NAPQI generation), and malnutrition, anorexia, or fasting (which deplete baseline GSH). All of these raise hepatotoxicity risk at lower paracetamol doses. Current UK guidance has moved to a single treatment line rather than stratifying by risk factors.
⚠️ Exam Trap: NAC infusions, particularly during the initial loading dose, can trigger anaphylactoid reactions (flushing, hives, bronchospasm) that are NOT IgE-mediated — more common in asthmatics. The fix is to pause the infusion briefly, give an antihistamine, then resume more slowly; this reaction is never a reason to stop NAC altogether, since its benefit clearly outweighs this risk.
08 Carbon monoxide poisoning is suspected in a family found unconscious at home. What is the treatment and why is high-flow 100% O₂ the key intervention?
CO binds haemoglobin roughly 240 times more tightly than oxygen does, forming carboxyhaemoglobin (COHb) that can no longer carry O₂ — a functional anaemia. It also blocks myoglobin and cytochrome c oxidase (Complex IV), shutting down cellular respiration and causing histotoxic hypoxia. Standard pulse oximetry can't distinguish COHb from oxyhaemoglobin, so SpO₂ reads falsely normal; COHb must instead be measured by co-oximetry on an arterial blood gas. The treatment is high-flow 100% O₂ via non-rebreather mask, which competitively displaces CO from haemoglobin. COHb's half-life drops from roughly 5 hours on room air to about 90 minutes on 100% O₂, and down to 20–30 minutes with hyperbaric O₂ at 3 atmospheres — reserved for cases involving loss of consciousness, neurological deficits, COHb above 25%, pregnancy (the fetus is especially vulnerable), or cardiac involvement.
💎 Clinical Pearl: The textbook cherry-red skin of CO poisoning (from bright-red COHb) is mostly a late or post-mortem sign — acutely, patients more often look pale or cyanotic. A telling history is several household members affected together (faulty boiler, enclosed vehicle), along with headache, nausea, and confusion, since CO preferentially damages white matter and the basal ganglia.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Smoke inhalation can bring concurrent cyanide toxicity, since burning plastics and upholstery release HCN — suspect this when lactic acidosis is out of proportion to haemodynamics and hypoxia persists despite adequate oxygen delivery. Hydroxocobalamin (converted to cyanocobalamin and excreted renally) is the preferred antidote, ahead of dicobalt edetate (toxic, causes vomiting and hypotension) or the sodium thiosulfate/nitrite combination.
09 A patient presents with acute opioid overdose — pinpoint pupils, respiratory rate 4/min, GCS 6. What is given, and what problem arises if the patient is opioid-dependent?
Naloxone (IV, IM, or intranasal) acts as a competitive antagonist across μ, κ, and δ opioid receptors, quickly reversing the respiratory depression, sedation, and miosis caused by opioids — onset is under 2 minutes IV, given as 0.4–2 mg doses repeated every 2–3 minutes up to a 10 mg ceiling. The catch in opioid-dependent patients is naloxone's short 60–90 minute half-life compared with most opioids (morphine roughly 4–6 hours, methadone 24–36 hours, buprenorphine 24–72 hours) — once naloxone wears off, the opioid effect can return and the patient may re-arrest. It can also precipitate acute withdrawal in dependent patients (agitation, vomiting, diarrhoea, tachycardia, severe distress), so dosing should be titrated to restore adequate breathing rather than full alertness, using small incremental doses to avoid triggering withdrawal.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Naltrexone, a long-acting oral antagonist (half-life around 13 hours, with its active metabolite 6-β-naltrexol lasting about 24 hours), is used both for opioid use disorder (preventing relapse after detox) and alcohol use disorder (blunting craving and reward). A monthly extended-release IM formulation (Vivitrol) helps with adherence, and the naltrexone-bupropion combination (Contrave) is FDA-approved for weight management.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Buprenorphine, a partial μ-agonist and κ-antagonist used in opioid substitution therapy, shows a ceiling effect on respiratory depression that makes it relatively safer in overdose than full agonists — though combining high doses with benzodiazepines or alcohol can still be fatal. Naloxone only partially reverses buprenorphine, since its partial-agonist binding is harder to displace, sometimes requiring higher naloxone doses.
10 A 30-year-old with alcohol use disorder is prescribed disulfiram. He drinks wine at a party and develops severe flushing, nausea, vomiting, hypotension, and palpitations within 10 minutes. Explain the pharmacology.
Disulfiram irreversibly inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH) — the enzyme that converts acetaldehyde → acetic acid. Ethanol → (alcohol dehydrogenase) → acetaldehyde → (ALDH — BLOCKED) → acetaldehyde accumulates → disulfiram-ethanol reaction (DER): vasodilation (flushing, hypotension), tachycardia, nausea/vomiting, headache, sweating. Aversion therapy — the unpleasant reaction deters drinking. Duration of inhibition: disulfiram irreversibly inhibits ALDH → effect lasts 1–2 weeks after stopping the drug (new enzyme must be synthesised). Warn patients to avoid all alcohol-containing products (mouthwash, cooking wine, certain medicines).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Other pharmacological treatments for alcohol use disorder: acamprosate (GABA-A agonist + NMDA antagonist — reduces craving/withdrawal symptoms, best started after detox); naltrexone (reduces opioid-mediated reward from alcohol — reduces pleasure and craving); nalmefene (opioid partial agonist/antagonist — reduces drinking intensity in non-abstinent patients). Combination naltrexone + acamprosate may be superior to monotherapy.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Disulfiram inhibits multiple enzymes beyond ALDH: also inhibits dopamine-β-hydroxylase (reduces conversion of dopamine → noradrenaline), CYP2E1 (raises blood alcohol levels), and CYP2C9 (raises warfarin levels → increased INR). Multiple drug interactions — check all concurrent medications. Contraindicated in psychosis (dopamine effects), severe hepatic disease, cardiac disease.
11 A patient with severe sepsis has lactic acidosis (pH 7.18, lactate 8 mmol/L). The ICU team considers sodium bicarbonate infusion. What does the pharmacology say about this practice?
Sodium bicarbonate for lactic acidosis is controversial and generally not recommended as routine practice. Rationale for NaHCO₃: provides exogenous bicarbonate → buffers H⁺ → raises pH. Problems: (1) Paradoxical intracellular acidosis — HCO₃⁻ + H⁺ → CO₂ + H₂O; CO₂ freely diffuses into cells and mitochondria → intracellular pH may actually fall; (2) Hypokalaemia (H⁺/K⁺ shift — alkalosis drives K⁺ into cells); (3) Hypocalcaemia (alkalosis increases albumin binding of Ca²⁺); (4) Hypernatraemia (sodium load); (5) Does NOT improve haemodynamics or mortality in trials (BICAR-ICU). Exception: bicarbonate indicated in lactic acidosis with severe acidaemia (pH <7.1) AND AKI requiring renal replacement — improves haemodynamics in this specific context (BICAR-ICU subgroup).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Treating the underlying cause of lactic acidosis is the only evidence-based intervention: source control in sepsis (antibiotics, drainage), optimise cardiac output, correct hypovolaemia, stop causative drugs (metformin, linezolid, NRTIs). Dichloroacetate (activates pyruvate dehydrogenase → reduces lactate production) — lowers lactate in trials but does NOT improve mortality.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Type A lactic acidosis (tissue hypoxia — sepsis, cardiogenic shock, ischaemia) vs Type B (no hypoxia — metformin, linezolid, NRTIs, thiamine deficiency, malignancy). Both cause elevated lactate but management differs. Metformin lactic acidosis (MALA) in the context of AKI → withhold metformin, haemodialysis (clears metformin AND corrects acidosis).
12 A 45-year-old nurse is found to have regular exposure to hazardous chemotherapy drugs without adequate PPE. She asks which chemotherapy agents pose the highest dermal absorption and reproductive toxicity risk.
Most hazardous chemotherapy drugs for occupational exposure (NIOSH Table 1 hazardous drugs): highest risk drugs include alkylating agents (cyclophosphamide, ifosfamide, melphalan, busulfan — direct DNA alkylators, skin absorption, teratogenic, carcinogenic), antimetabolites (methotrexate, 5-FU — dermal absorption, reproductive toxicity), anthracyclines (doxorubicin — skin sensitiser, vesicant). Reproductive risks: cyclophosphamide causes premature ovarian failure and is teratogenic; methotrexate is abortifacient. PPE requirements: closed-system drug-transfer devices (CSTDs), double gloves (chemotherapy-rated nitrile, not latex), low-permeability gown, eye/face protection, preparation in certified biological safety cabinet (class II B2 exhaust).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Vesicant chemotherapy agents (cause tissue necrosis on extravasation): anthracyclines (doxorubicin, epirubicin — most severe; treat with dexrazoxane IV within 6 hours), vinca alkaloids (vincristine, vinblastine — treat with warm compresses + hyaluronidase), mechlorethamine. Irritant agents (cause inflammation but not necrosis): oxaliplatin, etoposide, paclitaxel.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Cyclophosphamide for cancer requires MESNA to prevent haemorrhagic cystitis (acrolein metabolite). Ifosfamide additionally causes encephalopathy (ifosfamide CNS toxicity — confusion, hallucinations, seizures) — treat with methylene blue (alternative electron acceptor — reverses ifosfamide-induced inhibition of mitochondrial electron transport) or supportive care.
13 A patient is brought in after intentional iron tablet overdose. He has vomiting, diarrhoea, haematemesis, and abdominal X-ray shows radio-opaque tablets. What is the specific chelating antidote and its mechanism?
Desferrioxamine (deferoxamine) — a hexadentate siderophore chelator produced by Streptomyces pilosus. It binds free iron (Fe³⁺) with extremely high affinity → forms ferrioxamine complex → renally excreted (produces 'vin rosé' pink-orange urine — indicating chelation is occurring). Desferrioxamine specifically chelates free iron and iron in ferritin/haemosiderin — it does NOT chelate iron in haemoglobin or transferrin (too tightly bound). Iron toxicity phases: GI phase (0–6h, direct GI corrosion) → latent phase (6–24h, apparent improvement) → systemic toxicity (24–72h, acidosis, hepatic failure, coagulopathy, cardiovascular collapse) → late sequelae (2–8 weeks, GI stricture). Treat in ICU with IV desferrioxamine if serum iron >90 µmol/L or severe symptoms.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Other chelating antidotes: EDTA (ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid) — lead, heavy metals; dimercaprol (BAL) — arsenic, mercury, lead; DMSA (succimer) — lead, mercury (oral, safer than BAL); penicillamine — Wilson's disease (copper), lead; prussian blue — thallium, caesium; hydroxocobalamin — cyanide (binds CN⁻ → cyanocobalamin).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Whole-bowel irrigation (polyethylene glycol at 1–2 L/hour) is used for iron tablet overdose to mechanically flush unabsorbed tablets from the GI tract — particularly if tablets are visible on X-ray. Activated charcoal does NOT bind iron (or lithium, alcohols, acids, bases) — do NOT give activated charcoal in iron overdose.
14 A 25-year-old with recreational drug use has taken MDMA (ecstasy) at a party. He presents confused, with hyperthermia (40.2°C), tachycardia, and hyponatraemia (Na 121 mmol/L). What is the mechanism of hyponatraemia?
MDMA causes hyponatraemia via two mechanisms: (1) SIADH — MDMA massively releases serotonin from raphe nuclei and serotonergic terminals → serotonin stimulates ADH (AVP) release from hypothalamus → water retention → dilutional hyponatraemia. (2) Compulsive water drinking (hyperdipsia) — MDMA users drink excess water to prevent hyperthermia/dehydration, often dramatically overcorrecting → dilutional hyponatraemia. Both mechanisms combine → severe hyponatraemia → cerebral oedema → confusion, seizures, cerebral herniation (particularly in young women — more susceptible due to hormonal effects on brain AQP4 channels). Treatment: controlled hypertonic saline (3%) for symptomatic hyponatraemia; correct at ≤10 mmol/L/day to avoid osmotic demyelination syndrome.
💎 Clinical Pearl: MDMA toxicity triad: hyperthermia + hyponatraemia + serotonin syndrome features (hyperthermia, clonus, agitation). Management: active cooling, benzodiazepines for agitation/seizures, correct Na⁺ carefully. Avoid dantrolene (used for malignant hyperthermia/NMS — does not address serotonin mechanism). Cyproheptadine (5-HT antagonist) for serotonin syndrome if severe.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Hyponatraemia correction: correct too slowly → ongoing cerebral oedema and death; correct too fast → osmotic demyelination syndrome (ODS) — central pontine myelinolysis → quadriplegia, dysarthria, locked-in syndrome. Maximum safe correction rate: 10 mmol/L in first 24 hours, 18 mmol/L in 48 hours. Exception: in acute symptomatic hyponatraemia (<48h), faster initial correction (1–2 mmol/L/hour for first few hours) is safe to stop seizures before slowing.
15 A 55-year-old taking warfarin for AF is also prescribed broad-spectrum antibiotics for 10 days. Her INR rises to 6.2. Why?
Broad-spectrum antibiotics (especially those with poor GI absorption — cephalosporins, fluoroquinolones, metronidazole) disrupt gut flora → reduce intestinal bacterial synthesis of vitamin K2 (menaquinone) → reduced vitamin K available for hepatic carboxylation of clotting factors (II, VII, IX, X, protein C and S) → enhanced warfarin anticoagulant effect → INR rise. Additionally: some antibiotics (metronidazole, fluconazole) inhibit CYP2C9 → reduced warfarin (S-enantiomer) metabolism → pharmacokinetic interaction also contributes. Management: hold 1–2 warfarin doses, recheck INR, restart at lower dose; if INR >8 or bleeding → vitamin K1 (phytomenadione) orally or IV.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Vitamin K1 (phytomenadione/phytonadione) reverses warfarin anticoagulation. Oral route takes 12–24 hours (wait for hepatic factor synthesis). IV route: faster onset (~6–8h) but risk of anaphylactoid reaction — use slow IV infusion. For life-threatening bleeding: 4-factor prothrombin complex concentrate (4F-PCC) provides immediate factor replacement (fastest reversal). FFP is second-line (large volume, incomplete reversal).
⚠️ Exam Trap: The hepatic vitamin K cycle: dietary K1 → reduced by VKOR (vitamin K epoxide reductase) → active form → γ-carboxylation of Glu residues on clotting factors. Warfarin inhibits VKOR → factors produced without γ-carboxylation → non-functional (cannot bind Ca²⁺/phospholipid). This is also why warfarin has a delayed onset (~5 days) — must wait for existing carboxylated factors to clear (factor VII shortest half-life, ~6h → INR rises first).
16 A 70-year-old man taking multiple medications for hypertension, gout, and depression is found confused with a Na⁺ of 118 mmol/L. Review of his medication list reveals hydrochlorothiazide, allopurinol, sertraline, and ramipril. Which drug is most likely responsible?
Most likely: SIADH from sertraline (SSRI) — SSRIs cause SIADH in elderly patients (~0.5–1%), mechanism unclear but involves serotonin-stimulated ADH release and/or direct effect on renal collecting duct aquaporins. However, hydrochlorothiazide is also a major cause — thiazides cause hyponatraemia via: (1) Inhibiting distal tubule NaCl cotransporter → impaired diluting ability in the kidney; (2) Indirect ADH stimulation from volume depletion. In elderly patients, thiazide-induced hyponatraemia can be severe. Ramipril rarely causes hyponatraemia. Allopurinol does not. Management: identify culprit drug (stop if possible), fluid restriction for SIADH, demeclocycline or vaptans (tolvaptan — V2 receptor antagonist, aquaretic) for persistent SIADH.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Tolvaptan (and conivaptan) are vasopressin V2 receptor antagonists — block ADH action at renal collecting duct → free water excretion (aquaresis — water without Na⁺ → raises serum Na⁺). Used for euvolaemic SIADH and hypervolaemic hyponatraemia (HF, cirrhosis). Risk: overly rapid Na⁺ correction → ODS. Monitoring required; start in hospital.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Hyponatraemia from thiazides is hypotonic hyponatraemia with low urine Na⁺ if early (urinary Na⁺ may be high initially then falls as volume depletion activates aldosterone). SIADH has high urine Na⁺ (>40 mmol/L) and concentrated urine. Distinguishing features: thiazide → volume depletion + metabolic alkalosis + hypokalaemia; SIADH → euvolaemia, normal acid-base, variable K⁺.
17 A patient in a burns unit needs nutritional support. What drug given to burn patients reduces catabolism, and why is glutamine supplementation specifically recommended in critical illness?
Oxandrolone (anabolic steroid, synthetic testosterone analogue) — used in severe burns to counter the massive hypermetabolism and muscle catabolism (cortisol and catecholamine surge → protein breakdown, negative nitrogen balance). Oxandrolone stimulates androgen receptors → enhances nitrogen retention, muscle protein synthesis, wound healing, lean mass preservation. Standard in major burns (>40% TBSA) for 6–12 months. Glutamine in critical illness: glutamine is normally a non-essential amino acid but becomes conditionally essential in critical illness (endogenous synthesis cannot meet demand). Functions: primary fuel for enterocytes and lymphocytes → maintains gut mucosal integrity (reduces bacterial translocation) and immune function. Supplementation recommended for burns, trauma, and ICU patients on TPN (parenteral glutamine — gut cannot absorb enteral glutamine effectively in severe illness).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Insulin infusion (targeting glucose 6–10 mmol/L) is used in burns/critical illness ICUs — insulin has anabolic effects beyond glucose control: promotes amino acid uptake into muscle, reduces muscle proteolysis, stimulates GH axis. Tight glucose control (4–6 mmol/L) is no longer recommended after NICE-SUGAR trial — increased mortality from hypoglycaemia.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Enteral nutrition is always preferred over parenteral in critical illness (including burns) — preserves gut mucosal integrity, reduces infection rate, maintains gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT). Start enteral nutrition within 24–48 hours. TPN only if enteral route is impossible or contraindicated (high-output fistula, ileus, bowel obstruction).
18 A patient develops hyperkalaemia (K⁺ 7.1 mmol/L) with peaked T waves and widened QRS on ECG. List the emergency treatment sequence in the correct order with mechanisms.
Emergency treatment of severe hyperkalaemia (ECG changes): (1) IV calcium gluconate 10 mL 10% (or calcium chloride) — immediately stabilises cardiomyocyte membrane potential by raising threshold potential → reduces cardiac excitability. Onset: 1–3 minutes. Does NOT lower K⁺ level — cardiac membrane protection only. (2) IV insulin (10 units actrapid) + 50 mL 50% dextrose — insulin activates Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase → drives K⁺ into cells. Onset 20–30 min, lasts 4–6h. (3) Nebulised salbutamol 10–20 mg — β2 agonist → activates Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase in muscle → additional K⁺ shift into cells. Additive with insulin. (4) Calcium resonium (sodium polystyrene sulfonate) or patiromer — GI K⁺ binders → remove K⁺ from body. Onset hours. (5) Haemodialysis — fastest K⁺ removal in refractory cases or oliguria.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Sodium bicarbonate (IV) for hyperkalaemia — causes K⁺ shift via H⁺/K⁺ exchange only in the context of metabolic acidosis. It is NOT reliable as monotherapy for hyperkalaemia without acidosis, and its role in the treatment sequence is now de-emphasised by most guidelines (NICE).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Calcium gluconate does NOT lower K⁺ — students confuse it with 'treating' the hyperkalaemia. It only protects the heart for ~30–60 minutes. Definitive K⁺ lowering requires insulin-glucose, salbutamol, K⁺ binders, or dialysis. If calcium is given but no K⁺-lowering treatment follows, the patient will deteriorate as the calcium effect wears off.
19 A 40-year-old woman on long-term phenytoin for epilepsy develops macrocytic anaemia. Her folate level is low. Explain the mechanism and treatment.
Phenytoin causes folate deficiency via two mechanisms: (1) Impairs intestinal folate absorption — phenytoin inhibits intestinal conjugase (pteroylpolyglutamate hydrolase) → dietary polyglutamate folate cannot be hydrolysed to absorbable monoglutamate form → folate malabsorption. (2) Increases folate catabolism. Result: low serum folate → impaired thymidylate synthesis → megaloblastic erythropoiesis → macrocytic anaemia. Treatment: folic acid supplementation (5 mg/day). Important caveat: folic acid supplementation in epilepsy can lower phenytoin levels (folate may induce CYP2C9/2C19, or compete for enterohepatic recycling) → may increase seizure frequency. Monitor phenytoin levels closely when starting folate.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Other phenytoin-related metabolic effects: osteomalacia/osteoporosis — phenytoin induces CYP3A4 → accelerates vitamin D catabolism → reduced 25-OH-D → impaired calcium absorption → secondary hyperparathyroidism → bone demineralisation. Supplement calcium + vitamin D in long-term phenytoin users. Also causes gingival hyperplasia, hirsutism, coarsening of facies (cosmetically distressing, particularly in young women).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Macrocytic anaemia from phenytoin is folate-deficiency megaloblastic anaemia — do NOT confuse with vitamin B12 deficiency (different mechanism, though both cause macrocytosis and megaloblastic changes). Check both folate AND B12 in macrocytosis. Phenytoin also causes a lupus-like syndrome (anti-histone antibodies) — reversible on stopping the drug.
20 A patient is prescribed warfarin for mechanical heart valve. She is also started on amiodarone for paroxysmal AF. One week later her INR is 7.8. What is the interaction and how should it be managed?
Amiodarone is a potent inhibitor of CYP2C9 (the major enzyme metabolising the active S-warfarin enantiomer) and also inhibits CYP3A4 (R-warfarin). Result: dramatically reduced warfarin metabolism → warfarin accumulates → excessive anticoagulation → INR rises. Importantly, amiodarone has a half-life of 40–55 days (due to massive tissue distribution — adipose tissue, liver, lungs) — the CYP2C9 inhibition PERSISTS for months after stopping amiodarone. Management: (1) Hold 1–2 warfarin doses; (2) Reduce warfarin dose by 30–50% when amiodarone is started; (3) Monitor INR weekly until stable; (4) Remember that warfarin dose requirements will gradually increase again over months if amiodarone is stopped (as CYP2C9 inhibition wanes).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Amiodarone's extraordinary half-life (40–55 days) means: (1) Weeks to reach therapeutic antiarrhythmic levels (IV loading used for acute arrhythmias); (2) Drug interactions and adverse effects persist long after cessation; (3) Amiodarone pulmonary toxicity, thyroid dysfunction, and hepatotoxicity may appear or worsen months after stopping. Monitor TFTs, LFTs, CXR every 6 months during treatment.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Amiodarone is an iodine-rich drug (~37% iodine by weight) → can cause both hypo- and hyperthyroidism. Type 1 amiodarone-induced thyrotoxicosis (AIT) — iodine-induced excess thyroid hormone synthesis in pre-existing thyroid disease (treat with thionamides). Type 2 AIT — destructive thyroiditis from iodine toxicity (treat with corticosteroids). Distinguishing the two requires thyroid imaging and colour flow Doppler.
21A patient with chronic cholestatic liver disease develops easy bruising and a prolonged prothrombin time that corrects with parenteral vitamin K. Explain. (Ch 77)
Vitamin K deficiency from fat malabsorption. Vitamin K is fat-soluble and needs bile for absorption; cholestasis impairs this → deficiency of the vitamin-K-dependent clotting factors II, VII, IX, X → prolonged PT/INR and bleeding. Because the defect is substrate deficiency (not liver synthetic failure), parenteral vitamin K corrects it.
⚠️ Exam Trap: If the PT does NOT correct with vitamin K, the problem is hepatocellular synthetic failure, not simple deficiency. The other fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E) are also depleted in fat malabsorption.
22Why must folic acid never be given alone to a patient with an unexplained megaloblastic anaemia? (Ch 77)
If the cause is vitamin B12 deficiency, folate will correct the anaemia (masking it) but the neurological damage — subacute combined degeneration of the cord — progresses unchecked and may become irreversible. Always check B12 and folate together and replace B12 first (or both).
💎 Clinical Pearl: B12 deficiency causes neuro signs (dorsal column/pyramidal); folate deficiency causes neural-tube defects in pregnancy but not SCD.
23An obese patient with type 2 diabetes is started on semaglutide and loses substantial weight. What is the mechanism and a key contraindication? (Ch 77)
Semaglutide is a GLP-1 receptor agonist: it stimulates glucose-dependent insulin secretion, slows gastric emptying, and acts centrally to suppress appetite — together reducing intake and body weight. Contraindicated with a personal/family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN2 (rodent C-cell tumour signal); also caution re pancreatitis.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Tirzepatide adds GIP agonism for even greater weight loss; GI upset is the commonest adverse effect of this class.
24A patient on orlistat for obesity develops night blindness and a prolonged INR. Why? (Ch 77)
Orlistat inhibits intestinal/pancreatic lipase, blocking dietary fat absorption — and with it the absorption of the fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E and K. Vitamin A deficiency → night blindness; vitamin K deficiency → reduced clotting factors → prolonged INR. Supplement fat-soluble vitamins, taken separated from the drug.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Orlistat's hallmark side effect is steatorrhoea (oily, fatty stools) from unabsorbed fat.
25An alcoholic patient is brought in confused; the intern is about to start IV dextrose. What must be given first and why? (Ch 77)
Thiamine (vitamin B1) before glucose. A glucose load in a thiamine-deficient patient consumes the last thiamine (a cofactor for glucose metabolism) and can precipitate Wernicke encephalopathy (ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, confusion), which may progress to irreversible Korsakoff psychosis.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Thiamine deficiency also causes beriberi — "wet" (high-output cardiac failure) and "dry" (peripheral neuropathy).
26A neonate given chloramphenicol develops abdominal distension, cyanosis and cardiovascular collapse. Name the syndrome and its mechanism. (Ch 78)
Grey-baby syndrome. Neonates have immature hepatic glucuronidation (and reduced renal excretion), so chloramphenicol accumulates to toxic levels → circulatory collapse, grey discolouration, hypotension. It illustrates how immature metabolism makes a normal weight-scaled dose dangerous.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Also avoid sulfonamides in neonates — they displace bilirubin from albumin and, with an immature blood-brain barrier, risk kernicterus.
27An 82-year-old on multiple medications keeps falling. Which drug classes named in the Beers criteria should you review first? (Ch 78)
The Beers criteria flag potentially inappropriate medicines in older adults: long-acting benzodiazepines and Z-drugs (sedation, falls), strong anticholinergics (confusion, retention), and NSAIDs (GI bleeding, renal/CV risk). Reduced clearance and increased sensitivity mean "start low, go slow" and regular deprescribing review.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Increased body fat raises the volume of distribution of lipophilic drugs (e.g. diazepam), prolonging their effect in the elderly.
28A woman planning pregnancy is on several drugs. Which classic teratogens must be switched, and give one harm of each? (Ch 78)
Switch the classic teratogens: warfarin (nasal/skeletal defects), ACE inhibitors/ARBs (renal dysgenesis, oligohydramnios in 2nd/3rd trimester), valproate (neural tube defects, neurodevelopmental harm), isotretinoin (severe craniofacial/cardiac/CNS defects), tetracyclines (teeth/bone), methotrexate and thalidomide (phocomelia).
⚠️ Exam Trap: ACE inhibitor fetopathy is a 2nd/3rd-trimester effect — the drug must be stopped as soon as pregnancy is planned/confirmed.
29A patient with eGFR 25 needs an aminoglycoside. How do you adjust dosing and why? (Ch 78)
Aminoglycosides are renally excreted and nephrotoxic. In renal impairment, reduce the dose and/or extend the dosing interval guided by eGFR/CrCl and therapeutic drug monitoring (trough levels), and avoid other nephrotoxins. The goal is effective peak concentrations without accumulation.
💎 Clinical Pearl: eGFR is preferred over serum creatinine alone because creatinine varies with muscle mass, age and sex.
30Before starting azathioprine, a clinician checks an enzyme level. Which enzyme, and why does it matter? (Ch 78)
TPMT (thiopurine methyltransferase). Patients with low/absent TPMT activity cannot inactivate azathioprine/6-mercaptopurine → drug accumulates → severe, potentially fatal myelosuppression. Genotyping/phenotyping guides dose reduction or avoidance — a classic pharmacogenomic example.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Other genotype-guided drugs: HLA-B*57:01 (abacavir), HLA-B*15:02 (carbamazepine SJS), CYP2C19 (clopidogrel).
31A farmer arrives drowsy, sweating, with pinpoint pupils, copious secretions and muscle fasciculations. Diagnosis, antidotes, and the atropine end-point? (Ch 79)
Organophosphate poisoning (cholinergic toxidrome — DUMBELS/SLUDGE). Antidotes: atropine (blocks muscarinic effects) titrated to drying of bronchial secretions (the key end-point, not pupils or heart rate) + pralidoxime to reactivate acetylcholinesterase before "ageing". Add airway protection, decontamination, and benzodiazepines for seizures.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Pralidoxime must be given early — once the phosphorylated enzyme "ages" it can no longer be reactivated.
32An unconscious patient with respiratory depression and pinpoint pupils wakes after naloxone but re-sedates 30 minutes later. Why, and what do you do? (Ch 79)
Opioid overdose (the triad: coma, pinpoint pupils, respiratory depression). Naloxone reversed it, but its half-life is shorter than most opioids, so as it wears off the opioid re-occupies the μ-receptor → re-sedation. Manage with repeat naloxone doses or a continuous infusion and prolonged observation (especially long-acting opioids/fentanyl).
💎 Clinical Pearl: The same short-half-life caveat applies to flumazenil for benzodiazepines.
33A patient takes a large paracetamol overdose. Which antidote, how does it work, and why does timing matter? (Ch 79)
N-acetylcysteine (NAC) — it replenishes hepatic glutathione, which neutralises the toxic metabolite NAPQI formed when the normal conjugation pathways saturate. It is most effective when given early (within ~8 hours), but is still given late in significant ingestions; the treatment nomogram guides the decision.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Activated charcoal helps only if given early after ingestion; it does not replace NAC.
34Which poisonings are effectively treated with haemodialysis, and what physicochemical properties allow it? (Ch 79)
Dialysable toxins are small, water-soluble, low protein-bound, and have a low volume of distribution: salicylate, lithium, methanol, ethylene glycol, valproate, metformin, theophylline. A large volume of distribution or high protein binding (e.g. digoxin, TCAs) makes dialysis ineffective because most drug sits in tissue, not plasma.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Urinary alkalinisation (bicarbonate) enhances renal elimination of weak acids such as salicylate and phenobarbital via ion trapping.
35Which common poisons are NOT adsorbed by activated charcoal? (Ch 79)
Activated charcoal does not bind metals, alcohols, hydrocarbons, caustics, lithium or iron. It is most useful given early for many drug overdoses; multiple-dose charcoal additionally interrupts enterohepatic recycling (theophylline, carbamazepine, dapsone, phenobarbital, quinine).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Charcoal is contraindicated/ineffective for caustic ingestion and where the airway is unprotected in a drowsy patient (aspiration risk).
36Whose frog-heart experiment proved chemical neurotransmission, and what was "Vagusstoff"? (Ch 80)
Otto Loewi (with Henry Dale, Nobel 1936). Stimulating the vagus of one frog heart released a substance ("Vagusstoff") that slowed a second heart when the perfusate was transferred — proving nerves signal chemically. Vagusstoff was later identified as acetylcholine, founding autonomic pharmacology.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Dale's classification of cholinergic vs adrenergic transmission underlies the entire ANS drug map.
37Which two landmark drugs did James Black design, and why is his work called "rational drug design"? (Ch 80)
Propranolol (the first β-adrenergic blocker) and cimetidine (the first H₂-receptor antagonist). Rather than screening by chance, Black started from a defined receptor and engineered molecules to block it — the birth of rational, receptor-based drug design (Nobel 1988).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Both drugs founded whole therapeutic classes (β-blockers in cardiology; H₂-blockers in peptic ulcer disease).
38Who discovered cyclic AMP, who discovered G-proteins, and why do these matter for pharmacology? (Ch 80)
Earl Sutherland discovered cyclic AMP as a second messenger (Nobel 1971); Alfred Gilman and Martin Rodbell discovered G-proteins (Nobel 1994). Together they explained the intracellular machinery that receptors switch on — the basis of GPCR pharmacology, the largest single class of drug targets.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Don't swap them — cAMP = Sutherland; G-proteins = Gilman & Rodbell.
39Who developed the radioimmunoassay, and why was it transformative? (Ch 80)
Rosalyn Yalow (Nobel 1977, with the work done alongside Solomon Berson, who had died). The radioimmunoassay (RIA) used antibody binding with a radiolabelled tracer to measure hormones, drugs and other molecules at extremely low concentrations — revolutionising endocrinology and therapeutic drug monitoring.
💎 Clinical Pearl: RIA is the conceptual ancestor of ELISA and modern immunoassays.
40Which discovery by Allison and Honjo reshaped oncology, and name the "father of Indian pharmacology". (Ch 80)
James Allison and Tasuku Honjo discovered immune checkpoint inhibition (CTLA-4 and PD-1 respectively; Nobel 2018) → checkpoint-inhibitor drugs (ipilimumab, nivolumab, pembrolizumab) that release the immune system against cancer. The "father of Indian pharmacology" is Colonel Sir Ram Nath Chopra.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Checkpoint inhibitors cause characteristic immune-related adverse events (colitis, thyroiditis, pneumonitis, hepatitis).
📝 Quiz — Chapter 74 Ocular Pharmacology (15 Questions)
1. Which outflow pathway do prostaglandin analogues primarily enhance, and what receptor mediates this?
Uveoscleral outflow (fluid flows through ciliary muscle into suprachoroidal space). Mediated by FP receptors (PGF₂α receptor), linked to Gq/11-PLC-IP3-Ca²⁺ pathway. This is the target exploited because it bypasses the trabecular meshwork.
2. Why is betaxolol less efficacious than timolol for glaucoma, yet preferred in asthma?
Ocular β receptors (ciliary epithelium/blood vessels) are predominantly β₂ subtype (75–90%). Betaxolol is β₁-selective → less blockade of the target receptors → less IOP reduction. However, because it doesn't block pulmonary β₂ receptors, it is safer in asthma.
3. A patient with hazel eyes on latanoprost notes one iris is now brown. Is this reversible?
No — it is permanent. Latanoprost (via FP receptor stimulation) increases melanin production in iris stromal melanocytes. This is a structural change, not a vascular or edematous one. Patients should be warned before starting therapy.
4. Cocaine eye drops fail to dilate a patient's left pupil (right dilates normally). What does this confirm and what drug would localise the lesion?
Confirms left Horner syndrome (sympathetic denervation — no NE to block reuptake, so cocaine can't dilate). Use hydroxyamphetamine 1%: if it dilates, the postganglionic neuron is intact (1st/2nd order Horner); if it fails to dilate, the 3rd-order (postganglionic) neuron is damaged.
5. What is the mechanism by which timolol eye drops can cause systemic bradycardia?
Drainage via nasolacrimal duct → nasal mucosa (highly vascular) → absorption bypasses hepatic first-pass metabolism → systemically bioavailable timolol blocks cardiac β₁ receptors → bradycardia. Nasolacrimal occlusion after instillation reduces systemic absorption.
6. What corneal layer structure makes an amphipathic drug ideal for transcorneal penetration?
The cornea is a "fat-water-fat" trilaminar structure: hydrophobic epithelium (barrier for hydrophilic drugs) → hydrophilic stroma (barrier for lipophilic drugs) → hydrophobic endothelium. An amphipathic molecule with both lipophilic and hydrophilic properties traverses all three layers. Drug penetration is approximately linearly related to tear film concentration.
7. Echothiophate is an irreversible AChE inhibitor used for glaucoma. Name two serious adverse effects unique to its long-term use.
Posterior subcapsular cataracts (chronic ciliary spasm/zonular tension alters lens metabolism) and iris cysts. Also: retinal detachment (risk), punctal stenosis. Systemic toxicity risk exists because organophosphates can be absorbed. Often used with mydriatic to reduce iris cysts.
8. Aflibercept differs structurally from bevacizumab. How, and what is the clinical significance?
Aflibercept is a recombinant fusion protein (decoy receptor: portions of human VEGFR-1 and VEGFR-2 fused to IgG1 Fc). It binds VEGF-A, VEGF-B, AND placental growth factor (PlGF) — broader coverage than bevacizumab (anti-VEGF-A only mAb). Given every 2 months after loading. CATT trial showed similar visual acuity outcomes between aflibercept, ranibizumab, and bevacizumab.
9. A patient on hydroxychloroquine for lupus asks about eye side effects. What is the mechanism, what does it look like, and when does it appear?
Chloroquine/HCQ accumulates in retinal pigment epithelium (melanin binding) → toxic maculopathy called "bull's-eye maculopathy" (ring of hyperpigmentation around fovea) → central visual acuity loss. Typically requires 5–7 years at therapeutic doses (risk appears after ~6 years with normal dosing of ≤5 mg/kg/day). Annual ophthalmology screening after 5 years. Stopping does NOT reverse damage but prevents further progression.
10. What photoreceptor types exist and what visual function does each serve? What molecule is the chromophore for both?
Rods → dim light vision (low intensity); Cones (3 types: red/green/blue) → high-intensity light, colour vision. Both use 11-cis-retinal as chromophore (bound to opsin proteins — rhodopsin in rods). Photon isomerises 11-cis → all-trans → activates transducin → PDE6 → ↓ cGMP → CNG channels close → hyperpolarisation.
11. Why does PDE5 inhibitor use (sildenafil) sometimes cause visual disturbance?
PDE5 inhibitors have mild cross-inhibition of PDE6 (controls cGMP levels in photoreceptors). Inhibiting PDE6 → elevated retinal cGMP → altered photoreceptor signalling → transient bluish haze or light sensitivity (altered colour discrimination). This is why pilots are warned against sildenafil use before flying.
12. What is the preferred route for ocular antifungal therapy and why is treatment prolonged?
Topical natamycin (5% suspension, only commercially available topical antifungal) is first-line for fungal keratitis. Treatment is prolonged because: (1) poor ocular penetration of antifungal drugs through intact/scarred cornea; (2) fungi are intrinsically more resistant than bacteria. Often supplemented with systemic antifungals (e.g. voriconazole) for deep infections.
13. What distinguishes open-angle glaucoma from angle-closure glaucoma anatomically and pharmacologically?
Open-angle: Trabecular meshwork is accessible but dysfunctional (unknown mechanism); 80–95% of cases. Treated with drugs ↓ production or ↑ outflow. Angle-closure: Iris physically occludes the trabecular meshwork (pupillary block). Pharmacological dilation (anticholinergics, sympathomimetics, antihistamines) can precipitate acute attack in susceptible eyes. Definitive treatment = laser iridotomy (creates alternate channel).
14. Rho kinase inhibitors have a unique and distinctive side effect on the cornea. What is it and what is the underlying reason?
Cornea verticillata: gold-brown pigment deposits in the corneal epithelium in a whorl-like pattern. The underlying mechanism is incompletely understood but thought to relate to phospholipidosis or altered lysosomal function in corneal epithelial cells. Also causes subconjunctival hemorrhage (vasodilatory effect). Compare with amiodarone (cardiac drug) which also causes cornea verticillata — a classic shared adverse effect.
15. A neonate is given brimonidine eye drops. What systemic emergency may occur and why?
CNS depression and apnea. Brimonidine is lipophilic (penetrates blood-brain barrier) and a selective α₂ agonist. Nasolacrimal absorption bypasses first-pass → systemically active drug reaches immature CNS. α₂ receptors in brainstem → central hypnotic/apnoeic effect. Brimonidine is absolutely contraindicated in children under 2 years of age for this reason.

Retinoids: Nuclear Receptor Logic

Retinoids work by binding nuclear receptors (RARs = retinoic acid receptors; RXRs = retinoid X receptors) which heterodimerize and bind specific DNA sequences (RAREs = retinoic acid response elements) to activate transcription. The key therapeutic split:

  • RAR agonists (tretinoin, adapalene, tazarotene, acitretin) → primarily affect differentiation and proliferation → used for acne, psoriasis, photoaging.
  • RXR agonists (bexarotene, alitretinoin) → primarily induce apoptosis of malignant cells → used for CTCL, Kaposi sarcoma.

Isotretinoin's uniqueness: It has NO clear receptor affinity — likely works indirectly via metabolic conversion to active retinoids within skin. Its effect on sebaceous glands (marked sebum reduction, normalising follicular keratinisation, reducing C. acnes) is unmatched, explaining its curative potential for severe acne. Single course achieves remission in ~70% of patients; ~30% relapse within 3 years.

Retinoids are like software updates for keratinocytes — they reprogram the default "abnormal differentiation and proliferation" seen in acne and psoriasis back to normal. RAR agonists update the "growth program"; RXR agonists install the "self-destruct protocol" in malignant cells.

Acitretin's 3-Year Contraception Requirement — The Alcohol Trap

Acitretin has a t½ of ~50 hours — normally fine. BUT: in the presence of alcohol, acitretin undergoes esterification in vivo to form etretinate, which is stored in adipose tissue and has a t½ of 80–160 days. Since etretinate is also highly teratogenic, the teratogenicity risk persists long after stopping acitretin if the patient consumed alcohol during therapy. Hence the 3-year rule. (Alcohol here includes mouthwash, cough syrups — any source.)

Why Facial Steroids Cause Perioral Dermatitis and Rosacea

Fluorinated topical steroids (e.g. fluocinonide, betamethasone valerate) suppress the skin's normal immune surveillance. On the thin facial skin (high absorption) with sebaceous follicles, prolonged use disrupts the follicular microenvironment → dysbiosis, inflammatory follicular papules around the mouth (perioral dermatitis) and telangiectasia/flushing (rosacea-like). The damage worsens on withdrawal — a "rebound" phenomenon. Use only non-fluorinated agents (hydrocortisone, desonide) on the face, if at all.

IL-17 vs. IL-23 Biologics — Why Candida Risk Differs

IL-17 (secreted by Th17 cells) directly stimulates neutrophil migration and promotes epithelial defense against Candida. Blocking IL-17 directly (secukinumab, ixekizumab, bimekizumab, brodalumab) removes this mucocutaneous defense → ↑ candidal infections. IL-23 is upstream of IL-17 (drives Th17 differentiation), but blocking IL-23 does not completely abolish existing Th17 activity — residual IL-17 maintains partial mucosal defense. Hence IL-23 inhibitors (guselkumab, risankizumab, tildrakizumab) have fewer Candida infections.

Brodalumab REMS — Suicidal Ideation

Brodalumab blocks IL-17RA (the receptor for IL-17A, C, D, E, and F). Clinical trials noted a signal of suicidal ideation and completed suicides. The proposed mechanism relates to IL-17's role in neuroinflammation — patients with psoriasis have high baseline psychiatric comorbidity (depression). Trials have NOT established causality (no difference from comparators in analysis), but the FDA issued a boxed warning and requires REMS certification. This is a regulatory/risk management decision, not confirmed pharmacological causation.

PUVA (psoralen + UVA) and isotretinoin should NOT be combined — additive photosensitivity risk. Similarly, tetracyclines + systemic retinoids → risk of pseudotumor cerebri (both independently cause this). Never combine.
Calcipotriene (topical vitamin D analogue for psoriasis) is inactivated by concurrent use of ACIDIC topical agents (salicylic acid, lactic acid). Don't prescribe them together. Also: maximum 100 g/week limit → risk of hypercalcemia/hypercalciuria if exceeded.
Dupilumab's most common adverse effect is CONJUNCTIVITIS — this is paradoxical for an immunosuppressant but is thought to relate to IL-4/IL-13 blocking disrupting conjunctival goblet cell function and tear film. Tralokinumab may cause less conjunctivitis. Expect this in MCQs.

📝 Quiz — Chapter 75 Dermatological Pharmacology (15 Questions)
1. What is the rate-limiting step for percutaneous drug absorption and which body site has the thinnest stratum corneum?
The stratum corneum is the rate-limiting step. Thinnest at eyelid and scrotum (hence highest absorption). Thickest at palms and soles. Thin SC = higher absorption = higher systemic exposure = ↑ HPA suppression risk with potent steroids.
2. What are the two nuclear receptor families that retinoids activate, and which disorders does each treat?
RARs (retinoic acid receptors): mediate differentiation/proliferation effects → acne, psoriasis, photoaging. RXRs (retinoid X receptors): mediate apoptosis in malignant cells → CTCL (bexarotene), Kaposi sarcoma (alitretinoin). Isotretinoin has no clear receptor affinity.
3. Why does acitretin require 3 years of contraception after stopping, but isotretinoin only 1 month?
Acitretin + alcohol → esterification in vivo → etretinate (stored in adipose tissue, t½ 80–160 days). Etretinate is teratogenic and persists long after acitretin is stopped. Isotretinoin has a t½ of 10–20 hours and no such long-acting metabolite is formed.
4. Name the first-line biologic for moderate-to-severe psoriasis according to most guidelines, and explain its mechanism.
Secukinumab or Ixekizumab (IL-17A mAbs) or IL-23 inhibitors (guselkumab, risankizumab) are now first-line in many guidelines due to high efficacy. PG analogues in ophthalmology correspond here — both displaced older agents by superior efficacy. Secukinumab is human IgG1κ → binds free IL-17A → prevents keratinocyte stimulation → reduces hyperproliferation, barrier dysfunction, and neutrophil recruitment.
5. What is the signature adverse effect of dupilumab and what is its proposed mechanism?
Conjunctivitis (most frequent adverse effect). Proposed mechanism: IL-4 and IL-13 signalling normally maintains conjunctival goblet cell function and tear film mucins. Blocking IL-4Rα with dupilumab disrupts this → goblet cell dysfunction → reduced tear film → conjunctival inflammation. Tralokinumab (IL-13 selective) appears to cause less conjunctivitis.
6. Which topical retinoid is the most irritating and which is the least irritating?
Most irritating: Tazarotene (++++ irritancy). Least irritating: Adapalene (+ irritancy) and Trifarotene (+). Tretinoin is intermediate (++). Irritancy correlates with receptor affinity — tazarotene is pan-RAR, highly potent. This determines patient tolerability and compliance.
7. Why are fluorinated topical steroids contraindicated on the face?
Face has thin, sebaceous skin with high percutaneous absorption. Fluorinated steroids at this site cause: (1) perioral dermatitis (inflammatory follicular eruption), (2) rosacea-like eruptions, (3) skin atrophy, telangiectasia. Non-fluorinated agents (hydrocortisone, desonide, alclometasone) are preferred for facial use.
8. What boxed warning is unique to brodalumab (not shared by other IL-17/IL-23 biologics) and why was it added?
Suicidal ideation and behaviour. A signal was detected in phase III trials. Mechanistically: IL-17RA is also expressed in the CNS; blocking all IL-17 isoforms (A, C, D, E, F) may have neuroinflammatory effects. Note: causality has NOT been established — the signal may reflect high baseline depression prevalence in psoriasis. REMS program required before dispensing.
9. Describe the mechanism of action of calcipotriene (a vitamin D analogue) for psoriasis.
Calcipotriene binds the vitamin D receptor (VDR). The drug-VDR complex associates with RXR-α and binds vitamin D response elements in DNA → increases expression of genes modulating epidermal differentiation and suppressing inflammation → reduces keratinocyte proliferation and normalises differentiation in psoriatic plaques. Limit: 100 g/week → hypercalcemia/hypercalciuria risk if exceeded.
10. What is the mechanism of action of crisaborole for atopic dermatitis, and what is its pharmacokinetic advantage?
Crisaborole is a PDE4B inhibitor (topical) — inhibits phosphodiesterase 4B → ↑ intracellular cAMP → ↓ inflammatory cytokines (TNFα, IFNγ, IL-17, IL-23). Its pharmacokinetic advantage: low molecular weight (251 Da, due to boron-containing benzoxaborole structure) → effective skin penetration despite topical application. This allows effective PDE4 inhibition in the epidermis without significant systemic exposure.
11. Compare the mechanism of isotretinoin vs. topical tretinoin for acne.
Both are retinoids but differ markedly: Tretinoin (topical) → RAR activation in the epidermis → normalises follicular keratinisation, reduces comedones, anti-inflammatory. Isotretinoin (systemic) → no clear receptor affinity; markedly suppresses sebaceous gland activity (↓ gland size, ↓ sebum production), normalises follicular keratinisation, reduces C. acnes via hostile follicular environment. Isotretinoin is curative (70%) while tretinoin is suppressive (disease returns on stopping).
12. A patient using PUVA (psoralen + UVA) asks about combining with isotretinoin for psoriasis. What is your advice?
Contraindicated. Both psoralens and retinoids cause photosensitivity (retinoids induce epidermal thinning and reduce the protective keratinised layer). The combination markedly increases the risk of severe phototoxic reactions and sunburn. Additionally, systemic retinoids + tetracyclines (which may be prescribed concurrently for acne) risk pseudotumor cerebri — another combination to avoid.
13. What distinguishes etanercept from other anti-TNFα biologics structurally and functionally?
Etanercept is a soluble recombinant TNF receptor fusion protein (p75 TNF receptor Fc IgG1 fusion) — NOT a monoclonal antibody. It binds both TNFα AND TNFβ. Unlike infliximab/adalimumab/certolizumab (IgG1 mAbs), etanercept lacks complement-dependent cytotoxicity activity because it doesn't bind membrane-bound TNFα as effectively. This explains its lower efficacy in granulomatous conditions (like Crohn's disease) vs. monoclonal antibody TNFα inhibitors, and potentially lower infection risk.
14. Calcipotriene is inactivated by a commonly co-prescribed topical agent. Name it and explain the interaction.
Salicylic acid and lactic acid (acidic topical agents). Calcipotriene is chemically unstable in acidic conditions → inactivated. Since salicylic acid is commonly prescribed for its keratolytic properties in psoriasis, this is a clinically important incompatibility. Space application times or choose different keratolytic.
15. Name the JAK inhibitor approved for alopecia areata and one for atopic dermatitis; what class-wide boxed warning do all oral JAK inhibitors share?
Baricitinib (JAK1/2) is FDA-approved for severe alopecia areata and moderate-severe rheumatoid arthritis. Abrocitinib (JAK1) and upadacitinib (JAK1) are approved for atopic dermatitis. Class-wide boxed warning: ↑ risk of serious infections, malignancy, ↑ all-cause mortality (including cardiovascular death), MI, stroke, and thrombosis (DVT/PE). Based on ORAL-SURVEILLANCE study comparing tofacitinib vs. anti-TNF in RA patients ≥50 years with CV risk factors.

Lead Toxicology — No Safe Level for Children:

  • Lead mimics divalent metals (Ca²⁺, Zn²⁺) → disrupts Ca²⁺ transporters, PKC, calmodulin, zinc-finger proteins (including DNA repair proteins).

Chelation: Why "More Potent" Doesn't Mean Better

CaNa₂EDTA is highly effective at chelating lead — it forms a stable complex and promotes urinary excretion. But it has a critical flaw: it mobilises lead from soft tissues (bone, soft organs) and, because it can redistribute metal to the brain, worsen neurotoxicity. It also doesn't penetrate cells (charged molecule at pH 7.4).

Succimer (DMSA) is hydrophilic, also charged, also doesn't cross cell membranes — but it is highly selective for heavy metals and does NOT effectively chelate essential metals (Zn, Cu, Fe). This means: (1) less redistribution to brain, (2) fewer essential metal deficiencies, (3) oral bioavailability. For a child with BLL 45–70 μg/dL who is ambulatory, succimer wins hands-down for outpatient management.

Think of chelators like different fishing nets: EDTA is a large trawl net — catches lots of fish (metals) including the ones you want to keep (Ca, Zn), and stirs up everything in the water including sending some fish (lead) into protected areas (brain). Succimer is a selective line with lead-specific bait — catches mostly what you want, doesn't disturb the rest, and doesn't stir up the pond bed.

Dimercaprol (BAL) Contraindications — the Logic

Dimercaprol works by two sulfhydryl groups forming a stable ring with metals. For iron, cadmium, and selenium: the dimercaprol-metal complex is actually MORE toxic than the free metal — it is less stable, redox-reactive, and concentrates in the kidney. For cadmium specifically, chelation therapy with any agent increases nephrotoxicity — hence "supportive care only" for cadmium. Dimercaprol is also oil-based (peanut oil) — contraindicated in peanut allergy. Given IM only — painful sterile abscesses are common.

Methyl Mercury Fetal Toxicity — The Molecular Mimicry Trap

MeHg⁺ forms complexes with cysteine that structurally resemble methionine → recognised by methionine transporters → actively transported across membranes including placenta and blood-brain barrier. In the fetal brain, microtubules are extremely sensitive to mercury (disrupts tubulin polymerisation). Fetal developing neurons (migrating, forming synapses) are in a state of maximum vulnerability. The mother has mature adult CNS with completed myelination and synaptic stabilisation → far more tolerant. Hence, a mother can consume MeHg-contaminated fish at levels causing only mild symptoms while her fetus develops Minamata disease (profound CNS damage, intellectual disability, cerebral palsy).

Aflatoxin B₁ + Hepatitis B: Synergy Mechanism

Three synergistic pathways:

  • HBx protein (encoded by HBV X gene) binds and inhibits p53 → impairs nucleotide excision repair of aflatoxin-induced N7-guanine adducts → mutations propagate.
  • HBV upregulates CYP3A4 → more activation of aflatoxin B₁ to its carcinogenic 8,9-epoxide; simultaneously decreases GSH S-transferase → less detoxification.
  • HBV causes hepatocyte death → compensatory proliferation → any aflatoxin-induced mutations that survive are rapidly expanded into clones. This is non-genotoxic promotion.

Result: Aflatoxin alone = 3.4× ↑ HCC risk; HBV alone = 7.3× ↑ risk; Both together = 59× ↑ risk. Vaccination against HBV is therefore a chemoprevention strategy against aflatoxin-mediated HCC.

Penicillamine is cross-reactive with penicillin — up to 1/3 of patients develop allergic reactions (urticaria, maculopapular rash). More importantly: it causes nephrotoxicity (membranous glomerulonephritis → nephrotic syndrome), leukopenia, aplastic anaemia, and can induce myasthenia gravis with long-term use. Despite its name, it is NOT an antibiotic. Contraindicated in pregnancy (without Wilson disease — risk-benefit favours therapy in Wilson disease).

Additional factors of 10 can be added for database uncertainties. This is called the "precautionary principle" — assumes worst-case for each uncertainty. An MCQ may ask you to calculate RfD given NOAEL — divide by 100 in the standard case.
Deferoxamine has extraordinarily high affinity for ferric iron (Fe³⁺, Ka = 10³¹) vs calcium (Ka = 10²) — this selectivity is its major advantage. BUT: it does NOT remove iron from haemoglobin, myoglobin, or cytochromes (the iron in these is protein-bound in a specific coordination environment). It chelates free and storage iron (ferritin, haemosiderin). Also: rapid IV infusion → hypotension. Chronic high-dose → ocular/auditory neurotoxicity.

📝 Quiz — Chapter 76 Environmental Toxicology (15 Questions)
1. What is the Reference Dose (RfD) and how is it calculated from animal data?
RfD = NOAEL / 100 (standard case). NOAEL = highest dose with no statistically significant adverse effect. Divided by 10 for interspecies variability (animal→human) and 10 for interindividual human variability. Additional uncertainty factors (10 each) may be added for database limitations. This reflects the "precautionary principle" — assumes worst-case for each unknown.
2. A child has a blood lead level of 55 μg/dL. What treatment is indicated and what route?
Oral succimer (DMSA) — indicated for children with BLL 45–70 μg/dL as outpatient therapy. Succimer is orally bioavailable, hydrophilic (does not enter CNS → no lead redistribution to brain), selective for heavy metals. BLL >70 μg/dL = medical emergency → IV CaNa₂EDTA ± IM dimercaprol (inpatient).
3. Why is chelation therapy contraindicated for cadmium poisoning?
The dimercaprol-cadmium complex (and complexes with other chelators) is more toxic than free cadmium itself, particularly to the kidneys. Cadmium's primary target is the renal tubule — chelation redistributes cadmium to the kidney and increases nephrotoxicity. No chelator has demonstrated clinical benefit for cadmium; treatment is supportive only.
4. What is the mechanism of aflatoxin B₁ carcinogenesis and why does co-infection with HBV increase risk 59-fold?
AFB₁ → CYP1A2/3A4 → 8,9-epoxide → N7-guanine adduct → G→T transversion → p53 codon 249 mutation (Arg→Cys) → uncontrolled hepatocyte proliferation. HBV synergy via: (1) HBx protein inhibits p53 → impairs NER of AFB₁ adducts; (2) HBV upregulates CYP3A4 → more AFB₁ activation; (3) HBV causes hepatocyte death → compensatory proliferation → initiated cells expand. Individual risks: AFB₁ = 3.4×, HBV = 7.3×, combined = 59× (synergistic, not additive).
5. What enzyme is most sensitive to lead inhibition in heme biosynthesis and what are the downstream effects?
Ferrochelatase (which inserts Fe²⁺ into protoporphyrin IX to form haem) and ALAD (δ-aminolevulinate dehydratase, very sensitive at BLL <10 μg/dL). Effects: ↑ erythrocyte protoporphyrin IX (zinc protoporphyrin), ↑ urinary ALA, hypochromic microcytic anaemia morphologically similar to iron deficiency.
6. Why should sodium EDTA (Na₂EDTA) NOT be used instead of calcium EDTA (CaNa₂EDTA) for lead poisoning?
Na₂EDTA lacks the calcium — it will chelate endogenous Ca²⁺ from the blood → hypocalcaemic tetany if administered rapidly IV. CaNa₂EDTA exchanges its calcium for metals with higher EDTA affinity (Pb > Ca) without causing hypocalcaemia — the calcium released is physiologically inconsequential at therapeutic doses.
7. Methyl mercury causes severe fetal brain damage even in asymptomatic mothers. Explain the molecular and physiological basis.
MeHg⁺-cysteine complex mimics methionine → transported across placenta and blood-brain barrier via amino acid transporters (molecular mimicry). Fetal brain is maximally vulnerable (neurons actively migrating, forming synapses — microtubule-dependent processes). Mercury disrupts tubulin polymerisation → impairs neuronal migration. Adult CNS is mature and far more tolerant. Hence, maternal intake causes fetal Minamata disease (severe CNS damage, cerebral palsy, ID) with only mild maternal symptoms.
8. What distinguishes genotoxic from non-genotoxic carcinogens? Give one example of each.
Genotoxic: directly damage DNA (adducts, strand breaks, oxidation via ROS). Example: benzo[a]pyrene (tobacco) → diol epoxide → DNA adducts. The Ames test detects genotoxins. Non-genotoxic: increase cancer incidence without directly damaging DNA; act via receptor-mediated proliferation (phorbol esters → PKC), inflammation (asbestos), compensatory regeneration (ethanol → hepatotoxicity), or epigenetic silencing (arsenic → DNA methylation changes). Lead is a classic non-genotoxic carcinogen (inhibits zinc-finger DNA repair proteins).
9. A patient with Wilson disease is allergic to penicillin. Can they still take penicillamine?
Penicillamine cross-reacts with penicillin → up to 1/3 of patients develop allergic reactions (urticaria, maculopapular rash, fever). Penicillin allergy is therefore a relative contraindication. The preferred alternative is trientine (triethylenetetramine dihydrochloride) — orally effective copper chelator with a safer profile, though less potent than penicillamine.
10. What is the main route of arsenic excretion and how does biotransformation affect its toxicity?
Arsenic is excreted primarily in urine (t½ ~1–3 days — faster than most toxic metals). Biotransformation: inorganic arsenate (As⁵⁺) → arsenite (As³⁺) → methylated forms (MMA, DMA) via AS3MT enzyme using SAM as methyl donor. Previously thought to be detoxification; now known that trivalent methylated intermediates (MMA³⁺) are actually MORE toxic than inorganic arsenite (higher sulfhydryl affinity). So methylation is a bioactivation pathway, not purely detoxification.
11. Name three IARC Group 1 carcinogens with their target organs and mechanisms.
(1) Aflatoxin B₁ → liver (HCC) → metabolic activation (CYP→8,9-epoxide) → N7-guanine adducts → p53 mutation. (2) Arsenic (inorganic) → liver, bladder, lung, skin → inhibition of DNA repair, epigenetic changes, oxidative stress. (3) Hexavalent chromium (Cr⁶⁺) → lung → absorbed via anion transporters → reduced intracellularly to Cr³⁺ → DNA adducts, oxidative DNA damage, inflammation. Bonus: benzene → leukaemia; asbestos → mesothelioma (non-genotoxic via inflammation).
12. What is BPA's mechanism of action as an endocrine disruptor and which phase of development is most critical?
BPA is structurally similar to oestrogen → binds ERα (agonist) and ERβ (antagonist) → acts as a selective oestrogen receptor modulator. At low concentrations: interferes with non-genomic ER signalling (extranuclear complexes, Ca²⁺ release). Also metabolised to BPA-glucuronide (inactive) by CYP2C subfamily. Most critical windows: embryonic development, gestational stages, neonatal period, peripubertal period. Developmental exposure (in utero) programs adult metabolic and reproductive outcomes (decreased insulin secretion in offspring of BPA-exposed dams).
13. Deferoxamine is described as having "extraordinarily high" affinity for iron. Quantify this and explain why it doesn't remove iron from haemoglobin.
Deferoxamine Ka for Fe³⁺ = 10³¹ M⁻¹ vs. Ca²⁺ = 10² M⁻¹ — approximately 10²⁹ greater selectivity for iron. It does NOT remove iron from haemoglobin or cytochromes because these proteins coordinate iron within a specific protein tertiary structure (porphyrin + protein coordination) that is thermodynamically and kinetically stable — haem iron is not "free" iron accessible to the chelator. Deferoxamine chelates only iron in its free, transferrin, ferritin, and haemosiderin forms.
14. Describe the immunotoxic mechanism of TCDD and its primary cellular target.
TCDD (2,3,7,8-tetrachlorodibenzo-p-dioxin) binds the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) with extremely high affinity and is resistant to CYP-mediated metabolism → sustained AhR activation (unlike other AhR ligands). In T cells: promotes newly activated CD4⁺ T cells → regulatory T cell phenotype (immunosuppression). Also inhibits B cell antibody production, induces tolerogenic dendritic cells, alters CD8⁺ antiviral responses. Most consistent human finding: ↓ CD4⁺ T helper cell subsets. TCDD has human half-life of 7–11 years (extremely persistent).
15. A chemopreventive strategy uses chlorophyllin for aflatoxin-exposed populations. What is its mechanism?
Chlorophyllin (water-soluble chlorophyll salts) acts as an "interceptor molecule" — it tightly binds aflatoxin B₁ in the GI tract, forming a complex that is not absorbed (reduces bioavailability of aflatoxin). Additionally: inhibits CYP activity → less metabolic activation; acts as antioxidant (↓ ROS). Phase II clinical trial: 100 mg chlorophyllin with each meal → significant ↓ in urinary aflatoxin-N7-guanine adducts (direct measure of DNA damage reduced). This is a primary prevention strategy for HCC in aflatoxin-endemic regions.

Solubility is destiny

Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) are absorbed with dietary fat and stored in liver and adipose tissue. Two consequences follow: they become deficient whenever fat absorption fails (cholestasis, coeliac disease, cystic fibrosis, orlistat), and because they are stored rather than excreted, they can accumulate to toxic levels. Water-soluble vitamins (B-complex, C) are not stored to any great extent — excess is excreted in urine — so the dominant clinical issue is deficiency, and toxicity is rare.

Incretins and appetite

GLP-1 is a gut hormone released after eating. GLP-1 receptor agonists exploit three of its actions: glucose-dependent insulin release (so little hypoglycaemia alone), slowed gastric emptying, and central appetite suppression in the hypothalamus. Together these cut calorie intake and produce clinically important weight loss — which is why drugs first developed for type 2 diabetes became landmark obesity treatments. Tirzepatide adds GIP agonism for a larger effect.

Think of the vitamins like two kinds of household supplies. Fat-soluble vitamins are the canned goods in the pantry — they keep for months (storage), so you rarely run out, but you can over-stock to the point of harm. Water-soluble vitamins are fresh milk — they don't keep, the excess is poured away daily, so the real danger is simply running out.
Never give folic acid alone to correct a megaloblastic anaemia before excluding B12 deficiency — folate will fix the anaemia but the untreated B12 deficiency can progress to irreversible subacute combined degeneration of the cord. Check both. And in a malnourished or alcoholic patient, give thiamine before glucose to avoid precipitating Wernicke encephalopathy.
GLP-1 and GIP/GLP-1 agonists are contraindicated where there is a personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN2 (rodent C-cell tumour signal), and carry a risk of pancreatitis and significant GI effects. Orlistat causes steatorrhoea and depletes fat-soluble vitamins — supplement A, D, E, K, taken apart from the drug.

One question unlocks every special population: which ADME step changed?

A neonate has immature metabolising enzymes and immature kidneys, a higher proportion of body water and less plasma protein — so drugs cleared by conjugation (chloramphenicol) accumulate, and highly protein-bound drugs displace bilirubin. An older adult has declining renal and hepatic clearance, more body fat (so lipophilic drugs like diazepam have a larger volume of distribution and linger), and heightened sensitivity — hence "start low, go slow" and the Beers list of drugs to avoid. In pregnancy, increased volume of distribution, GFR and hepatic metabolism can lower some drug levels, while placental transfer exposes the fetus to teratogens.

Organ impairment: match the failing organ to the clearance route

If the liver is failing (graded by Child-Pugh), drugs it metabolises accumulate and first-pass falls, so hepatically-cleared and sedating drugs are risky. If the kidney is failing (graded by eGFR/CrCl), renally-excreted drugs accumulate, so you cut the dose or lengthen the interval and avoid nephrotoxins. The unifying skill is to identify a drug's main elimination route and match it to the impaired organ.

Think of the body as a factory with an intake dock, storage, a processing plant (liver) and a waste-disposal unit (kidney). Each special population is a factory with one department running differently — a trainee crew (neonate, slow processing), an ageing plant (elderly, everything slower), or a plant temporarily rerouting resources (pregnancy). Fix the dose by asking which department is off-spec, not by using the standard settings.
Classic pregnancy teratogens to memorise: warfarin (nasal/skeletal defects), ACE inhibitors/ARBs (renal dysgenesis, oligohydramnios — 2nd/3rd trimester), valproate (neural tube defects, neurodevelopmental harm — highest of any AED), isotretinoin (severe craniofacial/cardiac/CNS), tetracyclines (teeth/bone), methotrexate and thalidomide (phocomelia). Also remember metformin's lactic-acidosis risk when eGFR falls, and hold it around iodinated contrast.
Pharmacogenomics can convert a standard dose into a toxic one: TPMT deficiency → azathioprine/6-MP myelosuppression; CYP2D6 ultra-rapid metabolisers → codeine toxicity; HLA-B*57:01 → abacavir hypersensitivity; HLA-B*15:02 → carbamazepine SJS. Genotype where indicated before prescribing.

Supportive care first — antidotes are the exception

The single most important message in clinical toxicology is that most poisoned patients recover on good supportive care alone — airway, breathing, circulation, glucose, temperature, and treatment of seizures. Only a minority of poisons have a specific antidote. So the algorithm always starts with resuscitation, then identification via the toxidrome (the pattern of vitals, pupils, skin and secretions), plus targeted tests (anion/osmolar gaps, ECG, and levels where a threshold exists — paracetamol, salicylate, lithium, digoxin, iron, carboxyhaemoglobin, methanol/ethylene glycol).

Decontamination and enhanced elimination — when they actually help

Activated charcoal adsorbs many drugs if given early, but it does not bind metals, alcohols, hydrocarbons, caustics, lithium or iron. Enhanced elimination is reserved for specific toxins: urinary alkalinisation for weak acids (salicylate, phenobarbital); multiple-dose charcoal to interrupt enterohepatic recycling (theophylline, carbamazepine, dapsone); and haemodialysis for small, water-soluble, low-protein-bound, low-volume-of-distribution toxins (salicylate, lithium, methanol, ethylene glycol, valproate, metformin, theophylline).

Managing a poisoning is like fighting a house fire. First you get everyone out and stabilise the building (resuscitation) — that saves most lives. Then you work out what's burning (the toxidrome). Only some fires need a special extinguisher (an antidote); for the rest, water and ventilation (supportive care and, occasionally, dialysis) do the job. Reaching for the special extinguisher before securing the building is a classic error.
Naloxone and flumazenil both wear off faster than the drugs they reverse — observe for re-sedation and re-dose or infuse. Give flumazenil cautiously: in a chronic benzodiazepine user or mixed overdose (e.g. with a TCA) it can precipitate seizures. In organophosphate poisoning, titrate atropine to secretion control and give pralidoxime before enzyme ageing.
Aluminium phosphide and cyanide are rapidly lethal mitochondrial poisons with narrow windows — cyanide has antidotes (hydroxocobalamin), but aluminium phosphide has essentially none, so management is aggressive supportive care. Always involve the Poison Information Centre early for risk assessment.

A short history of big ideas

Loewi's frog-heart experiment proved that nerves talk to organs by releasing a chemical ("Vagusstoff", later identified as acetylcholine) — with Dale's receptor work, this founded autonomic pharmacology. James Black then showed you could design a drug against a specific receptor rather than stumble upon it: propranolol for β-receptors and cimetidine for histamine H₂-receptors, two of the most influential drugs ever made. Sutherland's cyclic AMP and the Gilman/Rodbell G-proteins explained the machinery inside the cell that a receptor switches on — the basis of most modern drug targets. Yalow's radioimmunoassay made it possible to measure hormones and drugs at vanishingly small concentrations, and Allison and Honjo's checkpoint discoveries (CTLA-4, PD-1) unleashed the immune system against cancer.

These discoveries are like the invention of the alphabet, then grammar, then the printing press for pharmacology: neurotransmission gave us the letters (chemical signals), receptors and second messengers gave us the grammar (how signals combine), and rational design plus immunotherapy were the printing press — turning understanding into a flood of new medicines.
Common viva confusions: cyclic AMP = Sutherland; G-proteins = Gilman & Rodbell; radioimmunoassay = Yalow (Berson had died, so was not named). James Black won for both the β-blocker and the H₂-antagonist. The "father of Indian pharmacology" is Col. Sir Ram Nath Chopra.
