Clinical Pharmacology
  • Species choice depends on the job: mice/rats for screening, guinea pig for histamine-sensitive ileum work, rabbit for pyrogen/eye tests, frog for rectus abdominis and isolated heart.
  • Speed and predictability rank by route: IV fastest and fully absorbed, then IP, then SC/IM, then oral slowest and most variable.
  • Safe injection volume is capped by the animal's weight and route — exceeding it risks pain, a skewed result, or death.
  • Blood-draw site depends on volume needed and whether the animal must survive (retro-orbital/tail/saphenous for survival; cardiac puncture if terminal).
  • The 3Rs (Replacement, Reduction, Refinement) are enforced in India through CPCSEA oversight and the institution's own IAEC.
  • Mouse (20–30 g): general screening, analgesia tests, MES/PTZ seizure models, LD₅₀ toxicity.
  • Rat (150–250 g): behaviour tests, blood pressure, ulcer/colitis models, uterus & fundus assays.
  • Guinea pig (300–600 g): ileum bioassay, tracheal chain, anaphylaxis, local anaesthetic wheal.
  • Rabbit (1.5–3 kg): pyrogen test, eye experiments, blood pressure.
  • Frog: rectus abdominis, isolated perfused heart, gastrocnemius–sciatic nerve prep.
  • IV (tail vein): ~0.2 mL mouse / ~0.5 mL rat — instant, 100% bioavailable, must be non-irritant.
  • IP: ~1 mL mouse / ~5 mL rat — fast, large volume, but passes through the liver's portal circulation first.
  • SC: ~0.5 mL mouse / ~1–2 mL rat — slow depot release at the scruff of the neck.
  • IM: ~0.05 mL mouse / ~0.1 mL rat — limited by small muscle mass.
  • Oral (gavage): ~0.5 mL mouse / ~5 mL rat — slowest and most variable route.
  • IV puts the drug straight onto the "highway" of systemic circulation — immediate, 100% of dose reaches the blood.
  • IP is a faster "slip-road" but runs past the liver's portal first-pass, so some drug is lost en route.
  • SC and IM act like quiet side-streets, releasing the drug slowly and steadily.
  • Oral dosing takes the longest route, through gut and liver — slowest, least predictable, yet the most common route for real medicines.
  • Replacement: use cell culture, computer models, or simpler organisms instead of animals whenever possible.
  • Reduction: use no more animals than careful statistics genuinely require.
  • Refinement: cut pain and distress through analgesia, careful handling, and humane endpoints.
  • Every protocol needs sign-off from the institution's IAEC, operating under national CPCSEA guidelines.
  • Safety warning: overshooting safe injection volume can both harm the animal and distort the data; animals can also carry zoonotic infections, so gloving and hygiene matter.
  • Smaller animals have more surface area relative to mass and a faster metabolism, so they need a larger dose per kilogram.
  • Km factor = body weight (kg) ÷ surface area (m²); it's a constant for a given species.
  • HED (mg/kg) = Animal dose (mg/kg) × (Animal Km ÷ Human Km)
  • Allometric scaling: physiological variables rise with body weight raised to a power — roughly 0.75 for metabolic rate, roughly 0.67 for surface area.
  • Paget & Barnes tables give the same conversion using pre-built factors instead of calculating Km directly.
  • Mouse 0.02 kg → Km = 3; Rat 0.15 kg → Km = 6.
  • Guinea pig 0.40 kg → Km = 8; Rabbit 1.8 kg → Km = 12.
  • Monkey 3 kg → Km = 12; Dog 10 kg → Km = 20.
  • Human adult 60 kg → Km = 37; Child 20 kg → Km = 25.
  • A mouse has far more surface area relative to its bulk, so it sheds heat and clears drug much faster than a person.
  • Surface area itself rises roughly as (body weight)^0.67, which is exactly what the Km factor captures.
  • Worked example: 50 mg/kg mouse dose × (3 ÷ 37) = 4.05 mg/kg HED ≈ 243 mg at 60 kg; ÷10 safety factor ≈ 24 mg first-in-human dose.
  • Warning: carrying a dose across species by plain mg/kg overdoses the human — always route through Km and then apply a safety factor.
  • Core principle: the unknown is measured against a standard under matched conditions, reading out the biological response itself.
  • Graded response: a continuous, dose-dependent reading on one tissue (e.g. how much an ileum contracts).
  • Quantal (all-or-none) response: each animal simply responds or doesn't; probit analysis on this data gives ED₅₀/LD₅₀.
  • The four matching methods: matching, bracketing, interpolation, and multiple-point (three-point/four-point) assays.
  • Antagonist bioassay: strength expressed as pA₂, derived from how far the agonist's dose–response curve shifts right (Schild analysis).
  • Matching: adjust the unknown until its response visually matches a known standard dose — quick but crude.
  • Bracketing: find two standard doses that respond just above and just below the unknown.
  • Interpolation: read the unknown's response straight off the standard's log dose–response curve (most common method).
  • Three-point assay: two standard doses plus one unknown, allowing a validity (parallelism) check.
  • Four-point (2+2) assay: two doses each of standard and unknown — most accurate, tests parallelism and gives a potency ratio with statistics.
  • Graded response works like a dimmer switch: the same tissue contracts more as dose climbs, tracing a full dose–response curve.
  • Quantal response works like a light switch across a population: each animal is marked yes/no, and the proportion crossing the threshold at each dose builds a cumulative curve.
  • The ED₅₀ or LD₅₀ emerges from that cumulative quantal curve.
  • Matching simply eyeballs the two responses until they look equal.
  • Bracketing pins the unknown between one dose that's clearly larger and one that's clearly smaller.
  • Interpolation treats the full standard curve as a measuring ruler — but only the linear (~20–80%) middle stretch can be trusted.
  • Multiple-point assays alternate standard and unknown doses; if their log dose–response lines run parallel, the gap between them gives the potency ratio directly — and that parallelism is itself proof the assay is valid.
  • Warning: a bioassay only holds up when standard and unknown act through the same mechanism on the same tissue — non-parallel curves mean the potency figure is meaningless.
  • The organ bath needs the right PSS, correct temperature (37°C mammalian, 32°C rat uterus, room temp for frog), constant aeration, and a fixed contact time.
  • Which PSS to use depends on the tissue — Tyrode, Ringer, Krebs, De Jalon and Locke differ in ions, glucose, and buffer.
  • Every tissue responds to a characteristic agonist through a specific receptor — this is what the bioassay rests on.
  • Magnification = writing-arm length ÷ tissue-arm length
  • Dose cycle: add dose, record peak, wash, then rest before the next dose — this sequence must stay constant for a valid curve.
  • Tyrode — guinea-pig ileum/mammalian gut, glucose + bicarbonate buffer, air or carbogen, 37°C.
  • Krebs(-Henseleit) — most mammalian smooth/cardiac muscle, bicarbonate buffer + glucose, carbogen (95% O₂/5% CO₂), 37°C.
  • De Jalon — rat uterus, low Ca²⁺, needs an oestrogen-primed animal, air, 32°C.
  • Ringer — frog heart/rectus and other amphibian tissue, simple salts, no glucose needed, air, room temperature.
  • Locke — mammalian (rabbit) heart, glucose-containing, air/O₂, 37°C.
  • Guinea-pig ileum responds to histamine (H₁) and acetylcholine (muscarinic M₃).
  • Rat fundus strip responds to 5-HT/serotonin via 5-HT₂ receptors.
  • Rat uterus (De Jalon) responds to acetylcholine, oxytocin, and 5-HT via M₃/oxytocin receptors.
  • Guinea-pig atria respond to adrenaline (rate & force) via β₁-adrenergic receptors.
  • Frog rectus abdominis responds to acetylcholine via depolarising nicotinic (NM) receptors.
  • Guinea-pig tracheal chain responds to histamine/agonists (H₁) and relaxants (β₂).
  • Removed tissue needs everything blood normally supplied: ions for excitability, glucose for fuel, oxygen via aeration, buffered pH, and matching body temperature.
  • Bicarbonate buffers only hold pH 7.4 as long as CO₂ keeps bubbling through — which is why Krebs solution specifically needs carbogen, not plain air.
  • Warning: a flat or fading trace usually means a failed preparation (wrong PSS, no aeration, wrong temperature, tissue fatigue), not an inactive drug — always confirm the tissue still responds to a standard dose first.
  • Chromatography (TLC, column, GC, HPLC) works like a race between a mobile phase carrying the sample and a stationary phase holding it back.
  • HPLC is column chromatography run under high pressure — the standard tool for measuring drug levels in plasma/urine, underpinning most PK assays.
  • Beer–Lambert law: A = ε·c·l (absorbance is proportional to concentration)
  • ELISA measures proteins/hormones/antibodies via an enzyme-driven colour change; PCR amplifies and quantifies specific DNA/RNA.
  • Centrifugation separates components by density — plasma from cells, or organelles via differential/density-gradient spins.
  • TLC/paper chromatography: identity and purity (Rf value) via differential migration on a thin layer.
  • Column & HPLC: drug concentration in plasma/urine via high-pressure partition between phases.
  • Gas chromatography (GC): volatile compounds via vapour-phase separation, often paired with mass spec (GC-MS).
  • UV–Vis spectrophotometry: concentration of a coloured/UV-absorbing solute via the Beer–Lambert law.
  • ELISA: proteins/hormones/antibodies via enzyme-linked antigen–antibody colour reaction.
  • PCR/RT-PCR: specific DNA/RNA via thermal-cycle amplification; electrophoresis separates by size and charge; centrifuge separates by density.
  • The mobile phase carries molecules forward; the stationary phase tries to hold them back — whichever pulls harder wins for that molecule.
  • Molecules clinging to the stationary phase fall behind, so a mixture that entered as one blend exits spread out over time.
  • A detector records each compound as a peak; the peak's area corresponds to the quantity present.
  • A = ε·c·l — absorbance rises in lockstep with concentration and path length.
  • Known standards build a calibration curve; the unknown's absorbance is mapped onto that curve to read off its concentration.
  • Warning: a reading above the topmost standard (outside the linear range) falsely reads low — dilute and rerun rather than trusting it directly.
  • Rotarod: fall-off time measures motor coordination — screens muscle relaxants and sedatives.
  • Actophotometer: locomotor count distinguishes CNS stimulants (increase) from depressants (decrease).
  • MES (maximal electroshock): abolishing tonic hind-limb extension screens drugs for grand-mal epilepsy (e.g. phenytoin).
  • PTZ (pentylenetetrazole): raised seizure threshold screens drugs for absence/myoclonic epilepsy (e.g. ethosuximide, valproate).
  • Hot-plate/tail-flick = central (opioid) analgesia; acetic-acid writhing = peripheral (NSAID) analgesia; Straub tail = opioid sign; haloperidol catalepsy = antipsychotic sign.
  • CVS: isoproterenol injection models MI (screens cardioprotectives); DOCA+salt models hypertension; FeCl₃ on a vessel models thrombosis.
  • GIT: pylorus ligation/ethanol/NSAID models ulcers; TNBS or acetic acid models colitis; cerulein models pancreatitis.
  • Inflammation: carrageenan into the paw models acute inflammation (measures paw oedema volume); cotton pellet implant models chronic inflammation (measures granuloma dry weight).
  • Since you can't wait for natural disease, you stage a controlled rehearsal of one measurable feature (an electric jolt for a seizure, an irritant for inflammation).
  • MES evokes a spreading tonic–clonic seizure (like grand mal), so drugs blocking its spread (phenytoin) score positive; PTZ lowers seizure threshold (like absence epilepsy), so threshold-raising drugs (ethosuximide) score positive there.
  • Warning: a model has face validity (looks like the disease), construct validity (same mechanism), and predictive validity (results translate to patients) — many compounds fail in humans because the model only had face validity.
  • Acute toxicity: single (or 24h) dose, gives LD₅₀ and dose range, tested in at least 2 species.
  • Sub-acute/sub-chronic: repeated dosing for ~14–90 days to find target organs and a NOAEL (no-observed-adverse-effect level).
  • Chronic: months to a lifetime, supports long-term human use.
  • Special toxicology: reproductive/teratogenicity (Segments I–III), genotoxicity (Ames test), carcinogenicity (2-year rodent bioassay).
  • LD₅₀ = dose killing 50% of animals; modern OECD guidance prefers humane fixed-dose/up-and-down methods using far fewer animals, all under GLP.
  • Acute (single dose/24h): LD₅₀, dose range, immediate target organs.
  • Sub-acute (14–28 days): repeated-dose target organs, dose selection.
  • Sub-chronic (~90 days): NOAEL, organ toxicity with extended exposure.
  • Chronic (6 months–2 years): long-term safety for chronic human use.
  • Reproductive (Seg I–III): teratogenicity and reproductive harm; Genotoxicity (Ames etc.): mutagenic potential; Carcinogenicity (~2 yr rodent): tumour induction.
  • The fraction of animals dying climbs along an S-shaped dose–mortality curve as dose increases.
  • LD₅₀ is the dose at that curve's midpoint — the single number summarising acute lethality.
  • A steep curve means the population is uniform in sensitivity; a shallow curve means wide variation.
  • The same logic with a therapeutic endpoint gives ED₅₀; the ratio of LD₅₀ to ED₅₀ is the therapeutic index.
  • Most carcinogens are also mutagens (they damage DNA).
  • The Ames test uses Salmonella strains that can't make histidine; if the test chemical mutates their DNA back to working, colonies grow on histidine-free plates.
  • More colonies = more mutagenic — a fast, cheap early-warning before committing to a 2-year rodent carcinogenicity study.
  • Warning: classical LD₅₀ needs large animal numbers and varies by species/strain/sex/age/route, so regulators now favour fixed-dose and up-and-down methods (OECD), all under Good Laboratory Practice (GLP).
  • Pyrogens are fever-producing contaminants, chiefly bacterial endotoxin (lipopolysaccharide) from Gram-negative cell walls.
  • Rabbit pyrogen test: inject the product IV into rabbits and measure the rise in body temperature (in-vivo, whole-response test).
  • LAL (Limulus Amebocyte Lysate)/Bacterial Endotoxin Test: endotoxin clots a lysate from horseshoe-crab blood — fast, sensitive, in-vitro, the modern replacement.
  • Cell culture: primary (fresh, finite) vs continuous/immortal lines, grown in medium + serum at 37°C/5% CO₂ under strict aseptic technique.
  • MTT assay measures viability: living cells reduce yellow MTT dye to purple formazan, and absorbance is proportional to viable cell count.
  • Sterilising kills bacteria but endotoxin (their cell-wall debris) is heat-stable and survives autoclaving.
  • Even dead bacteria leave behind endotoxin that triggers fever through the host's own cytokines.
  • The LAL test exploits horseshoe-crab immunity: their blood cells clot on contact with endotoxin, so a clot in the tube signals contamination.
  • Growing cells outside the body requires a nutrient medium, serum (FBS) for growth factors, buffered pH via 5% CO₂, and 37°C warmth — all kept sterile.
  • A single contaminating bacterium or fungus can outgrow the cell culture overnight if asepsis fails.
  • In the MTT assay, only metabolically active cells turn the dye purple — a darker well means more living cells, and a drug that lightens the well is cytotoxic.
  • Warning: contamination (bacteria, fungi, mycoplasma) is the most common disaster in culture and is only prevented by aseptic technique, not cured afterward.
  • Data types: qualitative (nominal & ordinal) vs quantitative (discrete & continuous) — this drives everything else.
  • Descriptive statistics (mean±SD, median, proportion) vs inferential statistics (tests and confidence intervals that generalise to the population).
  • Parametric tests (t-test, ANOVA, Pearson) suit normal data; non-parametric tests (Mann-Whitney, Wilcoxon, Kruskal-Wallis, Spearman) suit skewed/ordinal data.
  • Categorical comparisons use chi-square (large counts) or Fisher's exact test (small counts).
  • Power = 1 − β; Type I error = α (false positive); Type II error = β (false negative)
  • 2 independent groups: unpaired (Student's) t-test, or Mann-Whitney U if non-parametric.
  • 2 paired measurements: paired t-test, or Wilcoxon signed-rank if non-parametric.
  • ≥3 independent groups: one-way ANOVA (+post-hoc), or Kruskal-Wallis if non-parametric.
  • ≥3 paired/repeated groups: repeated-measures ANOVA, or Friedman if non-parametric.
  • Association of 2 numerical variables: Pearson correlation/regression, or Spearman rank if non-parametric; proportions/categories: chi-square or Fisher's exact.
  • The p-value answers: if the drug truly did nothing, how often would chance alone produce a difference this big?
  • It is NOT the probability the drug works, and a non-significant result doesn't prove there's no effect — it may just mean too few animals (low power).
  • Running many t-tests instead of one ANOVA inflates the false-positive rate (three tests ≈ 14% overall risk instead of 5%).
  • Warning: testing many outcomes guarantees some "significant" results by chance (p-hacking) — pre-specify the primary endpoint and calculate sample size before the experiment, not after.
  • Cmax and Tmax describe absorption — a faster, bigger peak means faster, fuller absorption.
  • Bioavailability F = AUC(oral) ÷ AUC(IV)
  • t½ (from the terminal slope) sets the dosing interval and time to steady state (≈4–5 half-lives).
  • Compartment models: one-compartment (single exponential decay) vs two-compartment (rapid distribution then slower elimination).
  • TDM (therapeutic drug monitoring) is reserved for narrow-therapeutic-index drugs where blood level predicts effect/toxicity better than dose alone.
  • Digoxin: narrow window — toxicity (arrhythmia) overlaps with the therapeutic level.
  • Lithium: very narrow index, renal handling, dangerous toxicity.
  • Phenytoin: zero-order kinetics — small dose changes cause large level changes.
  • Theophylline (narrow window, many interactions); aminoglycosides (nephro/ototoxic, trough levels guide safety); vancomycin; cyclosporine/tacrolimus; carbamazepine/valproate.
  • Cardiovascular: standardised BP measurement, effect of posture/exercise/propranolol on BP/HR, GTN response, ECG.
  • Respiratory: salbutamol vs placebo on PEFR; lung function testing after exercise.
  • Kidney: frusemide effect on urine volume and Na⁺/K⁺ excretion, comparing diuretic classes.
  • Eye (autonomic): hyoscine/tropicamide (mydriatic) vs pilocarpine (miotic) effects on pupil diameter and accommodation.
  • The climb is absorption outpacing elimination; the peak (Cmax at Tmax) is where they balance; the fall is elimination taking over.
  • AUC (area under the curve) is total exposure, estimated by summing thin trapezoids between sample points.
  • t½ = 0.693 / k (k = elimination rate constant from the log-linear terminal slope)
  • Warning: two formulations can share the same AUC yet differ in Cmax/Tmax — important when speed of onset matters; a TDM level is only meaningful if drawn at the right time (steady state, correct trough).
  • Protocol anatomy: title → rationale/background → aim & objectives → hypothesis → literature review → methodology → ethics/IAEC → references.
  • Biomedical waste (India, BMW Rules 2016) uses four colour-coded streams for disposal.
  • Impact factor = average citations a journal's recent articles receive per year — a metric of the journal, not of any one paper.
  • Computational pharmacology: in-silico screening, molecular docking, and QSAR predict drug activity before any wet-lab experiment.
  • New paradigms: translational medicine (bench to bedside), reverse pharmacology (clinical lead to mechanism), microdosing/Phase 0 (a sub-pharmacological human dose to read early PK).
  • Yellow: anatomical waste, soiled waste, expired medicines, chemical/microbiology waste → incineration/deep burial.
  • Red: contaminated recyclable plastics (tubing, IV sets, gloves, catheters) → autoclave/disinfect, then recycle.
  • White (puncture-proof): sharps — needles, syringes, scalpels, blades → autoclave/dry heat, then shred.
  • Blue: broken/discarded glass and metallic body implants → disinfect, then recycle.
  • A protocol forces every decision — question, design, sample size, success criteria — to be fixed before any animal or money is spent, so the study can't be quietly reshaped to fit the result.
  • Waste colours are a safety reflex: yellow for biological/chemical material to destroy, red for disinfect-and-reuse plastics, white for anything that can stab you, blue for glass and metal.
  • In-silico docking ranks candidate molecules against a target's structure before any chemical synthesis.
  • Microdosing (Phase 0) gives a human a dose so tiny (~1%) it can't cause a pharmacological effect, yet ultra-sensitive assays can still trace its PK.
  • This lets researchers drop a drug with poor human kinetics before committing to an expensive full Phase I trial.
  • Warning: impact factor rates a journal, not the quality of any single paper — and waste segregation must happen at the point of generation, not afterward.
  • Flow cytometry: forward scatter = size, side scatter = granularity, fluorescent anti-CD antibodies = cell identity; Annexin V/PI apoptosis quadrants.
  • RT-qPCR: RNA → cDNA → amplify; lower Ct = more template; normalise to a housekeeping gene (ΔΔCt).
  • Cell-based assays: MTT/MTS viability, proliferation, reporter-gene and receptor-binding assays (link to cell culture & pyrogen testing).
  • Isolated organ: Langendorff heart (rate/force/coronary flow); Dale's vasomotor reversal (adrenaline reversed by α-block, noradrenaline not).
  • Bioassay: four-point (2+2) needs parallel lines; pA₂ = antagonist potency (doubling of agonist dose).
  • Behavioural: rota-rod, actophotometer, Eddy's hot plate; plus protocol/IND-NDA drafting, critical appraisal, and HPLC/PK calculations (loading = Vd×C; maintenance rate = CL×C).

🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Three choices have to be made before any experiment can start: the animal, the route, and who signs off ethically. Once you have a handle on choosing species, the routes-and-volumes table, where to draw blood, and the 3Rs / CPCSEA framework, you can put together almost any protocol in the manual.

The high-yield elements

  • The species depends on the job at hand: mice and rats cover general screening, the guinea pig is used for its histamine-sensitive ileum and for anaphylaxis work, the rabbit is reached for in pyrogen testing and eye studies, and the frog supplies the rectus abdominis and the isolated heart.
  • Speed and predictability track the route: IV sits at the top (instant, fully bioavailable), then IP (fast and able to take a larger volume, though it passes through the portal circulation first), then SC/IM, with oral trailing as the slowest and most variable.
  • How much you can inject is limited by the animal's weight and the chosen route — go over that limit and you risk pain, a diluted/skewed result, or the animal's death.
  • Where you draw blood comes down to how much you need and whether the animal has to live afterward (retro-orbital, tail vein, or saphenous for survival; terminal cardiac puncture otherwise).
  • Ethical clearance isn't optional: the 3Rs (Replacement, Reduction, Refinement) are put into practice in India through CPCSEA oversight and the institution's own IAEC.

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Routes — different on-ramps to the same highway

Picture the systemic circulation as a highway and each route of administration as a different way onto it. IV puts the car directly onto the highway at full speed — immediate, with 100% of the dose reaching the blood. IP is a broad slip-road that runs past the liver's toll-booth first (the portal first-pass), so it's quick and can carry a larger volume, but some drug is lost along the way. SC and IM behave like quiet side-streets, releasing the drug slowly and steadily. Oral dosing takes the long way through the gut and liver — the slowest, least predictable route, yet the one ordinary medicines almost always use.

The 3Rs & CPCSEA — the animal house's rulebook

The 3Rs form the ethical backbone of any animal work: Replacement (turn to cell culture, computational models, or simpler organisms whenever that's possible), Reduction (use no more animals than the statistics genuinely require — which is exactly why careful design and sample-size calculations count as an ethical step, not just a statistical one), and Refinement (cut down on pain and distress through analgesia, careful handling, and humane endpoints). Within India, each protocol gets approval and ongoing oversight from the institution's IAEC, operating under the national CPCSEA guidelines.

💡 The Analogy — the animal house as a regulated kitchen

An animal lab runs much like a licensed commercial kitchen: you don't get to cook however you please. An outside inspector (CPCSEA) writes the rules, a local manager (IAEC) has to approve every recipe (protocol) before cooking starts, waste has to be kept to a minimum (Reduction), lab-grown substitutes replace real ingredients wherever feasible (Replacement), and cleanliness and humane treatment are non-negotiable (Refinement). Skip the paperwork, and the kitchen gets shut down — no sign-off means no experiment and no data you can publish.

⚠️ Warning — volume & zoonosis traps: pushing past the safe injection volume both dilutes the drug and harms the animal, so what looks like a "negative result" might just be an artefact of too much fluid. Treat animals as possible carriers of zoonoses (e.g., lymphocytic choriomeningitis, leptospirosis, rat-bite fever, salmonella): glove up, never eat in the animal facility, and treat wounds right away.

Body weight is not what dose scales with across species — body surface area is, because that's what metabolic rate tracks. Strip the topic down and it's really just one idea (scaling by surface area) plus one equation (the Human Equivalent Dose, calculated through Km factors).

The high-yield elements

  • The reason it's surface area: smaller animals carry more surface area relative to their mass and run a faster metabolism, which clears drugs quicker and means they need a larger dose per kilogram.
  • Km factor is body weight (kg) divided by surface area (m²), and stays constant for a given species.
  • HED equation: HED (mg/kg) = Animal dose (mg/kg) × (Animal Km ÷ Human Km).
  • Allometric scaling: physiological variables rise with body weight to some exponent (roughly 0.75 for metabolic rate, roughly 0.67 for surface area).
  • Paget & Barnes tables reach the same conversion using pre-tabulated factors rather than working through Km directly.

Why surface area wins over weight

How fast the body's internal "furnace" runs — and so how fast a drug gets cleared — follows surface area rather than mass. A mouse is almost all surface relative to its bulk: it sheds heat quickly, its metabolism runs hot, and it burns through a drug much faster than a person does. That means matching the same tissue concentration calls for a bigger dose per kilogram in the mouse. Surface area itself rises roughly as (body weight)0.67, and that relationship is exactly what the Km factor bundles together.

EQ 2·1 HED (mg/kg) = Animal dose (mg/kg) × (Km animal ÷ Km human)

Working the mouse example through: 50 × (3 ÷ 37) = 4.05 mg/kg, which becomes roughly 243 mg total at 60 kg; dividing by the ÷10 safety factor leaves a first-in-human dose of around 24 mg.

💡 The Analogy — small engines rev higher

Set a tiny scooter engine beside a truck engine. The scooter is small but spins at a furious RPM; the truck is massive but turns over lazily. Fuel consumption doesn't track engine weight — it tracks how hard the engine works, which in turn tracks its surface-to-volume ratio. The mouse plays the part of the screaming scooter: pound for pound it burns through drug much faster than the lumbering human truck, so it needs proportionally more fuel — i.e., dose — to keep going.

⚠️ Warning — the linear-scaling trap: carrying a dose across species by plain mg/kg is a mistake to avoid — going from rodent to human this way ends up badly overdosing the person. Route the calculation through surface area (Km) every time, then apply the safety factor. This exact mistake is behind real first-in-human disasters.

03

Bioassay

Principle · graded vs quantal · matching, bracketing, interpolation, multiple-point · antagonist assay
Tier 1 · Highest yield
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
A bioassay works out the potency or concentration of an unknown from the size of the biological response it triggers, always judged against a standard of the identical substance on the identical tissue. For exam purposes it boils down to two distinctions — graded vs quantal response — and the four matching methods, with the antagonist assay added on.

The high-yield elements

  • Core principle: the unknown gets measured against the standard under matched conditions; what's read out is the biological response itself, not any chemical reaction.
  • Graded response: the response climbs with dose on a continuous scale (say, how much an ileum contracts) — this is how most tissue assays work.
  • Quantal (all-or-none) response: each animal simply responds or doesn't (dies, convulses, etc.) — probit analysis on this kind of data produces the ED₅₀ / LD₅₀.
  • The matching methods: matching, bracketing, interpolation, and the multiple-point assays (three-point and four-point).
  • Antagonist bioassay: the antagonist's strength is expressed as pA₂, derived from how far the agonist's dose–response curve shifts rightward in parallel (Schild analysis).

Graded vs quantal — two different ways to score a response

A graded response works like a dimmer switch: the same piece of tissue contracts more as the dose climbs, letting a single preparation trace an entire dose–response curve. A quantal response behaves like a light switch applied across a population: every animal is simply marked yes or no (convulsed or not, died or survived), and tallying how many cross the threshold at each dose builds a cumulative curve from which the ED₅₀ or LD₅₀ emerges.

The four matching methods — narrowing in on the unknown

Matching simply eyeballs the two until they look equal. Bracketing pins the unknown between one standard dose that's obviously larger and one that's obviously smaller. Interpolation treats the full standard curve as a measuring ruler. The multiple-point assays (three-point and four-point) alternate standard and unknown doses so that, provided their log dose–response lines run parallel, the horizontal distance separating them gives the log potency ratio directly — with that same parallelism serving as built-in proof the assay holds up.

💡 The Analogy — weighing on a balance

Doing a bioassay is like weighing an unknown object on an old-fashioned two-pan balance against known weights. Matching means swapping weights in and out until the pans even out. Bracketing means finding the weight that's just a touch too heavy and the one just a touch too light. Interpolation means calibrating a spring scale against known weights first, then reading the unknown straight off the dial. The four-point assay weighs each object twice over and checks the scale's linearity before trusting any number — it takes longer, but it's the version you'd want backing up an exam answer.

⚠️ Warning — the comparison trap: a bioassay only holds up when the standard and the unknown work through the same mechanism on the same tissue (curves running parallel). Step outside the linear range, or get non-parallel curves, and the potency figure means nothing. Biological variation can never be eliminated entirely — it's kept in check through randomisation, repetition, and letting each tissue serve as its own control.

Isolated tissue preparations (in vitro)

Organ bath · physiological salt solutions · tissue–agonist pairs · levers & magnification
Tier 1 · Highest yield
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
An organ bath sustains a strip of tissue in warmed, aerated physiological salt solution (PSS); an agonist gets added, and a lever (or transducer) blows up the resulting contraction onto a kymograph trace. What matters for exams is knowing the correct PSS for each tissue and the characteristic agonist–receptor pairing for every preparation.

The high-yield elements

  • What the organ bath needs: the appropriate PSS, the right temperature (37 °C for mammalian tissue, 32 °C for the rat uterus, room temperature for frog tissue), constant aeration (plain air, or carbogen — 95% O₂ / 5% CO₂ — for bicarbonate-buffered solutions), and a fixed contact time.
  • The PSS depends on the tissue: Tyrode, Ringer, Krebs, De Jalon and Locke solutions differ from one another in their ions, glucose content, and buffer.
  • Every tissue responds to a characteristic agonist through a particular receptor — this is what the bioassay rests on.
  • The lever amplifies tiny movements; magnification equals the writing-arm length divided by the tissue-arm length.
  • Dose cycle: dose is added, the peak is recorded, the tissue is washed, then rested before the next dose — this sequence must stay constant for the curve to be valid.

The organ bath functions as life support

A piece of gut, uterus, or atrium is living tissue removed from the body it once belonged to — staying responsive means substituting everything the blood used to supply: the correct ions (needed for excitability and contraction), glucose (fuel), oxygen (delivered by bubbling), buffered pH, and matching body temperature. 💡 The Analogy — an aquarium with a pen attached

Think of the organ bath as a miniature, temperature-controlled aquarium built for a single piece of muscle. The PSS plays the role of seawater, the aeration acts as the air-stone, and the water jacket serves as the heater. Laboratory instruments & analytical techniques

Chromatography · spectrophotometry · HPLC · ELISA · PCR · centrifugation
Tier 2 · Important
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Nearly every analytical technique performs one of two jobs: pulling apart a mixture, or then detecting and measuring the component that matters. Chromatography (TLC, column, GC, HPLC) handles the separating; spectrophotometry, ELISA, and PCR handle the detecting and measuring.

The high-yield elements

  • Chromatography works like a race between a mobile phase that carries the sample along and a stationary phase that holds it back; whatever sticks more travels slower, and that's what separates the components.
  • HPLC is column chromatography run under high pressure — it's the standard tool for measuring drug levels in plasma or urine, underpinning most PK assays.
  • Spectrophotometry follows the Beer–Lambert law: absorbance rises proportionally with concentration, which is why a calibration curve can convert one into the other.
  • ELISA measures proteins, hormones, or antibodies via an enzyme-driven colour change; PCR amplifies and then quantifies a specific stretch of DNA or RNA.
  • Centrifugation separates components by density (plasma from cells; differential or density-gradient spins for organelles).

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Chromatography — a contest that turns into a race

Every chromatographic technique pits two phases against each other over each molecule in the sample. The mobile phase attempts to carry the molecule forward; the stationary phase attempts to keep hold of it. Molecules that cling tightly to the stationary phase fall behind, while those drawn more to the mobile phase move ahead quickly — so a mixture that entered as one blend exits spread out over time. A detector positioned at the end records each compound as a peak, and that peak's area corresponds to the quantity present.

Beer–Lambert — using colour as a measuring stick

When light passes through a solution, part of it gets absorbed; greater concentration and a longer path both mean more absorption. The relationship A = ε·c·l means absorbance climbs in lockstep with concentration, so known standards are used to build a calibration curve, and the unknown's absorbance is simply mapped back onto that curve to get its concentration.

💡 The Analogy — a cross-country race with sticky mud

Imagine runners (standing in for the molecules) crossing a muddy field (the column). The mud — the stationary phase — clings to some runners more than others, while a tailwind (the mobile phase) pushes everyone onward regardless. Runners unaffected by the mud cross the line first; the ones who get bogged down trail in later — so a group that set off together ends up spread out, arriving one at a time. Tally how many cross the finish line in each cluster, and you learn not just which runners were racing but how many of each.

Structure ID, trace metals, and doing it under GLP

Beyond separation and simple detection, three heavier tools appear in the MD syllabus. NMR spectroscopy reads how atomic nuclei resonate in a magnetic field to deduce a molecule's structure — the definitive way to confirm what a compound actually is. Mass spectrometry ionises molecules and sorts the fragments by mass-to-charge ratio; coupled to GC or LC it becomes the confirmatory gold standard for identifying and quantifying drugs and metabolites at trace levels. Atomic absorption spectrometry measures a specific metal (a practical-exam favourite is lead in water or blood) by how much element-specific light the atomised sample absorbs. For antibody-based assays, RIA (radiolabelled) was the original ultra-sensitive method but has largely been replaced by ELISA (enzyme label — safer, no radiation).

None of these numbers mean anything unless the method is trustworthy. Good Laboratory Practice (GLP) governs how safety data are generated and recorded, and every quantitative assay must undergo analytical method validation — demonstrating accuracy, precision, specificity, linearity, limit of detection/quantification, and robustness — before its results can be relied upon.

⚠️ Warning — the calibration trap: a quantitative method can never be more reliable than its standard curve. An unknown reading above the topmost standard (outside the linear range) will falsely read low; such samples need diluting back into range, and the calibration curve has to be rerun with every batch.

A model is a rehearsal of the disease

You can't wait for a mouse to develop epilepsy or arthritis, so you stage a controlled rehearsal of the key feature you can measure: an electric jolt for a convulsion, an irritant in the paw for inflammation, a salt-and-steroid load for hypertension. Give the drug and watch whether it changes that one measurable thing. The art is choosing a model whose biology matches the human disease — its validity — so a positive result actually predicts a clinical effect.

The MES/PTZ pair is the classic example of mechanistic validity: a maximal electroshock evokes a spreading tonic–clonic seizure (grand mal), so drugs that block its spread (phenytoin) score positive; PTZ lowers the seizure threshold like the thalamocortical circuits of absence epilepsy, so threshold-raising drugs (ethosuximide) score positive there instead.

💡 The Analogy — a fire drill for each kind of fire

Testing a drug in animals is like running fire drills to test an extinguisher. A grease fire (MES/grand-mal) and an electrical fire (PTZ/absence) need different extinguishers — passing one drill doesn't guarantee the other. A truly broad extinguisher (valproate) puts out both. And a drill is only useful if it sets the same kind of fire you'll face in the real building: a model with poor validity is a drill using a candle to test for a warehouse blaze.

⚠️ Warning — the validity trap: a model has three kinds of validity — face (looks like the disease), construct (same underlying mechanism) and predictive (drugs that work here work in patients). Many promising compounds fail in humans precisely because the model only had face validity. Always state which endpoint and which validity a result rests on.

Toxicology study & safety testing

Acute → chronic ladder · LD₅₀ · special toxicology · OECD / GLP
Tier 1 · Highest yield
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Toxicity testing answers three questions: how much harm (dose), how soon (duration), and what kind (target organ / special toxicity). The structure is a duration ladder — acute → sub-acute → sub-chronic → chronic — plus the special studies (reproductive, genotoxicity, carcinogenicity) and the LD₅₀.

The high-yield elements

  • Acute toxicity — single (or 24 h) dose, gives LD₅₀ and the dose range; ≥2 species.
  • Sub-acute / sub-chronic — repeated dosing for weeks (≈14–90 days) to find target organs and a no-observed-adverse-effect level (NOAEL).
  • Chronic — months to a lifetime; supports long-term human use.
  • Special toxicology: reproductive/teratogenicity (Segments I–III), genotoxicity (Ames test for mutagenicity), carcinogenicity (2-year rodent bioassay).
  • LD₅₀ = dose killing 50% of animals — a quantal endpoint from a dose–mortality curve. Modern guidance (OECD) prefers humane fixed-dose / up-and-down methods using far fewer animals; all done under GLP.

LD₅₀ — the population's tipping point

Individuals vary in sensitivity, so at a low dose almost none die and at a high dose almost all do; in between, the fraction dying climbs along an S-shaped dose–mortality curve. The LD₅₀ is the dose at the curve's midpoint — the single number that summarises acute lethality. A steep curve means the population is uniform (a small dose increase tips many over); a shallow curve means wide variation in sensitivity. The same logic with a therapeutic endpoint gives ED₅₀, and their ratio is the therapeutic index.

The Ames test — borrowing bacteria to spot mutagens

A mutagen damages DNA, and most carcinogens are mutagens. The Ames test uses Salmonella strains that cannot make histidine; if a test chemical mutates their DNA back to a functional gene, colonies suddenly grow on histidine-free plates. More colonies = more mutagenic — a fast, cheap early-warning for carcinogenic potential before committing to a 2-year rodent study.

💡 The Analogy — stress-testing a bridge

Safety testing is stress-testing a new bridge before opening it to traffic. First a single heavy truck to find the breaking point (acute, LD₅₀); then convoys day after day to find which girder fatigues first (repeated-dose, target organ); then years of simulated weather and load (chronic, carcinogenicity). You always test to failure in animals first, with a big safety margin, so the bridge never collapses under real people.

⚠️ Warning — the LD₅₀ trap: a precise classical LD₅₀ needs large numbers of animals and gives a number that varies with species, strain, sex, age and route — so it is a poor stand-alone safety measure. Regulators now favour the fixed-dose, acute-toxic-class and up-and-down procedures (OECD), which classify hazard humanely with far fewer animals. All of it must run under Good Laboratory Practice (GLP).

Pyrogens — the heat-stable saboteur

Sterilising a fluid kills the bacteria but doesn't necessarily remove their debris: endotoxin (lipopolysaccharide from Gram-negative cell walls) is heat-stable and survives autoclaving, and even injected dead it triggers fever through the host's cytokines. So "sterile" is not the same as "pyrogen-free", and injectables need a separate pyrogen test. The LAL test exploits a quirk of horseshoe-crab immunity: their amoebocyte blood cells clot on contact with endotoxin (a defence that walls off invaders) — so a clot in the tube means endotoxin is present.

Cell culture — a body in a bottle

To grow cells outside the body you must recreate their world: a nutrient medium for food, serum (FBS) for growth factors, a buffered pH held by the incubator's 5% CO₂, and 37 °C warmth — all kept ruthlessly sterile, because a single bacterium or fungus will outgrow your cells overnight. The MTT assay then reports how many cells are alive: only metabolically active cells reduce the yellow dye to purple crystals, so a darker well means more living cells (and a drug that lightens the well is cytotoxic).

💡 The Analogy — the crab as a smoke alarm

The LAL test turns the horseshoe crab's immune system into a smoke alarm for bacterial contamination. In the wild, the crab's blood cells instantly gel around invaders to trap them; in the lab we put a drop of that blood in a tube, add the sample, and if it sets like jelly, the "alarm" has gone off — endotoxin is present. No fire (no live animal fever) required to know there's smoke.

⚠️ Warning — "sterile ≠ pyrogen-free": filtration and autoclaving remove or kill organisms but may leave heat-stable endotoxin behind. In culture, the commonest disaster is contamination (bacteria, fungi, mycoplasma) — prevented only by aseptic technique, not cured after the fact.

Biostatistics in pharmacology

Data types · choosing the test · p-value & errors · sample size
Tier 1 · Highest yield
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Choosing a statistical test is a three-question decision: what type of data (numerical vs categorical), how many groups, and are they paired (same subjects) or independent? Answer those and the test almost picks itself.

The high-yield elements

  • Data types: qualitative — nominal & ordinal; quantitative — discrete & continuous. The type drives everything.
  • Descriptive (mean ± SD, median, proportion) vs inferential (tests & confidence intervals that generalise to the population).
  • Parametric (normal data: t-test, ANOVA, Pearson) vs non-parametric (skewed/ordinal: Mann–Whitney, Wilcoxon, Kruskal–Wallis, Spearman).
  • Categorical comparisons: chi-square (large) or Fisher's exact (small counts).
  • The p-value is the probability of seeing a difference this big if there were truly none; < 0.05 is the conventional cut-off. Type I (α) = false positive; Type II (β) = false negative; power = 1 − β.

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

The p-value — how surprised should you be?

Start by assuming the drug does nothing (the null hypothesis). The p-value answers: if that were true, how often would chance alone produce a difference at least this big? A p of 0.03 means "only 3% of the time" — surprising enough that we reject the null. It is not the probability the drug works, and a non-significant p doesn't prove no effect — it may just mean too few animals (low power).

EQ 9·1 Power = 1 − β  |  Type I = α (false positive) · Type II = β (false negative)

Running many t-tests instead of one ANOVA inflates the chance of a false positive — each test carries its own 5% risk, so three tests ≈ 14% overall. ANOVA tests all groups at once and post-hoc corrections rein the error back in.

Meta-analysis — pooling many trials into one answer

A meta-analysis statistically combines the results of several studies (usually from a systematic review) into a single pooled effect estimate — increasing power and precision beyond any single trial. Two models decide how the studies are weighted:

  • Fixed-effect model assumes every study is estimating the same true effect, and differences are due to chance alone — appropriate only when studies are very similar.
  • Random-effects model assumes the true effect varies between studies (different populations, doses, settings) and estimates an average of that distribution — the safer default when studies differ.

The pooled result is displayed on a forest plot: each study is a box (its size ∝ its weight) with a horizontal confidence-interval line, and the pooled estimate is the diamond at the bottom; a vertical "line of no effect" (RR/OR = 1, or difference = 0) shows at a glance which studies — and the pooled estimate — are significant. Heterogeneity (how much studies genuinely disagree) is quantified by I²: roughly <25% low, ~50% moderate, >75% high — high I² argues for a random-effects model and caution in pooling. A funnel plot checks for publication bias: plotting effect size against study precision should give a symmetrical inverted funnel; asymmetry (missing small negative studies) suggests bias. Use a meta-analysis when several comparable trials exist and you need the most precise, generalisable estimate; rely on a single large well-conducted RCT when the trials are too few or too heterogeneous to combine meaningfully.

💡 The Analogy — signal through static

Statistics is trying to hear a faint signal through radio static. The treatment effect is the signal; biological variation is the static. The t-statistic is essentially signal ÷ noise. You can hear a weak signal two ways: make the signal louder (a bigger effect) or turn the static down (less variability or — the lever you control — a bigger sample, which averages the noise away). The p-value just tells you how likely the "signal" you heard was actually random crackle.

⚠️ Warning — the multiplicity & power traps: testing many outcomes or many sub-groups guarantees some "significant" results by chance (p-hacking) — pre-specify the primary endpoint and correct for multiple comparisons. And an underpowered study that finds "no difference" hasn't shown the drug is ineffective; it may simply have had too few animals to detect a real effect. Calculate sample size before the experiment.

Clinical pharmacology & PK/PD

Plasma curve (Cmax, Tmax, AUC, t½) · TDM · volunteer experiments · Phase 0
Tier 1 · Highest yield
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Clinical pharmacology reads the plasma concentration–time curve: its peak (Cmax), time-to-peak (Tmax), total area (AUC = exposure) and decay slope (t½) define how a drug behaves in people. Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM) applies this to drugs where the safe window is narrow.

The high-yield elements

  • Cmax & Tmax describe absorption; a faster/larger peak = faster, fuller absorption.
  • AUC (area under the curve, by the trapezoidal rule) = total systemic exposure; the basis of bioavailability (F = AUCoral/AUCIV) and bioequivalence.
  • t½ from the terminal slope sets the dosing interval and time to steady state (≈4–5 t½).
  • Compartment models: one-compartment (single exponential decay) vs two-compartment (rapid distribution then slower elimination).
  • TDM is reserved for narrow-therapeutic-index drugs where plasma level predicts effect/toxicity better than dose.

Reading the curve

The plasma curve is the drug's autobiography in one picture. The climb is absorption winning over elimination; the peak (Cmax at Tmax) is the moment they balance; the fall is elimination taking over. The area under the whole curve (AUC) is total exposure — add up thin trapezoids between sample points to estimate it. Plot the falling tail on a log scale and it straightens into a line whose slope gives the elimination rate constant, hence t½ = 0.693/k.

TDM matters when the dose is a poor guide to the blood level (phenytoin's zero-order kinetics) or when the toxic and therapeutic levels nearly touch (digoxin, lithium, aminoglycosides). For these, you measure the level itself and titrate to a target — usually a trough for safety, a peak for efficacy.

💡 The Analogy — filling and draining a bathtub, watched live

A single dose is like pouring one bucket into a bathtub with the plug out. The water level rises while you pour faster than it drains (absorption), peaks when pouring and draining match (Cmax/Tmax), then falls as the drain wins. The total water that passed through is the AUC — your exposure. For a dangerous drug with a thin safe band (lithium), you don't just trust the size of the bucket; you keep dipping a ruler in to read the actual level — that's TDM.

⚠️ Warning — the bioequivalence & TDM traps: two formulations can share an AUC yet differ in Cmax/Tmax — important for drugs where speed of onset matters. And a TDM level is only meaningful with the right timing (steady state reached, correct trough sampling) — a level drawn at the wrong moment misleads more than no level at all.

Flow Cytometry, RT-PCR & Cell-Based Assays

Counting cells, reading genes, and testing drugs in a dish
Tier 2 · Practicals
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Three modern bench methods dominate the semester practicals: flow cytometry (interrogates thousands of single cells one by one), RT-qPCR (measures how strongly a gene is expressed), and cell-based assays (test a drug's effect — viability, apoptosis, proliferation — in cultured cells). Each answers a different question about the same experiment.

The high-yield elements

  • Flow cytometry streams single cells past a laser; scattered light gives size (forward scatter) and granularity (side scatter), while fluorophore-tagged antibodies against surface markers (CD antigens) identify and count cell types (immunophenotyping — e.g. CD4 count in HIV, leukaemia panels).
  • Apoptosis assay: Annexin V binds externalised phosphatidylserine (early apoptosis) and a viability dye (PI / 7-AAD) marks late/necrotic cells — the classic quadrant plot.
  • FACS (fluorescence-activated cell sorting) physically separates the cells of interest.
  • RT-qPCR workflow: extract RNA → reverse-transcribe to cDNA → amplify with a fluorescent reporter → the Ct (cycle threshold) is inversely proportional to starting mRNA (more template → lower Ct); normalise to a housekeeping gene (ΔΔCt).
  • Cell-based assays: viability/cytotoxicity (MTT/MTS), proliferation, reporter-gene and receptor-binding assays — the front line of in-vitro screening (ties to cell culture & the pyrogen/LAL work in Module 8).

Flow cytometry — a turnstile that inspects every cell

Cells are forced into single file and shot past a laser one at a time. The light that scatters forward scales with cell size; light scattered to the side reflects internal complexity/granularity. Meanwhile, antibodies tagged with different fluorophores stick to specific CD surface markers, so each cell also reports its identity by which colours it emits. Combine these and you can count and classify thousands of cells per second — the basis of a CD4 count, a leukaemia immunophenotype, or an apoptosis assay where Annexin V (early, binds flipped-out phosphatidylserine) and a DNA dye like PI (late, enters only once the membrane fails) sort cells into live / early-apoptotic / late-apoptotic / necrotic quadrants.

RT-qPCR — counting a gene's messages

To measure how active a gene is, you capture its mRNA, copy it into stable cDNA (reverse transcription), then amplify with a fluorescent reporter. The cycle at which fluorescence crosses a threshold — the Ct — tells you how much you started with: abundant template crosses early (low Ct), scarce template late (high Ct). Comparing to a housekeeping gene (ΔΔCt) corrects for how much RNA you loaded.

💡 The Analogy — an airport security lane

Flow cytometry is an airport scanner reading each passenger in single file: a body scanner notes size and bulk (forward/side scatter), while coloured badges (fluorescent antibodies) reveal each person's role (CD markers). RT-qPCR is different — it's like photocopying a message and timing how quickly a legible stack appears: start with many copies and the stack forms in few rounds (low Ct); start with one and it takes many.

⚠️ Warning: flow cytometry needs a single-cell suspension and careful compensation for spectral overlap between fluorophores, plus proper isotype/unstained controls — sloppy gating invents cell populations. In RT-qPCR, genomic-DNA contamination and an unstable housekeeping gene are the classic sources of false expression changes.

Isolated organs — pharmacology with the confounders removed

An isolated preparation (Langendorff heart, guinea-pig ileum, rat fundus) lets you study a drug's action on one tissue free of reflexes, hormones and metabolism. The Langendorff heart is perfused backwards down the aorta so the coronary arteries fill and the heart keeps beating, letting you read rate, force and coronary flow directly. Noradrenaline has negligible β₂ action, so blocking α just abolishes its effect — no reversal.

Bioassay — measuring by comparison

When you can't easily measure a substance chemically, you measure it by the size of the biological response it produces, compared against a known standard on the same tissue. The four-point assay uses two doses each of standard and test; if the log dose–response lines are parallel (same mechanism), the horizontal distance between them gives the potency ratio. The pA₂ summarises a competitive antagonist's strength: it's the negative logarithm of the antagonist concentration that forces you to double the agonist dose to get the original response — a higher pA₂ means a more potent antagonist.

💡 The Analogy — weighing on a balance scale

A bioassay is a balance scale: you don't read the unknown's weight directly, you find how much of a known standard produces the same tilt (response) on the same tissue. Parallel dose–response lines are the guarantee that you're comparing like with like — two weights measured on the same, evenly-calibrated scale.

⚠️ Warning: a bioassay is only valid if the standard and test act by the same mechanism (parallel lines) on a sensitive, well-set-up tissue; non-parallel lines invalidate the potency estimate. In PK calculations, watch units and remember loading dose = Vd × target concentration, maintenance rate = CL × target concentration.

💎 Practical Pearl: Good experimental design and a correct sample-size calculation are ethical acts — they operationalise Reduction.
⚠️ Exam Trap: No ethics approval = no experiment and no publishable data; approval must precede any procedure.
04Define a bioassay. What single feature distinguishes it from a chemical assay?
A bioassay estimates the potency/concentration of a substance by the magnitude of the biological response it evokes, by comparison with a standard of the same substance on the same tissue. Unlike a chemical assay (which measures molecular quantity), it measures biological activity.
💎 Practical Pearl: Essential when the active principle is unknown, impure, or has no chemical assay (e.g., early hormone/cytokine work).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Validity demands standard and unknown act by the same mechanism on the same tissue — i.e. parallel log dose–response curves.
05Distinguish graded from quantal responses with an example of each and what each yields.
Graded: continuous, dose-dependent in one preparation (e.g., height of ileum contraction) → yields a dose–response curve. Quantal (all-or-none): each animal scored yes/no across a population (convulsion, death) → yields ED₅₀ / LD₅₀ by probit analysis.
💎 Practical Pearl: The therapeutic index TI = LD₅₀/ED₅₀ comes from two quantal curves.
⚠️ Exam Trap: A steep quantal curve means a uniform population; it does not mean the drug is safe — a steep LD₅₀ curve can sit dangerously close to the ED₅₀ curve.
06Name the four/five matching methods of bioassay and which is most accurate.
Matching, bracketing, interpolation, three-point, and four-point (2+2) assays. The four-point assay is most accurate — two doses each of standard and unknown let you test parallelism (validity) and compute the potency ratio with confidence limits.
💎 Practical Pearl: If the standard and unknown lines are parallel, the horizontal gap between them is the log potency ratio.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Interpolation is only valid over the linear (≈20–80%) portion of the log dose–response curve.
07Which physiological salt solution and temperature do you use for the rat uterus, and what pre-treatment does the animal need?
De Jalon solution at 32 °C, aerated; the rat is oestrogen-primed to standardise uterine responsiveness. Agonists include acetylcholine, oxytocin and 5-HT.
💎 Practical Pearl: De Jalon is low in calcium to keep spontaneous activity quiet so drug responses stand out.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Don't default to 37 °C — the uterus prep runs cooler (32 °C) than the guinea-pig ileum (37 °C).
08Why must Krebs solution be aerated with carbogen (95% O₂/5% CO₂) rather than plain air?
Krebs is a bicarbonate-buffered solution; its pH 7.4 is held only by an equilibrium with dissolved CO₂. Bubbling carbogen supplies both oxygen for the tissue and the CO₂ needed to keep the bicarbonate buffer at pH 7.4. Plain air lacks enough CO₂ and the pH drifts alkaline.
💎 Practical Pearl: Tyrode (also bicarbonate-buffered) behaves similarly; Ringer for frog tissue is simpler and aerated with air at room temperature.
⚠️ Exam Trap: A drifting baseline or failing tissue is often a buffering/aeration problem, not a drug effect.
09On a frontal writing lever, the tissue arm is 1 cm and the writing arm is 10 cm. A muscle shortens 1.5 mm — how big is the trace, and what is the magnification?
Magnification = writing arm ÷ tissue arm = 10 ÷ 1 = 10×; a 1.5 mm contraction is drawn as a 15 mm deflection.
💎 Practical Pearl: Levers magnify tiny movements onto a smoked kymograph drum; modern labs use force transducers + chart/software instead.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Excessive magnification or lever weight distorts the response — the lever must be light and friction-free.
10Which agonist and receptor are used in the guinea-pig ileum and frog rectus abdominis preparations?
Guinea-pig ileum: histamine (H₁) and acetylcholine (muscarinic M₃). Frog rectus abdominis: acetylcholine acting on nicotinic (NM) receptors — a depolarising, slow contraction.
💎 Practical Pearl: The frog rectus is the classic prep to demonstrate depolarising (nicotinic) versus the smooth-muscle muscarinic response of the ileum.
⚠️ Exam Trap: ACh acts on muscarinic receptors in the ileum but nicotinic receptors in the rectus — same agonist, different receptor and response.
11How does HPLC separate a drug from plasma components, and what does the detector peak area tell you?
HPLC pumps the sample at high pressure through a column; components separate by how strongly each partitions between mobile and stationary phases. The detector (UV/MS) records each as a peak whose area is proportional to the amount of that component — read off a calibration curve.
💎 Practical Pearl: HPLC is the workhorse for measuring drug levels in PK studies and TDM.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Strongly retained compounds elute later (longer retention time), not earlier.
12State the Beer–Lambert law and how a spectrophotometer turns absorbance into a concentration.
A = ε·c·l (absorbance = molar absorptivity × concentration × path length). Absorbance rises linearly with concentration, so you build a calibration curve from known standards and read the unknown's absorbance back to a concentration.
💎 Practical Pearl: Keep readings in the linear (low-absorbance) range; dilute samples that read above the top standard.
⚠️ Exam Trap: At high concentrations Beer–Lambert deviates (non-linear) — an out-of-range sample reads falsely low.
13A new anticonvulsant is positive in the MES test but negative in the PTZ test. What does this predict clinically?
MES (maximal electroshock) models generalised tonic–clonic (grand-mal) seizures; a positive MES with negative PTZ predicts efficacy in grand-mal but probably not in absence (petit-mal) seizures — a phenytoin/carbamazepine-like profile.
💎 Practical Pearl: PTZ models absence/myoclonic seizures (ethosuximide positive). A drug positive in both (e.g., valproate) is broad-spectrum.
⚠️ Exam Trap: MES = abolish tonic extension; PTZ = raise seizure threshold — don't swap which models which seizure type.
14Which model screens for muscle relaxants, and which distinguishes central from peripheral analgesia?
Rotarod (fall-off time) screens muscle relaxants/sedatives. Hot-plate & tail-flick test central (opioid) analgesia; acetic-acid writhing tests peripheral (NSAID-type) analgesia.
💎 Practical Pearl: Actophotometer counts locomotion → distinguishes CNS stimulants (↑) from depressants (↓).
⚠️ Exam Trap: A drug that simply impairs coordination (rotarod) can masquerade as analgesia in motor-based tests — use a sensory endpoint to be sure.
15Name the inducing agent for: carrageenan paw model, isoproterenol model, DOCA-salt model, and TNBS model.
Carrageenan → acute paw oedema (NSAID screen). Isoproterenol → myocardial infarction. DOCA + salt → hypertension. TNBS (intrarectal) → colitis (IBD model).
💎 Practical Pearl: Paw oedema is quantified with a plethysmometer; cotton-pellet granuloma (dry weight) screens chronic anti-inflammatories.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Carrageenan oedema is biphasic — early (histamine/serotonin/bradykinin) and late (prostaglandin) phases; NSAIDs act mainly on the late phase.
16Define LD₅₀ and explain why a steep dose–mortality curve matters.
LD₅₀ = the dose that kills 50% of test animals, read off the dose–mortality (quantal) curve. A steep curve means a uniform population (small dose increase tips many over); a shallow curve means wide variation in sensitivity.
💎 Practical Pearl: Therapeutic index = LD₅₀/ED₅₀; a steep, low LD₅₀ near the ED₅₀ signals danger.
⚠️ Exam Trap: LD₅₀ varies with species, strain, sex, age and route — a poor stand-alone safety figure, now replaced by humane OECD methods.
17Put the toxicity studies in order of duration and state what the sub-chronic study primarily defines.
Order: acute → sub-acute (~14–28 d) → sub-chronic (~90 d) → chronic (months–2 y). The sub-chronic study primarily defines target-organ toxicity and the NOAEL (no-observed-adverse-effect level).
💎 Practical Pearl: Special toxicology adds reproductive (Segments I–III), genotoxicity (Ames) and carcinogenicity (2-year rodent) studies.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Acute toxicity needs ≥2 species, at least one non-rodent.
18What does the Ames test detect, and how does it work?
It detects mutagenicity (an early signal of carcinogenic potential) using histidine-dependent Salmonella: if the test chemical mutates their DNA back to a functional gene, colonies grow on histidine-free plates — more colonies = more mutagenic.
💎 Practical Pearl: Adding liver S9 fraction lets the test catch pro-mutagens that need metabolic activation.
⚠️ Exam Trap: A positive Ames flags mutagenicity, not proven carcinogenicity — confirm with rodent bioassays.
19A batch of IV fluid is sterile but a patient spikes a fever after infusion. What was likely present, and how is it detected?
Pyrogen — bacterial endotoxin (lipopolysaccharide), which is heat-stable and survives autoclaving even though the bacteria are killed. Detected by the rabbit pyrogen test (temperature rise after IV injection) or the LAL / bacterial endotoxin test (endotoxin clots horseshoe-crab amoebocyte lysate).
💎 Practical Pearl: Depyrogenation needs dry heat (~250 °C) or removal steps — sterilisation alone is insufficient.
⚠️ Exam Trap: "Sterile" ≠ "pyrogen-free"; the LAL test is faster, more sensitive and animal-free, so it has largely replaced the rabbit test.
20What does the MTT assay measure, and what does a darker well indicate?
Cell viability/cytotoxicity: metabolically active (living) cells reduce yellow MTT to purple formazan. Absorbance ∝ number of viable cells, so a darker well = more living cells; a cytotoxic drug lightens the well.
💎 Practical Pearl: IC₅₀ (concentration cutting viability by 50%) is read from the MTT dose–response curve.
⚠️ Exam Trap: MTT reports metabolic activity, not cell number directly — agents that alter metabolism without killing can confound it.
21Three groups (control, standard, test) are compared on a continuous, normally-distributed outcome. Which test, and why not three t-tests?
One-way ANOVA (with a post-hoc test). Running three separate t-tests inflates the false-positive rate — each carries a 5% risk, so three ≈ 14% overall. ANOVA tests all groups together and post-hoc correction controls the error.
💎 Practical Pearl: If the data are skewed/ordinal, use the non-parametric Kruskal–Wallis instead.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Repeated measurements on the same animals need repeated-measures ANOVA (or Friedman), not one-way ANOVA.
22What does p < 0.05 mean, and how do Type I and Type II errors differ?
p < 0.05 = if the null hypothesis (no effect) were true, a difference this large would arise by chance <5% of the time. Type I (α) = false positive (claiming an effect that isn't real); Type II (β) = false negative (missing a real effect). Power = 1 − β.
💎 Practical Pearl: Increase sample size to lower β (raise power) without changing α.
⚠️ Exam Trap: p is NOT the probability the drug works, and a non-significant result doesn't prove "no effect" — it may be underpowered.
23Two test (numerical) variables are related but the data are skewed. Which correlation do you use?
Spearman rank correlation (the non-parametric counterpart of Pearson). It works on ranks, so it tolerates non-normality and ordinal data.
💎 Practical Pearl: Correlation ≠ causation, and a correlation coefficient says nothing about the slope's clinical size.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Pearson assumes a linear relationship and normal data; a strong non-linear relationship can give a near-zero Pearson r.
24On a plasma concentration–time curve, what do Cmax, Tmax, AUC and the terminal slope each represent?
Cmax = peak concentration; Tmax = time to peak (both describe absorption rate/extent); AUC = total systemic exposure (trapezoidal rule); terminal slope on a log scale gives the elimination rate constant k, hence t½ = 0.693/k.
💎 Practical Pearl: Oral bioavailability F = AUCoral/AUCIV; bioequivalence compares AUC and Cmax.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Two formulations can share AUC yet differ in Cmax/Tmax — relevant where onset speed matters.
25Name four drugs that classically require therapeutic drug monitoring and the single feature they share.
Digoxin, lithium, phenytoin, aminoglycosides (also theophylline, vancomycin, cyclosporine). Shared feature: a narrow therapeutic index where plasma level predicts effect/toxicity better than the dose.
💎 Practical Pearl: Phenytoin needs TDM because its zero-order kinetics make small dose changes cause large level changes.
⚠️ Exam Trap: A TDM level is only meaningful with correct timing — steady state reached and the right (usually trough) sample.
26In the eye experiments, contrast the pupil effect of tropicamide, pilocarpine and hyoscine.
Tropicamide (antimuscarinic) → mydriasis + cycloplegia. Hyoscine (antimuscarinic) → mydriasis, reduced salivation, ↑HR. Pilocarpine (muscarinic agonist) → miosis (and lowers intra-ocular pressure).
💎 Practical Pearl: Antimuscarinics dilate (block the sphincter); muscarinic agonists constrict (contract the sphincter).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Mydriatic antimuscarinics can precipitate angle-closure glaucoma in susceptible eyes — a real safety caution in the practical.
27What is microdosing / Phase 0, and why is it valuable?
Administering a sub-pharmacological dose (≈1%) to humans early, using ultra-sensitive assays (e.g., AMS) to read PK, before a full Phase I. It lets a candidate with hopeless human pharmacokinetics be dropped cheaply and with almost no subject risk.
💎 Practical Pearl: Part of the "fail early, fail cheap" philosophy alongside in-silico docking and QSAR.
⚠️ Exam Trap: A microdose reveals PK, not efficacy or safety at therapeutic doses — kinetics need not be linear from a microdose up to the full dose.
28Match the biomedical-waste bin colours (India, 2016) to: a used needle, an expired drug, a soiled IV set, and a broken glass ampoule.
Used needle → White (puncture-proof sharps). Expired drug → Yellow. Soiled IV set (contaminated plastic) → Red. Broken glass ampoule → Blue.
💎 Practical Pearl: Yellow = incineration/deep burial; Red = autoclave→recycle; White = autoclave→shred; Blue = disinfect→recycle.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Segregate at the point of generation; never put sharps into the red plastics bin.
29You treat a cell line with a candidate drug and need to prove the cells die by apoptosis rather than necrosis. Which flow-cytometry assay, and how are the quadrants read? (Module 12)
An Annexin V / PI (or 7-AAD) apoptosis assay. Annexin V binds phosphatidylserine flipped to the outer membrane in early apoptosis; PI enters only once the membrane is compromised (late apoptosis/necrosis). Quadrants: Annexin⁻/PI⁻ = live; Annexin⁺/PI⁻ = early apoptotic; Annexin⁺/PI⁺ = late apoptotic; Annexin⁻/PI⁺ = necrotic.
💎 Practical Pearl: Forward scatter = cell size, side scatter = granularity; fluorescent anti-CD antibodies identify cell type (immunophenotyping).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Requires a single-cell suspension and fluorophore compensation + proper controls — poor gating invents populations.
30In an RT-qPCR experiment, the treated sample has a lower Ct for the target gene than the control. What does that mean? (Module 12)
A lower Ct (cycle threshold) means MORE starting template — fluorescence crosses the threshold in fewer cycles because there was more of that mRNA to begin with. So the target gene is more highly expressed in the treated sample (after normalising to a housekeeping gene by the ΔΔCt method).
💎 Practical Pearl: Workflow: extract RNA → reverse-transcribe to cDNA → amplify with a fluorescent reporter → read Ct.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Genomic-DNA contamination or an unstable housekeeping gene are the classic causes of false expression changes.
31What do forward scatter and side scatter measure in flow cytometry, and how are specific cell types identified? (Module 12)
Forward scatter (FSC) ∝ cell size; side scatter (SSC) ∝ internal granularity/complexity. Specific cell types are identified by fluorophore-tagged antibodies against surface CD markers (immunophenotyping) — e.g. CD4 counts in HIV, leukaemia/lymphoma panels.
💎 Practical Pearl: FACS additionally sorts and physically collects the cells of interest.
32Which assay is used for a quick in-vitro readout of drug cytotoxicity/viability, and what is the principle? (Module 12)
The MTT (or MTS) assay. Metabolically active (living) cells reduce the tetrazolium dye to a coloured formazan product; the absorbance is proportional to the number of viable cells, so a fall in signal indicates cytotoxicity.
💎 Practical Pearl: Other cell-based assays: proliferation (BrdU), reporter-gene, and receptor-binding assays — the front line of in-vitro screening.
33Why must RT-qPCR expression results be normalised to a housekeeping gene? (Module 12)
The amount of total RNA captured and reverse-transcribed varies between samples. A housekeeping gene (e.g. GAPDH, β-actin) is assumed constant across conditions, so expressing the target relative to it (ΔΔCt) corrects for loading differences and isolates the true change in the target.
⚠️ Exam Trap: If the housekeeping gene is itself affected by the treatment, the normalisation is invalid — validate reference genes for your system.
34An exam tracing shows adrenaline raising BP; after a drug is given, adrenaline now lowers BP. Name the phenomenon and the drug class, and state what happens with noradrenaline. (Module 13)
Dale's vasomotor reversal after an α-blocker. Adrenaline acts on both α (vasoconstriction) and β₂ (vasodilation); normally α dominates (BP rises), but with α blocked the β₂ vasodilation is unmasked, so adrenaline now lowers BP. Noradrenaline is NOT reversed — it has negligible β₂ action, so α-blockade merely abolishes its pressor effect.
💎 Practical Pearl: This is the classic demonstration that adrenaline is a mixed α/β agonist.
35Describe the Langendorff preparation and what it is used to measure. (Module 13)
The Langendorff isolated heart is perfused retrogradely down the aorta, which closes the aortic valve and drives perfusate into the coronary arteries, keeping the heart beating ex vivo. It measures heart rate, contractile force and coronary flow free of systemic reflexes — used to screen inotropes/chronotropes and study ischaemia-reperfusion/cardioprotection.
💎 Practical Pearl: Because it removes neural/hormonal confounders, effects seen are direct actions on the myocardium/coronaries.
36Explain how a four-point (2+2) bioassay estimates the potency of an unknown, and the key validity requirement. (Module 13)
Two doses of the standard and two of the test are applied to a sensitive isolated tissue to construct two log dose–response lines. If the lines are parallel (same mechanism), the horizontal distance between them gives the potency ratio, from which the unknown's concentration is calculated.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Non-parallel lines invalidate the assay — they indicate the standard and test are not acting by the same mechanism.
37Define pA₂ and what a higher pA₂ indicates, using atropine vs acetylcholine as the example. (Module 13)
pA₂ is the negative logarithm of the molar concentration of a competitive antagonist that requires a doubling of the agonist dose to restore the original response (a dose-ratio of 2). A higher pA₂ = a more potent antagonist. For atropine antagonising acetylcholine on guinea-pig ileum, the pA₂ quantifies atropine's affinity.
💎 Practical Pearl: pA₂ is derived from a Schild plot; for a purely competitive antagonist the Schild slope ≈ 1.
38At a PK station you must give a loading and maintenance regimen. State the two formulae and the parameters each needs. (Module 13)
Loading dose = Vd × target plasma concentration ÷ bioavailability (F) — achieves the target level immediately (needs Vd). Maintenance dosing rate = CL × target concentration ÷ F — replaces what is cleared (needs clearance). Half-life (t½ = 0.693 × Vd ÷ CL) sets the dosing interval and time to steady state (~4–5 half-lives).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Loading dose depends on Vd (not clearance); maintenance rate depends on clearance (not Vd) — mixing these up is the classic error.

Loading dose depends on Vd; maintenance rate depends on clearance.
