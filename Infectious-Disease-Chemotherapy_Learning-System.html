General Principles of Antimicrobial Therapy

🎯 Core clinical pharmacokinetics & resistance mechanics
Core Foundation
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
How well an antimicrobial works clinically comes down to its pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic (PK/PD) index — getting the right drug exposure relative to the pathogen's Minimum Inhibitory Concentration (MIC) is what dosing strategy, resistance prevention, and ultimately treatment success all rest on.

The Three PK/PD Efficacy Indices

IndexDescriptionAntibiotic ClassesClinical Dosing Strategy
Cmax / MICConcentration-dependent killing: the higher the peak level, the faster bacteria die.Aminoglycosides, Daptomycin, FluoroquinolonesHigh-dose, once-daily (pushes the peak up while keeping toxicity in check).
T > MICTime-dependent killing: what matters is how long the concentration stays above the MIC.β-lactams (Penicillins, Ceph, Carbapenems)Frequent dosing or extended/continuous infusions (keeps levels above MIC longer).
AUC24 / MICExposure-dependent killing: governed by the total drug exposure accumulated across 24 hours.Vancomycin, Tetracyclines, Macrolides, LinezolidTune the total daily dose (often with therapeutic drug monitoring as a guide).

Major Mechanisms of Bacterial Resistance

  • Enzymatic Inactivation: The drug gets chemically destroyed before it can act. E.g., β-lactamases cleaving penicillin rings; aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes (acetyltransferases).
  • Target Site Modification: The drug's binding target changes shape so the drug binds less tightly. E.g., mutations in gyrA (quinolone resistance); mecA gene encoding PBP2a (MRSA); VanA gene converting D-Ala-D-Ala to D-Ala-D-Lac (VRE).
  • Reduced Permeability: The bacterium shuts the doors the drug would enter through. E.g., porin loss in Gram-negative outer membranes (blocks carbapenems).
  • Active Efflux: The bacterium actively ejects the drug once it gets in. E.g., Tet efflux pumps in Enterobacterales.

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism & Dosing Dynamics

β-lactams kill purely on a time-dependent basis (T > MIC). Past about four times the pathogen's MIC, pushing the concentration any higher buys you nothing extra in killing speed. On top of that, β-lactams carry essentially no post-antibiotic effect (PAE) against Gram-negative rods such as Pseudomonas — so the moment blood levels dip below the MIC between doses, the organism starts growing again right away. Stretching the infusion out (say, to 4 hours) holds the concentration above the MIC for a far greater share of the dosing interval compared with a quick 30-minute bolus, and that translates into better cure rates in serious infections.

💡 The Analogy — The Drowning vs. The Siege

Picture bacterial killing as a matter of keeping the enemy underwater.

  • Concentration-dependent drugs (like aminoglycosides) work like a single depth charge: one enormous blast (high $C_{max}$) sends a shockwave that wipes out the target instantly, and the effect lingers even once the water settles back down (PAE).
  • Time-dependent drugs (like β-lactams) work like filling a flooding chamber: the water level has to stay just over their heads (the MIC line) for as much time as possible. A brief, forceful spray (bolus) drains away through the pipes wastefully, while a slow, steady, extended flow keeps them submerged continuously without needing a larger total volume.

⚠️ Warning — The Bacteriostatic vs. Bactericidal Myth: Boards love to separate bactericidal agents (kill bacteria outright) from bacteriostatic ones (just halt growth), but whether that distinction actually matters depends on the host. In someone with a functioning immune system, bacteriostatic drugs work just as well. The exception is neutropenic patients, or those with endocarditis or meningitis — there, bactericidal drugs become mandatory because the host's own defenses can't pick up the slack in clearing the organism.

DNA Disruptors — Sulfonamides, Quinolones & Nitroimidazoles

Chapters 57 · replication blocks · folic acid & topoisomerase targets
Core Class
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
There are two main routes to shutting down DNA replication: cutting off the supply of nucleotide building blocks (via the folate pathway) or jamming the replication hardware directly (via topoisomerase blockers and agents that break DNA strands).

The Three Major Classes

  • Folate Antagonists (Sulfonamides & Trimethoprim):
    • Mechanism: Sulfamethoxazole (SMX) mimics PABA and blocks dihydropteroate synthase (DHPS); Trimethoprim (TMP) separately blocks dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR).
    • Synergy: Pairing them as TMP-SMX (Bactrim) produces a synergistic sequential blockade that shuts down tetrahydrofolate synthesis and, with it, thymidine production.
    • Key ADRs: Crystalluria, Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS/TEN), hemolytic anemia in those with G6PD deficiency, neonatal kernicterus (bilirubin gets displaced off albumin), and hyperkalemia (TMP blocks collecting-duct ENaC channels).
  • Fluoroquinolones ("-floxacin": Ciprofloxacin, Levofloxacin, Moxifloxacin):
    • Mechanism: Block DNA gyrase (stops supercoiled DNA from relaxing — the main target in Gram-negatives) and Topoisomerase IV (stops daughter chromosomes from separating — the main target in Gram-positives).
    • Key ADRs: Tendonitis and tendon rupture (from collagen breakdown), QTc prolongation (via hERG $K^+$ channel block), aortic aneurysm/dissection, dysglycemia, and cartilage toxicity (so avoid in children and pregnancy).
  • Nitroimidazoles (Metronidazole, Tinidazole):
    • Mechanism: Inactive until activated — anaerobic organisms reduce the drug's nitro group via pyruvate:ferredoxin oxidoreductase (PFOR), generating toxic free radicals that break DNA strands.
    • Key ADRs: Metallic taste, an alcohol-triggered disulfiram-like reaction (aldehyde dehydrogenase blockade), and peripheral neuropathy.

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism & High-Yield ADR Interactions

Both TMP-SMX and Fluoroquinolones strongly inhibit CYP2C9 — the enzyme mainly tasked with breaking down S-warfarin, the more active isomer. Block CYP2C9 and warfarin levels climb sharply, sending the INR up and risking life-threatening hemorrhage. Metronidazole adds a third culprit, since it also inhibits CYP2C9 — together these three common antimicrobial classes form a "triple trap" for warfarin patients. As a precaution, cut the warfarin dose by roughly 30-50% empirically whenever starting any of them.

💡 The Analogy — Cutting the Power vs. Jamming the Gears

Picture the bacterium as a factory churning out copies of its blueprint (DNA):

  • Folate antagonists (Bactrim) work by cutting the ink supply (nucleotides) — no folate means no raw material left to print new DNA.
  • Fluoroquinolones work by jamming the spooling gears. Untwisting the DNA strands during replication needs a detangler — DNA gyrase. Quinolones freeze that enzyme in place, snapping the chromosome with double-stranded breaks.
  • Metronidazole behaves like a magnetic storm that only activates inside anaerobic cells. Its nitro group turns into a highly reactive radical that tears the DNA helix apart beyond repair.

⚠️ Warning — The G6PD Hemolysis Trap: Sulfonamides (along with dapsone) act as strong oxidizing agents. People with G6PD deficiency can't regenerate enough NADPH to keep glutathione levels up, so when their red cells encounter sulfonamides, the result is oxidative damage, Heinz body formation, and potentially severe hemolytic anemia. Screen anyone at elevated risk before starting therapy.

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism & Surfactant Inactivation

Pneumonia is an absolute no for daptomycin. It works well against MRSA in the bloodstream and most tissues, but the alveoli are a different story — pulmonary surfactant immediately soaks it up and inactivates it. The drug's lipid tail binds surfactant phospholipids instead of the bacterial membrane, so it never gets where it needs to go. For MRSA pneumonia, reach for Linezolid (penetrates lung tissue well) or Vancomycin (the standard IV option).

💡 The Analogy — Bricklayers and Balloon Poppers

Picture the bacterial outer wall as a brick house holding back high water pressure:

  • β-lactams work by stealing the mortar from the bricklayers (transpeptidase / PBPs). The wall keeps getting built without mortar, and the internal osmotic pressure eventually bursts it open.
  • Vancomycin works by capping the ends of the bricks (D-Ala-D-Ala). The bricklayers are still on the job, but they have nothing to hook the bricks together with. Since vancomycin is a bulky molecule, it can't get through the outer membrane of Gram-negative organisms — hence its strictly Gram-positive reach.
  • Daptomycin works more like puncturing a balloon. It ignores the brick wall entirely and drives a calcium anchor straight into the inner membrane, opening a channel that potassium pours out of. Lose that electrical gradient and the cell dies almost immediately.

⚠️ Warning — The Ceftazidime vs. Penicillin Allergy Exception: Even though penicillin-cephalosporin cross-reactivity is generally low (around 3%), Aztreonam (a monobactam) stands out as the one β-lactam considered safe for patients with documented penicillin anaphylaxis. There's a catch, though: Aztreonam's side chain is identical to Ceftazidime's, so a patient with a confirmed ceftazidime allergy should not receive Aztreonam — cross-reactivity there approaches 100%.

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism & High-Yield Toxicities

Linezolid is a weak, reversible inhibitor of Monoamine Oxidase (MAO). When combined with serotonergic agents like SSRIs (sertraline, fluoxetine) or SNRI antidepressants, it prevents the breakdown of serotonin, leading to Serotonin Syndrome (characterized by autonomic instability, hyperreflexia, and clonus). Linezolid also causes myelosuppression (specifically thrombocytopenia) and mitochondrial toxicity (lactic acidosis, optic neuropathy) during extended courses (longer than 10-14 days).

💡 The Analogy — Ribosomal Assembly Line Hijacks

Think of bacterial protein synthesis as an automated assembly line rolling out polypeptides:

  • Aminoglycosides act like corrupting the barcode scanner. They bind the 30S subunit and cause it to read the wrong codes, producing useless, mutated proteins that accumulate and rupture the cell membrane.
  • Tetracyclines act like blocking the input docks. They prevent new cargo trucks (tRNA carrying amino acids) from parking at the ribosome's A site.
  • Macrolides and Clindamycin act like jamming the conveyor belt. They prevent the growing chain from sliding forward (blocking translocation or peptide bond formation).
  • Linezolid acts by locking the factory doors. It prevents the two subunits (30S and 50S) from uniting to form the active 70S assembly line.

⚠️ Warning — Aminoglycoside Uptake & Resistance: Aminoglycoside transport across the bacterial inner membrane is an oxygen-dependent process powered by the electron transport chain. Therefore, aminoglycosides have zero activity against anaerobes (e.g., Bacteroides, Clostridium). Furthermore, their nephrotoxicity is due to drug accumulation in renal cortical cells, which is why once-daily high-dose strategies are preferred (saturates renal uptake transporters while maximizing bactericidal peak concentration).

Antifungal Chemotherapy — Polyenes, Azoles & Echinocandins

Chapter 61 · ergosterol & glucan synthesis blocks
Core Class
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Fungal cells differ from mammalian cells by containing ergosterol (instead of cholesterol) in their cell membrane and a protective chitin/glucan cell wall. Antifungals target these structural differences to achieve selective toxicity.

The Four Key Classes

  • Polyenes (Amphotericin B, Nystatin):
    • Mechanism: Binds directly to ergosterol, forming transmembrane pores that cause rapid leakage of ions ($K^+, Mg^{2+}$) and cell death.
    • ADRs: Infusion reactions ("shake and bake" fevers, chills), nephrotoxicity (causes renal vasoconstriction and direct tubular injury), and severe hypokalemia/hypomagnesemia (renal wasting).
  • Azoles (Fluconazole, Itraconazole, Voriconazole, Posaconazole, Isavuconazole):
    • Mechanism: Inhibits 14α-demethylase (a fungal CYP enzyme, CYP51), blocking the conversion of lanosterol to ergosterol.
    • ADRs: Hepatotoxicity, QTc prolongation (except Isavuconazole, which shortens it), visual disturbances (transient blurry vision/color flashes with Voriconazole), and drug-drug interactions (inhibits host CYP3A4).
  • Echinocandins ("-fungin": Caspofungin, Micafungin, Anidulafungin):
    • Mechanism: Inhibits β-(1,3)-D-glucan synthase, blocking the synthesis of the essential cell wall component glucan.
    • ADRs: Extremely well-tolerated. Minor infusion-related histamine release.
  • Flucytosine (5-Fluorocytosine / 5-FC):
    • Mechanism: Deaminated by fungal cytosine deaminase to 5-fluorouracil (5-FU), which blocks thymidylate synthase and DNA synthesis.
    • ADRs: Reversible bone marrow suppression (thrombocytopenia, leukopenia).

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism & Amphotericin Nephrotoxicity

Amphotericin B causes nephrotoxicity via two pathways: 1. Renal Vasoconstriction: Direct constriction of the afferent arterioles, dropping GFR. 2. Tubular Injury: The drug binds to cholesterol in human renal tubular cells (with lower affinity than ergosterol, but still significant), forming pores in the distal convoluted tubule and collecting duct. This causes hydrogen, potassium, and magnesium to leak out into the urine, leading to renal tubular acidosis, hypokalemia, and hypomagnesemia. Prevention: Pre-infusion saline loading (1L normal saline) restores renal blood flow and dramatically limits nephrotoxicity.

💡 The Analogy — The Wall, the Blueprint, and the Bricks

Think of fungal protection as a double-layered barrier:

  • Polyenes (Amphotericin B) act like drilling physical holes in the inner membrane by binding the ergosterol lipids.
  • Azoles act like shutting down the factory that manufactures ergosterol. Without ergosterol, the membrane becomes leaky and fragile.
  • Echinocandins act like eating the outer brick wall. They block the glucan mortar, leaving the cell membrane vulnerable to swelling and osmotic burst.
  • Flucytosine acts like a toxic blueprint copy. Fungal cells convert 5-FC to 5-FU, which jams the DNA-building machinery, while human cells lack the cytosine deaminase enzyme to activate the drug.

⚠️ Warning — The Voriconazole Visual Phenotype: Voriconazole causes transient visual disturbances (altered color perception, flashes of light, photophobia) in up to 30% of patients. This is due to reversible inhibition of CYP-mediated retinoid metabolism in the retina. Educate patients that this effect is benign and resolves rapidly.

Antivirals — Herpes, Influenza & Viral Hepatitis (HBV/HCV)

Chapters 62 & 63 · replication stops · thymidine kinase & direct-acting HCV blocks
Core Classes
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Viruses are obligate intracellular parasites that hijack host machinery. Selective antiviral drugs target viral-specific enzymes, such as viral thymidine kinases, viral neuraminidases, and viral hepatitis polymerases/proteases.

Anti-Herpesvirus & CMV Agents

  • Acyclovir & Valacyclovir (HSV/VZV):
    • Mechanism: Acyclovir is phosphorylated to acyclovir monophosphate by viral thymidine kinase (TK). Host kinases then convert it to acyclovir triphosphate. This analogue competes for viral DNA polymerase, causing chain termination when incorporated into the growing DNA strand.
    • Valacyclovir: Valyl ester prodrug. 3- to 5-fold higher oral bioavailability than acyclovir.
    • ADRs: Crystalline nephropathy (IV formulation; precipitate in tubules. Prevent with hydration).
  • Ganciclovir & Valganciclovir (CMV):
    • Mechanism: Phosphorylated by CMV UL97 kinase, leading to viral DNA polymerase inhibition.
    • ADRs: Severe bone marrow suppression (neutropenia, anemia, thrombocytopenia).

Anti-Influenza Agents

  • Oseltamivir (Tamiflu) & Zanamivir: Neuraminidase inhibitors. Prevent sialic acid cleavage, trapping newly replicated virions on the host cell membrane and preventing release.
  • Baloxavir Marboxil: Cap-dependent endonuclease inhibitor. Blocks viral transcription initiation.

Hepatitis B & C Agents

  • Hepatitis B (HBV): Treated with nucleoside/tide reverse transcriptase inhibitors (Tenofovir, Entecavir), which suppress viral replication indefinitely but rarely achieve cure (due to persistent cccDNA in hepatocytes).
  • Hepatitis C (HCV): Curable with direct-acting antivirals (DAAs):
    • NS3/4A Protease Inhibitors ("-previr" e.g., Glecaprevir): Prevent processing of viral polyprotein.
    • NS5A Replication Complex Inhibitors ("-asvir" e.g., Velpatasvir): Block viral assembly.
    • NS5B Polymerase Inhibitors ("-buvir" e.g., Sofosbuvir): Terminate RNA synthesis.

📝 Section 3 · Plain-Language Notes

Mechanism & Selectivity of Action

Acyclovir's selectivity is due to viral thymidine kinase (TK). Human cells do not contain this enzyme. Unphosphorylated acyclovir cannot interact with DNA polymerase or cause chain termination. Thus, only cells infected with HSV or VZV express the TK enzyme that converts acyclovir to its active form, leaving healthy bystander cells unharmed. Resistance: Occurs due to mutations in the viral TK gene. If TK is mutated, acyclovir cannot be phosphorylated. To bypass this, we use Foscarnet or Cidofovir, which do not require activation by viral TK to inhibit viral DNA polymerase.

💡 The Analogy — The Trojan Horse and the Sticky Net

Think of viral replication and release:

  • Acyclovir acts like a Trojan Horse bricks. It looks like a normal nucleotide, but only the viral commander (thymidine kinase) accepts it and places it in the wall. Once placed, it has no connector pins, halting further wall construction (chain termination).
  • Oseltamivir acts like gluing the exit doors. The influenza virus replicates successfully inside the cell, but neuraminidase acts like scissors that clip the virus free from the cell membrane. Oseltamivir jams these scissors, leaving the virions stuck to the host cell surface.
  • HCV DAAs act like a multi-pronged assembly line shut down. The protease inhibitors ("-previr") cut the power to the shears that split the raw proteins, the NS5A blockers ("-asvir") break the shipping boxes, and the NS5B blockers ("-buvir") jam the printer printing viral RNA.

⚠️ Warning — The Acyclovir Hydration Rule: Intravenous acyclovir has low solubility in urine. In dehydrated patients, it quickly crystallizes in the renal collecting tubules, causing acute kidney injury (obstructive nephropathy). Always co-administer IV fluids and ensure the patient remains well hydrated during therapy.

Mechanism & Genetic Determinants of ADRs

Abacavir Hypersensitivity is strictly linked to HLA-B*5701. Abacavir binds with high specificity to the peptide-binding groove of the HLA-B*5701 protein, changing the shape of the antigen-binding cleft. This causes the immune system to recognize self-peptides as foreign, initiating a massive T-cell-mediated hypersensitivity reaction (characterized by fever, rash, GI distress, and respiratory symptoms). Rule: Screening is mandatory. Carrying the allele is a absolute contraindication. If a patient experiences a reaction, never rechallenge them, as subsequent exposure can cause fatal anaphylactic shock.

💡 The Analogy — The Copy Machine and the Assembly Booster

Think of the HIV cycle inside the host cell:

  • NRTIs act like inserting blank keys into the key cutting machine. The machine grabs the key, but because it is blank and missing the back ring (lacks 3'-OH group), the machine gets stuck and cannot cut any more keys (obligate chain termination).
  • NNRTIs act like sticking a wedge under the machine's gears. They do not mimic keys; they bind next to the gear, warping its shape so it cannot spin (allosteric inhibition).
  • INSTIs act like stealing the staple gun. The viral DNA is printed, but it cannot be stapled (integrated) into the host's chromosome.
  • Protease Inhibitors act like dulling the scissors at the packaging line. The virus is built as a long raw protein chain. Protease normally clips this chain into active enzymes. Inhibiting it yields immature, useless viral packages.

⚠️ Warning — The Ritonavir / Cobicistat CYP Interaction: Ritonavir and Cobicistat are used in low doses solely as "boosters" because they are potent inhibitors of CYP3A4. By blocking CYP3A4, they prevent the degradation of co-administered protease inhibitors (like darunavir), keeping their plasma levels high. However, this also blocks the metabolism of other CYP3A4 substrates, leading to toxicities with drugs like statins, amiodarone, or benzodiazepines.

Mycobacterial & Antiparasitic Chemotherapy

Chapters 65–68 · mycobacteria, malaria, protozoa & helminth targets
Core Classes
🧭 Section 1 · The 80/20 High-Yield Map
Parasitic and mycobacterial pathogens require specialized agents that target their unique lipid membranes, metabolic pathways, and neuromuscular systems.

1. Tuberculosis First-Line Regimen: "RIPE"

  • Isoniazid (INH):
    • Mechanism: Activated by mycobacterial KatG (catalase-peroxidase); inhibits InhA (enoyl-ACP reductase), blocking mycolic acid synthesis (disrupts cell wall).
    • ADRs: Peripheral neuropathy (INH binds pyridoxal phosphate, causing Vitamin B6 deficiency; prevent by administering pyridoxine), hepatotoxicity, drug-induced lupus.
  • Rifampin (RIF):
    • Mechanism: Inhibits bacterial DNA-dependent RNA polymerase (blocks transcription).
    • ADRs: Orange/red discoloration of urine, tears, and sweat; potent CYP inducer (lowers levels of oral contraceptives, warfarin, etc.).
  • Pyrazinamide (PZA):
    • Mechanism: Active in the acidic phagosomes of macrophages. Disrupts membrane potential.
    • ADRs: Hepatotoxicity, hyperuricemia (inhibits renal uric acid excretion, provoking gout).
  • Ethambutol (EMB):
    • Mechanism: Inhibits arabinosyltransferase, blocking cell wall arabinogalactan synthesis.
    • ADRs: Optic neuritis (reversible loss of visual acuity, red-green color blindness).

2. Malaria Chemotherapy

  • Artemisinins (Artesunate, Artemether): Activated by iron (heme) in the parasite food vacuole to generate free radicals that damage parasite proteins. Standard first-line in combination therapy (ACT).
  • Chloroquine: Blocks heme biocrystallization into non-toxic hemozoin. Free heme accumulates, lysing the parasite. Resistance is mediated by the PfCRT transporter, which pumps chloroquine out.
  • Primaquine & Tafenoquine: Eradicates hypnozoites (dormant liver stage) of P. vivax and P. ovale. Must screen for G6PD deficiency prior to administration to avoid severe hemolytic anemia.

3. Antiprotozoal & Antihelminthic Agents

  • Metronidazole / Tinidazole: Radical-mediated DNA strand breakers (amebiasis, giardiasis, trichomoniasis).
  • Albendazole / Mebendazole: Inhibits β-tubulin polymerization, blocking microtubule assembly and glucose uptake in worms.
  • Ivermectin: Activates glutamate-gated chloride channels in invertebrate muscle/nerve cells, causing hyperpolarization and paralysis. Used for strongyloidiasis, scabies, filariasis.
  • Praziquantel: Increases cell membrane permeability to calcium, causing muscle contraction and paralysis of tapeworms and flukes (schistosomes).

Mechanism & G6PD Toxicity Constraints

Primaquine selectively targets liver hypnozoites (the dormant stage of P. vivax and P. ovale). However, primaquine generates highly reactive oxygen species inside red blood cells. Normally, RBCs use G6PD to produce NADPH, which maintains glutathione in its reduced state to neutralize oxidative radicals. In G6PD-deficient patients, the oxidative stress from primaquine destroys hemoglobin, forming Heinz bodies and causing acute intravascular hemolytic anemia. Always screen for G6PD activity prior to starting therapy.

💡 The Analogy — Neuromuscular and Metabolic Shuts Downs

Think of destroying these complex pathogens:

  • Isoniazid acts like sabotaging the concrete mixer that makes the armor (mycolic acid) for mycobacteria, making their cell walls weak and leaky.
  • Chloroquine acts by poisoning the parasite with its own waste. The malaria parasite digests hemoglobin, releasing toxic heme. It normally crystallizes this waste into non-toxic rocks (hemozoin). Chloroquine blocks the crystal-maker, letting the toxic heme build up and dissolve the parasite.
  • Albendazole acts by melting the skeleton. By blocking microtubule polymerization, the worm loses its cell shape, transport systems, and ability to absorb glucose.
  • Ivermectin and Praziquantel act like different forms of paralysis. Ivermectin locks chloride channels open, freezing the worm's muscles in a relaxed state (flaccid paralysis); Praziquantel floods the muscles with calcium, freezing them in a continuous spasm (spastic paralysis).

⚠️ Warning — The Rifampin Drug-Induction Trap: Rifampin is one of the most potent inducers of cytochrome P450 enzymes (specifically CYP3A4, 2C9, and 2C19) in clinical medicine. It dramatically speeds up the metabolism of co-administered drugs, making them ineffective. Key targets: oral contraceptive pills (leads to unplanned pregnancy), warfarin (spikes clot risk), and protease inhibitors in HIV. Substitute with Rifabutin in HIV patients, as it has less CYP-inducing potency.

01 A 25-year-old woman with penicillin allergy (documented anaphylaxis) needs cefazolin for surgical prophylaxis. Her surgeon says the 10% cross-reactivity rule means she cannot have it. Is this correct?
No — the oft-cited 10% figure is based on outdated skin-test data using impure penicillin reagents. Modern evidence shows true cross-reactivity between penicillins and cephalosporins is ≤2%, and cross-reactivity depends on shared R1 side chains, not the shared beta-lactam ring. Cefazolin has a unique R1 side chain structurally distinct from penicillins — its cross-reactivity risk is extremely low (<0.01%) even in confirmed penicillin allergy. Most infectious disease guidelines now endorse cefazolin use in penicillin-allergic patients after careful risk assessment.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Ampicillin and cefadroxil share identical R1 side chains — avoid cefadroxil in ampicillin-allergic patients. Amoxicillin and cefprozil/cefaclor also share similar side chains. Side-chain matching (not the beta-lactam ring) predicts true cross-reactivity.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Aztreonam (monobactam) has essentially NO cross-reactivity with penicillins — EXCEPT with ceftazidime, which shares an identical R1 side chain. In cystic fibrosis patients allergic to ceftazidime, aztreonam must also be avoided.
02 A patient on tobramycin for Pseudomonas pneumonia develops rising creatinine and bilateral high-frequency hearing loss on day 8. Explain why the hearing loss is irreversible while the kidney injury often recovers.
Both toxicities are trough-level dependent (accumulation-driven). Nephrotoxicity: aminoglycosides accumulate in proximal tubule cells → oxidative stress → cell necrosis. Proximal tubule cells can regenerate from stem cells at the S3 segment → renal function usually recovers after stopping the drug. Ototoxicity: aminoglycosides accumulate in cochlear outer hair cells → oxidative damage → cell death. Cochlear hair cells cannot regenerate in humans → permanent high-frequency sensorineural hearing loss.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Once-daily extended-interval aminoglycoside dosing (high peak for concentration-dependent killing, long drug-free interval for toxicity clearance from inner ear/tubules) has lower ototoxicity and nephrotoxicity rates than traditional dosing. Use the Hartford nomogram for monitoring.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Vestibulotoxicity (balance, oscillopsia) may present separately from cochleotoxicity (hearing loss) — both irreversible. Screen with audiometry baseline and periodically for courses >5 days. Never resume an aminoglycoside that caused confirmed ototoxicity regardless of the clinical need.
03 A 16-year-old football player is prescribed ciprofloxacin for a UTI. Two weeks later he ruptures his Achilles tendon during training. What is the mechanism, and who is at highest risk?
Fluoroquinolone-induced tendinopathy: fluoroquinolones chelate Mg²⁺ in tenocytes → inhibit mitochondrial function and matrix metalloprotease regulatory pathways → collagen matrix degradation + impaired repair → tendon degeneration. The Achilles tendon is most commonly affected. Risk factors: age >60, concurrent corticosteroids (dramatically increases risk), renal failure, prior tendon injury, solid organ transplant recipients (often on steroids). Fluoroquinolones are generally avoided in children (also impair developing cartilage).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Additional fluoroquinolone black-box warnings: QT prolongation, peripheral neuropathy (may be permanent), CNS effects (seizures, psychosis in elderly), aortic aneurysm/dissection. The FDA emphasises reserving fluoroquinolones for infections with no equally effective alternative.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Ciprofloxacin chelates divalent cations (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺, Fe²⁺, Zn²⁺) in the GI tract → reduced absorption by up to 90%. Must be taken 2 hours before or 6 hours after antacids, dairy products, iron, or calcium supplements — a frequently missed cause of treatment failure.
04 Vancomycin is infused over 15 minutes and the patient develops diffuse facial and truncal erythema, pruritus, and hypotension — but no urticaria and no wheeze. What is this, and how is it managed?
Red man syndrome (RMS) — a rate-dependent, non-IgE-mediated direct mast cell degranulation reaction. Vancomycin (and ciprofloxacin, amphotericin B) directly degranulate mast cells → histamine release → vasodilation, flushing, pruritus, and hypotension. Management: (1) Stop infusion immediately; (2) Give diphenhydramine IV; (3) When resolved, restart infusion at half the rate (≤10 mg/min, standard is 1 g over 60 minutes). Pre-treat with antihistamine if history of RMS.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Document RMS as 'infusion reaction', NOT as 'vancomycin allergy' — it is not immune-mediated and the patient can safely receive vancomycin again with slower infusion. Mislabelling it as an allergy leads to unnecessary use of inferior alternatives.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Vancomycin nephrotoxicity is now monitored using AUC-guided dosing (target AUC₂₄/MIC ≥400 mg·h/L) rather than trough-only monitoring — trough-only dosing led to over-dosing and nephrotoxicity. Vancomycin + piperacillin-tazobactam combination is associated with synergistic nephrotoxicity.
05 Linezolid is started for VRE bacteraemia. The patient is also on sertraline for depression. Two days later she develops fever, agitation, diaphoresis, tremor, and bilateral lower limb clonus. What happened?
Serotonin syndrome — linezolid is a weak, reversible MAO inhibitor (inhibits MAO-A predominantly). Combined with sertraline (SSRI → blocks 5-HT reuptake), synaptic serotonin levels become dangerously elevated → hyperthermia, autonomic instability, neuromuscular hyperactivity (clonus, hyperreflexia, tremor). Serotonin syndrome triad: altered mental status + autonomic instability + neuromuscular abnormalities. Management: stop both drugs; cyproheptadine (5-HT antagonist); supportive care; benzodiazepines for agitation.
💎 Clinical Pearl: If VRE infection requires linezolid and a serotonergic drug cannot be stopped, use daptomycin as an alternative for non-pulmonary VRE (daptomycin is NOT an MAOI). Note: daptomycin is inactivated by surfactant → ineffective for VRE pneumonia.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Linezolid long-term toxicity (beyond 4 weeks): myelosuppression (monitor CBC weekly), lactic acidosis (mitochondrial protein synthesis inhibition — same mechanism as bacteria but affects mitochondrial ribosomes), peripheral and optic neuropathy. These are NOT serotonin-related.
06 A TB patient on rifampin/isoniazid/pyrazinamide/ethambutol (RIPE) develops jaundice, AST 12× ULN, and nausea at week 6. What do you do?
Drug-induced hepatitis from RIPE therapy — hold ALL four anti-TB drugs immediately when AST/ALT >3× ULN with symptoms, or >5× ULN without symptoms. Determine which drug is responsible (usually INH, PZA, or rifampin). After LFTs normalise to <2× ULN, reintroduce sequentially: start with rifampin (least hepatotoxic) → add isoniazid after 1 week → consider omitting pyrazinamide if it was the culprit. Monitor LFTs after each reintroduction.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Pyrazinamide is the most hepatotoxic first-line TB drug and the most likely cause if hepatitis occurs in the first 2 months. If PZA cannot be reintroduced, extend treatment duration (9–12 months instead of 6). Baseline LFTs before RIPE therapy and monthly monitoring are essential.
⚠️ Exam Trap: A mild transient rise in bilirubin in the first 2 weeks of rifampin therapy is common — rifampin competes with bilirubin for hepatic excretion (not true hepatotoxicity). This alone does NOT require stopping treatment. Only a rise in transaminases indicates hepatocellular damage.
07 Acyclovir is infused for herpes simplex encephalitis. On day 3, creatinine rises from 0.9 to 2.8 mg/dL. Explain the nephrotoxicity mechanism and how to prevent it.
Acyclovir crystallises in renal tubules at high concentrations — crystal nephropathy. Acyclovir is poorly soluble at physiological urine pH; if the drug concentrates (dehydration, rapid infusion, CKD) it precipitates in the distal tubule and collecting duct → tubular obstruction → AKI. Prevention: adequate IV hydration (ensure urine output ≥1 mL/kg/h), infuse each dose over ≥1 hour, avoid concurrent nephrotoxins. Usually reversible with hydration and dose reduction.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Acyclovir resistance in HSV/VZV: most commonly due to mutations in viral thymidine kinase (TK) gene → TK-deficient strains cannot phosphorylate acyclovir. Treatment: foscarnet (directly inhibits viral DNA polymerase — does not require TK activation) or cidofovir.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Valacyclovir is the oral prodrug of acyclovir (L-valyl ester → acyclovir after first-pass hydrolysis) with ~55% oral bioavailability vs acyclovir's ~10–20%. Same mechanism and toxicity profile — the same dose (in mg of acyclovir equivalent) carries the same crystal nephropathy risk if patient is dehydrated.
08 An HIV-positive patient on tenofovir/emtricitabine/efavirenz starts rifampin for TB. His HIV viral load rebounds 4 weeks later. Explain.
Rifampin is a potent CYP3A4 and P-gp inducer. Efavirenz (NNRTI) is a CYP3A4 substrate — rifampin reduces efavirenz AUC by ~25%, potentially falling below therapeutic levels. However, efavirenz is the most rifampin-tolerant NNRTI; the standard approach is to increase efavirenz dose to 800 mg (from 600 mg) in patients ≥60 kg. Alternatively, switch to a double-dose dolutegravir (50 mg BID) regimen — dolutegravir is induced by rifampin but the higher dose compensates and is currently preferred.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Rifampin + protease inhibitors (PIs): absolutely contraindicated — rifampin reduces PI levels by 75–90%, leading to virological failure and resistance. Use rifabutin (weaker CYP inducer) instead of rifampin when PI-based ART cannot be changed.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Rifabutin is NOT rifampin — it is a distinct drug with much weaker CYP3A4 induction and can be used alongside most PIs (with rifabutin dose adjustment to 150 mg 3×/week when boosted with ritonavir). Never substitute rifabutin for rifampin in TB treatment without infectious disease input.
09 Amphotericin B deoxycholate is started for cryptococcal meningitis. On day 4, K⁺ = 2.6, Mg²⁺ = 0.5, creatinine = 2.9 mg/dL. Explain all three abnormalities.
All three result from amphotericin B's mechanism: it binds ergosterol in fungal membranes forming pores → fungal cell lysis. It also (less avidly) binds cholesterol in mammalian cell membranes: (1) Distal tubule pores → K⁺ and Mg²⁺ wasting (renal tubular acidosis type 1, distal); (2) Afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction → reduced GFR → nephrotoxicity. Prevention: aggressive saline pre-hydration (1 L 0.9% NaCl before each dose), daily electrolyte replacement, consider switching to liposomal amphotericin B (AmBisome) — 5–10× less nephrotoxic.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Liposomal amphotericin B (L-AmB) preferentially distributes to sites of fungal infection (where ergosterol-rich membranes attract liposomes) vs healthy tissues — significantly reduced renal and infusion-related toxicity. Use L-AmB when nephrotoxicity risk is high or CrCl falls below 25 mL/min.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Amphotericin B infusion reactions (fever, chills, rigors 1–3 h after infusion start) are very common (~80%) and NOT the same as nephrotoxicity. Pre-medicate with paracetamol, diphenhydramine, and hydrocortisone. Meperidine (pethidine) is used for severe rigors. These reactions lessen after the first few infusions.
10 A patient with P. vivax malaria is treated with chloroquine. To prevent relapse from liver hypnozoites, primaquine is prescribed. Before giving it, you must check one specific test. What and why?
Check G6PD activity before primaquine. Primaquine is an oxidant drug that generates reactive oxygen species — in G6PD-deficient RBCs (unable to replenish NADPH via hexose monophosphate shunt), oxidative stress → Heinz body formation → haemolytic anaemia, potentially severe or fatal. G6PD deficiency is most prevalent in malaria-endemic regions (Africa, Mediterranean, SE Asia, Middle East) — exactly the populations who need radical cure.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Tafenoquine (single-dose 8-aminoquinoline for radical cure) requires quantitative G6PD testing (not just qualitative) before use — it causes more severe haemolysis than primaquine at borderline G6PD levels. Tafenoquine is contraindicated if G6PD activity <70% of normal.
⚠️ Exam Trap: P. falciparum has NO hypnozoites — radical cure with primaquine is NOT needed for falciparum malaria. Primaquine is required only for P. vivax and P. ovale (hypnozoite-forming species). Confusing this is a classic exam error.
11 A patient on metronidazole for Trichomonas vaginalis infection has a glass of wine at dinner and develops severe flushing, tachycardia, nausea, and headache within 30 minutes. Explain.
Metronidazole inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH) — the enzyme that converts acetaldehyde (from ethanol oxidation) to acetic acid. Acetaldehyde accumulates → causes the disulfiram-like reaction: vasodilation, flushing, tachycardia, nausea, headache, hypotension. This is the same mechanism as disulfiram (Antabuse) used in alcohol aversion therapy. Patients must avoid alcohol during metronidazole therapy AND for 48 hours after the last dose (48–72 h with tinidazole).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Metronidazole has a broad anaerobic spectrum: C. difficile, Bacteroides fragilis, Giardia, Entamoeba, Trichomonas. It is also the drug of choice for bacterial vaginosis. Long-term use risks peripheral neuropathy and cerebellar ataxia.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Metronidazole + warfarin: metronidazole inhibits CYP2C9 → reduces warfarin (S-enantiomer) metabolism → significantly elevated INR. This is a genuine and dangerous pharmacokinetic interaction — always check INR and consider reducing warfarin dose when starting metronidazole.
12 TMP-SMX is prescribed for Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia prophylaxis. The patient develops megaloblastic anaemia and leucopenia after 3 weeks. Explain.
TMP-SMX doubly blocks folate synthesis: SMX inhibits dihydropteroate synthase (DHPS — blocks PABA incorporation into folate); TMP inhibits dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR — blocks folate reduction to THF). In bacteria, this is selective because bacterial DHFR has much higher affinity for TMP than human DHFR. However, at high doses or in folate-deficient patients, human DHFR inhibition occurs → impaired thymidylate and purine synthesis → megaloblastic anaemia, neutropenia. Treatment: folinic acid (leucovorin) — provides pre-reduced folate, bypassing the DHFR block.
💎 Clinical Pearl: TMP also blocks K⁺ secretion in the distal nephron (mimics amiloride, an ENaC blocker) → hyperkalaemia in CKD patients. Additionally, TMP inhibits renal creatinine secretion (via OCT2 transporter) → apparent creatinine rise without true GFR change. Know these renal effects — commonly confused with true AKI.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Use folinic acid (leucovorin) — NOT folic acid — to treat TMP-induced megaloblastic anaemia. Folic acid still requires DHFR to be activated (reduced to THF) — blocked by TMP. Folinic acid (5-formyl-THF) bypasses DHFR entirely and directly enters the folate pool.
13 Fluconazole fails to clear a Candida krusei bloodstream infection. Why, and what should be used instead?
Candida krusei is intrinsically resistant to fluconazole — it has reduced expression of the azole target enzyme (14α-demethylase/CYP51) and altered membrane composition reducing azole binding. Fluconazole cannot achieve adequate activity regardless of dose. Use: echinocandin (caspofungin, micafungin, anidulafungin) — inhibits β-1,3-glucan synthase → cell wall synthesis disruption → fungicidal. Echinocandins are first-line for all invasive candidiasis per IDSA guidelines, particularly for azole-resistant species and critically ill patients.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Candida glabrata (now reclassified as Nakaseomyces glabrata) also has reduced azole susceptibility (acquired resistance via ERG11 mutations and efflux pumps) — echinocandin preferred. Candida auris is resistant to fluconazole AND often to echinocandins — a major emerging pathogen requiring specialist input.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Echinocandins have poor CNS and urine penetration — do NOT use for Candida meningitis or urinary tract candidiasis. Use fluconazole (if susceptible) for UTI candidiasis; liposomal amphotericin B for Candida meningitis.
14 Voriconazole is started for invasive aspergillosis. The patient, who is a CYP2C19 poor metaboliser (East Asian ethnicity), develops adverse effects at standard dosing. Why?
Voriconazole is primarily metabolised by CYP2C19 (and to a lesser extent CYP3A4, CYP2C9). CYP2C19 poor metabolisers (~15–25% of Asians, 3–5% of Caucasians) have ~4× higher voriconazole AUC compared to extensive metabolisers at the same dose → supratherapeutic levels → toxicity (visual disturbances, hepatotoxicity, QT prolongation, periostitis with chronic use, photosensitivity). Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM) is essential — target trough 1–5.5 µg/mL.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Voriconazole is also a potent inhibitor of CYP3A4, CYP2C9, and CYP2C19 — causing multiple drug interactions. Isavuconazole (newer triazole) has less pharmacokinetic variability, fewer drug interactions, better tolerability, and no QT prolongation (actually mildly shortens QT — watch in patients with short QT syndrome).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Voriconazole causes transient, reversible visual disturbances (photopsia, altered colour perception, blurred vision) in up to 30% of patients — typically occur within 30 minutes of dosing and resolve spontaneously. These are caused by voriconazole inhibiting retinal CYP enzymes — NOT optic neuritis, and NOT a reason to stop therapy unless severe or persistent.
15 A patient with HIV, CD4 count 45 cells/µL, develops fever, headache, neck stiffness, and photophobia. CSF India ink stain is positive. What antibiotic is given, and what specific monitoring parameter prevents a key complication?
Cryptococcal meningitis — treat with liposomal amphotericin B + flucytosine (5-FC) induction × 2 weeks (IDSA/WHO guidelines), then fluconazole consolidation and maintenance. Key complication: raised intracranial pressure (ICP) from CSF outflow obstruction by Cryptococcus capsular polysaccharide. Monitor opening pressure at each lumbar puncture — if >25 cmH₂O, perform therapeutic LP (drain 10–30 mL CSF) daily until pressure normalises. Mortality from cryptococcal meningitis is largely driven by uncontrolled elevated ICP, not antifungal failure.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Flucytosine (5-FC) mechanism: enters fungal cells via cytosine permease → converted to 5-fluorouracil (5-FU) → inhibits thymidylate synthase → impairs fungal DNA synthesis. Monitor flucytosine levels (target 25–100 µg/mL peak) and CBC (bone marrow suppression risk, especially with renal failure).
⚠️ Exam Trap: Do NOT give acetazolamide, steroids, or mannitol for cryptococcal meningitis ICP — only therapeutic LPs reduce fungal burden-related ICP effectively. Steroids are not indicated in cryptococcal meningitis (unlike bacterial meningitis where dexamethasone reduces mortality).
16 Oseltamivir is given 24 hours after influenza symptom onset. A colleague says it is too late. Is this correct?
Only partially. Oseltamivir (neuraminidase inhibitor → prevents viral release from infected cells) reduces illness duration by ~1 day and severity if started within 48 hours of symptom onset in otherwise healthy adults — modest benefit after 48 h. However, in high-risk patients (hospitalised, immunocompromised, elderly, pregnant, severe obesity, underlying cardiopulmonary disease) oseltamivir provides benefit beyond 48 hours and should be given regardless of symptom duration. At 24 hours, it is definitely appropriate to treat.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Oseltamivir covers influenza A AND B. Baloxavir marboxil (cap-dependent endonuclease inhibitor, single dose) — novel mechanism, non-inferior to oseltamivir, active against oseltamivir-resistant strains. Amantadine/rimantadine (M2 ion channel blockers) — only influenza A, high resistance rates, essentially obsolete.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Oseltamivir does NOT treat RSV, SARS-CoV-2, or other respiratory viruses — it is influenza-specific. Also: oseltamivir resistance (H275Y mutation in H1N1 neuraminidase) → use zanamivir (inhaled) or baloxavir for oseltamivir-resistant strains.
17 Doxycycline is prescribed for community-acquired pneumonia in a 10-year-old child. The parent objects citing 'teeth damage.' How do you respond?
The concern about tetracycline and teeth applies to children under 8 years — during active dentinogenesis, tetracyclines chelate calcium → incorporated into developing teeth and bone → permanent yellow-brown discolouration and enamel hypoplasia. Doxycycline chelates calcium far less avidly than older tetracyclines (oxytetracycline, demeclocycline) → significantly lower risk of dental staining. The AAP revised its guidance in 2012: doxycycline is acceptable in children ≥8 years and permissible even in younger children for short courses (<21 days) when benefits outweigh risks (e.g., Rocky Mountain spotted fever — where delay is fatal).
💎 Clinical Pearl: Doxycycline covers atypical CAP pathogens (Mycoplasma, Chlamydophila, Legionella) as well as typical bacteria — a key advantage over beta-lactams (which miss atypicals). For walking pneumonia in outpatients, doxycycline is a preferred agent.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Tetracyclines cause photosensitivity — advise sunscreen. They are contraindicated in pregnancy throughout (foetal bone/teeth effects). Doxycycline also causes oesophageal ulceration if taken without adequate water — remain upright for 30 min after dosing.
18 A 68-year-old woman with bacteraemic UTI due to ESBL-producing E. coli is on ciprofloxacin empirically, but sensitivity testing shows resistance. What antibiotic class is now first-line for ESBL infections?
Carbapenems (meropenem, ertapenem, imipenem-cilastatin) are the treatment of choice for serious ESBL-producing Enterobacteriaceae infections. ESBL (extended-spectrum beta-lactamases) hydrolyse penicillins, cephalosporins (including 3rd and 4th generation), and aztreonam — but NOT carbapenems. For less severe ESBL-UTI without bacteraemia, pivmecillinam or fosfomycin may be options based on susceptibility, but carbapenems remain gold standard for systemic infections.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Carbapenemase-producing Enterobacteriaceae (CPE/CRE) — resistant to ALL carbapenems. Treatment options: ceftazidime-avibactam (KPC, OXA-48, some MBLs), ceftolozane-tazobactam (Pseudomonas), meropenem-vaborbactam (KPC), aztreonam-avibactam (MBL). These combinations are increasingly needed — requires microbiology and ID input.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Piperacillin-tazobactam (PTZ) is often reported as 'sensitive' against ESBL producers in vitro — but clinical outcomes are significantly worse than carbapenems in ESBL bacteraemia (MERINO trial: PTZ inferior to meropenem, higher mortality). Do NOT use PTZ as definitive therapy for ESBL bacteraemia even if sensitive in vitro.
19 A patient with HIV (CD4 50 cells/µL) develops progressive weakness, visual loss, and cognitive decline over 3 months. Brain MRI shows multifocal demyelinating white matter lesions with no mass effect. What is the diagnosis and its pharmacological association?
Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML) — caused by reactivation of JC virus (John Cunningham polyomavirus) in the CNS of profoundly immunocompromised patients. Associated with: (1) Advanced HIV (low CD4); (2) Natalizumab (anti-α4 integrin, used for MS/Crohn's) — blocks lymphocyte CNS entry → JC virus reactivation in brain; (3) Rituximab, efalizumab, other immunosuppressants. Treatment: immune reconstitution is the only effective therapy — start or optimise ART for HIV-PML. For natalizumab-PML: stop the drug; immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (IRIS) may paradoxically worsen before improving.
💎 Clinical Pearl: JC virus serology (anti-JCV antibody index) is used for risk stratification before natalizumab initiation. High antibody index + natalizumab >24 months + prior immunosuppressant = very high PML risk → consider switching to a safer alternative (ocrelizumab, ofatumumab).
⚠️ Exam Trap: PML lesions on MRI are non-enhancing (no blood-brain barrier breakdown, no mass effect) — unlike CNS lymphoma (ring-enhancing) or toxoplasmosis (ring-enhancing, mass effect). Toxoplasmosis in HIV is ring-enhancing, responds to empirical pyrimethamine + sulfadiazine + folinic acid.
20 Nitrofurantoin is prescribed for a UTI in a woman with eGFR 25 mL/min. The pharmacist contacts you to discuss the prescription. What is the concern?
Nitrofurantoin requires renal excretion to achieve therapeutic urinary concentrations. In significant CKD (eGFR <30 mL/min): (1) Urinary nitrofurantoin levels fall below therapeutic range → treatment failure; (2) Systemic metabolites accumulate → toxicity: pulmonary fibrosis, peripheral neuropathy, hepatotoxicity, haemolytic anaemia. Nitrofurantoin is therefore contraindicated when eGFR <30 mL/min (some guidelines say <45 for prophylaxis). Use fosfomycin trometamol (single dose) or trimethoprim (with K⁺ monitoring) as alternatives.
💎 Clinical Pearl: Nitrofurantoin ONLY treats lower UTI (cystitis) — it does NOT achieve therapeutic levels in renal parenchyma, blood, or tissues. Never use for pyelonephritis, bacteraemia, or any upper urinary tract infection. Resistance rates remain low (<3%) because resistance requires simultaneous loss of multiple nitroreductase enzymes.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Nitrofurantoin can cause acute (type I hypersensitivity — eosinophilic pneumonitis, rapid onset, reversible) or chronic (fibrotic, insidious, may be irreversible) pulmonary reactions. Monitor for new cough or dyspnoea, especially in patients on long-term prophylaxis.
