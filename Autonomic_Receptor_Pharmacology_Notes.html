Section 1 · Autonomic Nervous System & Neurotransmission

Covers neurotransmission across the autonomic and somatic motor nervous systems.

▸★★★Discuss adrenergic and cholinergic neurotransmission in detail — co-transmission, steps at cholinergic & adrenergic synapses, co-transmitters/neuromodulators, and clinical relevance.

Neurotransmission = the process of chemical signalling across a synapse: synthesis → storage → release → receptor action → termination. G&G (Ch.10) describes the autonomic synapse as the prototype.

Steps in cholinergic transmission

  1. Synthesis: Choline (via choline transporter, rate-limiting) + Acetyl-CoA → ACh by choline acetyltransferase (ChAT).
  2. Storage: ACh packaged into vesicles by VAChT.
  3. Release: Nerve action potential → Ca²⁺ influx → exocytosis (SNARE proteins).
  4. Action: ACh acts on nicotinic (ionotropic) / muscarinic (GPCR) receptors.
  5. Termination: Rapid hydrolysis by acetylcholinesterase (AChE) → choline (recycled) + acetate.

Steps in adrenergic (noradrenergic) transmission

  1. Synthesis: Tyrosine → DOPA (tyrosine hydroxylase, rate-limiting) → Dopamine → (into vesicle via VMAT) → Noradrenaline (by dopamine-β-hydroxylase); in adrenal medulla → adrenaline (PNMT).
  2. Storage: NA in vesicles (VMAT-2).
  3. Release: Ca²⁺-dependent exocytosis.
  4. Action: α & β adrenoceptors (all GPCRs).
  5. Termination: mainly reuptake-1 (NET) into neuron (recycled), + extraneuronal uptake-2; metabolism by MAO (mitochondria) & COMT (extraneuronal).

Co-transmission, co-transmitters & neuromodulators

  • Co-transmission: a single nerve releases more than one transmitter. Classic neurotransmitter + co-transmitter act together.
  • Examples: Sympathetic — NA + ATP + Neuropeptide Y (NPY); Parasympathetic — ACh + VIP + NO; NANC (non-adrenergic non-cholinergic) nerves — NO, ATP, VIP.
  • Neuromodulator: substance that modifies (potentiates/attenuates) transmission without itself being the primary transmitter (e.g. adenosine, prostaglandins, presynaptic peptides).

Clinical relevance

  • Each step is a drug target: synthesis (α-methyltyrosine), storage (reserpine depletes VMAT), release (botulinum blocks ACh release; guanethidine blocks NA release), reuptake (cocaine, TCAs, SSRIs), metabolism (MAO inhibitors, AChE inhibitors).
  • Explains autonomic drug actions, Parkinson's (dopamine), depression (monoamines), myasthenia (ACh), organophosphate poisoning.

Vasomotor reversal of Dale

Adrenaline acts on both α1 (vasoconstriction) and β2 (vasodilation). Normally the α1 (pressor) effect dominates → BP rises. After an α-blocker (e.g. phenoxybenzamine/ergot), the α-mediated constriction is abolished, leaving the unopposed β2 vasodilation → adrenaline now lowers BP. This reversal of the pressor response to a depressor response is "vasomotor reversal of Dale."

Graph: BP vs time — Adrenaline alone: sharp rise. After α-blocker + adrenaline: a fall in BP (reversed response). Noradrenaline (pure α) response is only blunted, not reversed.

Clinical significance

  • Explains why adrenaline is contraindicated as the pressor agent in a patient on α-blockers (paradoxical hypotension).
  • Differentiates adrenaline (reversed) from noradrenaline (only abolished) — diagnostic concept.
  • Relevant in management of accidental adrenaline overdose / interactions.

▸★★★Classify adrenergic blockers. Pharmacological actions, uses & adverse effects of β-blockers; compare 1st/2nd/3rd generations; non-CV uses & effect on transplanted heart.

Classification of adrenergic blockers

  • α-blockers: non-selective (phenoxybenzamine-irreversible, phentolamine-reversible); selective α1 (prazosin, tamsulosin); selective α2 (yohimbine).
  • β-blockers: non-selective, β1-selective, with-vasodilatory (3rd gen) — see below.
  • α+β blockers: labetalol, carvedilol.
  • Adrenergic neuron blockers: reserpine, guanethidine.

Pharmacological actions of β-blockers

  • CVS: ↓HR, ↓contractility, ↓CO, ↓renin → ↓BP; antianginal & antiarrhythmic.
  • ↓Aqueous humour (eye); bronchoconstriction (β2); ↓glycogenolysis (mask hypoglycaemia); ↓tremor.

Uses

Hypertension, angina, post-MI, heart failure (specific agents), arrhythmias, glaucoma (timolol), and non-CV uses below.

Adverse effects

Bradycardia/heart block, fatigue, cold extremities, bronchospasm (asthma — caution), masking hypoglycaemia, dyslipidaemia, ↓exercise tolerance, sexual dysfunction, vivid dreams (lipophilic), rebound on abrupt withdrawal.

3rd-gen advantages: lower peripheral resistance, neutral/favourable metabolic profile (nebivolol ↑insulin sensitivity), mortality benefit in HF (carvedilol), useful in HTN with metabolic syndrome.

Non-cardiovascular uses

Migraine prophylaxis (propranolol), essential tremor, performance anxiety/stage fright, thyrotoxicosis, portal hypertension (variceal bleed prophylaxis), glaucoma, infantile haemangioma (propranolol), pheochromocytoma (after α-block).

Effect on transplanted (denervated) heart

The transplanted heart lacks autonomic innervation; it relies on circulating catecholamines and intrinsic rate. β-blockers can still act (receptors intact) and blunt the response to circulating catecholamines, impairing exercise heart-rate increase — used cautiously. Drugs acting via nerves (e.g. atropine, digoxin's vagal effect) are ineffective.

▸★★Role of α-agonists in glaucoma and α-blockers in BPH; other non-ocular uses of anticholinergics & receptor-subtype-specific anticholinergics.

α-agonists in glaucoma

  • α2-agonists (brimonidine, apraclonidine): ↓ aqueous humour production + ↑ uveoscleral outflow → ↓ IOP; also neuroprotective. Used in open-angle glaucoma/ocular hypertension.

α-blockers in BPH

  • α1-blockers relax smooth muscle of bladder neck, prostate capsule & prostatic urethra (dynamic component) → improve urine flow.
  • Uroselective α1A: tamsulosin, silodosin, alfuzosin (less hypotension). Non-selective: prazosin, terazosin, doxazosin (also treat HTN).
  • ADR: postural hypotension, "first-dose" syncope, retrograde ejaculation, intraoperative floppy iris syndrome (tamsulosin).

▸★★SAR of sympathomimetics and β-adrenergic receptor antagonists; correlation of chemical structure with β1-selectivity, ISA, MSA & lipophilicity of β-blockers.

SAR of sympathomimetics (phenylethylamine nucleus)

  • Catechol (3,4-OH) groups: max α/β potency but rapid COMT metabolism & poor oral/CNS entry (adrenaline, NA, isoprenaline).
  • Loss of catechol OH: ↑ oral activity & duration, ↑ CNS penetration, ↑ indirect action (ephedrine, amphetamine).
  • N-substitution (size): small → α activity; bulky (isopropyl/tert-butyl) → β selectivity (isoprenaline, salbutamol).
  • α-carbon methyl: resists MAO, ↑ duration, indirect action (ephedrine, amphetamine).
  • β-OH: needed for direct receptor activity & storage.

SAR of β-blockers — from agonist (isoprenaline) to antagonist

β-blockers are congeners of the agonist isoprenaline — a catechol ring linked via an ethanolamine side chain to a bulky N-isopropyl group. Systematic modification of three regions (aromatic ring, side chain, terminal amine) converts the agonist into an antagonist and tunes β1-selectivity, ISA, MSA and lipophilicity. Dichloroisoprenaline (1958, ring-OH→Cl) was the first β-blocker (still a partial agonist); propranolol (1963) was the clinical breakthrough.

  • Loss of the 3,4-catechol –OH groups abolishes intrinsic (agonist) activity while affinity is retained → converts agonist to antagonist.
  • Insertion of an –O–CH2– (oxymethylene) bridge between the ring and side chain converts the arylethanolamine into an aryloxypropanolamine (–O–CH2–CHOH–CH2–NH–R) — the scaffold of most modern β-blockers (propranolol, metoprolol, atenolol), greatly increasing antagonist potency.

β1-selectivity (cardioselectivity)

Conferred by para-substitution on a single benzene ring; absence of a second ring/ortho-meta bulk keeps affinity higher for cardiac β1 than bronchial/vascular β2. Cardioselective agents (metoprolol, atenolol, acebutolol, bisoprolol, nebivolol) cause less bronchoconstriction and less interference with hypoglycaemia recovery — but selectivity is relative and lost at high doses, so caution is still needed in asthma/COPD.

ISA and MSA

  • ISA/partial agonism (pindolol, acebutolol, labetalol): weak agonist tone limits resting bradycardia, may blunt lipid worsening; not preferred for post-MI secondary prevention.
  • MSA (quinidine-like Na⁺-channel block) is a property of lipophilic agents (propranolol, oxprenolol, acebutolol); underlies corneal anaesthesia (unsuitable for topical glaucoma use) but clinically insignificant for antiarrhythmic effect at usual plasma levels.

Stereochemistry

Antagonist activity resides mainly in the levorotatory S(–) enantiomer; S(–)-metoprolol is marketed as the active single enantiomer (half the racemate dose). Labetalol is a mixture of 4 diastereomers with differing α/β actions; for sotalol, the l-isomer carries β-blockade while both isomers share class III antiarrhythmic action.

High-yield exam pointers

  • All β-blockers are isoprenaline analogues; loss of catechol –OH + bulky N-substituent = antagonist.
  • Aryloxypropanolamine (–OCH2– bridge) = scaffold of propranolol-type blockers.
  • Bulky amine (isopropyl/tert-butyl) → β-affinity; small amine → α-activity.
  • Para-substituted single benzene ring → β1-selectivity; naphthalene/non-substituted bulky ring → non-selective.
  • ISA = pindolol; MSA = propranolol; lipophilic = hepatic + CNS; hydrophilic = renal, longer t½.
  • Third-generation: carvedilol/labetalol (α1-block), nebivolol (NO/β3).
Recent advances: Third-generation vasodilating β-blockers dominate current use — nebivolol's β1-selectivity plus endothelial NO release and carvedilol's α-block give favourable haemodynamic/metabolic profiles (less dyslipidaemia/insulin resistance than propranolol). Single-enantiomer products (S-metoprolol succinate) exploit stereoselective SAR to lower dose/pill burden. Structure-guided pharmacology is now informed by β1-adrenoceptor crystal/cryo-EM structures, confirming the long-held SAR roles of the protonated bulky amine and side-chain β-OH in the orthosteric pocket.

▸★★★Explain cholinergic transmission; pharmacological actions & therapeutic uses of antimuscarinic drugs; note on vasoselective anticholinergics & subtype-specific agents.

Cholinergic transmission (summary)

ACh synthesised (ChAT) → stored (VAChT) → Ca²⁺-dependent release → acts on nicotinic (Nm at NMJ, Nn at ganglia/CNS — ligand-gated cation channels) and muscarinic (M1–M5 GPCRs) receptors → terminated by AChE.

MuscarinicG-proteinLocation/effect
M1GqCNS, gastric glands (acid), ganglia
M2GiHeart (↓rate, ↓conduction)
M3GqSmooth muscle, glands (secretion, contraction), eye

Pharmacological actions of antimuscarinics (atropine prototype)

  • Eye: mydriasis, cycloplegia, ↑IOP (risk in angle-closure).
  • CVS: tachycardia (block M2).
  • GIT/GU: ↓motility & secretions, antispasmodic, urinary retention.
  • Secretions: ↓salivary, sweat, bronchial (dry mouth, hyperthermia).
  • Respiratory: bronchodilation.
  • CNS: sedation/anti-tremor (tertiary amines), delirium in toxicity.

Therapeutic uses

Pre-anaesthetic medication, bradyarrhythmia, organophosphate/anticholinesterase & mushroom poisoning, COPD/asthma (ipratropium/tiotropium), overactive bladder, IBS/GI spasm, mydriatic/cycloplegic for fundoscopy, motion sickness (hyoscine), Parkinsonism, peptic ulcer (pirenzepine).

Vasoselective / subtype-specific anticholinergics

Subtype selectivity reduces off-target ADRs: M1 — pirenzepine/telenzepine (gastric, ↓antisecretory ADRs); M3 — darifenacin, solifenacin (bladder-selective, ↓CNS/cardiac effects); inhaled tiotropium (lung-selective via topical delivery). "Vasoselective" antimuscarinic action is minimal as vessels lack cholinergic innervation — but local/organ-selective targeting is the principle.

▸★★Structure-activity relationship (SAR) of anticholinergics (muscarinic antagonists) — basic pharmacophore, structural classes, stereochemistry & correlation with potency/selectivity/CNS penetration.

Basic pharmacophore

Antimuscarinic agents are built around three pharmacophoric elements common to ACh-mimetic/antagonist chemistry: (1) a cationic head (tertiary or quaternary nitrogen, mimicking ACh's quaternary ammonium, providing the key electrostatic/cation-π interaction with the receptor's anionic binding pocket), (2) a bulky aromatic/cyclic acidic moiety (e.g. tropic acid in atropine, mandelic acid in homatropine) replacing ACh's small acetate group — this bulk is what confers antagonism rather than agonism, and (3) an ester or ether linkage connecting the two, plus a hydroxyl group contributing to receptor affinity via H-bonding.

Stereochemistry

Activity resides predominantly in one enantiomer — e.g. (–)-hyoscyamine is far more potent than (+)-hyoscyamine; atropine itself is the racemate (dl-hyoscyamine), so its potency is roughly half that of pure l-hyoscyamine. This chiral selectivity reflects the stereospecific fit of the cationic head and acidic-moiety substituents into the muscarinic binding pocket — a classic example of stereochemistry determining potency independent of bulk physicochemical properties.

Antimuscarinic toxidrome (clinical correlate of the pharmacophore)

"Hot as a hare (hyperthermia — ↓sweating), dry as a bone (↓secretions), red as a beet (cutaneous vasodilation), blind as a bat (mydriasis/cycloplegia), mad as a hatter (CNS delirium — only with tertiary/CNS-penetrant agents)." The CNS components occur only with tertiary-amine agents that cross the BBB — quaternary agents (ipratropium, glycopyrrolate) at therapeutic doses produce a peripheral-only picture, directly illustrating the structure-CNS-penetration relationship.

High-yield exam pointers

  • Quaternary nitrogen = poor BBB penetration → peripheral-selective, inhaled/GI agents (ipratropium, tiotropium, glycopyrrolate); tertiary amine = CNS-penetrant (atropine, scopolamine, benzhexol).
  • l-hyoscyamine > atropine (racemate) > d-hyoscyamine in potency — classic stereochemistry exam point.
  • Bulky acidic-moiety substitution (vs ACh's small acetate) is what converts an agonist scaffold into an antagonist.
  • M1/M3 subtype-selective agents (pirenzepine; darifenacin/solifenacin) achieve selectivity through structural bulk/rigidity, not just dose.
Recent advances: Structure-guided design continues to refine M3-selective, slow-off-rate (kinetically selective) agents (tiotropium, umeclidinium, glycopyrronium) enabling once-daily inhaled dosing in COPD/asthma with minimal cardiac (M2) or CNS crossover, extending the classical SAR principles (charge, bulk, stereochemistry) into modern long-acting bronchodilator design.

Organophosphate (OP) poisoning & oximes

  • OPs phosphorylate AChE → ACh accumulation → muscarinic (DUMBELS), nicotinic (fasciculation, weakness) & CNS effects.
  • Treatment: ABC + decontamination; Atropine (blocks muscarinic effects, titrate to dry secretions) + Oxime (pralidoxime/PAM, obidoxime).
  • Oxime mechanism: reactivates AChE by removing the phosphate group — must be given before "aging" (irreversible covalent bond) for efficacy; helps nicotinic (muscle) effects that atropine cannot.

Atropine (anticholinergic) poisoning

"Hot as a hare, dry as a bone, red as a beet, blind as a bat, mad as a hatter." Treatment: supportive cooling, physostigmine (a reversible anticholinesterase that crosses BBB → reverses central + peripheral antimuscarinic effects), benzodiazepines for agitation.

▸★★Excitatory neurotransmitters in the CNS — glutamate & aspartate; NMDA/AMPA/kainate/metabotropic glutamate receptors; drugs acting on them, excitotoxicity & therapeutic potential.

The principal excitatory neurotransmitters in the mammalian CNS are the acidic amino acids glutamate and aspartate, acting on ionotropic (fast, ligand-gated channel) and metabotropic (slow, GPCR) receptors distributed throughout cortex, hippocampus and spinal cord — mediating normal excitatory synaptic transmission, synaptic plasticity (LTP — learning/memory), and, when in excess, neuronal injury (excitotoxicity).

NMDA receptor — structure & key properties

  • Heterotetramer (NR1 + NR2 subunits); requires simultaneous glutamate binding + co-agonist (glycine/D-serine) + membrane depolarisation to relieve Mg²⁺ block — acts as a "coincidence detector," central to long-term potentiation (synaptic basis of learning/memory).
  • High Ca²⁺ permeability — physiological Ca²⁺ influx triggers plasticity; pathological excess influx (excitotoxicity) activates proteases/lipases/free-radical generation → neuronal death.

Drugs acting on excitatory glutamatergic transmission

  • NMDA antagonists: ketamine/esketamine (anaesthesia, treatment-resistant depression), memantine (moderate-severe Alzheimer's — low-affinity, voltage-dependent "open-channel" blocker, allows physiological transmission while blocking pathological tonic activation), amantadine (Parkinson's/antiviral), dextromethorphan (antitussive; antidepressant in combination), nitrous oxide, magnesium sulfate (eclampsia — partly NMDA-related).
  • AMPA antagonist: perampanel (adjunctive antiepileptic).
  • Glycine-site partial agonists/modulators: investigational cognitive enhancers (D-cycloserine).
  • mGluR modulators: investigational for anxiety, schizophrenia, Fragile X syndrome, Parkinson's (basimglurant, mavoglurant — research stage).

Excitotoxicity & therapeutic potential

Excess glutamate release (ischaemia/stroke, traumatic brain injury, status epilepticus, neurodegenerative disease) → sustained NMDA receptor activation → massive Ca²⁺ influx → mitochondrial dysfunction, free-radical generation, protease/lipase activation → neuronal death — implicated in stroke, ALS, Alzheimer's, Huntington's and Parkinson's disease pathogenesis. NMDA blockade (memantine, riluzole in ALS — also blocks glutamate release) is a rational neuroprotective strategy, though clinical translation in acute stroke has been limited by psychotomimetic/dissociative ADRs of potent NMDA blockers.

Diagram: Glutamatergic synapse — presynaptic vesicular glutamate release → postsynaptic NMDA receptor (needs glycine co-agonist + depolarisation to relieve voltage-dependent Mg²⁺ plug) and AMPA receptor (fast Na⁺ entry) → Ca²⁺ influx through NMDA channel → normal level activates plasticity (CREB/gene transcription); excess level → excitotoxic cascade (calpain/caspase activation, free radicals, mitochondrial failure) → cell death.

High-yield exam pointers

  • Glutamate/aspartate = principal excitatory CNS neurotransmitters; GABA/glycine = principal inhibitory.
  • NMDA receptor needs glutamate + glycine/D-serine + depolarisation (coincidence detector) — basis of LTP.
  • Memantine = moderate-affinity, voltage-dependent NMDA blocker for Alzheimer's; ketamine = anaesthetic/rapid antidepressant NMDA blocker.
  • Excitotoxicity (Ca²⁺ overload via NMDA) implicated in stroke, ALS, neurodegeneration — riluzole in ALS partly works by ↓ glutamate release.
  • Perampanel = AMPA-receptor antagonist antiepileptic.
Recent advances: Dextromethorphan–bupropion (Auvelity) and esketamine nasal spray exploit NMDA-receptor modulation for rapid-onset antidepressant action in treatment-resistant depression. Research continues into subunit-selective NMDA modulators (NR2B-selective agents) and mGluR-targeted compounds aiming to retain neuroprotective/antidepressant benefit while minimising psychotomimetic ADRs.

Mechanism: Gi/Go coupling → ↓ adenylyl cyclase (↓cAMP), open K⁺ channels (hyperpolarisation, post-synaptic) and close N-/P-type Ca²⁺ channels (pre-synaptic) → ↓ neurotransmitter (substance P, glutamate) release → ↓ pain transmission at spinal & supraspinal levels; µ-receptor activation in the ventral tegmental area/nucleus accumbens ↑ dopamine → reward/euphoria, the basis of dependence liability.

Drug classes acting on opioid receptors

  • Full agonists: morphine, fentanyl, methadone, oxycodone, heroin (diacetylmorphine — prodrug, rapidly hydrolysed to 6-MAM/morphine, high abuse potential due to rapid CNS entry).
  • Partial/mixed agonist-antagonists: buprenorphine (high-affinity partial µ-agonist, ceiling effect on respiratory depression — safer in overdose), pentazocine, nalbuphine, butorphanol (κ-agonist/µ-antagonist or partial agonist — can precipitate withdrawal in dependent patients).
  • Antagonists: naloxone (short-acting, IV/IM/intranasal — overdose reversal), naltrexone (long-acting oral/depot — relapse prevention), methylnaltrexone/naloxegol (peripherally restricted — opioid-induced constipation without reversing analgesia).

Tolerance, physical dependence & withdrawal

Chronic µ-agonist exposure → receptor desensitisation/downregulation and compensatory upregulation of the cAMP pathway (locus coeruleus) → tolerance to analgesia/euphoria/sedation (but less to miosis/constipation) and a withdrawal syndrome on cessation: dysphoria, lacrimation, rhinorrhoea, yawning, piloerection ("cold turkey"), mydriasis, muscle aches, GI cramps/diarrhoea, anxiety, insomnia — autonomic ("noradrenergic storm" via locus coeruleus disinhibition) but not life-threatening (unlike alcohol/BZD withdrawal).

Non-addictive drugs of abuse

Substances abused without classic physical dependence: hallucinogens (LSD, psilocybin), some inhalants, dextromethorphan, certain "designer" psychedelics — abused for psychoactive/dissociative effect rather than physical dependence.

Drugs acting on biogenic-amine transporters

Block reuptake of NA/5-HT/DA: SSRIs (SERT), SNRIs (SERT+NET), TCAs, cocaine & amphetamine (DAT/NET — abuse potential), bupropion (NDRI), atomoxetine (NET — ADHD).

High-yield exam pointers

  • µ, κ, δ — all Gi-coupled; ↓cAMP, ↑K⁺ conductance, ↓Ca²⁺ entry → ↓transmitter release.
  • Methadone & buprenorphine = maintenance therapy (harm reduction); naltrexone = abstinence-based (must detox first); naloxone = acute overdose reversal.
  • Buprenorphine's ceiling effect on respiratory depression makes it safer for office-based maintenance (e.g. Suboxone with naloxone to deter IV misuse).
  • Opioid withdrawal = unpleasant but not lethal (contrast with BZD/alcohol withdrawal, which can be fatal).
  • Clonidine/lofexidine ↓ autonomic withdrawal symptoms via α2-mediated ↓ locus coeruleus noradrenergic outflow.

Multimodal / mechanism-based analgesia framework

Modern pain management stratifies therapy by the dominant pain mechanism — nociceptive (tissue injury — NSAIDs, paracetamol), neuropathic (nerve injury — gabapentinoids, TCAs/SNRIs, topical agents), nociplastic (central sensitisation, e.g. fibromyalgia — SNRIs, pregabalin) and inflammatory (NGF/cytokine-driven) — rather than escalating a single opioid-centric ladder. Multimodal analgesia combines agents acting at different sites/mechanisms for additive efficacy with lower doses of each (opioid-sparing).

The absence-seizure paradox — a classic exam trap

Although GABA is broadly anti-seizure, indiscriminately enhancing GABA-A transmission in the thalamocortical circuit can worsen absence seizures: GABA-B receptor activation in the thalamus promotes the rebound burst-firing (T-type Ca²⁺-channel-mediated) that generates the 3 Hz spike-and-wave discharge of absence epilepsy. This is why vigabatrin and tiagabine (which raise/prolong synaptic GABA) can precipitate or worsen absence seizures, and why ethosuximide (T-type Ca²⁺-channel blocker, not GABAergic) — not a GABA-enhancing drug — is first-line for absence seizures. Valproate works in absence seizures via its T-type Ca²⁺-channel-blocking action, not its GABAergic action.

Newer GABA-A modulators

Ganaxolone — a synthetic neurosteroid, positive allosteric modulator at a distinct (non-BZD) site on the GABA-A receptor (the neurosteroid-binding site) — FDA-approved in 2022 specifically for seizures associated with CDKL5 deficiency disorder, the first approved synthetic neurosteroid antiepileptic, opening a new GABA-A-modulation drug class distinct from BZDs/barbiturates.

High-yield exam pointers

  • Four distinct GABAergic sites: GABA-A PAM (BZD/barbiturate), GAT-1 inhibitor (tiagabine), GABA-T inhibitor (vigabatrin), presynaptic release modulators (gabapentinoids/valproate) — classic "classify by site" exam question.
  • Vigabatrin's black-box ADR = irreversible peripheral visual-field constriction (retinal toxicity) — mandatory ophthalmologic monitoring.
  • Absence-seizure paradox: GABA-A enhancers (vigabatrin/tiagabine) can worsen absence seizures via thalamic GABA-B-mediated rebound bursting — ethosuximide (T-type Ca²⁺ blocker) is the mechanistically correct first-line drug instead.
  • Ganaxolone (2022) = first synthetic neurosteroid GABA-A PAM approved, specifically for CDKL5 deficiency disorder seizures.
  • Gabapentin/pregabalin act on Ca²⁺-channel α2δ subunits, not GABA receptors directly — a frequently mis-tested point.
Recent advances: Ganaxolone's 2022 FDA approval for CDKL5 deficiency disorder established the neurosteroid-binding site as a new druggable GABA-A target distinct from the classical BZD/barbiturate sites. ▸★★★Pharmacotherapy & drug targets of Alzheimer's disease — cholinergic & amyloid hypotheses, current drugs, disease-modifying monoclonal antibodies, and recent advances.

Alzheimer's disease (AD) is the commonest cause of dementia, characterised pathologically by extracellular amyloid-β (Aβ) plaques and intracellular neurofibrillary tangles (hyperphosphorylated tau), with progressive loss of cholinergic neurons (basal forebrain/nucleus basalis of Meynert) and cortical/hippocampal atrophy. Current drug targets address either symptomatic cholinergic/glutamatergic deficits or the underlying amyloid pathology.

1. Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors — symptomatic, mild-moderate AD

  • Donepezil: selective, long t½ (~70 h), once-daily, first-line; reversible.
  • Rivastigmine: inhibits both AChE and butyrylcholinesterase; available as transdermal patch (improved tolerability/compliance); also approved for Parkinson's disease dementia.
  • Galantamine: AChE inhibitor + nicotinic receptor allosteric modulator.
  • ADR: cholinergic excess — nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, bradycardia/syncope (caution with other bradycardic drugs), muscle cramps, vivid dreams.

2. NMDA receptor antagonist — moderate-severe AD

Memantine — low-to-moderate affinity, voltage-dependent, "open-channel" NMDA blocker; blocks pathological tonic glutamate-mediated excitotoxicity while sparing physiological synaptic transmission needed for learning/memory (unlike high-affinity blockers like ketamine). Used alone or combined with an AChE inhibitor in moderate-severe disease. ADR: dizziness, confusion, constipation — better tolerated than AChE inhibitors.

3. Disease-modifying anti-amyloid monoclonal antibodies

  • Lecanemab and donanemab — bind soluble aggregated/fibrillar Aβ → promote microglial clearance → ↓ plaque burden; modestly slow cognitive/functional decline in early symptomatic (MCI/mild) AD confirmed by amyloid-PET/biomarker positivity.
  • Aducanumab — earlier-generation anti-amyloid mAb (accelerated approval, clinical benefit debated, largely discontinued commercially).
  • ADR: ARIA (amyloid-related imaging abnormalities) — ARIA-E (oedema/effusion) and ARIA-H (microhaemorrhage/siderosis); risk ↑ in APOE ε4 homozygotes — requires baseline/serial MRI monitoring.

High-yield exam pointers

  • Cholinergic hypothesis → AChE inhibitors (donepezil/rivastigmine/galantamine) for mild-moderate disease.
  • Glutamate excitotoxicity hypothesis → memantine for moderate-severe disease; combinable with AChE inhibitor.
  • Amyloid hypothesis → lecanemab/donanemab = first disease-modifying agents (not purely symptomatic) — require biomarker confirmation & MRI monitoring for ARIA.
  • Rivastigmine = dual AChE + BuChE inhibitor, patch formulation, also used in Parkinson's dementia.
  • APOE ε4 = major genetic risk factor and predicts ARIA risk with anti-amyloid mAbs.
Recent advances: Lecanemab (2023) and donanemab (2024) are the first FDA-approved disease-modifying therapies, directly targeting amyloid pathology rather than only symptoms — a paradigm shift, though benefit is modest and ARIA monitoring burden is significant. Blood-based biomarkers (plasma p-tau217, Aβ42/40 ratio) are emerging as less invasive diagnostic/monitoring tools than CSF analysis or amyloid-PET. Anti-tau therapeutics and combination amyloid+tau approaches remain under active investigation.
▸★★Neuromodulation — concept, mechanisms & examples of neuromodulators in the CNS and ANS.

Neuromodulation refers to the process by which a substance (a "neuromodulator") alters/fine-tunes the strength or efficacy of synaptic transmission without itself being the primary fast neurotransmitter — typically acting via slower, longer-lasting, often presynaptic or volume-transmission (non-synaptic diffusion) mechanisms, frequently via GPCRs and second messengers, rather than directly gating an ion channel.

Neuromodulation vs classical neurotransmission

Mechanisms of neuromodulation

  • Presynaptic autoreceptors: modulate transmitter release from the same neuron (e.g. α2-adrenergic, D2, 5-HT1A, GABA-B autoreceptors) — negative feedback.
  • Heteroreceptors: one neurotransmitter system modulates release of another (e.g. presynaptic opioid receptors on noradrenergic terminals).
  • Retrograde signalling: postsynaptic neuron releases a modulator (e.g. endocannabinoids — anandamide, 2-AG) that acts back on presynaptic CB1 receptors to suppress further transmitter release (depolarisation-induced suppression of inhibition/excitation, DSI/DSE).
  • Volume transmission: diffuse, non-synaptic release affecting many neurons over a wider area/time course (e.g. monoamine "tone" from locus coeruleus/raphe/VTA projections, NO as a gaseous diffusible modulator).
  • Second-messenger cascades: GPCR-coupled neuromodulators alter neuronal excitability via cAMP/PKA, IP3/DAG/PKC, or direct G-protein-gated ion channels (GIRK) — changing the threshold/gain of subsequent fast synaptic inputs rather than directly causing them.

Clinical relevance

Many CNS drugs act as neuromodulators rather than direct channel agonists/antagonists: SSRIs raise synaptic serotonin tone (modulatory, not a direct fast signal), antipsychotics block D2 modulatory tone, gabapentinoids modulate (not mimic) GABA signalling via Ca²⁺ channels. Understanding neuromodulation explains why many psychotropic drugs take days-weeks for full clinical effect (receptor/gene-expression adaptation) despite immediate receptor occupancy.

High-yield exam pointers

  • Retrograde endocannabinoid signalling (DSI/DSE) is a classic exam example of neuromodulation.
  • Volume transmission (diffuse, non-synaptic) is a defining feature distinguishing modulators from classical point-to-point neurotransmitters.
  • Adenosine/caffeine and NO are commonly tested "non-classical" modulators.
Recent advances: Optogenetic and chemogenetic (DREADD) tools now allow selective experimental activation/silencing of specific neuromodulatory circuits, refining understanding of dopaminergic/serotonergic circuit-specific roles in psychiatric disease and informing next-generation circuit-targeted therapeutics (e.g. for depression, addiction).

  • Glaucoma: echothiophate (irreversible, long-acting) and physostigmine — ciliary muscle contraction → ↑ trabecular outflow; now largely superseded by newer agents but historically important and still used in select chronic open-angle/accommodative esotropia cases.

  • Antidote to antimuscarinic (atropine) poisoning: physostigmine — crosses BBB, reverses both peripheral and central anticholinergic toxicity (unlike neostigmine, which is quaternary and does not cross BBB).
  • Postoperative paralytic ileus & non-obstructive urinary retention: neostigmine increases GI/bladder smooth-muscle tone and motility.
  • Diagnosis of myasthenia gravis and intra-operative confirmation of adequate reversal of neuromuscular block — edrophonium/neostigmine.
  • Insecticides/nerve agent context (organophosphates) — not therapeutic but clinically vital to recognise toxicity (SLUDGE/DUMBELS syndrome, miosis, bronchorrhoea, fasciculations) and treat with atropine + pralidoxime (2-PAM, reactivates AChE before "ageing").

Muscarinic: salivation, lacrimation, urination, diarrhoea, GI cramps, bradycardia, miosis, bronchospasm/bronchorrhoea. Nicotinic (NMJ/ganglia): fasciculations, weakness, cholinergic crisis (paradoxical weakness mimicking myasthenic crisis — distinguished by edrophonium test). Treatment of overdose: atropine (blocks muscarinic effects only) ± pralidoxime for organophosphates.

▸★★★Pharmacotherapy of glaucoma in detail — aqueous humour dynamics, classification of antiglaucoma drugs, individual classes with adverse effects, combination therapy, therapeutic approach in POAG & acute angle-closure, drugs precipitating glaucoma, and recent advances.

Glaucoma is a group of chronic, progressive optic neuropathies with retinal ganglion-cell/nerve-fibre-layer loss and visual-field defects with optic-disc cupping. Although usually associated with raised IOP (>21 mmHg), damage can progress at statistically normal IOP (normal-/low-tension glaucoma). Elevated IOP is the only modifiable risk factor, so therapy aims entirely at lowering it — by reducing aqueous-humour secretion and/or enhancing its outflow.

Aqueous humour dynamics — rational basis of therapy

  • Secreted by ciliary processes (carbonic-anhydrase/β-receptor–driven active process) → posterior chamber → pupil → anterior chamber.
  • Drains by two routes: conventional (trabecular) outflow via trabecular meshwork → canal of Schlemm → episcleral veins (~80–95%), and the uveoscleral pathway through the ciliary muscle (~10%).
  • Every antiglaucoma drug acts at one of these nodes — ↓secretion (β-blockers, α2-agonists, CAIs) or ↑outflow (prostaglandins, Rho-kinase inhibitors, miotics, sympathomimetics).
Diagram: Sagittal section of anterior eye — ciliary process (secretion) → posterior chamber → pupil → anterior chamber → trabecular meshwork → canal of Schlemm (conventional outflow) / uveoscleral pathway. Superimposed drug sites: β-blocker/α2-agonist/CAI = ↓secretion at ciliary body; PG analogue = ↑uveoscleral; Rho-kinase inhibitor/miotic = ↑trabecular.

Goal of therapy

Individualised "target IOP" (usually 20–30% reduction from baseline, reviewed at 4–6 weeks); greater reduction sought with high baseline/advanced field loss; lower absolute target in normal-tension glaucoma. Stepwise: monotherapy → switch/add second agent → multidrug topical regimen → oral CAI → laser/surgery.

1. Prostaglandin analogues — current first-line

Latanoprost is the most-used glaucoma drug; with travoprost, bimatoprost and tafluprost has largely replaced β-blockers as first-line. Prodrugs hydrolysed to the active acid, binding FP receptors (Gq/11–PLC–IP3–Ca²⁺) → ↑ uveoscleral outflow via remodelling of ciliary-muscle ECM/collagen. IOP reduction ~25–35% (similar to timolol); effective even in normal-pressure glaucoma; once-daily evening dosing. ADR (local only): conjunctival hyperaemia, irritation, permanent iris-pigmentation increase (esp. hazel eyes), eyelash growth/darkening, periorbital fat atrophy, cystoid macular oedema (aphakic/pseudophakic). Bimatoprost = prostamide, equally/more potent; tafluprost = preservative-free. Non-responders to one analogue may respond to another.

2. β-adrenergic blockers

Next most common class; ↓ IOP 20–35% by ↓ aqueous secretion (β2-blockade at ciliary epithelium ↓ cAMP–PKA), with secondary ↓ ciliary blood flow. Do not affect pupil size/ciliary tone (no induced myopia/brow ache, unlike miotics). Timolol (non-selective, no MSA/ISA): onset <1 h, lasts ~12 h; effect persists 1–2 weeks after stopping. ~30% fail monotherapy. Betaxolol (β1-selective): less efficacious but safer in airway disease. ADR: ocular — stinging, corneal hypoesthesia, allergic blepharoconjunctivitis; systemic (via nasolacrimal absorption) — bronchospasm (can be fatal in asthma/COPD), bradycardia, heart block, CHF, masked hypoglycaemia, depression, impotence. Nasolacrimal occlusion (~5 min pressure) minimises systemic absorption.

3. α2-adrenergic agonists

Brimonidine (selective α2, lipophilic): ↓ IOP 20–27% by ↓ production + ↑ uveoscleral outflow (Gi → ↓cAMP); used for short-term IOP-spike prophylaxis and long-term add-on. Apraclonidine (polar, no BBB crossing): ↓ IOP ~25%; restricted to short-term peri-laser control (tachyphylaxis, high allergic conjunctivitis rate). ADR: ocular allergy/hyperaemia, mydriasis, dry mouth, sedation, mild ↓BP; contraindicated in infants/young children (CNS depression, apnoea, hypothermia, coma — brimonidine contraindicated <2 years).

4. Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (CAI)

Inhibit CA-II in ciliary epithelium → ↓HCO3⁻ → ↓ aqueous secretion. Topical (dorzolamide 2%, brinzolamide 1%): ↓ IOP ~15–26%; second-line/add-on; stinging, bitter taste, blurred vision, punctate keratitis. Oral (acetazolamide, methazolamide): short-term use (acute angle-closure, peri-operative) or unmet target; systemic effects — paraesthesiae, anorexia, malaise, depression, hypokalaemia, metabolic acidosis, nephrolithiasis (sulfonamide — caution in sulfa allergy).

5. Rho-kinase (ROCK) inhibitors — newest topical class

Netarsudil 0.02% (OD evening), ripasudil 0.4%: inhibit ROCK → ↓ actin stress-fibre density in trabecular meshwork/Schlemm's canal → ↑ conventional outflow only. Efficacy ~ β-blockers. ADR: conjunctival hyperaemia (very common), subconjunctival haemorrhage, blepharitis, cornea verticillata (whorl-like corneal pigment deposition); systemic effects rare.

6. NO-donating PG analogue

Latanoprostene bunod 0.024% = latanoprost + NO-donating moiety — dual mechanism: latanoprost backbone ↑uveoscleral outflow + liberated NO relaxes trabecular cytoskeleton → ↑conventional outflow; once-daily.

7. Cholinergic miotics (last resort in POAG)

Pilocarpine/carbachol (direct muscarinic agonists), echothiophate (irreversible anticholinesterase) → ciliary-muscle contraction → pulls scleral spur → opens trabecular meshwork → ↑outflow facility (do NOT ↓ secretion). In angle-closure, miosis pulls peripheral iris away from meshwork, breaking pupillary block. Reserved for last-line use: miosis (dim vision), induced myopia/ciliary spasm, brow ache, retinal tears/detachment; echothiophate adds cataract/iris-cyst risk.

8. Sympathomimetic prodrug & osmotic agents

Dipivefrine (adrenaline prodrug, corneal esterase-activated) — add-on only (ocular smarting, hyperaemia). Mannitol (IV 20%, 1.5–2 g/kg)/glycerol (oral 10%) — fastest IOP fall in acute crises; caution in CHF/renal failure.

Combination & fixed-dose therapy

  • Rational combinations pair complementary mechanisms: timolol+dorzolamide, timolol+brimonidine, brinzolamide+brimonidine, latanoprost+timolol, netarsudil+latanoprost (Rocklatan).
  • Irrational: combining two drugs of the same class (e.g. two PG analogues) — minimal extra benefit, multiplies ADRs. Separate two different drops by 5–10 min.
  • Topical NSAIDs can theoretically blunt PG-analogue efficacy (caveat, general pharmacology).

Therapeutic approach — POAG

  1. Start monotherapy with a PG analogue (latanoprost) — once-daily, best 24-h control, no systemic effects; topical β-blocker (timolol) is the main alternative.
  2. If target not reached → switch agent/class, or add a second agent of different class.
  3. Brimonidine/dorzolamide/netarsudil (occasionally dipivefrine) as add-ons or when PG/β-blockers contraindicated.
  4. Miotics and oral acetazolamide = last resort before laser trabeculoplasty/trabeculectomy.

Therapeutic approach — acute angle-closure crisis

IOP rises precipitously to 40–60+ mmHg (often post-mydriasis) with red, congested, painful eye + headache — ophthalmic emergency.

  1. IV mannitol 20% (1.5–2 g/kg) or oral glycerol 10% — fastest decongestion.
  2. Acetazolamide 0.5 g IV then oral.
  3. Pilocarpine 1–4% once IOP starts falling (iris sphincter unresponsive at very high IOP).
  4. Topical timolol 0.5% ± apraclonidine/brimonidine ± PG analogue.
  5. Topical corticosteroids (↓inflammation/synechiae); laser/surgical iridotomy = definitive cure.

Note: anticholinergics, sympathomimetics and other mydriatics can precipitate angle closure in predisposed eyes — avoided.

Contraindications & special situations

  • β-blockers: avoid in asthma/COPD, sinus bradycardia, heart block, CHF; caution with systemic β-blocker/CCB co-therapy, diabetics (mask hypoglycaemia).
  • α2-agonists: contraindicated in neonates/young children; caution with MAOIs, severe CVS disease.
  • Oral CAIs: avoid in sulfa hypersensitivity, hepatic/renal impairment, hypokalaemia, acidosis, renal stones.
  • PG analogues: caution in active uveitis, aphakic/pseudophakic eyes; counsel re: permanent iris darkening.
  • Drugs that can precipitate/worsen glaucoma: systemic/topical anticholinergics and mydriatics (angle-closure in narrow angles); topical/systemic/inhaled corticosteroids (open-angle, "steroid-responder" glaucoma — extracellular trabecular-meshwork material accumulation; ~5% responders without family history, up to ~90% with positive family history). Use lowest potency/dose, monitor IOP.

High-yield exam pointers

  • Lowering IOP is the only proven modifiable intervention — target ≈20–30% reduction.
  • PG analogues (latanoprost) = current first-line (↑uveoscleral outflow, FP receptor, no systemic effects, but pigmentation/macular oedema).
  • Timolol = ↓secretion; contraindicated in asthma/bradycardia/heart block; betaxolol in airway disease.
  • Brimonidine = ↓secretion+↑uveoscleral outflow; contraindicated in infants.
  • Netarsudil = ↑trabecular outflow only; hallmark ADR = cornea verticillata + hyperaemia.
  • Steroid-induced glaucoma = classic drug-induced open-angle IOP rise.
  • Acute angle-closure = emergency: mannitol/glycerol + IV acetazolamide + pilocarpine + timolol → iridotomy.
Recent advances: Rho-kinase inhibitors (netarsudil, ripasudil) — newest topical class via the trabecular meshwork/Schlemm's-canal cytoskeleton, efficacy comparable to β-blockers, useful as fixed combination (netarsudil/latanoprost). Latanoprostene bunod exemplifies dual conventional + uveoscleral outflow enhancement. ▸★Current status & issues with COX-2 inhibitors; role in osteoarthritis & other conditions.

Rationale

Selective COX-2 inhibitors (coxibs) — celecoxib, etoricoxib, parecoxib — inhibit inducible COX-2 (inflammation) while sparing constitutive COX-1 (gastric mucosa, platelets) → fewer GI ulcers/bleeds & no antiplatelet effect.

Issues / current status

  • Cardiovascular risk: ↑ thrombotic events (MI/stroke) due to imbalance (↓ vascular prostacyclin, unopposed thromboxane) → rofecoxib & valdecoxib withdrawn.
  • Celecoxib & etoricoxib remain available with CV/renal cautions; contraindicated in established CV disease.
  • Renal effects & hypertension similar to non-selective NSAIDs.

Uses

Osteoarthritis & rheumatoid arthritis (esp. with high GI-bleed risk), acute pain, ankylosing spondylitis, dysmenorrhoea, familial adenomatous polyposis (celecoxib). Use lowest dose, shortest duration.

Recent (PRECISION trial): celecoxib CV safety comparable to naproxen/ibuprofen at moderate doses, partly rehabilitating the class.

▸★★★Pharmacotherapy of depression — monoamine hypothesis & its limitations, classification of antidepressants by mechanism, individual classes & newer rapid-acting agents.

Cross-reference: the table-card above briefly lists newer depression agents alongside Alzheimer's/migraine drugs — this card gives the full classification & mechanism framework underlying modern antidepressant pharmacotherapy.

Monoamine hypothesis & its limitations

Classical hypothesis: depression results from a functional deficiency of monoamines (5-HT, NA, ± DA) at CNS synapses. All older antidepressants (TCAs, MAOIs, SSRIs/SNRIs) raise synaptic monoamine levels, supporting the hypothesis — but the therapeutic lag (2–4 weeks) despite immediate biochemical monoamine elevation, and a substantial 30–40% treatment-non-response rate, indicate the hypothesis is incomplete; downstream adaptive changes (receptor downregulation, BDNF/neurotrophic signalling, neuroplasticity, hippocampal neurogenesis) are now considered central to true antidepressant action — explaining why rapid monoamine-independent agents (ketamine/esketamine) can produce effects within hours.

Newer/rapid-acting agents — mechanistic detail

  • Esketamine (S-enantiomer of ketamine, intranasal) — NMDA-receptor antagonism on GABAergic interneurons → disinhibition of glutamatergic pyramidal neurons → AMPA-receptor-mediated synaptic potentiation/BDNF release → rapid (within hours-days) antidepressant effect in treatment-resistant depression; requires REMS monitoring (dissociation, sedation, abuse potential, BP monitoring).
  • Zuranolone — first oral neurosteroid GABA-A PAM approved specifically for postpartum depression, 14-day course, rapid onset distinguishing it from the weeks-long lag of classical antidepressants.
  • Dextromethorphan-bupropion (Auvelity) — dextromethorphan provides NMDA antagonism (CYP2D6-metabolised; bupropion co-administered as a CYP2D6 inhibitor to boost dextromethorphan exposure) — an oral, rapid-acting alternative to parenteral/intranasal ketamine-class agents.
  • Psilocybin (5-HT2A agonist, classic psychedelic) — under trial for treatment-resistant depression; proposed mechanism involves acute 5-HT2A-mediated neuroplasticity/default-mode-network disruption with sustained effect after limited dosing sessions; not yet approved, used only in controlled trial/therapy-assisted settings.

High-yield exam pointers

  • Therapeutic lag (2–4 weeks) despite immediate monoamine elevation is the classic evidence AGAINST a pure monoamine-deficiency model — favours a neuroplasticity/BDNF-based explanation.
  • Esketamine/ketamine and zuranolone/brexanolone are the two distinct rapid-acting mechanistic classes (glutamatergic-NMDA vs GABAergic-neurosteroid) — do not conflate them.
  • Mirtazapine's mechanism (α2-autoreceptor antagonism ↑ NA/5-HT release) directly links back to the presynaptic autoreceptor concept covered in Section 1.
  • Bupropion lowers seizure threshold (avoid in seizure disorders/eating disorders); MAOIs require dietary tyramine restriction (classical, irreversible agents only — moclobemide as reversible MAO-A inhibitor is safer).
Recent advances: Esketamine and zuranolone represent the two newest distinct mechanistic classes in depression pharmacotherapy — both deliver days-to-hours onset versus the weeks-long lag of classical monoaminergic agents. Dextromethorphan-bupropion (Auvelity) offers an oral rapid-acting alternative without the dissociative/REMS burden of esketamine. Psilocybin-assisted therapy remains investigational but is among the most active current research fronts in treatment-resistant depression.

▸★Blood-brain barrier & blood-CSF barrier and their relevance in drug delivery.

G&G Ch.17 covers the BBB and drug transport to the brain.

Structure

  • BBB: brain capillary endothelial cells with tight junctions, no fenestrae, surrounded by astrocyte foot-processes & pericytes; efflux transporters (P-glycoprotein, BCRP) pump drugs out.
  • Blood-CSF barrier: choroid plexus epithelium with tight junctions.

Determinants of CNS entry

Favoured by: small size, high lipid solubility, non-ionised, low protein binding, not a P-gp substrate, or use of carrier transporters (e.g. L-dopa via LAT1).

Relevance / strategies for drug delivery

  • Limits entry of antibiotics, chemotherapy, biologics → CNS infections/tumours hard to treat.
  • Strategies: lipophilic prodrugs, carrier-mediated transport, nanoparticles/liposomes, "Trojan horse" antibody shuttles (transferrin/insulin receptor), intrathecal/intraventricular delivery, transient osmotic BBB disruption (mannitol), focused ultrasound.
  • Inflammation (meningitis) ↑ BBB permeability → better antibiotic penetration.
▸★CAR-T cell therapy — principle, applications & adverse effects.

Principle

  1. Harvest patient's T-cells (leukapheresis).
  2. Genetically engineer (viral vector) to express a Chimeric Antigen Receptor — an antibody-derived antigen-recognition domain fused to T-cell activation domains (CD3ζ + costimulatory CD28/4-1BB).
  3. Expand ex-vivo → reinfuse → CAR-T recognises tumour antigen (e.g. CD19) MHC-independently → targeted killing.

Applications

  • Anti-CD19: B-cell ALL, DLBCL/lymphoma (tisagenlecleucel, axicabtagene).
  • Anti-BCMA: multiple myeloma (idecabtagene, ciltacabtagene).
  • Trials in solid tumours.

Adverse effects

  • Cytokine release syndrome (CRS) — fever, hypotension, hypoxia → treat with tocilizumab (anti-IL-6R) ± steroids.
  • ICANS (immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity) — confusion, seizures.
  • B-cell aplasia/hypogammaglobulinaemia, tumour lysis syndrome, on-target off-tumour toxicity, very high cost.

▸★★★Atypical antipsychotic drugs — dopamine/serotonin hypothesis of schizophrenia, classification, mechanism, individual agents, adverse effects & comparison with typical antipsychotics.

Schizophrenia's dopamine hypothesis (mesolimbic D2 hyperactivity → positive symptoms) and the recognition that mesocortical dopaminergic/serotonergic hypofunction underlies negative/cognitive symptoms led to second-generation ("atypical") antipsychotics — agents combining D2 antagonism with significant 5-HT2A antagonism (and other receptor actions), giving efficacy against both positive and (modestly) negative symptoms with a lower risk of extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS) than first-generation (typical) agents.

Why "atypical"? — key distinguishing mechanism

  • High 5-HT2A : D2 antagonism ratio — serotonin antagonism in the nigrostriatal pathway disinhibits dopamine release, partially offsetting D2 blockade → fewer EPS/lower prolactin elevation than typical agents.
  • Many atypicals show fast dissociation from D2 receptors ("loose binding" — Kapur's fast-off theory), allowing normal phasic dopamine signalling to proceed, reducing EPS/prolactin while preserving antipsychotic effect.
  • Additional receptor actions (5-HT1A partial agonism, α1/H1/M1 antagonism, D3/D4 affinity) contribute to individual drug profiles (sedation, weight gain, anticholinergic effects).

  • Aripiprazole = D2/D3 partial agonist ("dopamine stabiliser") — distinct mechanism, lowest metabolic burden among widely used atypicals.
  • Metabolic syndrome is the defining long-term ADR concern for atypicals (vs EPS for typicals).

▸★Guidelines for management of insomnia — non-pharmacological & pharmacological approach, drug choice & duration.

Insomnia (difficulty initiating/maintaining sleep, or non-restorative sleep with daytime impairment, ≥3 nights/week for ≥3 months = chronic) management follows a stepwise approach — behavioural therapy first, pharmacotherapy reserved for short-term use or when CBT-I is insufficient/unavailable.

Step 1 — Non-pharmacological (first-line)

  • Cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia (CBT-I): sleep restriction, stimulus control, sleep hygiene education, relaxation training, cognitive restructuring — guideline-endorsed first-line treatment with durable benefit.
  • Sleep hygiene: regular sleep-wake schedule, avoid caffeine/alcohol/nicotine before bed, limit screen exposure, comfortable sleep environment, avoid daytime napping.

Principles of drug choice & duration

  • Use lowest effective dose, shortest duration (ideally <4 weeks) — chronic nightly hypnotic use is discouraged due to tolerance/dependence/rebound insomnia on withdrawal.
  • Match drug to symptom type: sleep-onset difficulty → short-acting agent (zaleplon, ramelteon); sleep-maintenance difficulty → longer-acting/DORA (eszopiclone, suvorexant).
  • Avoid benzodiazepines/Z-drugs in elderly where possible (fall risk, cognitive impairment) — prefer DORA or melatonin agonist.
  • Identify & treat secondary causes (depression, anxiety, OSA, restless-leg syndrome, medication-induced) before/alongside symptomatic therapy.

High-yield exam pointers

  • CBT-I = first-line per all major guidelines (AASM, ACP) — superior long-term outcomes vs drugs alone.
  • Z-drugs preferred over classical BZDs for pharmacological short-term treatment (selective ω1, less dependence).
  • DORAs (suvorexant, lemborexant, daridorexant) = orexin antagonism — newer mechanism distinct from GABA-A potentiation.
  • Avoid long-term antihistamine/BZD use, especially in the elderly.
Recent advances: Dual orexin receptor antagonists (daridorexant, 2022) represent the newest mechanistic class for insomnia, targeting the wake-promoting orexin system rather than potentiating GABA — offering effective sleep-maintenance benefit with lower next-day residual sedation and abuse-potential concerns than benzodiazepines/Z-drugs.
▸★Patient-controlled analgesia (PCA) — principle, drugs used, settings/parameters & advantages/limitations.

Patient-controlled analgesia (PCA) is a delivery technique allowing patients to self-administer small, pre-set "on-demand" doses of an (usually IV) analgesic via a programmable infusion pump, exploiting the considerable inter-individual variability in opioid requirement and allowing the patient to titrate analgesia to their own pain level within physician-set safety limits.

Principle

  • Patient presses a demand button when pain is felt → pump delivers a fixed bolus dose.
  • A programmed lockout interval (minimum time between doses, typically 5-10 min) prevents overdose by ensuring the previous dose has had time to act before another can be delivered.
  • Optional background/basal infusion may supplement bolus dosing (used cautiously — increases respiratory depression risk without proportionate analgesic benefit in most settings).
  • A 4-hour (or 1-hour) dose limit may be set as an additional safety ceiling.

Drugs commonly used in PCA

DrugTypical bolusLockoutNotes
Morphine1 mg5-10 minMost common; active metabolite (M6G) accumulates in renal impairment
Fentanyl10-20 mcg5-10 minFaster onset, shorter duration — useful in renal impairment
Hydromorphone0.2 mg5-10 minMore potent alternative to morphine
Tramadol10-20 mg5-10 minWeaker, useful for moderate pain/lower respiratory-depression risk

Advantages

  • Matches analgesia to individual pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic variability — avoids the "peaks and troughs" of intermittent IM/IV bolus dosing by nursing staff.
  • ↑ patient satisfaction & sense of control; generally equal or better analgesia with similar/lower total opioid consumption versus PRN nursing-administered dosing.
  • Reduces nursing workload for repeated dose administration.
  • Built-in safety (lockout interval) reduces risk of accidental overdose compared to free access.

Limitations/precautions

  • Requires patient who can understand & physically operate the device (unsuitable for cognitively impaired/very young/confused patients).
  • "PCA by proxy" (family/staff pressing the button for the patient) is a recognised safety hazard — bypasses the patient's own sedation-level safety check.
  • Still requires regular monitoring for respiratory depression/sedation (especially with background infusion).
  • Pump programming errors are a recognised source of medication error — double-checking of settings (drug concentration, bolus, lockout) is essential.

High-yield exam pointers

  • Lockout interval = key safety feature preventing overdose.
  • PCA relies on the patient becoming sedated/unable to press the button before overdose occurs — self-limiting safety principle (lost with "PCA by proxy").
  • Morphine = most commonly used; fentanyl preferred in renal impairment.
  • Background/basal infusion increases respiratory depression risk without proportional analgesic gain in most adult acute-pain settings.
Recent advances: Smart PCA pumps with integrated capnography/pulse-oximetry monitoring and automatic infusion suspension on detecting respiratory depression improve safety. Non-IV PCA routes (intranasal fentanyl PCA, transdermal iontophoretic fentanyl PCA systems) and PCA epidural analgesia (PCEA) for labour/postoperative pain are increasingly used alternatives to IV PCA.
▸★★★Status of newer antiepileptic drugs (AEDs) — classification by mechanism, individual agents, pharmacological basis of action & recent advances.

"Newer" (second/third-generation) AEDs were developed largely from the 1990s onward, designed for improved tolerability, fewer drug interactions (less hepatic enzyme induction/inhibition than phenytoin/carbamazepine/valproate) and novel mechanisms targeting specific channels, transporters or synaptic-vesicle proteins, rather than purely the classical Na⁺-channel block of older agents.

Pharmacological basis & advantages over older AEDs

  • Reduced hepatic CYP induction/inhibition (vs phenytoin/carbamazepine/phenobarbital) → fewer drug-drug interactions, important in polytherapy & with OCPs/anticoagulants.
  • More predictable pharmacokinetics (linear, less protein-binding displacement) — levetiracetam/gabapentin/pregabalin renally excreted, minimal metabolism.
  • Novel mechanisms (SV2A, AMPA antagonism, KCNQ opening) provide options for AED-resistant/refractory epilepsy by attacking different points in the seizure circuit.
  • Generally better tolerability profiles, though individual ADRs persist (perampanel — psychiatric; topiramate — cognitive; lamotrigine — Stevens-Johnson/DRESS, especially with rapid titration or valproate co-administration).

  • Levetiracetam = SV2A binding — distinct novel mechanism, minimal hepatic metabolism, broad-spectrum, favoured for rapid IV loading in status epilepticus alternative regimens.
  • Lacosamide = enhances slow (not fast) Na⁺-channel inactivation — a refinement over classical Na⁺-blockers.
  • Perampanel = first selective AMPA-receptor antagonist AED.
  • Cannabidiol (Epidiolex) = approved specifically for Dravet/Lennox-Gastaut syndromes — landmark cannabinoid-derived AED.
  • Vigabatrin's irreversible visual-field defect is a classic exam ADR.

Fenfluramine repurposed (low-dose) for Dravet syndrome (serotonergic mechanism). Soticlestat (cholesterol 24-hydroxylase inhibitor) and other novel-target compounds are in late-stage trials for developmental/epileptic encephalopathies, reflecting a continuing shift toward mechanistically novel, syndrome-specific AEDs.
▸★★Designer drugs — definition, classification, examples & clinical/forensic significance.

Designer drugs (also "novel psychoactive substances," NPS) are synthetic compounds chemically modified from known illicit/controlled drugs (or designed de novo) specifically to mimic their psychoactive effects while evading existing drug-control legislation and standard toxicology screens — a moving target as new analogues are continually synthesised once older ones are scheduled/banned.

Classification & examples

Synthetic cannabinoids"Spice," "K2" (JWH-018 and analogues)Potent CB1/CB2 full agonists (more potent than THC) — severe psychosis, tachycardia, seizures, death reported
Synthetic cathinones ("bath salts")Mephedrone, MDPV, methyloneDAT/NET/SERT inhibition (cocaine/amphetamine-like) — agitation, hyperthermia, psychosis, violent behaviour

Why "designer"? — pharmacological/legal rationale

  • Minor structural modification of a scheduled drug's molecule retains/enhances receptor activity while creating a chemically distinct (initially unscheduled/"legal") entity — historically marketed as "legal highs," "research chemicals," or "bath salts" to circumvent the law.
  • Rapid proliferation of analogues outpaces conventional drug-scheduling (analogue laws/generic scheduling by core structure have been introduced in response).
  • Often undetected by routine immunoassay drug screens (designed for parent/classical drugs) — complicates clinical recognition & forensic toxicology, requiring specialised LC-MS/MS testing.

Clinical & forensic significance

  • Unpredictable potency/purity (no quality control) → high overdose/toxicity risk; effects can be far more severe/prolonged than the parent drug they mimic.
  • Management is largely supportive (sedation with benzodiazepines for agitation/psychosis, cooling for hyperthermia, naloxone trial for suspected synthetic-opioid co-exposure even though efficacy may be reduced against ultra-potent fentanyl analogues).
  • Major public-health/forensic concern: synthetic-opioid (fentanyl-analogue) contamination of the illicit drug supply is a leading driver of the current overdose-death epidemic in several countries.

High-yield exam pointers

  • Designer drugs = structurally modified analogues of controlled substances, created to evade drug laws and routine toxicology screens.
  • Synthetic cannabinoids = full CB1 agonists (more potent/dangerous than natural cannabis THC, a partial agonist).
  • Synthetic cathinones = "bath salts" — monoamine transporter inhibitors, severe agitation/hyperthermia.
  • Fentanyl analogues = the most lethal current category — extreme potency, major overdose-death driver.
  • Routine immunoassay drug screens often miss NPS — clinical suspicion + specialised testing needed.
Recent advances: "Analogue"/generic scheduling laws (regulating entire chemical classes rather than single named compounds) have been adopted in several jurisdictions to keep pace with rapidly evolving designer-drug markets. Point-of-care and LC-MS/MS-based expanded toxicology panels are increasingly deployed in emergency/forensic settings to detect emerging NPS, including designer benzodiazepines and novel synthetic opioids contaminating the illicit drug supply.
▸★★Management of chronic pain — WHO analgesic ladder, multimodal pharmacotherapy, adjuvant agents & recent advances.

Chronic pain (persisting >3 months, often outlasting the original tissue injury, frequently with a neuropathic/central-sensitisation component) requires a multimodal, individualised approach combining pharmacological classes with different mechanisms, alongside non-pharmacological/interventional measures — rather than simple step-up opioid escalation.

  • Patient education & realistic goal-setting (function-focused rather than complete pain elimination).

  • Reserve for moderate-severe pain not responding to multimodal non-opioid therapy; use lowest effective dose, regular reassessment of benefit vs harm.
  • Screen for opioid-misuse risk factors before initiation; use prescription drug monitoring programs; avoid co-prescription with benzodiazepines (respiratory depression risk).
  • Consider abuse-deterrent formulations; taper rather than abruptly stop if discontinuing.

High-yield exam pointers

  • WHO 3-step ladder: non-opioid → weak opioid → strong opioid, ± adjuvant at every step.
  • Neuropathic pain first-line = gabapentinoids, TCAs, SNRIs (not primarily opioids).
  • Multimodal analgesia (combining classes) reduces total opioid requirement & ADR burden ("opioid-sparing" strategy).
  • Topical agents minimise systemic exposure — useful in elderly/localised pain.
Recent advances: Growing emphasis on opioid-sparing multimodal protocols (especially perioperative) using gabapentinoids, NSAIDs, regional blocks and low-dose ketamine to reduce opioid consumption amid the opioid-crisis context. Novel non-opioid analgesics in development include NaV1.7-selective sodium-channel blockers (aiming for opioid-equivalent analgesia without abuse potential) and anti-NGF monoclonal antibodies (tanezumab) for osteoarthritis pain, though regulatory approval has been complicated by joint-safety signals.

Beyond the established second-generation (atypical) antipsychotics, a "third generation" and several mechanistically novel agents have emerged, moving away from pure D2/5-HT2A receptor blockade toward partial agonism, alternative neurotransmitter targets (muscarinic, TAAR1) and improved metabolic/EPS profiles — addressing unmet needs in treatment-resistant disease and ADR burden.

  • Ulotaront: TAAR1/5-HT1A agonist — modulates dopaminergic/glutamatergic tone indirectly without direct D2 blockade; investigated for schizophrenia with a non-D2 mechanism, though pivotal trial results have been mixed.

Paliperidone palmitate (1-, 3-, 6-monthly), aripiprazole lauroxil/monohydrate, risperidone LAI — improve adherence in relapsing schizophrenia, reduce relapse/hospitalisation versus oral therapy in real-world studies.

High-yield exam pointers

  • Aripiprazole/brexpiprazole/cariprazine = D2/D3 partial agonists ("third generation") — lower EPS/prolactin/metabolic burden than full D2 antagonists.
  • Cariprazine's D3 preference is linked to potential negative-symptom/cognitive benefit.
  • KarXT (xanomeline-trospium) = first approved antipsychotic acting via muscarinic (M1/M4) rather than dopaminergic/serotonergic receptors — landmark mechanism shift.
  • TAAR1 agonists (ulotaront) = another non-D2 mechanism under investigation.
  • LAIs improve adherence — key strategy for relapse prevention in chronic schizophrenia.

Cariprazine's D3-preferring partial agonism continues to be explored for negative symptoms. Research into TAAR1 agonists and combination strategies (muscarinic + dopaminergic modulation) reflects a broader shift toward diversifying antipsychotic pharmacology beyond the 70-year-old D2-blockade paradigm.
▸★Teratogenicity of antiepileptic drugs — mechanisms, individual drug risk profiles & principles of management in pregnancy.

Most classical AEDs carry teratogenic risk, but the magnitude varies markedly between agents — informing preconception counselling and AED selection in women of childbearing potential. The clinical challenge is balancing teratogenic risk against the risk of uncontrolled maternal seizures (which themselves cause fetal hypoxia, trauma & are an independent risk to pregnancy outcome), so AEDs are not simply stopped in pregnancy.

Mechanisms of AED teratogenicity

  • Folate antagonism: valproate, phenytoin, carbamazepine, phenobarbital interfere with folate metabolism → neural tube/craniofacial defect risk; high-dose folic acid supplementation (4-5 mg/day, higher than standard) is recommended preconceptionally for women on AEDs, especially valproate.
  • Oxidative stress/free-radical intermediates: reactive arene-oxide metabolites of phenytoin/carbamazepine implicated in fetal hydantoin-type syndromes.
  • HDAC inhibition (valproate-specific): valproate's histone-deacetylase inhibitory action alters gene expression during critical embryonic developmental windows — proposed mechanism for its especially high malformation/neurodevelopmental risk.
  • Dose-dependence: risk for most AEDs (esp. valproate, lamotrigine, topiramate) increases with higher doses and polytherapy compared to monotherapy.

Principles of management in pregnancy

  • Preconception counselling — switch to lowest-risk effective agent (lamotrigine/levetiracetam preferred) before conception where feasible; avoid valproate in women of childbearing potential unless no suitable alternative.
  • Monotherapy at the lowest effective dose preferred over polytherapy — malformation risk roughly additive/synergistic with multiple AEDs.
  • High-dose folic acid supplementation from before conception through at least the first trimester.
  • Do not abruptly stop AEDs in a pregnant patient with epilepsy — uncontrolled seizures pose direct fetal/maternal risk (hypoxia, trauma, status epilepticus); manage under specialist (neurologist + obstetrician) joint care.
  • Antenatal monitoring: detailed anomaly ultrasound, monitor AED levels (pregnancy alters pharmacokinetics — e.g. lamotrigine clearance increases), vitamin K in late pregnancy if enzyme-inducing AEDs used (↓ neonatal haemorrhagic risk).
  • Pregnancy/AED registries (e.g. EURAP) continue to refine comparative risk data for newer agents.

High-yield exam pointers

  • Valproate = highest teratogenic risk (neural tube defects + significant cognitive/IQ impairment) — avoid in women of childbearing age if possible.
  • Lamotrigine and levetiracetam = relatively safer newer agents — preferred where seizure type permits switch.
  • Folate antagonism is the principal proposed mechanism for neural tube defects with older AEDs.
  • Never abruptly discontinue AEDs in pregnancy — seizure risk outweighs in most cases; switch/optimise instead.
  • High-dose folic acid supplementation recommended preconceptionally in all women on AEDs.
Recent advances: Long-term outcome data (e.g. from the NEAD study) have solidified valproate's distinct neurodevelopmental risk (↓IQ in exposed children) independent of structural malformation, driving regulatory restrictions (e.g. EU/UK pregnancy prevention programmes) on valproate use in women of childbearing potential. Pregnancy AED registries (EURAP, North American AED Pregnancy Registry) continue to refine risk estimates for newer agents (lacosamide, perampanel, brivaracetam), most of which still lack mature safety data.

▸🩺 CaseA farmer is brought in drowsy with pinpoint pupils, profuse salivation, lacrimation, sweating, muscle fasciculations and a smell of insecticide. Diagnosis and management?
Clue: DUMBELS toxidrome — which enzyme is inhibited?

Organophosphate poisoning — inhibits acetylcholinesterase → ACh excess (muscarinic DUMBELS + nicotinic fasciculation/weakness + CNS effects).

Management: ABC + decontamination; atropine (titrate to dry secretions) for muscarinic effects; pralidoxime (oxime) to reactivate AChE before enzyme 'aging' (helps nicotinic/muscle weakness); supportive care.

▸🩺 CaseA 30-year-old woman has fatigable ptosis and diplopia, worse in the evening. Which drugs help, and how do you distinguish a myasthenic from a cholinergic crisis?
Clue: ACh deficiency at the neuromuscular junction.

Myasthenia gravis — autoantibodies to nicotinic ACh receptors. Symptomatic: pyridostigmine/neostigmine (anticholinesterase raises ACh); plus immunosuppression (steroids, azathioprine), thymectomy; crisis → IVIG/plasmapheresis.

Myasthenic crisis = under-treatment (improves with edrophonium); cholinergic crisis = over-treatment (excess muscarinic features, worsens with edrophonium).

▸🩺 CaseA patient with episodic headache, palpitations, sweating and severe hypertension is diagnosed with pheochromocytoma and listed for surgery. Why must alpha-blockade precede beta-blockade?
Clue: Unopposed alpha.

Give phenoxybenzamine (alpha-blocker) first to control vasoconstriction. Starting a beta-blocker first removes beta-2 vasodilation → unopposed alpha-mediated vasoconstriction → hypertensive crisis. After adequate alpha-blockade, add a beta-blocker for tachyarrhythmia.

▸🩺 CaseA patient who overdosed on propranolol has severe bradycardia and hypotension unresponsive to atropine. Specific antidote?
Clue: Bypass the beta-receptor.

Glucagon — activates cardiac adenylyl cyclase independently of the beta-receptor (raises cAMP → increases rate and contractility). Add IV fluids, atropine, high-dose insulin-euglycaemia therapy, and pacing if needed.

▸🩺 CaseA patient develops urticaria, wheeze and hypotension minutes after a bee sting. First-line drug and the underlying principle?
Clue: Physiological antagonism.

Adrenaline IM (0.5 mg of 1:1000) — alpha-1 vasoconstriction (raises BP), beta-1 inotropy, beta-2 bronchodilation + mast-cell stabilisation. It is a physiological (functional) antagonist of histamine (acts via different receptors). Adjuncts: oxygen, IV fluids, antihistamine, steroid.

▸🩺 CaseAn asthmatic using timolol eye drops for glaucoma develops worsening wheeze. Explain.
Clue: Beta-2 blockade.

Non-selective beta-blockers (timolol) block bronchial beta-2 receptors → bronchoconstriction; even ophthalmic drops are absorbed systemically. Use an alternative in asthma (e.g. brimonidine or a topical carbonic anhydrase inhibitor).

▸🩺 CaseA patient on an SSRI is given tramadol and develops agitation, fever, tremor, clonus and hyperreflexia. Diagnosis and management?
Clue: Excess serotonin.

Serotonin syndrome — excess serotonergic activity (SSRI + tramadol/MAOI/triptan). Stop the offending drugs, supportive cooling, benzodiazepines; cyproheptadine (5-HT2 antagonist) for severe cases.

▸🩺 CaseA patient on a monoamine oxidase inhibitor eats aged cheese and develops a severe throbbing headache and hypertensive crisis. Mechanism?
Clue: Tyramine.

The cheese reaction — MAO normally degrades dietary tyramine; an MAOI lets tyramine enter nerve terminals → massive noradrenaline release → hypertensive crisis. Manage with phentolamine; advise a tyramine-free diet.

▸🩺 CaseA child who ingested Datura is flushed and febrile with dilated pupils, dry skin, urinary retention and delirium. Toxidrome and antidote?
Clue: Hot, dry, red, blind, mad.

Anticholinergic (antimuscarinic) toxidrome. Supportive cooling, benzodiazepines for agitation, and physostigmine (crosses the blood-brain barrier, reversing central and peripheral effects) for severe toxicity.
