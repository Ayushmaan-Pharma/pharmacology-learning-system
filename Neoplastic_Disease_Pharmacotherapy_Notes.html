Section 1 · Preclinical Toxicity Studies & Regulatory Aspects

▸★★★Aims, methodology & limitations of acute & chronic toxicity studies; battery of safety studies before IND; note on OECD guidelines, CCSEA, 3R/4R, alternatives & rodent euthanasia.

Acute toxicity

  • Aim: determine short-term toxicity of a single/24-h dose; approximate lethal dose, target organs, dose range for further studies.
  • Method: single escalating doses in ≥2 species (rodent + non-rodent), ≥2 routes incl. clinical route; observe 14 days; gross pathology. Modern OECD methods — fixed-dose (420), up-and-down (425), acute-toxic-class (423) — replace classical LD₅₀ (fewer animals).

Sub-acute & chronic toxicity

  • Aim: repeated-dose toxicity → NOAEL, cumulative & target-organ effects, reversibility.
  • Method: sub-acute 14–28 days; chronic up to 6–12 months in 2 species, 3 dose levels + control; haematology, clinical chemistry, histopathology; duration matched to intended clinical use.

Full safety battery before IND

  • Acute, sub-acute & chronic toxicity.
  • Safety pharmacology (CVS — hERG/QT, CNS, respiratory).
  • Genotoxicity (Ames, micronucleus, chromosomal aberration).
  • Reproductive/developmental toxicity (Segment I–III, teratogenicity).
  • Carcinogenicity (chronic-use drugs), local tolerance, immunotoxicity, toxicokinetics.

OECD guidelines

Internationally harmonised, validated test methods for chemical/drug toxicity (e.g. TG 423/425 acute oral, repeated-dose, genotoxicity, carcinogenicity), ensuring reproducible, ethical, mutually-accepted data (MAD).

CCSEA, 3R/4R & alternatives

  • CCSEA (formerly CPCSEA) — statutory body regulating ethical animal experimentation in India via IAEC.
  • 3R: Replacement (in-vitro, organoids, in-silico), Reduction (better statistics), Refinement (↓ suffering); 4th R = Rehabilitation.
  • Alternatives: cell lines, organ-on-chip, QSAR/PBPK, zebrafish, reconstructed human epidermis, monocyte activation test.

Euthanasia methods for rodents

CO₂ inhalation (gradual fill — commonest), anaesthetic overdose (pentobarbital); physical (cervical dislocation/decapitation in small rodents, by trained staff, often after sedation). Must ensure rapid unconsciousness & confirm death (AVMA/CCSEA humane endpoints).

Limitations

Species differences → poor human prediction; high cost/time; ethical concerns; cannot detect idiosyncratic/immune ADRs; high-dose extrapolation issues.

Recent: FDA Modernization Act 2.0 (2022) permits non-animal alternatives for IND data.

Role of animal toxicity studies

  • Establish safety profile, target organs, NOAEL → estimate the safe starting dose for first-in-human trials.
  • Detect genotoxicity, teratogenicity, carcinogenicity before human exposure.
  • Provide toxicokinetic & reversibility data; mandatory regulatory requirement (IND/Schedule Y/NDCT Rules).

Section 2 · General Cancer Chemotherapy & Recent Advances

▸★★★Pharmacotherapeutic advances in cancer therapy — paradigm shift from cytotoxic to precision/immuno-oncology; mechanistic classification (examples); altered metabolic signature of cancer & mechanisms of drug resistance.

Conceptual framework — why the shift happened

Cancer pharmacotherapy has shifted over two decades from non-selective cytotoxic chemotherapy (exploits high tumour growth fraction but spares no rapidly-dividing tissue) toward precision/personalised oncology targeting specific molecular vulnerabilities and harnessing host immunity. Expanding knowledge of driver mutations, signal-transduction pathways, angiogenesis, immune checkpoints and tumour antigens has produced targeted small molecules, mAbs, ADCs, checkpoint inhibitors, bispecific antibodies, CAR-T cells and cancer vaccines — increasingly selected by biomarker/companion diagnostic rather than tumour site of origin.

  • Cell-cycle/growth-fraction logic: phase-specific cytotoxics act mainly in S/M; phase-nonspecific agents kill at any point. High-growth-fraction tumours (leukaemia, lymphoma) respond well to cytotoxics; slow-growing solid tumours respond poorly — the ceiling of conventional chemotherapy.
  • Apoptosis & checkpoints: drug-induced DNA damage triggers apoptosis only if checkpoint proteins (p53, chk-1/2) are intact; their loss confers resistance.
  • Rational combinations: non-cross-resistant drugs with differing mechanisms/toxicities maximise log-kill and delay resistance; targeted drugs also overcome chemoresistance by normalising tumour blood flow and releasing antigens that prime immune responses.
Diagram (evolution of cancer therapy): Cytotoxic chemotherapy → Hormonal therapy → Targeted small molecules/mAbs → Angiogenesis inhibitors → Immune checkpoint inhibitors → CAR-T/bispecifics → Tumour-agnostic/biomarker-guided therapy.

Modality detail — key agents & mechanisms

  • Imatinib — first selectively-targeted anticancer drug; inhibits BCR-ABL (+ PDGFR, c-KIT); >90% remission in chronic-phase CML, DOC for c-KIT+ GIST; CYP3A4 metabolised, t½ ~18 h; resistance via BCR-ABL point mutations. Dasatinib/nilotinib (2nd-gen) overcome most resistant mutations, can prolong QT.
  • EGFR inhibitors (gefitinib, erlotinib) block EGFR autophosphorylation — effective in EGFR-mutant NSCLC; toxicity = acneiform rash, diarrhoea, rare ILD. Lapatinib — oral EGFR/HER2 dual TKI for trastuzumab-refractory HER2+ breast cancer.
  • Bortezomib — covalently inhibits the proteasome → prevents IκB degradation → NF-κB not released; prime indication multiple myeloma; dose-limiting peripheral neuropathy + thrombocytopenia.
  • Rituximab — anti-CD20; kills B-cells via ADCC + CDC; backbone of NHL/CLL. Trastuzumab — anti-HER2; characteristic ↓LVEF/cardiomyopathy. Cetuximab — anti-EGFR; acneiform rash, hypomagnesaemia. Bevacizumab — anti-VEGF-A; hypertension, thromboembolism, GI perforation.
  • ADCs marry antibody selectivity to a potent cytotoxic payload via a linker — binding → receptor-mediated endocytosis → intracellular payload release, sparing antigen-negative cells (T-DM1/T-DXd for HER2; brentuximab vedotin for CD30/Hodgkin; gemtuzumab for CD33/AML).
  • Checkpoint inhibitors — CTLA-4 (CD28 homologue, higher B7 affinity) dampens priming, blocked by ipilimumab (first ICI, FDA 2011, melanoma; 2018 Nobel — Allison & Honjo); PD-1/PD-L1 dampens the tumour-microenvironment effector phase, blocked by pembrolizumab/nivolumab (anti-PD-1) and atezolizumab/durvalumab (anti-PD-L1); relatlimab targets the newer LAG-3 checkpoint. ~50% response in metastatic melanoma with anti-PD-1+anti-CTLA-4 combination. irAEs (rash/vitiligo, colitis, hepatitis, hypophysitis, thyroiditis) managed by withholding drug + high-dose steroids; pseudoprogression can mimic treatment failure.
  • Blinatumomab — first-in-class CD19×CD3 BiTE bridging T-cell to B-cell, MHC-independent killing; approved for Ph− relapsed/refractory B-ALL; toxicity = cytokine release syndrome (CRS) + neurotoxicity.
  • CAR-T cells — scFv (tumour antigen recognition) fused to costimulatory (4-1BB/CD28) + CD3-ζ domains; tisagenlecleucel (first CD19 CAR-T, 2017, >80% remission in refractory B-ALL); idecabtagene vicleucel (anti-BCMA) for myeloma. CRS treated with tocilizumab (anti-IL-6R) ± steroids; resistance via CD19 antigen loss (salvage anti-CD22 CAR-T).
  • Sipuleucel-T — autologous APCs pulsed with PAP-GM-CSF for hormone-refractory prostate cancer. T-VEC — oncolytic HSV expressing GM-CSF for melanoma.
  • Hormonal therapy — breast (tamoxifen, fulvestrant, aromatase inhibitors); prostate (GnRH agonists ± antiandrogens — flutamide, bicalutamide).
  • Angiogenesis inhibitors — bevacizumab + chemo in mCRC/NSCLC/RCC/glioblastoma; sunitinib/sorafenib (oral VEGFR-2 multikinase TKIs) for RCC/HCC/GIST/thyroid Ca; class risk = hypertension, bleeding, perforation, impaired healing.
  • Tumour-agnostic/biomarker-guided therapy — pembrolizumab was first approved by tumour genotype (any MSI-H/dMMR solid tumour, later TMB ≥10 mut/Mb) rather than site. Companion diagnostics (HER2, EGFR, MSI/dMMR, TMB) select likely responders. Liquid biopsy (ctDNA) tracks tumour burden, detects resistance mutations, follows clonal evolution non-invasively.
  • Also part of the modern armamentarium (universally accepted, brief note): PARP inhibitors (olaparib, niraparib, rucaparib — synthetic lethality in BRCA-mutant ovarian/breast/prostate cancer) and NTRK-fusion tumour-agnostic agents (larotrectinib, entrectinib).

Altered metabolic signature of cancer

  • Warburg effect: tumour cells favour aerobic glycolysis over oxidative phosphorylation even with O₂ → rapid ATP & biosynthetic precursors.
  • Glutamine addiction, ↑ lipid & nucleotide synthesis → exploited as drug targets (e.g. IDH inhibitors, glutaminase inhibitors).

Mechanisms of drug resistance

  • ↑ Efflux (P-glycoprotein/MDR1 — multidrug resistance).
  • ↓ Drug uptake/activation; ↑ inactivation (glutathione); poor delivery, target mutation, repair-enzyme overexpression.
  • ↑ DNA repair; altered/over-expressed target; ↓ apoptosis (p53 mutation, ↑ Bcl-2, loss of apoptotic pathways).
  • Tumour heterogeneity, mutational load & clonal selection — drives the shift to biomarker-guided, mechanism-specific therapy.
Recent advances: Antibody-drug conjugates are among the fastest-growing classes — trastuzumab deruxtecan has extended benefit to HER2-low breast cancer, broadening the eligible population beyond classic HER2-positive disease. Bispecific antibodies are expanding beyond blinatumomab into lymphoma/myeloma (BCMA/CD3, CD20/CD3 constructs). CAR-T therapy continues to move into earlier treatment lines and solid-tumour research; CRS management with tocilizumab is standard of care. mRNA neoantigen cancer vaccines combined with checkpoint inhibitors are in active clinical development. Practice is increasingly biomarker-first (MSI-H/dMMR, TMB, ctDNA), consolidating the shift from cytotoxic to precision and immuno-oncology. Tumour-agnostic therapy (larotrectinib for NTRK), PARP inhibitors (olaparib — BRCA) and CDK4/6 inhibitors round out the modern precision-oncology toolkit.

Alkylating agents

Transfer alkyl groups to DNA (N7-guanine) → cross-linking → strand breaks → apoptosis; cell-cycle non-specific. Classes: nitrogen mustards (cyclophosphamide, chlorambucil), nitrosoureas (carmustine — cross BBB), platinum compounds (cisplatin), alkyl sulfonates (busulfan). Toxicity: myelosuppression, infertility, secondary malignancy; cyclophosphamide → cystitis (prevent with mesna).

Antimetabolites

Structural analogues of normal metabolites → inhibit DNA/RNA synthesis (S-phase). Methotrexate (dihydrofolate reductase inhibitor — rescue with leucovorin), 5-FU (thymidylate synthase), 6-MP (purine synthesis; ↑ toxicity with allopurinol/TPMT deficiency), cytarabine, gemcitabine.

Plant alkaloids / spindle inhibitors

  • Vinca alkaloids (vincristine, vinblastine, vinorelbine): bind tubulin → prevent microtubule assembly → metaphase arrest. Vincristine — peripheral neuropathy (little myelosuppression); vinblastine — myelosuppression.
  • Taxanes (paclitaxel, docetaxel): stabilise microtubules → prevent disassembly.
  • Etoposide (podophyllotoxin — topoisomerase II); camptothecins (irinotecan/topotecan — topoisomerase I).

Cytotoxic antibiotics

From Streptomyces. Doxorubicin/daunorubicin (anthracyclines — intercalation + topo-II + free radicals → dose-dependent cardiotoxicity, reduced by dexrazoxane); bleomycin (DNA strand breaks → pulmonary fibrosis, minimal myelosuppression); mitomycin, dactinomycin.

▸★★Discuss the log-kill hypothesis (phenomenon) in cancer chemotherapy and its clinical implications.

Skipper-Schabel log-kill hypothesis: a given dose of an anticancer drug kills a constant fraction (not a constant number) of tumour cells - i.e. first-order kinetics. A regimen giving a "3-log kill" destroys 99.9% of cells regardless of the starting number.

Implications

  • Because each cycle kills a fraction, repeated cycles are needed to reduce cell number below 1 (cure); the same logic favours treating when tumour burden is low (adjuvant therapy).
  • Intervals allow normal-tissue recovery; rationale for combination chemotherapy and adequate dose intensity.

Refinements

  • Gompertzian growth: large tumours grow slowly with a small growth fraction → relatively chemo-resistant; smaller tumours are more sensitive.
  • Goldie-Coldman: resistance arises by spontaneous mutation → start early, combine non-cross-resistant drugs.
  • Norton-Simon: supports dose-dense scheduling.
▸★Cell cycle kinetics in cancer chemotherapy — phases, cycle-specific vs cycle-nonspecific drugs, growth fraction & implications for drug scheduling.

Definition

Cell cycle kinetics describes how tumour cells transit the proliferative cycle and how this governs the rational, scheduled use of cytotoxic drugs. Because most cytotoxics damage DNA or the mitotic apparatus, efficacy is greatest in proliferating cells; cycle phase, growth fraction and cell-kill kinetics together underpin drug selection, dosing and combination design.

Phases of the cell cycle

  • G1 — pre-DNA-synthesis (gap) interval.
  • S — DNA synthesis.
  • G2 — post-synthetic interval (cell carries double DNA complement).
  • M — mitosis; yields two daughter G1 cells that re-enter the cycle or exit to G0.
  • G0 — quiescent/resting; a clonogenic fraction can be recruited back into cycle later.
  • Transitions are driven by cyclin–CDK complexes (e.g. cyclin D–CDK4/6 for G1→S) and policed by checkpoints (p53, chk-1/2).

Tumour growth kinetics

  • Log-kill hypothesis: a given dose kills a constant fraction (not number) of cells → first-order kinetics; e.g. each cycle kills ~99% = 2-log reduction. Since a single clonogenic cell can regenerate the tumour, every malignant cell must ultimately be eradicated for cure.
  • Growth fraction — proportion of cells actively cycling. High-growth-fraction tumours (leukaemias, lymphomas, large solid tumours) respond best to CCS drugs; small-growth-fraction tumours (colon, NSCLC) are relatively CCS-resistant. Clinically detectable disease ≈ 1 g ≈ 10⁹ cells.
  • Gompertzian kinetics — growth fraction is high in small tumours but falls as the tumour enlarges and outgrows its blood supply, so large tumours respond poorly until debulking (surgery/radiation) recruits G0 cells back into cycle.

Implications for scheduling & combination

  • Give CCS drugs in short pulses to let non-cycling cells re-enter the cycle; schedule CCS after CCNS (which recruits G0 cells) to maximise kill.
  • Combine drugs with different mechanisms, non-overlapping toxicities, no cross-resistance; deliver at highest tolerable dose, shortest tolerable interval (first-order kinetics), typically 6–11 cycles.
Diagram (cell-cycle wheel keyed to drug action): G0, G1, S, G2, M arranged in a wheel — CDK4/6 inhibitors act at G1→S; antimetabolites act in S; bleomycin/etoposide act in G2; vinca alkaloids/taxanes act in M; alkylating agents/cisplatin act CCNS across all phases.

High-yield exam pointers

  • CCNS kill resting and dividing cells; CCS kill only dividing cells (toxicity usually in S).
  • Antimetabolites = S phase; vinca alkaloids & taxanes = M phase; alkylating agents & cisplatin = CCNS.
  • Log-kill = constant fraction killed/cycle (first-order); 1 log = 90%, ~99% kill/cycle in effective regimens.
  • High growth fraction → CCS-sensitive; low growth fraction → CCS-resistant, CCNS still useful.
  • Gompertzian: debulking small residual disease ↑ growth fraction → chemo more effective (rationale for adjuvant therapy).

Section 3 · Targeted Therapy, Monoclonal Antibodies & Immunotherapy

▸★★★Targeted cancer therapies & pathway-targeted therapy; diagnostic & therapeutic role of monoclonal antibodies (incl. rituximab & immunoscintigraphy); mAbs vs small-molecule kinase inhibitors; note on checkpoint inhibitors & cancer vaccines.

Targeted cancer therapy — concept

Targeted (molecularly-targeted) cancer therapies are agents directed against specific molecular drivers of malignancy — mutated/overexpressed kinases, growth-factor receptors, signal-transduction proteins or surface antigens — rather than the general machinery of cell division. Unlike conventional cytotoxics (narrow therapeutic index, damage DNA/microtubules of all rapidly-dividing cells), targeted drugs exploit "oncogene addiction", so are more selective, better tolerated, and need biomarker-guided patient selection. Two broad types: orally-active small molecules (-nib) that penetrate cells and inhibit cancer-specific enzymes/pathways, and parenteral monoclonal antibodies (-mab) that attack cell-surface targets.

Signal-transduction basis (pathway-targeted therapy)

Receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs) dimerise on ligand binding, autophosphorylate, and recruit downstream effectors via four key pathways:

  • RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK (MAP-kinase) — drives proliferation via cyclin-D→CDK4/6→pRB phosphorylation→E2F-mediated G1→S transition; RAS is membrane-anchored by farnesylation.
  • PI3K–AKT–mTOR — promotes growth/translation/survival; negatively regulated by PTEN. Activating PIK3CA and inactivating PTEN mutations are common.
  • JAK–STAT — activated by type-I cytokine receptors.
  • Ubiquitin–proteasome system — post-translational control of cell-cycle/apoptotic proteins (target of proteasome inhibitors).

Cancers hijack these via receptor overexpression/mutation (EGFR, HER2, ALK), activating kinase mutations (RAS, RAF, MEK, PI3K), fusion oncoproteins (BCR-ABL, EML4-ALK), or loss of inhibitors (PTEN). Pathway-targeted drugs block these nodes.

Key mechanisms (high-yield)

  • Small-molecule TKIs compete with ATP at the intracellular kinase domain. Imatinib fixes BCR-ABL in the inactive conformation; dasatinib binds both active and inactive states. Afatinib (EGFR) and BRAF inhibitors bind covalently/with higher affinity.
  • Monoclonal antibodies bind cell-surface antigens, blocking ligand/signalling and recruiting immune kill — ADCC and CDC (rituximab).
  • ADCs marry antibody specificity to a cytotoxic payload delivered intracellularly (ado-trastuzumab emtansine, trastuzumab deruxtecan).
  • PARP inhibitors exploit synthetic lethality — blocking base-excision repair is lethal only when homologous recombination is already defective (BRCA-mutant cells).

Pharmacokinetics & adverse effects

Most oral TKIs are CYP3A4 substrates → interactions with inducers (rifampin, ↓levels) and inhibitors (↑levels); imatinib t½≈18 h, gefitinib t½≈40 h, vemurafenib t½≈57 h, sunitinib t½≈100 h. Class-typical toxicities: EGFR inhibitors → acneiform rash, diarrhoea, ILD; trastuzumab/lapatinib → ↓LVEF; BRAF inhibitors → cutaneous SCC/keratoacanthoma; anti-angiogenics → hypertension, bleeding, thromboembolism, proteinuria, GI perforation; bortezomib → peripheral neuropathy, thrombocytopenia; rapalogs → hyperglycaemia, pneumonitis, mucositis.

Biomarker-driven selection & resistance

Therapy is matched to a predictive biomarker: BCR-ABL (CML), EGFR mutation (NSCLC), HER2 overexpression, BRAF V600E, ALK translocation, RAS-wild-type (cetuximab), BRCA mutation (olaparib) — via companion diagnostics. Resistance arises from secondary "gatekeeper" mutations (BCR-ABL mutations; EGFR T790M → countered by osimertinib) or bypass/parallel-pathway activation (MET amplification, PI3K-AKT-mTOR, NRAS/MEK in BRAF therapy). Rational combinations (BRAF+MEK inhibitor) delay resistance.

Monoclonal antibodies — therapeutic & diagnostic

  • Therapeutic: block receptors/ligands, flag cells for immune destruction (ADCC/CDC), or deliver payloads (antibody–drug conjugates, e.g. T-DM1).
  • Rituximab — anti-CD20 chimeric mAb; depletes B-cells → non-Hodgkin lymphoma, CLL, also RA & autoimmune disease.
  • Diagnostic — immunoscintigraphy: radiolabelled mAbs (e.g. ¹¹¹In/⁹⁹ᵐTc-tagged) bind tumour antigens → imaged by gamma camera/SPECT to localise/stage tumours; basis of radioimmunotherapy (⁹⁰Y-ibritumomab).

Immune checkpoint inhibitors

  • Release the "brakes" on T-cells: anti-CTLA-4 (ipilimumab), anti-PD-1 (nivolumab, pembrolizumab), anti-PD-L1 (atezolizumab, durvalumab).
  • Uses: melanoma, NSCLC, RCC, bladder, Hodgkin, MSI-high tumours. ADR: immune-related adverse events (colitis, pneumonitis, thyroiditis, hepatitis) → managed with steroids.

Cancer vaccines

Prophylactic — HPV (cervical), HBV (hepatocellular); therapeutic — sipuleucel-T (prostate); emerging personalised mRNA neoantigen vaccines (melanoma trials).

High-yield exam pointers

  • Targeted ≠ cytotoxic: exploits oncogene addiction, needs a predictive biomarker, generally spares marrow/GI mucosa.
  • Imatinib = first targeted drug; DOC for chronic-phase CML and c-KIT+ GIST; binds inactive BCR-ABL.
  • Osimertinib overcomes EGFR T790M gatekeeper resistance.
  • BRAF inhibitor + MEK inhibitor combo (dabrafenib+trametinib) in V600E melanoma delays resistance; watch for cutaneous SCC.
  • Two cardinal class toxicities: trastuzumab → cardiotoxicity (↓LVEF); anti-angiogenics → hypertension/bleeding/perforation.
Recent advances: KRAS G12C inhibitors (sotorasib, adagrasib) drug the previously "undruggable" KRAS G12C mutation in pre-treated NSCLC; bypass-pathway secondary mutations drive acquired resistance. ADCs expanding indications — trastuzumab deruxtecan extended benefit to HER2-low metastatic breast cancer, redefining HER2 testing thresholds (DESTINY-Breast04). Tumour-agnostic, biomarker-defined approvals (NTRK-fusion inhibitors larotrectinib/entrectinib; BRAF, MSI-high) reflect a shift from organ-based to molecular classification. Trend toward next-generation covalent/allosteric inhibitors and combination blockade to pre-empt resistance.

▸★★Antibody-based therapy (monoclonal antibodies) — structure, nomenclature, mechanisms of action & adverse effects.

Definition

Monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) are identical immunoglobulins (usually IgG1, ~150 kDa) produced from a single B-cell clone (hybridoma technology, Köhler & Milstein) that bind one specific antigenic epitope. The Fab region (containing 3 CDRs) confers antigen specificity; the Fc region recruits immune effector mechanisms. They are a cornerstone of targeted therapy in oncology, autoimmune disease and transplantation.

Structure, types & nomenclature

The pre-2021 WHO suffix indicates species composition; immunogenicity falls as murine→chimeric→humanised→fully human (WHO revised mAb nomenclature Oct 2021, replacing "-mab" with four new stems).

Mechanisms of action

  • Receptor/ligand blockade — cetuximab (EGFR), bevacizumab (VEGF), trastuzumab (HER2).
  • Fc-mediated effector functions — ADCC, CDC, ADCP; e.g. rituximab (anti-CD20) lyses B-cells via CDC + ADCC.
  • Immune checkpoint inhibition — pembrolizumab/nivolumab (PD-1), ipilimumab (CTLA-4) release T-cell anti-tumour responses.
  • Antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) — deliver a cytotoxic payload, e.g. trastuzumab emtansine, gemtuzumab.
  • Bispecific/T-cell engagers — blinatumomab links CD3 (T-cell) to CD19 (tumour); related CAR-T cells (tisagenlecleucel, anti-CD19) are engineered autologous T cells.

Adverse effects

  • Infusion/hypersensitivity reactions from cytokine release (~3% with cetuximab).
  • Immunogenicity (HAMA/HACA antibodies) → loss of efficacy and anaphylaxis (highest with murine mAbs).
  • Target-specific: trastuzumab → cardiotoxicity/heart failure (worse with anthracyclines); rituximab → immunosuppression, opportunistic infection, HBV reactivation; anti-TNF agents → infection/TB reactivation; checkpoint inhibitors → immune-related adverse events (colitis, thyroiditis, hepatitis).

High-yield exam pointers

  • Suffix code: -momab murine, -ximab chimeric, -zumab humanised, -umab fully human.
  • Fab = specificity (CDRs); Fc = ADCC/CDC/ADCP.
  • Rituximab = anti-CD20 (NHL/CLL, RA); trastuzumab = anti-HER2 (breast Ca, cardiotoxic); pembrolizumab = anti-PD-1; adalimumab = anti-TNF (fully human).
  • Immunogenicity decreases murine→fully human; HAMA reduces efficacy.
Recent advances: ADCs are in a clinical renaissance — over 11 FDA-approved by 2023 (e.g. mirvetuximab, approved Nov 2022); next-generation linker/payload engineering aims to widen the therapeutic index. Bispecific antibodies and T-cell engagers are now entering treatment guidelines alongside checkpoint inhibitors and TKIs in solid tumours such as NSCLC. Biosimilar mAbs (rituximab, trastuzumab, adalimumab) are CDSCO/FDA-approved and improving access.
▸★Proteasome inhibitors — mechanism of action, drugs & adverse effects in multiple myeloma.

Definition

The 26S proteasome is a packaged complex of proteolytic enzymes that degrades ubiquitin-tagged intracellular proteins regulating the cell cycle, apoptosis and survival responses. Proteasome inhibitors are targeted anticancer agents that block this degradation, tipping malignant plasma cells toward apoptosis. They are a cornerstone of multiple myeloma (MM) therapy.

Mechanism of action

  • Within the ubiquitin–proteasome pathway, ubiquitin-tagged proteins are degraded by the 26S proteasome (catalytic 20S core).
  • Bortezomib binds the β5 subunit of the 20S core and inhibits its chymotrypsin-like activity (a boron-containing compound that binds the proteasome and blocks proteolysis).
  • Key consequence — NF-κB suppression: cytosolic NF-κB is normally held inactive by IκB. On stress (hypoxia, chemotherapy, DNA damage), IκB is ubiquitinated and degraded by the proteasome, freeing NF-κB to enter the nucleus and transcribe pro-survival/anti-apoptotic genes (cyclin D1, cIAPs, BCL-2). By blocking IκB degradation, bortezomib traps NF-κB in the cytosol, downregulating survival signalling.
  • Also causes accumulation of pro-apoptotic regulators (Bax, p21, p27, p53) and triggers the unfolded-protein response, committing MM cells to apoptosis.

  • Bortezomib = peripheral neuropathy + thrombocytopenia; ixazomib = first oral proteasome inhibitor.
  • Main indications: multiple myeloma and relapsed/refractory mantle-cell lymphoma.
  • Carfilzomib carries notable cardiac toxicity; bortezomib may be given subcutaneously to reduce neuropathy.
Recent advances: Ixazomib enabled all-oral triplet regimens (ixazomib–lenalidomide–dexamethasone) for MM. Proteasome inhibitor + immunomodulatory drug (IMiD) ± anti-CD38 (daratumumab) triplets/quadruplets are standard MM backbones in current practice.

▸★★★GnRH analogues — classification (agonists vs antagonists), mechanism (flare vs immediate suppression), clinical uses across oncology & benign disease, and adverse effects.

Definition / physiology

GnRH is a decapeptide from the hypothalamic kisspeptin (KNDy)-driven pulse generator, acting on a Gq/G11-coupled GPCR in the pituitary. Pulsatile GnRH stimulates gonadotropin (LH/FSH) release; continuous/supraphysiological exposure desensitises/downregulates the receptor → suppresses gonadotropins — the basis for both diagnostic (pulsatile) and therapeutic (continuous) use.

Hypogonadism symptoms (hot flushes, decreased libido, vaginal dryness, gynaecomastia in men), and osteoporosis risk with prolonged use — limiting benign-disease use to roughly 6 months unless add-back therapy is used.

Section 6 · Fixed Dose Combinations & Drug Interactions

▸★★Compare FDCs and co-formulations — advantages, disadvantages, clinical significance & rationale for antimicrobial combinations.

Definitions

  • Fixed-Dose Combination (FDC): two or more drugs combined in a single dosage form in fixed ratio (e.g. amoxicillin + clavulanate).
  • Co-formulation: drugs formulated/packaged together (often used interchangeably with FDC; may also mean co-packaged units) — combined for concurrent administration.

Advantages

  • Improved compliance (fewer pills), synergy, ↓ pill burden & cost, ↓ resistance (TB, HIV, malaria), convenience.

Disadvantages

  • Cannot titrate individual doses; ADR of one component hard to attribute; risk of irrational FDCs; pharmacokinetic mismatch (different half-lives); inflexible.

Clinical significance / Indian context

Useful for chronic, multi-drug conditions (HTN, diabetes, TB, HIV). India banned hundreds of irrational FDCs lacking therapeutic justification (CDSCO). Rational FDCs must show that the combination is more effective/safer than components alone.

Rationale for antimicrobial combinations

  • Prevent/delay resistance (TB — HRZE; HIV — ART; malaria — ACT).
  • Achieve synergy (β-lactam + aminoglycoside; amoxicillin + clavulanate β-lactamase inhibition; cotrimoxazole sequential blockade).
  • Broaden spectrum in mixed/empirical infections; ↓ toxicity of individual agents.

Section 7 · Other Important Pharmacotherapy Topics

▸★★★Pharmacotherapy (rationale & recent advances) of: RA, osteoporosis, gout, BPH, obesity, glaucoma, asthma (GINA), migraine, acid-peptic disease & H. pylori, IBD, IBS, PAH, CHF.

A consolidated revision of major disease pharmacotherapy — each with key drugs, rationale & recent advances.

1. Rheumatoid arthritis

NSAIDs/steroids (symptom) → csDMARDs (methotrexate anchor, sulfasalazine, leflunomide, HCQ) → biologics (TNF-α: infliximab, adalimumab, etanercept; IL-6: tocilizumab; anti-CD20: rituximab; abatacept) → JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib, baricitinib). Treat-to-target; screen latent TB before biologics.

2. Osteoporosis

Antiresorptive: bisphosphonates (alendronate, zoledronate), denosumab (anti-RANKL), SERM (raloxifene), calcitonin. Anabolic: teriparatide/abaloparatide (PTH analogues), romosozumab (anti-sclerostin). Plus calcium + vitamin D. Recent: romosozumab dual action.

3. Gout

Acute: NSAIDs, colchicine, steroids. Urate-lowering (chronic): allopurinol/febuxostat (xanthine oxidase inhibitors), probenecid (uricosuric), pegloticase (uricase). Recent: IL-1 blockers (canakinumab) for refractory flares.

4. Benign prostatic hyperplasia

α1-blockers (tamsulosin, silodosin — relax dynamic component), 5α-reductase inhibitors (finasteride, dutasteride — shrink gland), PDE5 inhibitor (tadalafil), combination therapy; anticholinergics for OAB symptoms.

5. Obesity

Orlistat (lipase inhibitor); GLP-1 agonists (liraglutide, semaglutide); tirzepatide (GIP/GLP-1); phentermine-topiramate, naltrexone-bupropion. Recent: semaglutide & tirzepatide give 15–22% weight loss — transformative.

6. Glaucoma

Prostaglandin analogues (latanoprost — first-line, ↑ outflow), β-blockers (timolol), α2-agonists (brimonidine), carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (dorzolamide), pilocarpine; Rho-kinase inhibitor (netarsudil). Acute angle-closure: IV acetazolamide + mannitol + pilocarpine.

7. Bronchial asthma (GINA)

No SABA-only therapy; all use ICS-containing treatment; preferred reliever ICS-formoterol. Stepwise ICS-formoterol (MART) → add LAMA → biologics (omalizumab, mepolizumab, dupilumab, tezepelumab). Acute: O₂ + SABA ± ipratropium + systemic steroids ± IV Mg.

8. Migraine

Acute: NSAIDs, triptans (5-HT1B/1D), gepants (rimegepant, ubrogepant), lasmiditan. Prophylaxis: β-blockers, topiramate, amitriptyline, valproate, anti-CGRP mAbs (erenumab, fremanezumab, galcanezumab). Recent: CGRP-targeted therapy.

9. Acid-peptic disease & H. pylori

PPIs (omeprazole) / H2-blockers; mucosal protectants (sucralfate, misoprostol). H. pylori: triple therapy (PPI + amoxicillin + clarithromycin) or bismuth quadruple. Recent: P-CABs (vonoprazan) — faster, stronger acid suppression.

10. Inflammatory bowel disease (UC & Crohn's)

5-ASA (mesalamine — esp. UC), corticosteroids (induction, incl. budesonide), immunomodulators (azathioprine, methotrexate), biologics (anti-TNF: infliximab, adalimumab; anti-integrin: vedolizumab; anti-IL-12/23: ustekinumab; anti-IL-23: risankizumab), JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib, upadacitinib — UC).

11. Irritable bowel syndrome

Antispasmodics, low-dose TCAs; IBS-C: lubiprostone, linaclotide, prucalopride; IBS-D: loperamide, rifaximin, eluxadoline, alosetron; low-FODMAP diet, probiotics.

12. Pulmonary arterial hypertension

Three pathways: ERAs (bosentan, ambrisentan, macitentan), PDE5 inhibitors/riociguat (NO-cGMP), prostacyclins (epoprostenol, treprostinil, selexipag). Upfront combination standard. Recent: sotatercept (anti-remodelling).

13. Congestive heart failure

Four pillars (HFrEF): ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan) > ACEi/ARB + β-blocker + MRA + SGLT2 inhibitor; plus loop diuretics (symptoms), ivabradine, vericiguat. SGLT2i benefit across EF spectrum (HFpEF too). Start all pillars early.

Cross-cutting recent advances: CGRP drugs (migraine), GLP-1/GIP agonists (obesity/diabetes), SGLT2 inhibitors (HF/CKD), biologics across RA/IBD/asthma, P-CABs (acid-peptic), sotatercept (PAH).

▸★★Active vs passive immunisation — basis, comparison & rationale for clinical use; types of vaccines & passive immunising agents; combined active+passive regimens.

Definition

Active immunisation uses vaccines (antigenic material) to make the recipient mount his/her own antibody and/or cell-mediated response, with immunological memory. Passive immunisation transfers ready-made antibodies (antisera, immune globulins, mAbs) from another person/animal/laboratory — immediate but temporary, with no memory. Active immunity is durable but needs a latent period of 1 to several weeks; passive immunity is instantaneous and is the only modality that is truly curative, while vaccines are essentially prophylactic.

Mechanism

  • Active: antigen processed/presented to B & T cells → clonal expansion, affinity maturation/class-switch (IgM→IgG), and long-lived memory B/T cells; recapitulates natural infection without disease.
  • Passive: preformed antibody directly binds/neutralises free toxin or organism (e.g. antivenom binds venom) — works only on free/circulating antigen, cannot reverse damage already fixed to tissue; protection wanes as transferred IgG is catabolised.
  • Natural counterparts: natural active = recovery from clinical infection; natural passive = transplacental/colostral IgG/IgA transfer.
Diagram (antibody titre vs time): Passive — rapid rise to protective threshold, rapid decline, no memory. Active primary response — latent period (1–many weeks), slow rise. Active booster/secondary response — rapid, high anamnestic rise.

Calcineurin inhibitors — source & molecular detail

Ciclosporin is a cyclic peptide from the fungus Beauveria nivea (formerly Tolypocladium inflatum), binding the cytosolic protein cyclophilin. (Contrast: sirolimus/everolimus also bind FKBP-12 but inhibit mTOR, not calcineurin.) Voclosporin is a newer ciclosporin analogue, FDA-approved 2021 specifically for lupus nephritis. Both ciclosporin and tacrolimus require therapeutic drug monitoring due to narrow therapeutic index and extensive CYP3A4 metabolism (many drug interactions — azoles/macrolides raise levels, rifampin lowers levels).

▸★★Immunosuppressant role of corticosteroids — mechanism, effects on immune cells, clinical uses, HPA-axis suppression & tapering.

Definition

Glucocorticoids (prednisolone, methylprednisolone, dexamethasone) are the oldest, cheapest and most widely used immunosuppressants. They suppress both innate and adaptive immunity, with cell-mediated immunity (CMI) suppressed more than humoral immunity, forming the backbone of induction/maintenance regimens in transplantation and of remission-induction in autoimmune/severe allergic disease. Their immunosuppressive and anti-inflammatory actions are inextricably linked — both reflect inhibition of leukocyte function.

Mechanism of immunosuppression

The lipophilic steroid penetrates the cell, binds the cytosolic glucocorticoid receptor (GR); the activated GR complex translocates to the nucleus (genomic, 30–60 min — delayed but outlasting onset effects).

  • Transactivation: GR dimer binds glucocorticoid response elements (GREs) → induces annexins/lipocortin → inhibits phospholipase A₂ → ↓arachidonic-acid-derived PGs, LTs, PAF.
  • Transrepression: GR antagonises NF-κB and AP-1 (↓histone acetylation, ↓MAPK) → downregulates IL-1, IL-2, IL-3, IL-6, TNF-α, GM-CSF, IFN-γ, plus COX-2 and iNOS.
  • Net immune effects: ↓IL-1 from macrophages and ↓IL-2 → T-cell proliferation not stimulated; ↓NK-cell activity; ↓endothelial ELAM-1/ICAM-1 impairs leukocyte adhesion/migration; impaired macrophage phagocytosis/microbicidal activity; interference with complement.
Diagram (GR genomic action): cortisol → cytosolic GR → nuclear translocation → GRE (transactivation → annexin/lipocortin) and NF-κB/AP-1 antagonism (transrepression → ↓cytokines).

Effects on immune cells/blood

  • Lymphopenia, eosinopenia, monocytopenia, basopenia within 4–6 h, lasting ~24 h — chiefly by redistribution into tissues, not destruction.
  • Potent inducer of lymphocyte apoptosis (T>B cells) — basis of lympholytic use in ALL/lymphomas.
  • Conversely, neutrophilia (↑marrow release, ↓margination/adherence).
  • CMI (delayed hypersensitivity, graft rejection) suppressed more than antibody production, which is relatively spared at therapeutic doses.

Adverse effects of chronic use

Cushingoid habitus, fragile skin/striae, easy bruising, hirsutism, hyperglycaemia/diabetes, proximal myopathy, osteoporosis with vertebral fractures, avascular necrosis of femoral head, posterior subcapsular cataract, glaucoma, growth retardation in children, peptic ulceration, psychiatric disturbance, delayed wound healing, and increased infection susceptibility (latent TB flare, opportunistic Candida — microbicidal capacity impaired and inflammation masked).

HPA-axis suppression & tapering

  • Dose- and duration-dependent HPA suppression → adrenocortical atrophy.
  • Abrupt withdrawal after >2–3 weeks → withdrawal syndrome (malaise, fever, anorexia, postural hypotension, electrolyte imbalance, myalgia, disease relapse) and, under stress, acute adrenal insufficiency/cardiovascular collapse.
  • Taper slowly — hypothalamus/pituitary need weeks–months to recover; exogenous ACTH does not hasten recovery (pituitary recovers before adrenals do).
  • HPA-sparing measures: shorter-acting steroid at lowest dose/shortest duration, single morning dose, alternate-day therapy (less immunosuppression too); long-acting dexamethasone unsuitable for alternate-day use.
  • Stress cover: during infection/trauma/surgery, do not stop the steroid — increase dose/give IV hydrocortisone.

Contraindications (relative)

Peptic ulcer, diabetes, hypertension, viral/fungal infections, TB, osteoporosis, herpes simplex keratitis, psychosis, epilepsy, CHF, renal failure.

Recent advances: Steroid-sparing strategies dominate modern practice — glucocorticoids increasingly used only for induction, with calcineurin inhibitors, mycophenolate and biologics maintaining remission to limit cumulative toxicity. Selective GR agonists/modulators (SEGRMs/SEGRAs), which favour transrepression over transactivation, are under investigation to dissociate anti-inflammatory benefit from metabolic adverse effects.

▸★★Therapies for chronic graft-versus-host disease (cGVHD) — pathophysiology, first-line & steroid-refractory salvage therapy.

Definition

Chronic GVHD is the major determinant of late, non-relapse morbidity/mortality after allogeneic haematopoietic cell transplantation (allo-HCT), occurring in ~30–70% of recipients. It is an alloimmune/autoimmune-like syndrome where donor T cells (with dysregulated B-cell help) react against host antigens, producing chronic inflammation and fibrosis of multiple organs; it classically presents beyond day +100 and resembles a multisystem autoimmune disorder. Therapy aims to control disease activity, preserve organ function and the graft-versus-tumour effect, and minimise corticosteroid exposure.

Pathophysiology (basis for therapy)

Three-phase model: (1) tissue injury/inflammation (conditioning toxicity, infection, prior acute GVHD, sun exposure) → (2) chronic inflammation with dysregulated B- and T-cell immunity and loss of tolerance → (3) tissue repair with fibrosis. Each phase is a drug target. Target organs: skin (sclerosis), mouth (lichenoid), eyes (sicca), liver, GI tract/oesophagus, genitalia, joints/fascia, lung (bronchiolitis obliterans). NIH severity score (mild/moderate/severe) guides whether topical or systemic therapy starts.

Diagram: Phase 1 tissue injury → Phase 2 B/T-cell dysregulation (targeted by ibrutinib–BTK, ruxolitinib–JAK1/2) → Phase 3 fibrosis (targeted by belumosudil–ROCK2, axatilimab–CSF1R).

First-line therapy

  • Mild, skin/mucosa-limited: topical therapy only — topical corticosteroids, topical calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus), steroid mouth rinses, artificial tears/ciclosporin eye drops.
  • Moderate–severe: systemic prednisone 0.5–1 mg/kg/day ± calcineurin inhibitor (ciclosporin/tacrolimus) or sirolimus (steroid-sparing, not clearly more effective). Adding MMF to first-line prednisone shows no added benefit. Taper after 1–2 weeks if stable; ~⅓ respond to initial steroids; many need multiple lines over years.

Steroid-refractory cGVHD — definition

Progression on prednisone 1 mg/kg/day ×2 weeks, stable disease on ≥0.5 mg/kg/day ×4–8 weeks, or inability to taper below 0.5 mg/kg/day due to flares.

▸★★Define null & alternate hypothesis; significance in clinical trials; Type I & Type II errors (examples); note on p-value.

Hypotheses

  • Null hypothesis (H₀): no difference/effect between groups (e.g. "drug A = placebo"). The default assumed true until disproven.
  • Alternate hypothesis (H₁/Hₐ): there is a true difference/effect (e.g. "drug A ≠ placebo"). Accepted if H₀ is rejected.

Significance in clinical trials

Statistical testing aims to reject or fail to reject H₀ using sample data; trials are designed to detect a clinically meaningful effect with adequate power, controlling error rates.

Type I and Type II errors

H₀ trueH₀ false
Reject H₀Type I error (α) — false positiveCorrect (power)
Fail to reject H₀CorrectType II error (β) — false negative
  • Type I (α): concluding a drug works when it doesn't (e.g. approving an ineffective drug). Conventionally α = 0.05.
  • Type II (β): missing a real effect (e.g. rejecting an effective drug). Power = 1 − β (usually ≥80%).

p-value

Probability of obtaining the observed (or more extreme) result if H₀ were true. p < 0.05 → statistically significant → reject H₀. It is not the probability that H₀ is true or the effect size; interpret alongside confidence intervals & clinical relevance.

▸★★Fixed-dose combinations, drug antagonism, and the rationale behind combination drug therapy in hypertension & other diseases.

Fixed-dose combinations

See Section 6 — two+ drugs in fixed ratio in one form; ↑ compliance, synergy, ↓ resistance, but inflexible dosing; irrational FDCs are a regulatory concern.

Drug antagonism (types)

TypeMechanism / example
Pharmacological (receptor)Competitive (atropine vs ACh), non-competitive
Physiological/functionalOpposite effects via different receptors (adrenaline vs histamine)
ChemicalDirect chemical inactivation (protamine + heparin; chelators + metals)
PharmacokineticOne drug alters ADME of another (↓ absorption, enzyme induction)

Rationale for combination therapy

  • Synergy/additive effect with lower individual doses → fewer dose-related ADRs.
  • Target multiple mechanisms of a multifactorial disease; prevent resistance; treat comorbidities.

In hypertension

  • Preferred: ACEi/ARB + CCB, ACEi/ARB + thiazide, CCB + thiazide; avoid ACEi + ARB together.
  • Single-pill combinations improve adherence; start two drugs if BP ≥20/10 mmHg above target; add spironolactone for resistant HTN.

In other diseases

TB (HRZE), HIV (ART), malaria (ACT), H. pylori (PPI + 2 antibiotics), diabetes (metformin + SGLT2i/DPP-4i), cancer (combination chemotherapy), Parkinson's (levodopa + carbidopa).

▸🩺 Case48 hours after starting chemotherapy for high-grade lymphoma, a patient has high potassium, high phosphate, high uric acid and acute kidney injury. Diagnosis and prevention?
Clue: Massive cell lysis.

Tumour lysis syndrome. Prevent/treat with aggressive IV hydration, allopurinol (prophylaxis) or rasburicase (high-risk/established hyperuricaemia), and electrolyte management; dialysis if severe.

▸🩺 CaseA breast-cancer patient on doxorubicin develops a falling ejection fraction. Mechanism and protection?
Clue: Cumulative dose, free radicals.

Anthracycline cardiotoxicity — dose-dependent, free-radical/iron-mediated. Limit cumulative dose, monitor LVEF; dexrazoxane (iron chelator) is cardioprotective; liposomal doxorubicin lowers risk.

▸🩺 CaseA patient on high-dose cyclophosphamide develops haematuria. Cause and prevention?
Clue: The acrolein metabolite.

Haemorrhagic cystitis from the metabolite acrolein. Prevent with mesna (binds acrolein) plus vigorous hydration.

▸🩺 CaseAfter high-dose methotrexate, what is given to protect normal cells, and what must be avoided?
Clue: Folate rescue.

Leucovorin (folinic acid) rescue bypasses the DHFR blockade in normal cells; ensure urine alkalinisation and hydration (methotrexate is nephrotoxic if it precipitates). Avoid NSAIDs/aspirin and probenecid (they reduce methotrexate excretion → toxicity).

▸🩺 CaseA patient on cisplatin develops rising creatinine and hearing loss. Mechanism and prevention?
Clue: Tubular toxicity.

Cisplatin causes dose-limiting nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity. Prevent with vigorous hydration and forced diuresis (mannitol), amifostine; monitor renal function and electrolytes (magnesium wasting); consider carboplatin.

▸🩺 CaseA patient on chemotherapy presents with fever and a neutrophil count of 0.4. Immediate action?
Clue: An oncological emergency.

Febrile neutropenia is an emergency. Take cultures, then give empirical broad-spectrum antibiotics (an anti-pseudomonal beta-lactam, e.g. piperacillin-tazobactam) within an hour; G-CSF support; neutropenic precautions.

▸🩺 CaseA melanoma patient on pembrolizumab develops severe diarrhoea and abdominal pain. Cause and treatment?
Clue: Immune-related adverse event.

Immune-related colitis from checkpoint inhibition. Treat with corticosteroids (add infliximab if refractory) and withhold the checkpoint inhibitor.

▸🩺 CaseA woman has HER2-positive breast cancer. Which targeted drug, what monitoring, and how is the endocrine choice decided?
Clue: HER2 antibody plus cardiac monitoring.

Trastuzumab (anti-HER2 monoclonal antibody) — monitor LVEF (cardiotoxicity, especially with anthracyclines). For hormone-receptor-positive disease: tamoxifen (premenopausal) versus an aromatase inhibitor (postmenopausal); add a CDK4/6 inhibitor in advanced HR-positive/HER2-negative disease.
