Potassium-sparing diuretics — basis & rationale

  • Aldosterone antagonists (spironolactone, eplerenone): block mineralocorticoid receptor in collecting duct → ↓ Na⁺ reabsorption, K⁺ retention.
  • ENaC blockers (amiloride, triamterene): directly block epithelial Na⁺ channel.
  • Rationale: weak diuretics alone, but conserve K⁺ → used with K⁺-wasting loop/thiazide to offset hypokalaemia; spironolactone gives mortality benefit in HF (RALES) & treats resistant HTN, primary hyperaldosteronism, ascites.

Indications

Heart failure, ascites/cirrhosis, hyperaldosteronism (Conn's), resistant hypertension, hirsutism/PCOS (spironolactone anti-androgen), Liddle's syndrome (amiloride).

Adverse effects

  • Hyperkalaemia (key danger — esp. with ACEi/ARB/renal failure), metabolic acidosis.
  • Spironolactone: gynaecomastia, menstrual irregularity (eplerenone more selective → fewer).

Diuretic resistance

Failure to achieve adequate diuresis despite full dose. Causes: poor adherence/excess Na intake, ↓ absorption (gut oedema), ↓ renal perfusion, nephron remodelling/distal hypertrophy, NSAIDs, hypoalbuminaemia, CKD. Management: IV/continuous infusion, ↑ dose, sequential nephron blockade (add thiazide/metolazone to loop), treat cause, restrict salt.

Non-diuretic uses

Acetazolamide — glaucoma, altitude sickness, epilepsy, alkalinise urine; mannitol — ↑ICP/cerebral oedema, acute glaucoma; thiazides — nephrogenic diabetes insipidus, idiopathic hypercalciuria/renal stones; loop — hypercalcaemia, hyperkalaemia.

▸★★Physiology of urine formation & drugs affecting it; inhibitors of renal epithelial Na⁺ channels & high-ceiling (loop) diuretics with clinical applications.

Physiology of urine formation

  1. Glomerular filtration (~180 L/day) of plasma.
  2. Tubular reabsorption: PCT (~65% Na, via Na/H, glucose, CA), thin/thick loop of Henle (NKCC2, countercurrent multiplier → medullary hypertonicity), DCT (NCC), collecting duct (ENaC, aquaporins under ADH, aldosterone).
  3. Tubular secretion (K⁺, H⁺, organic acids/bases).

Diuretics act at each segment (see Q1 table).

Renal epithelial Na⁺ channel (ENaC) inhibitors

  • Amiloride, triamterene — block luminal ENaC in late DCT/collecting duct → ↓ Na⁺ reabsorption & ↓ K⁺ secretion (K⁺-sparing).
  • Weak diuretics; uses: with thiazide/loop to prevent hypokalaemia, Liddle's syndrome, lithium-induced nephrogenic DI (amiloride).

High-ceiling (loop) diuretics

  • Mechanism: inhibit NKCC2 in thick ascending limb → most efficacious (up to 20–25% of filtered Na); abolish medullary gradient; ↑ Ca²⁺ & Mg²⁺ excretion; venodilation (acute pulmonary oedema).
  • Clinical use: acute pulmonary oedema/HF, oedema (renal/hepatic), hypertension with renal failure, hypercalcaemia, hyperkalaemia, forced diuresis.
  • ADR: hypokalaemia, hypocalcaemia, hypomagnesaemia, metabolic alkalosis, ototoxicity (esp. with aminoglycosides), hyperuricaemia, dehydration.

Section 2 · RAAS Modifiers, Antihypertensives & Heart Failure

▸★★★Drugs modulating RAAS — basis, MOA, uses, ADRs, rationale of ACEi+ARB / ARNI in HF & HTN; note on direct renin inhibitors & triphasic response to angiotensin-II.

The RAAS cascade & drug targets

Cascade: Angiotensinogen →(renin)→ Ang I →(ACE)→ Ang II → AT1 receptor (vasoconstriction, aldosterone, Na/water retention, remodelling) + stimulates aldosterone. Drug targets: renin (aliskiren), ACE (ACEi), AT1 (ARB), aldosterone (MRA), neprilysin (sacubitril ↑ natriuretic peptides).

ACE inhibitorsEnalapril, ramipril, lisinopril, perindopril↓ Ang II, ↑ bradykinin
ARBsLosartan, telmisartan, valsartan, olmesartanBlock AT1 receptor
Direct renin inhibitorAliskirenInhibit renin (first step)
MRASpironolactone, eplerenoneBlock aldosterone
ARNISacubitril/valsartanNeprilysin inhibition + ARB

Uses

Hypertension, heart failure (HFrEF), post-MI, diabetic nephropathy/proteinuric CKD, LV dysfunction.

Adverse effects

  • ACEi: dry cough & angioedema (bradykinin), hyperkalaemia, ↑ creatinine, first-dose hypotension, teratogenic (avoid in pregnancy). ARBs: similar but no cough.

Rationale: ACEi + ARB and ARNI

  • ACEi + ARB combination: dual RAAS blockade — generally avoided (ONTARGET) due to ↑ hyperkalaemia, hypotension & renal dysfunction without survival benefit.
  • ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan): neprilysin inhibition ↑ natriuretic peptides (vasodilation, natriuresis, anti-remodelling) + AT1 blockade → superior to enalapril in HFrEF (PARADIGM-HF). Do not combine with ACEi (angioedema; 36-h washout).

Direct renin inhibitor

Aliskiren blocks RAAS at the rate-limiting step; modest antihypertensive; limited use; not combined with ACEi/ARB in diabetics (ALTITUDE — harm).

Triphasic response to angiotensin-II

IV Ang II classically gives: (1) rapid pressor rise (direct vasoconstriction via AT1); (2) a brief secondary rise/plateau from sympathetic facilitation & catecholamine release; (3) a delayed sustained pressor via aldosterone-mediated Na/water retention. (Demonstrates Ang II's vascular, neural & renal actions.)

Relieve symptoms, slow remodelling, reduce hospitalisation & mortality.

β-blockers in CHF — pharmacology in depth

  • Rationale: chronic sympathetic activation in HF is initially compensatory but ultimately maladaptive — ↑ HR/contractility raises myocardial O₂ demand, promotes arrhythmia, down-regulates β1-receptors, and drives fibrosis/remodelling via catecholamine & renin-angiotensin cross-talk. β-blockade interrupts this vicious cycle.
  • Only 3 agents proven to reduce mortality in HFrEF — carvedilol (non-selective β1/β2 + α1-blocker; additional antioxidant/vasodilator effect — COPERNICUS), bisoprolol (β1-selective — CIBIS-II), metoprolol succinate ER (β1-selective — MERIT-HF). Short-acting metoprolol tartrate is NOT validated for HF.
  • Titration protocol: start at a very low dose only once the patient is euvolaemic/compensated; double the dose every 2 weeks as tolerated, to the maximum-tolerated or target dose; do not initiate during acute decompensation.
  • Contraindications/cautions: decompensated/wet HF, symptomatic bradycardia, 2nd/3rd-degree heart block without pacemaker, severe reactive airway disease (relative — cardioselective agents preferred).
  • Drug interactions: avoid combination with non-dihydropyridine CCBs (verapamil/diltiazem) — additive negative inotropy/bradycardia; caution with digoxin (additive AV-nodal block); NSAIDs blunt benefit (Na/water retention).

Other agents

  • Diuretics (loop) — symptomatic congestion relief; no proven mortality benefit, used for euvolaemia.
  • Ivabradine — If-channel blocker, if HR >70 in sinus rhythm despite max β-blocker (SHIFT).
  • Vericiguat — soluble guanylate-cyclase stimulator, ↑ cGMP-mediated vasodilation/anti-fibrotic effect; reduced the composite of CV death/HF hospitalisation in worsening HFrEF (VICTORIA).
  • Digoxin — symptom/rate control. Hydralazine + ISDN — esp. African-Americans or ACEi/ARNI-intolerant (V-HeFT).
  • Omecamtiv mecarbil — cardiac myosin activator, ↑ systolic ejection time without ↑ O₂ demand (investigational/modest benefit in GALACTIC-HF).

HFpEF

Long a therapeutic blind spot — neither ACEi/ARB nor β-blockers consistently improve outcomes. SGLT2 inhibitors are now the only drug class with proven mortality/hospitalisation benefit across the full EF spectrum (EMPEROR-Preserved — empagliflozin; DELIVER — dapagliflozin), making them first-line GDMT in HFpEF as well as HFrEF. Otherwise: manage comorbidities (HTN, AF, obesity), diuretics for congestion, MRA may have modest benefit.

Recent advances & guidelines (ESC 2021/2023, ACC/AHA 2022): start all four GDMT pillars early & in parallel (not sequentially titrated one at a time) — "four pillars together" approach shortens time to benefit; SGLT2 inhibitors now indicated across the entire EF spectrum (HFrEF, HFmrEF, HFpEF) regardless of diabetes status (EMPEROR-Preserved, DELIVER); finerenone (non-steroidal MRA) for HF with CKD/diabetes; vericiguat added for worsening HFrEF; omecamtiv mecarbil and other myotropes remain investigational.
▸★★Classify antihypertensives; MOA, uses & ADRs of ACEi & ARBs; combination therapy, resistant HTN, hypertensive emergencies & HTN in renal failure.

Classification of antihypertensives

  • Diuretics: thiazides, loop, K⁺-sparing.
  • RAAS blockers: ACEi, ARB, renin inhibitor, ARNI.
  • Calcium channel blockers: amlodipine (DHP), verapamil/diltiazem (non-DHP).
  • Sympatholytics: β-blockers, α1-blockers, central α2-agonists (clonidine, methyldopa).
  • Vasodilators: hydralazine, minoxidil, nitroprusside.

ACEi & ARBs (MOA/uses/ADRs)

See Section 2 Q1. Key: ACEi cause cough/angioedema; both contraindicated in pregnancy & bilateral renal artery stenosis; renoprotective in proteinuric CKD/diabetes.

Combination therapy principles

  • Preferred: ACEi/ARB + CCB, ACEi/ARB + thiazide, CCB + thiazide.
  • Avoid ACEi + ARB together; single-pill combinations improve adherence; start 2 drugs if BP ≥20/10 above target.

Resistant hypertension

BP uncontrolled on ≥3 drugs (incl. a diuretic) at optimal doses. Confirm adherence, exclude white-coat & secondary causes; add spironolactone (4th-line, PATHWAY-2); consider device therapy (renal denervation).

Hypertensive emergency

Severe BP + acute target-organ damage → IV agents, controlled lowering (~25% in first hour): labetalol, nicardipine, nitroprusside, GTN, esmolol, clevidipine; fenoldopam (renal-sparing).

HTN in renal failure

ACEi/ARB renoprotective (monitor K⁺/creatinine; loop diuretic preferred over thiazide at low GFR); CCBs effective & safe; salt restriction; often need multiple drugs.

Stepped-care & single-pill combination algorithm

Start 2-drug single-pill combination if BP ≥20/10 mmHg above target (ACEi/ARB + CCB, or ACEi/ARB + thiazide); add a third agent (CCB + ACEi/ARB + thiazide) if uncontrolled; resistant cases add spironolactone as 4th-line. Single-pill combinations improve adherence and are now preferred over monotherapy up-titration as first step.

Resistant hypertension

BP uncontrolled on ≥3 optimally-dosed drugs (including a diuretic), or controlled only on ≥4 drugs. Workup: confirm adherence, exclude white-coat effect (ABPM) & secondary causes (renal artery stenosis, primary aldosteronism, OSA, renal disease); add spironolactone (PATHWAY-2); consider device therapy (catheter-based renal denervation).

Present status & recent advances

  • Earlier & lower BP targets (ACC/AHA <130/80; SPRINT trial — intensive control ↓ CV events).
  • Single-pill combinations first-line; emphasis on out-of-office (home/ABPM) monitoring over clinic BP alone.
  • Renal denervation (catheter-based) — device therapy for resistant HTN.
  • Aprocitentan — dual endothelin (ETA/ETB) receptor antagonist; FDA-approved 2024 (Tryvio) for resistant hypertension as add-on therapy [PMID 36356632].
  • Baxdrostat — selective aldosterone synthase inhibitor, lowers aldosterone without affecting cortisol; reduces BP in resistant/uncontrolled HTN in trials [PMID 36342143, 37548462].
  • Zilebesiran — siRNA against hepatic angiotensinogen synthesis; subcutaneous, sustained BP lowering with twice-yearly (or less frequent) dosing in trials [PMID 39494631].
  • Finerenone (non-steroidal MRA) and sacubitril-based combinations extending into hypertension management beyond HF.

▸★★RAAS drugs (ACEi/ARB) and their use in COVID-19 patients — the controversy & its resolution.

Background

ACE2 — the chief counter-regulatory enzyme of the RAAS — is also the cellular receptor for SARS-CoV-2. This raised an early (2020) but ultimately refuted concern that RAAS inhibitors might worsen COVID-19 by upregulating viral receptors.

RAAS — two arms relevant to COVID

Classical (injurious) arm: ACE → Ang II → AT1 receptor = vasoconstriction, Na⁺ retention, inflammation, fibrosis, ROS, alveolar/endothelial injury.
Counter-regulatory (protective) arm: ACE2 → Ang-(1–7) → Mas receptor = vasodilatation, anti-inflammatory, anti-fibrotic, anti-proliferative. ACE2 degrades Ang II to Ang-(1–7), limiting AT1 signalling; it is NOT inhibited by ACEi and has no effect on bradykinin.
Viral entry: SARS-CoV-2 spike protein binds ACE2 on respiratory epithelium/endothelium; TMPRSS2 primes the spike, enabling fusion and endocytosis of the virion-ACE2 complex → ACE2 downregulation → unopposed Ang II/AT1 → apoptosis, IL-6 release, "thromboinflammation," ARDS.

The ACEi/ARB controversy

  • "Harm" hypothesis: RAAS inhibitors upregulate ACE2 expression → more viral receptors → greater susceptibility/severity → prompted calls to stop these drugs early in 2020.
  • "Benefit" hypothesis: by reducing Ang II/AT1 signalling and preserving the ACE2–Ang-(1–7)–Mas axis, RAAS inhibitors could be protective against COVID lung injury.
  • Resolution: analyses of ACE2 expression in animals/humans on RAAS inhibitors did not validate the harm hypothesis; epidemiological studies showed no effect or benefit. Major societies (ACC/AHA/HFSA, ESC, ESH) advised continuing ACEi/ARB unless otherwise contraindicated.

Key evidence

StudyDesignKey finding
BRACE CORONARCT, hospitalised COVID-19 on ACEi/ARB; continue vs suspend ×30 daysNo difference in days alive & out of hospital → routine discontinuation not justified
BRACE CORONA — severity sub-analysis659 patients, mild vs moderate diseaseIn moderate disease, continuation gave more days alive/out of hospital → continue
Meta-analyses (Baral 2020; Zhang 2022)~28,872 and pooled propensity-matched studiesRAAS inhibitors not associated with severe illness/mortality; signal of benefit in hypertensives

Practical recommendations

  • Continue ACEi/ARB in COVID-19 per standard indications — do not stop for COVID alone.
  • Stop only for the usual reasons: hypotension, hyperkalaemia, AKI.
  • ACEi/ARB/ARNI/aliskiren remain contraindicated in pregnancy regardless of COVID status.
  • Recombinant/soluble human ACE2 (decoy receptor, degrades excess Ang II) and Ang-(1–7)/Mas agonists remain investigational, not standard therapy.
Recent advances: BRACE CORONA confirmed no benefit from suspending ACEi/ARB in hospitalised COVID-19, with its severity sub-analysis favouring continuation in moderate disease; meta-analyses (RCT + propensity-matched) found no link between RAAS inhibitor use and severe illness/mortality, supporting guideline advice to continue. Bottom line (2024–25): RAAS inhibition is continued in COVID-19 per standard indications; recombinant soluble ACE2/Ang-(1–7) axis modulation remain investigational.

Section 3 · Antianginal Drugs & Ischemic Heart Disease

▸★★★Classify antianginals; pathophysiology & pharmacotherapy of stable & variant angina; MOA/uses/limitations of nitrates, β-blockers, CCBs & newer agents (metabolic modulators, K⁺-channel openers); recent advances.

Pathophysiology

Angina = myocardial O₂ demand > supply. Stable (exertional): fixed atheromatous stenosis. Variant (Prinzmetal): coronary vasospasm. Treatment aims to ↓ demand and/or ↑ supply.

Classification & mechanisms

ClassDrugsMechanism
Organic nitratesGTN, isosorbide di/mononitrate↑NO→↑cGMP→venodilation→↓preload→↓O₂ demand; coronary dilation
β-blockersMetoprolol, atenolol, bisoprolol↓HR, contractility → ↓demand (first-line stable angina; NOT in variant)

Therapy by type

  • Stable angina: acute relief — sublingual GTN; prophylaxis — β-blocker and/or CCB ± long-acting nitrate; add ranolazine/ivabradine/nicorandil if needed; plus aspirin + statin + risk-factor control.
  • Variant angina: CCBs + nitrates (avoid non-selective β-blockers — may worsen spasm).

Limitations

Nitrates — tolerance (need nitrate-free interval), headache, hypotension, reflex tachycardia. β-blockers — bradycardia, bronchospasm, fatigue. CCBs — oedema, constipation (verapamil), reflex tachycardia (DHP).

Recent: Ranolazine & trimetazidine for refractory angina without haemodynamic effects; combination metabolic + haemodynamic therapy; aggressive LDL lowering (PCSK9i).
▸★Treatment of unstable angina (acute coronary syndrome, NSTE-ACS).

Definition

Unstable angina (UA) is a non-ST-elevation acute coronary syndrome (NSTE-ACS), grouped with NSTEMI, caused by disruption of an atherosclerotic plaque with platelet-rich thrombus and partial coronary occlusion. UA is distinguished from NSTEMI by the absence of a troponin rise. Therapy aims to relieve ischaemia and prevent progression to MI.

Immediate measures — "MONA(C)"

Morphine 1–5 mg IV for refractory pain (class IIb; may delay P2Y12 absorption); Oxygen only if SpO₂ <90%; Nitroglycerin SL 0.3–0.4 mg q5min ×3 then IV for ongoing angina/HTN/HF; Aspirin; Clopidogrel/P2Y12 inhibitor. Plus bed rest, continuous ECG monitoring, and risk stratification (TIMI/GRACE/HEART score).

Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT)

  • Aspirin — loading 162–325 mg (non-enteric, chewed), then 81 mg/day indefinitely; irreversible COX-1 inhibition → ↓TXA₂.
  • P2Y12 inhibitor added to aspirin: clopidogrel (600 mg LD/75 mg OD), ticagrelor (180 mg/90 mg BD, reversible), prasugrel (60 mg/10 mg OD). Guidelines favour ticagrelor/prasugrel; clopidogrel as alternative.
  • GP IIb/IIIa inhibitors (eptifibatide, tirofiban, abciximab) — reserved for high-risk/bailout during PCI.

Anticoagulation (added to antiplatelet therapy)

UFH, LMWH (enoxaparin), fondaparinux 2.5 mg SC OD (factor Xa inhibitor — least bleeding), or bivalirudin during hospitalisation.

Anti-ischaemic & secondary prevention

  • β-blockers (oral metoprolol/carvedilol within 24 h) — mainstay; ↓HR, contractility, O₂ demand. Avoid in HF/shock, bradycardia, high-grade AV block, asthma.
  • Nitrates — symptom relief; avoid if SBP <90 mmHg or recent PDE5-inhibitor use.
  • CCBs (diltiazem/verapamil) if β-blocker contraindicated or coronary vasospasm; avoid short-acting nifedipine (reflex sympathetic activation).
  • High-intensity statin; ACE inhibitor if HFrEF/HTN/DM.

Reperfusion strategy

Early invasive strategy (angiography ± PCI/CABG within ~24 h) for intermediate-to-high-risk patients (↑troponin, ST changes, refractory angina). Ischaemia-guided conservative strategy for low-risk patients.

High-yield exam pointers

  • UA = NSTE-ACS with no troponin elevation (distinguishes from NSTEMI); partial occlusion → no immediate fibrinolysis.
  • Fibrinolytics are NOT used in UA/NSTE-ACS (no proven benefit, only for STEMI).
  • DAPT (aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor) is the cornerstone; ticagrelor/prasugrel preferred over clopidogrel where available.
  • Fondaparinux offers the best efficacy-safety balance (least major bleeding) among the anticoagulant options.
▸★★Experimental & clinical methods for evaluation of antianginal drugs; cytoprotective (metabolic) agents in angina.

Experimental methods

  • Coronary artery ligation (occlusion/reperfusion) in dogs/rats → infarct-size estimation.
  • Isolated heart (Langendorff) — coronary flow & contractility.
  • Isolated coronary artery strips for vasodilation; ischaemia-reperfusion models; pacing-induced ischaemia.

Clinical methods

  • Exercise/treadmill ECG stress test — time to ST-depression/angina, exercise duration.
  • Ambulatory ECG (Holter) for ischaemic episodes; frequency of anginal attacks & GTN consumption.
  • Stress echo, myocardial perfusion imaging; RCTs with endpoints (MI, mortality).

Cytoprotective / metabolic agents

Improve myocardial energy efficiency without altering haemodynamics: trimetazidine (inhibits fatty-acid β-oxidation → shifts to glucose oxidation), ranolazine (late Na⁺ current inhibition → ↓ Ca²⁺ overload), perhexiline. Useful as add-on in refractory/microvascular angina.

Section 4 · Anticoagulants, Antiplatelets & Fibrinolytics

▸★★★Pharmacology of drugs maintaining blood fluidity. Classify anticoagulants, antiplatelets & fibrinolytics; MOA/uses/ADRs/status of heparin, LMWH, DTIs, factor Xa inhibitors & NOACs; note on reversal agents & recent advances.

Classification

Heparin (UFH)

MOA: activates antithrombin III → inactivates thrombin (IIa) & Xa. Parenteral, immediate, monitored by aPTT. ADR: bleeding, HIT (heparin-induced thrombocytopenia), osteoporosis. Reversal: protamine sulfate.

LMWH (enoxaparin, dalteparin)

Mainly anti-Xa; SC, predictable kinetics, no routine monitoring (anti-Xa if needed), less HIT. Partly reversed by protamine. Fondaparinux = pure anti-Xa pentasaccharide.

Direct thrombin inhibitors

Dabigatran (oral), bivalirudin/argatroban (IV) — directly inhibit IIa; used in AF, VTE, PCI, HIT. Reversal of dabigatran: idarucizumab.

Factor Xa inhibitors / NOACs (DOACs)

Rivaroxaban, apixaban, edoxaban — oral, fixed dose, no routine monitoring, fewer interactions/bleeds than warfarin. Uses: AF stroke prevention, VTE treatment/prophylaxis. Reversal: andexanet alfa (or PCC).

▸★Fibrinolytic (thrombolytic) agents — classification, mechanism, uses & ADRs.

Definition

Fibrinolytics lyse an already-formed thrombus and recanalize an occluded vessel (chiefly coronary) by activating the body's natural fibrinolytic system — therapeutic, not prophylactic. They convert inactive plasminogen to active plasmin, which digests fibrin, unlike anticoagulants/antiplatelets that only prevent thrombus growth.

Plasminogen-plasmin system: Plasminogen → (t-PA / streptokinase / urokinase) → Plasmin → digests fibrin → fibrin degradation products. Circulating plasmin is neutralised by α2-antiplasmin (fibrin-bound plasmin is protected — shared fibrin/antiplasmin binding site); t-PA itself is inactivated by PAI-1/PAI-2.

Classification by generation

Individual agents — key features

  • Streptokinase: not an enzyme itself — forms an activator complex with plasminogen. Non-fibrin-specific → depletes circulating fibrinogen (lytic state). Antigenic (anaphylaxis 1–2%), needs a loading dose, cannot be re-used. Largely superseded.
  • Urokinase: human enzyme, directly converts plasminogen → plasmin; non-antigenic; largely out of use.
  • Alteplase (rt-PA): selectively activates fibrin-bound plasminogen; t½ 4–8 min → infusion ± heparin.
  • Reteplase: longer t½ (13–16 min) but less fibrin-selective → double bolus 10 U, 30 min apart.
  • Tenecteplase: highest fibrin selectivity, PAI-1 resistant; only agent given as a single IV bolus (0.5 mg/kg over ~10 s) — enables pre-hospital lysis; ASSENT-2 showed efficacy ≥ alteplase with less non-cerebral bleeding.

Clinical uses & time window

  • Acute STEMI — chief indication, alternative to primary PCI when unavailable/delayed; "golden hour," little benefit beyond ~6 h; not used in unstable angina/low-risk NSTEMI.
  • Massive/haemodynamically unstable PE.
  • Acute ischaemic stroke — IV alteplase only within 3–4.5 h after excluding intracranial haemorrhage.
  • DVT (extensive/proximal) and peripheral arterial occlusion (intra-arterial, within 72 h) — no role in chronic PVD.

Adverse effects & contraindications

Haemorrhage (including ICH) is the major toxicity; streptokinase additionally causes allergy/anaphylaxis, fever, hypotension. Antidote: tranexamic acid/EACA. Contraindicated in prior ICH, stroke/head injury within 3 months, active bleeding, recent major surgery/trauma, uncontrolled HTN, pregnancy.

Recent advances: primary PCI is preferred for STEMI where promptly available; fibrinolysis remains vital where PCI is unavailable, with rescue PCI if lysis fails ("pharmaco-invasive" strategy). Tenecteplase is increasingly the agent of choice (single weight-based bolus) and is being adopted for acute ischaemic stroke in many centres; streptokinase/urokinase are now rarely used, displaced by recombinant fibrin-specific agents.
▸★Classify antiplatelet drugs; mechanism, uses & ADRs.

Definition

Antiplatelet drugs inhibit platelet activation, adhesion or aggregation, blunting formation of the arterial platelet ("white") thrombus — more useful in arterial thrombosis (anticoagulants suit venous thrombosis). Acting at discrete sites allows additive/synergistic combinations.

▸★Aspirin resistance — definitions, mechanisms, detection & management.

Definition

Aspirin acts by irreversible acetylation of platelet COX-1, abolishing TXA₂ synthesis for the platelet's lifespan (~7–10 days). Aspirin resistance is the failure of aspirin to produce its expected antiplatelet effect, used in two distinct senses: clinical resistance (a recurrent atherothrombotic event despite regular aspirin — better termed "treatment failure") and laboratory/biochemical resistance (inadequate suppression of TXA₂-dependent platelet function on testing). Reported incidence varies widely (5–75%), reflecting differing definitions/assays.

Mechanisms / causes

  • Light-transmission aggregometry (arachidonic-acid induced) — reference method.
  • Point-of-care: VerifyNow Aspirin (Aspirin Reaction Units), PFA-100 closure time.

Clinical relevance & management

Elevated urinary 11-dehydro-TXB2 correlates with higher risk of MI, stroke and CV death. Routine platelet-function testing is NOT recommended — tailoring therapy to test results has shown no proven outcome benefit; assays are useful mainly to check compliance. Stepwise management: confirm adherence → switch enteric-coated to plain/soluble aspirin → time-separate ibuprofen (give aspirin first) → twice-daily aspirin in high-turnover states (diabetes) → for true treatment failure, add/switch to a P2Y12 inhibitor (clopidogrel/ticagrelor).

True resistance vs pseudoresistance: Non-compliance → Enteric-coat malabsorption → Ibuprofen interaction → Platelet turnover → COX-1 polymorphism → COX-2/isoprostane TXA₂ regeneration — most cases fall on the pseudoresistance end of this spectrum.
Recent advances: a 400-volunteer crossover study found apparent resistance reached up to 49% with a single dose of enteric-coated aspirin but 0% with immediate-release aspirin, and no case of true pharmacological resistance was found — confirming pseudoresistance (delayed/variable absorption) as the dominant phenomenon. Contemporary reviews attribute most post-stroke antiplatelet "treatment failure" to poor adherence and genetic polymorphisms, supporting adherence checks over blanket dose-escalation. ACC/AHA and ESC guidelines continue to advise against routine platelet-function/aspirin-resistance testing in stable patients.

▸★Corticosteroids in COVID-19 management — rationale, evidence & dosing.

Rationale

Severe COVID-19 is driven not by viral cytopathic effect alone but by a dysregulated host hyperinflammatory response ("cytokine storm") causing diffuse alveolar damage and ARDS. Systemic glucocorticoids (dexamethasone) are the cornerstone immunomodulatory therapy that improves survival in hospitalised, oxygen-requiring patients — they are anti-inflammatory, not antiviral, so benefit is confined to the inflammatory phase.

Mechanism

  • Glucocorticoid-receptor complex modulates gene transcription: induces annexin-1 (inhibits phospholipase A₂ → ↓ prostaglandins, leukotrienes, PAF) and negatively regulates NF-κB/AP-1.
  • Suppresses pro-inflammatory cytokine genes — ↓ IL-1, IL-2, IL-6, TNF-α, GM-CSF — blunting the cytokine storm; ↓ acute-phase reactants, ↓ ELAM-1/ICAM-1 (impaired leukocyte adhesion/chemotaxis).

Evidence — RECOVERY trial

Landmark open-label RCT (Horby et al., 2021): dexamethasone 6 mg PO/IV once daily for up to 10 days lowered 28-day mortality (22.9% vs 25.7%, RR 0.83, 95% CI 0.75–0.93). Largest benefit in invasively ventilated patients; benefit also in those on supplemental O₂; no benefit — and potential harm — in patients NOT requiring oxygen. Meta-analysis confirmed mortality benefit regardless of specific steroid, dose or oxygen level.

Indications & dosing

Use in hospitalised, hypoxic (moderate-severe/critical) disease — supplemental O₂, NIV/HFNC, or mechanical ventilation; avoid in mild/outpatient disease. Dexamethasone 6 mg/day × up to 10 days; equivalents = hydrocortisone, prednisolone, methylprednisolone. Higher doses (10–20 mg) gave no extra benefit. Combine with tocilizumab (IL-6R antagonist) or baricitinib (JAK1/2 inhibitor) when oxygen needs/inflammatory markers escalate.

Adverse effects

Hyperglycaemia (key in diabetics), fluid retention, leukocytosis, secondary bacterial & fungal infection, GI haemorrhage, adrenal suppression, myopathy.

High-yield exam pointers

  • Dexamethasone 6 mg × 10 days = mortality benefit only in oxygen-requiring COVID-19 — memorise the RECOVERY numbers.
  • Anti-inflammatory, NOT antiviral → harmful if given too early/in mild disease.
  • India: steroid overuse + uncontrolled hyperglycaemia → COVID-associated mucormycosis ("black fungus," 2021 wave).
Recent advances: WHO living guideline and NIH/IDSA recommend systemic corticosteroids for severe/critical COVID-19, with a conditional recommendation against use in non-severe disease. Rational steroid stewardship (right patient, 6 mg, ≤10 days, glycaemic monitoring) was reinforced post-2021 to curb the mucormycosis epidemic.
▸★Routes of drug delivery to the lungs — factors governing pulmonary deposition & inhalational devices.

Rationale for the inhaled route

The lung offers a large, highly vascular absorptive surface with direct access to airway target cells, allowing topical (inhaled) delivery for airway disease (asthma, COPD) as well as systemic delivery of peptides. Inhalation places drug directly at the site of action, so a lower dose achieves the effect with faster onset and fewer systemic side effects than the oral route — for β2-agonists/corticosteroids the inhaled route is always preferred.

Factors governing pulmonary deposition

  • Particle size (MMAD) is critical: optimum 2–5 µm. Larger particles impact in the upper airways/oropharynx; smaller particles stay suspended and are exhaled. Drugs targeting small airways need ~1 µm particles (HFA formulation).
  • Technique/coordination: with a conventional pMDI only ~10–20% of the dose reaches the lower airways; 80–90% is swallowed, absorbed from the GI tract and undergoes first-pass metabolism.

  • Soft-mist inhalers and DPIs reduce the coordination burden of conventional pMDIs.

Inflammation is now increasingly targeted directly (e.g. anti-IL-1β/colchicine programmes) rather than relying on statin pleiotropy alone.
▸★★Structure-activity relationship (SAR) of corticosteroids.

The basic nucleus

Corticosteroids are 21-carbon steroids built on the pregnane (cyclopentanoperhydrophenanthrene) nucleus — three 6-carbon rings (A, B, C) plus one 5-carbon ring (D), with a C17 side chain. Cortisol (hydrocortisone) is the prototype glucocorticoid, aldosterone the prototype mineralocorticoid. Deoxycorticosterone (DOCA) is the parent skeleton from which substitutions are reckoned — itself a selective mineralocorticoid.

Steroid nucleus: Rings A-B-C-D; key carbons — C1-C2 (Δ1 site), C3 (3-keto group), C4-C5 (Δ4 double bond), C9, C11 (11β-OH), C16, C17 (17α-OH), C21 (21-OH). The pharmacophore for receptor binding sits at Δ4-3-keto + 11β-OH + 17α-OH + 21-OH.

Features essential for activity

  • Δ4,5 double bond (ring A) + 3-keto group — obligatory for receptor binding; hepatic reduction yields inactive tetrahydro metabolites.
  • 11β-hydroxyl — essential for glucocorticoid activity. 11-keto pro-drugs (cortisone, prednisone) are inactive until hepatic reduction to the 11β-OH form (cortisol, prednisolone) — avoid in severe liver disease.
  • 17α-hydroxyl — required for full anti-inflammatory/glucocorticoid activity.
  • 21-hydroxyl + 20-keto with the C17 side chain — required; C21-OH is the site of esterification (formulation tool).

▸★★★Emergency & hormonal contraception — classification, mechanism of action & adverse effects.

Definition

Hormonal contraceptives are estrogen and/or progestin preparations used for reversible suppression of fertility. Emergency (post-coital) contraception (EC) is the use of a drug or copper IUD after unprotected or inadequately protected intercourse to prevent unintended pregnancy — a back-up, not a routine, method, and not abortifacient (oral EC does not disrupt an already-implanted pregnancy).

Combined oral contraceptive (COC) — composition & regimen

A 19-nortestosterone progestin (antiovulatory) + small-dose estrogen (EE) that enhances efficacy and ensures cycle control. EE 30 µg is the threshold dose, reducible to 20 µg with a potent progestin. 3rd-generation progestins (desogestrel, gestodene, norgestimate) are less androgenic but carry a marginally higher VTE signal. Regimen: 1 tablet daily ×21 days from day 5 of menses, then a 7-day pill-free gap (withdrawal bleed); the progestin protects against endometrial hyperplasia from unopposed estrogen.

Mechanism of action of regular hormonal contraceptives

  1. Inhibition of gonadotropin release (primary for COC) — progestin blunts LH pulse frequency, estrogen suppresses FSH; together they abolish the mid-cycle LH surge → anovulation.
  2. Thick, hostile cervical mucus (progestin effect) — impairs sperm penetration; operates with all methods except the post-coital pill.
  3. Out-of-phase endometrium — unsuitable for nidation (most important for minipills and the post-coital pill).
  4. Altered uterine/tubal motility disfavouring fertilization (contributes to minipill efficacy).
HPO-axis feedback: GnRH → anterior pituitary → FSH (↓ by estrogen) and LH surge (↓ by progestin) → ovary/follicle → "no ovulation" when feedback is hormonally suppressed.

Adverse effects & contraindications (combined pill)

Minor: nausea, headache, breakthrough bleeding, breast tenderness; later weight gain, acne/hirsutism, chloasma. Serious: VTE/PE (estrogen-driven), arterial events (MI, stroke), ↑BP, gallstones, rare hepatoma. Absolute C/I: thromboembolic/coronary/cerebrovascular disease, moderate-severe HTN/hyperlipidaemia, active liver disease, genital/breast malignancy, porphyria, impending major surgery; smokers >35 yr are high-risk. Non-contraceptive benefits: ↓ endometrial/ovarian cancer, ↓ menstrual blood loss, dysmenorrhoea/PMS relief, ↓ PID.

Mechanism of emergency contraceptive agents

The unifying principle: oral EC works chiefly by delaying/inhibiting ovulation — NOT abortifacient.

  • Levonorgestrel 1.5 mg — inhibits/delays ovulation by blunting the pre-ovulatory LH surge if given before the surge begins; ineffective once the surge has started — explains its time-sensitivity (≤72 h).
  • Ulipristal acetate — selective progesterone-receptor modulator; delays follicular rupture even at/just after LH-surge onset — a "later window" of action that extends efficacy to 120 h. May additionally impair endometrial receptivity.
  • Copper IUD — acts on gametes/fertilization, not ovulation: copper ions are toxic to sperm/ova and induce a sterile endometrial inflammatory reaction preventing fertilization/implantation — effective even after ovulation, hence highest efficacy.

Adverse effects of EC

Nausea/vomiting (main, much less with LNG/UPA than Yuzpe); menstrual changes (LNG brings menses earlier, UPA later); headache, breast tenderness. Enzyme inducers reduce EC efficacy; after UPA, delay restarting regular hormonal contraception (barrier ≥7 days).

High-yield exam pointers

  • Primary mechanism of COC = suppression of ovulation (abolished LH surge); minipill/post-coital pill rely more on cervical mucus + out-of-phase endometrium.
  • LNG 1.5 mg ≤72 h (works only before LH surge) vs UPA 30 mg ≤120 h (works at/after surge) — UPA is more versatile.
  • Copper IUD is the most effective EC and the preferred choice in obese women or delayed presentation.
  • Oral EC is NOT an abortifacient — key counselling/ethics point.
Recent advances: a randomised non-inferiority trial confirmed UPA is at least as effective as LNG within 72 h and superior in the 72–120 h window, establishing the 5-day oral EC standard. Cochrane 2019 (115 trials) ranked efficacy roughly as mid-dose mifepristone ≈ UPA > levonorgestrel > Yuzpe. The LNG 52-mg IUS is now shown non-inferior to the copper IUD for EC, expanding hormonal intrauterine options. Oral EC shows reduced efficacy at higher body weight; the copper IUD remains most reliable in obese women.
▸★★Bisphosphonates in postmenopausal osteoporosis — classification, mechanism & clinical use.

Definition: bisphosphonates (BPNs) are stable pyrophosphate analogues (P–C–P bond replacing P–O–P) that chelate Ca²⁺ and bind avidly to bone undergoing active remodelling. They are the most effective antiresorptive drugs and first-line therapy in postmenopausal osteoporosis, raising BMD and reducing vertebral, non-vertebral and hip fractures.

Classification (by generation)

GenerationDrugsRelative potency
1st (non-nitrogen)Etidronate, clodronate, tiludronate~1 (least potent)
2nd (amino-BPN)Alendronate, pamidronate, ibandronate10–100×
3rd (N-heterocyclic)Risedronate, zoledronateup to 10,000×

Mechanism

BPNs concentrate at remodelling sites, bind hydroxyapatite, and are taken up by osteoclasts in the acidic resorption lacuna. Nitrogen-containing BPNs inhibit farnesyl pyrophosphate (FPP) synthase in the mevalonate pathway → block prenylation of Ras-like GTPases → loss of osteoclast cytoskeleton/ruffled border → osteoclast inactivation/apoptosis. Non-nitrogen BPNs are metabolised to cytotoxic ATP analogues triggering osteoclast apoptosis by a distinct mechanism.

Diagram: HMG-CoA → mevalonate → FPP synthase (✗ blocked by N-BPN) → geranylgeranyl-PP → protein prenylation → osteoclast survival.

Pharmacokinetics & dosing

  • Oral bioavailability <1% (alendronate/risedronate), reduced further by food/divalent cations — take fasting, empty stomach, full glass of water, remain upright ≥30 min, don't chew.
  • ~50% sequestered in bone (skeletal t½ of alendronate ≈10 years); rest excreted unchanged renally — avoid if CrCl <30–35 mL/min.
  • Alendronate 10 mg/day or 70 mg/week; risedronate 5 mg/day or 35 mg/week; zoledronate 5 mg IV once yearly.

Uses & adverse effects

Uses: postmenopausal/age-related/glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis, Paget disease, hypercalcaemia of malignancy, osteolytic metastases. ADRs: esophagitis/GI irritation (oral), osteonecrosis of the jaw (rare, more with IV in malignancy), atypical subtrochanteric fractures (long-term use), acute flu-like reaction with first IV dose, hypocalcaemia.

Drug holiday: after ~5 years oral (~3 years IV zoledronate) therapy in patients not at high fracture risk, to limit ONJ/atypical-fracture risk; high-risk patients continue.

Recent advances: ACP network meta-analysis (2023) confirms bisphosphonates/denosumab reduce hip, vertebral and clinical fractures; abaloparatide/teriparatide/sequential romosozumab→alendronate may outperform bisphosphonates in very-high-risk older women. Guidelines (Osteoporosis Canada 2023, Austrian Society 2024) have shifted to risk-targeted, FRAX-based treatment thresholds; "anabolic-first then antiresorptive" sequencing with mandatory bridging is now standard.
▸★Radioactive iodine (¹³¹I) in thyroid disorders.

Definition: ¹³¹I is the radioisotope of choice for thyroid disorders, given orally as sodium ¹³¹I; exploits the thyroid's avid iodine trapping to deliver targeted internal radiation. Physical t½ 8 days (effective ~5 days); emits both γ-rays (imaging) and β-particles (tissue destruction).

Mechanism

  • Trapped via the Na⁺/I⁻ symporter (NIS) like stable ¹²⁷I, organified and stored in colloid.
  • β-particles penetrate only 0.5–2 mm → act almost exclusively on follicular cells from within → pyknosis, necrosis, fibrosis, sparing adjacent tissue.
  • γ-radiation is used for external scanning/uptake (RAIU) studies.

▸★★★"Prescribing PPIs — time to pause & rethink?" Role of PPIs in PUD, current status, misuse & ADRs; compare P-CABs with PPIs.

PPIs — role & mechanism

Omeprazole, pantoprazole, esomeprazole, rabeprazole. Irreversibly inhibit the gastric H⁺/K⁺-ATPase (proton pump) → most potent acid suppression. Uses: peptic ulcer, GERD, H. pylori eradication (with antibiotics), NSAID-ulcer prophylaxis, Zollinger-Ellison, stress-ulcer prophylaxis.

Misuse / "time to rethink"

  • Widely over-prescribed & continued without indication ("just in case"), inappropriate long-term use, lack of de-prescribing review.

Adverse effects of long-term use

  • ↓ absorption: vitamin B12, magnesium, calcium, iron → osteoporosis/fractures.
  • ↑ infection: C. difficile, community-acquired pneumonia, enteric infections.
  • Hypomagnesaemia, ↑ risk of CKD/AKI & interstitial nephritis, rebound acid hypersecretion, ?dementia (association), clopidogrel interaction (omeprazole — CYP2C19).

Section 9 · Special & Regulatory Topics

▸★★Constitution, functions & guidelines of CCSEA/IAEC/CPCSEA; methods of euthanasia approved for rodents; parallels with the IEC for clinical trials.

CCSEA / CPCSEA

CCSEA (Committee for Control & Supervision of Experiments on Animals; formerly CPCSEA) — statutory body under the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Act 1960 (MoEFCC). Registers animal facilities, frames guidelines, and oversees ethical animal use; ensures 3R compliance.

IAEC — constitution

  • Chairperson; Member-Secretary; biological/veterinary scientists; a scientist from a different discipline; a non-scientific socially-aware member; and a CCSEA-nominated main & link nominee (mandatory).

Functions

  • Review & approve animal protocols (justification, numbers, species, endpoints); ensure humane care, anaesthesia/analgesia, 3R; inspect facilities; maintain records; can stop non-compliant work.

Approved euthanasia methods for rodents

  • CO₂ inhalation (gradual fill — most common), overdose of anaesthetic (e.g. pentobarbital).
  • Physical (require trained personnel/justification): cervical dislocation (small rodents), decapitation — often after sedation.

▸★Principles for prescribing drugs in patients with renal and hepatic dysfunction.

Renal dysfunction

  • Estimate function (eGFR/CrCl, Cockcroft-Gault); adjust dose of renally-cleared drugs.
  • Methods: ↓ dose and/or ↑ dosing interval; give loading dose normally (Vd unchanged), reduce maintenance.
  • Avoid nephrotoxins (aminoglycosides, NSAIDs, contrast); watch active/renally-excreted metabolites (morphine, pethidine).
  • Account for dialysis (supplemental dosing for dialysable drugs); monitor K⁺ with RAAS blockers.

Hepatic dysfunction

  • No single reliable marker — use Child-Pugh score to gauge severity.
  • ↓ first-pass metabolism → ↑ bioavailability of high-extraction drugs (propranolol, morphine); ↓ clearance of hepatically-metabolised drugs.
  • Hypoalbuminaemia → ↑ free fraction of protein-bound drugs (phenytoin, warfarin).
  • Avoid hepatotoxins (paracetamol high dose, methotrexate); caution with sedatives (precipitate encephalopathy); altered coagulation → care with anticoagulants.
  • General principle: "start low, go slow," use drugs with wide therapeutic index, monitor clinically & with TDM where possible.

▸🩺 CaseA patient with sudden breathlessness, frothy sputum and bilateral basal crepitations is in acute pulmonary oedema. Immediate drugs?
Clue: Reduce preload quickly.

Sit up, high-flow oxygen, IV furosemide (venodilation + diuresis lowers preload), GTN (lowers preload/afterload), morphine in selected cases; treat the precipitant; CPAP/NIV if hypoxic.

▸🩺 CaseA 60-year-old man with HFrEF (EF 30%) is on furosemide only. What disease-modifying therapy should be added?
Clue: The four pillars.

Add the four pillars: ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan) or ACEi/ARB + beta-blocker (carvedilol/bisoprolol) + MRA (spironolactone) + SGLT2 inhibitor (dapagliflozin) — each reduces mortality; the diuretic treats symptoms only.

▸🩺 CaseA 72-year-old woman presents with new atrial fibrillation. How do you decide on anticoagulation and which agent?
Clue: CHA2DS2-VASc.

Assess stroke risk with CHA2DS2-VASc (and bleeding with HAS-BLED). If anticoagulation is indicated, prefer a DOAC (apixaban/rivaroxaban/dabigatran) over warfarin — except mechanical valves or moderate-severe mitral stenosis (warfarin). Add rate/rhythm control.

▸🩺 CaseA man with crushing chest pain has ST-elevation on ECG. Outline the acute pharmacotherapy.
Clue: Antiplatelet plus reperfusion.

MONA-B + DAPT + reperfusion: aspirin + a P2Y12 inhibitor (ticagrelor/prasugrel), an anticoagulant (heparin), nitrate, analgesia, beta-blocker; primary PCI preferred, otherwise a fibrinolytic (tenecteplase) if no contraindication; then statin and ACEi.

▸🩺 CaseA CKD patient on ramipril and spironolactone presents with potassium 6.5 and peaked T waves. What happened and how is it managed?
Clue: Dual RAAS blockade.

ACEi + MRA + renal impairment → hyperkalaemia. Emergency: IV calcium gluconate (cardioprotection), insulin-dextrose, salbutamol; stop the offending drugs; loop diuretic / potassium binder / dialysis. Avoid combining ACEi with ARB or MRA in advanced CKD without monitoring.

▸🩺 CaseA patient presents with BP 230/130, headache and papilloedema. Treatment principle?
Clue: Controlled lowering.

Hypertensive emergency → IV agents (labetalol, nicardipine, nitroprusside, GTN, esmolol); lower BP gradually (about 25% in the first hour) to avoid cerebral and renal hypoperfusion.

▸🩺 CaseA patient on simvastatin is started on clarithromycin and develops severe muscle pain and dark urine. Explain.
Clue: CYP3A4 inhibition.

Clarithromycin inhibits CYP3A4 → raised simvastatin levels → myopathy/rhabdomyolysis (check CK and renal function). Stop the statin, hydrate; avoid simvastatin with strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (use pravastatin or rosuvastatin, or hold the statin).

▸🩺 CaseA young woman has rest angina with transient ST-elevation but normal coronary arteries. Best therapy and what to avoid?
Clue: Coronary vasospasm.

Prinzmetal (variant) angina. Use calcium channel blockers + nitrates; avoid non-selective beta-blockers (unopposed alpha may worsen spasm). Stop smoking.

▸🩺 CaseA patient on round-the-clock isosorbide dinitrate finds it no longer relieves angina. Why, and the fix?
Clue: Tolerance.

Nitrate tolerance from continuous exposure. Provide a nitrate-free interval (about 8–12 hours/day) to restore responsiveness.

▸🩺 CaseA diabetic with heart failure needs better glycaemic control. Which add-on helps the heart, and which should be avoided?
Clue: SGLT2 inhibitor.

Add an SGLT2 inhibitor (dapagliflozin/empagliflozin) — lowers glucose and reduces HF hospitalisation and mortality. Avoid pioglitazone (fluid retention) and saxagliptin (increased HF hospitalisation) in heart failure.
